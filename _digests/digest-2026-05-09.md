## Today's Top 3

### The Culture of AI Engineering ⚡
**Noah Brier / Thesis** · rss · 4 mins

1. The "software factory" metaphor — used by StrongDM, Dan Shapiro's "Dark Factory" framework, and Factory.ai (Sequoia/Khosla-backed, agents called Droids) — is wrong because Ford's factory optimizes for defect elimination and zero variance, which is the wrong target. The real bottleneck is product-market fit: building something the market wants. A software *company* is better modeled on Andy Warhol's factory, which was also throughput-focused but organized around maintaining a single creative vision across all output.

2. AI didn't solve the coordination problem — it reshuffled it. Brier built Percolate from 0 to 100 people in under three years and spent half his time as CEO on culture, drawing on Ben Horowitz's definition: "how your company makes decisions when you're not there." That same problem now applies to agents. Open source shows the early failure mode: GitHub is now flooded with AI-generated pull requests that maintainers must sift through, a problem that didn't exist before.

3. Agents behave like unboarded new hires — the code works, but it ignores obvious abstractions and stylistic norms already present in the codebase. Companies write onboarding documents and run training for human colleagues but almost never do this for agents. The article promises a framework drawn from Stewart Brand's pace layers to achieve this alignment across humans and agents, though the full framework is paywalled.

**Quotable:**
> "It's a software *company*, not a software *factory*." — *on why the dominant metaphor for AI-assisted development gets the hardest problem wrong*

### What your body stops doing when you're stressed ⚡
**Big Think** · email · 11 mins

1. Columbia professor Martin Picard reframes mitochondria as "information processors" rather than mere "powerhouses." They communicate with each other, change their number and structure, adjust energy production, and are the only organelles with their own DNA (mtDNA, inherited exclusively from mothers). They don't just enable life — they integrate how we live into the flow of energy through the body.

2. Every biological process — cortisol rising, neurons firing, the microbiome functioning — sits downstream of energy flow, making energy a more foundational explanatory layer than genes, hormones, or diet alone. Interventions already known to improve health (sleep, exercise, meditation, diet) all work by increasing or redistributing energy within the body.

3. Stress is a measurable metabolic drain: Picard's lab exposed human cells to cortisol-like signals and found they increased energy expenditure to roughly 60% above baseline. That surplus must come from somewhere, and cells under higher chronic stress demand were shown to age faster — meaning the stress response literally siphons energy away from long-term cellular repair and maintenance.

4. Picard and colleague Nirosha Murugan formalized the "energy resistance principle": electrons flow from food through mitochondria to oxygen, and health depends on keeping resistance to that flow in a Goldilocks zone. Too little resistance and energy passes through without being converted to ATP (like pedaling a bike with the chain off). Too much resistance and the system backs up, increasing oxidative stress, inflammation, and cellular damage — the hallmarks of aging and disease.

5. GDF15 (growth differentiation factor 15) is a measurable molecular signal that rises when energy flow becomes strained. Picard suggests it could eventually be tracked via saliva-based wearables to give individuals a real-time "energy score" — a proxy for whether the body is under energetic stress.

6. There is a continuous feedback loop between mental states and mitochondrial biology: the brain consumes roughly 20% of the body's total energy, and Picard's studies show that psychological states can influence mitochondrial function, which in turn shapes psychological experience. This provides a mechanistic account of why ruminating about a stressor causes physical symptoms and why variations in mitochondrial biology may contribute to mental disorders.

**Quotable:**
> "We tend to see humans as collections of biochemical parts, and our medical practices focus on what goes wrong with the structure of humans. But that framework has struggled to explain basic questions, like why stress harms us or how mental states shape physical health." — *Martin Picard, Columbia professor and mitochondrial psychobiologist*

### Going Private: Talking 'bout a revolution ⚡
**Bloomberg** · email · 11 mins

1. Apollo committed to providing daily pricing on its entire $830 billion credit book by end-September 2026 — a first for private markets at this scale. CEO Marc Rowan framed it as a trust imperative: "When public markets reprice, private markets should too." The move is expected to pressure rivals who will struggle to explain why they can't do the same, though one unnamed senior executive at a competing firm dismissed daily pricing as "an art rather than a science" and said institutional investors were broadly content with quarterly marks.

2. Apollo also partnered with Intercontinental Exchange (ICE) to assign a standardized ICE ID to every asset in its portfolio — what Rowan called "the beginning of standardization across this marketplace." The firm's traded-asset volumes now exceed $13 billion, framing liquidity infrastructure as a prerequisite for the public-private convergence Apollo is betting on. Both moves — daily pricing and market-making infrastructure — are offensive plays designed to pull retail and institutional capital into private markets by reducing the opacity that critics cite as the sector's core vulnerability.

3. Apollo crossed $1 trillion in AUM on record Q1 inflows of $115 billion ($300 billion over the prior 12 months), dwarfing peers: Blackstone took in $68.5 billion, KKR $27.8 billion, and Ares $29.5 billion in the same period. Fee-related earnings jumped 30% to $728 million, and fee-generating AUM rose 40% to $836 billion. The flagship private equity strategy posted a small loss of 0.3%, but hybrid value (between debt and equity) returned 4%, and opportunistic credit gained 2.5%.

4. DoubleLine Capital CEO Jeffrey Gundlach, who manages nearly $100 billion, accused financial advisers at the Milken Institute Global Conference of short-changing clients by keeping private credit products "opaque and not granularly described." Roughly $4.6 billion is currently trapped behind redemption gates at major private credit funds that branded themselves "semi-liquid" but could not honor withdrawal requests. Gundlach compared the dynamic to the dot-com era and mortgage-backed securities boom: "That's why everybody wants their money back: they're starting to realize they might be the bag-holder."

5. Blackstone and fellow lenders injected fresh capital into Medallia — the customer-experience software firm owned by Thoma Bravo, which had refused to put in new equity — effectively taking control of the company. Non-accrual loans at the $13.9 billion Blackstone Secured Lending Fund (BXSL) jumped roughly 4 percentage points to 4.7% in Q1, driven by Medallia (marked at 60.3), dental chain Affordable Care (69.8), and previously undisclosed building-products distributor Paramount Global Services owned by Platinum Equity (65). Those three names account for over 88% of non-accruals across BXSL's 316-loan portfolio; net asset value per share fell 2.5% to $26.26.

6. The credit secondaries market is on pace to hit $50 billion in transaction volume in 2026, double 2025 levels, as investors seek exits from private credit funds amid moribund IPO markets and choppy M&A. HarbourVest Partners' Greg Ciesielski (head of credit secondaries at the $150 billion firm) argued that most sellers are pension funds rebalancing allocations — not distressed holders — and that pricing dispersion across managers will narrow as third-party valuers become standard. Blue Owl Capital is separately preparing its debut credit secondaries fund, with Co-CEO Marc Lipschultz calling it "a great place for a really thoughtful buyer" given the surplus of sellers.

**Quotable:**
> "Semi-liquid is kind of a diabolical name. Half the time it's liquid. It's liquid when you don't want your money, and it's illiquid when you do want your money." — *Jeffrey Gundlach, DoubleLine Capital CEO, at the Milken Institute Global Conference*

## AI & Technology

### The revolution will be ticketed 📌
**Benn Stancil** · email · 9 mins

1. AI companies pitch civilization-scale ambitions — Anthropic has received preemptive offers to raise $50B at an $850–900B valuation; OpenAI told early investors to "consider their investment a donation" since money's role might be unclear in the world they'd create — yet when they incorporate, the IRS dropdown forces them to select "Information Technology." IT is the hall monitor, the mandatory anti-phishing training with the disabled skip button, the flaky VPN. The gap between the self-image and the bureaucratic classification is the joke the whole piece turns on.

2. Block, Coinbase, and Cloudflare each announced major layoffs in rapid succession, all explicitly crediting AI productivity gains. Block cut from over 10,000 to under 6,000 employees (~50% reduction); Coinbase cut ~14%; Cloudflare cut more than 1,100 employees globally and reported AI usage up 600% in three months. Whether AI is the real cause or a convenient cover for over-hiring corrections is contested, but all three companies are actively restructuring around it.

3. Block, Coinbase, and Cloudflare used strikingly near-identical language in their announcements — "intelligence at the core, humans on the edge" appeared in both Block's and Coinbase's communications, and both independently landed on the same "player-coach" metaphor for managers who must also do individual contributor work. This convergence suggests not a genuine discovery but a shared ideology spreading through tech leadership, with each CEO authoring their own version of the same template.

4. Microsoft built the personal computer and Windows, but the actual transformation of the modern corporation was designed by internal IT teams — "Jeff in IT" — testing new ideas against their specific problems across millions of companies. The same logic applies now: OpenAI and Anthropic can build models and generic products, but Box CEO Aaron Levie predicts the work required to implement agents inside enterprises will "exceed anything we imagine today." The long tail of internal teams doing that implementation will be far larger than the AI companies themselves, and their experiments will become tomorrow's standards.

5. Anthropic signed a deal with SpaceX to use all compute capacity at the Colossus 1 data center in Memphis (300+ megawatts), also expressing interest in multiple gigawatts of space-based compute. Using a bike race analogy, a competitor near the front of the peloton can draft and sprint back into the lead; a competitor in 29th place cannot. The secondary speculation: if AI models converge toward commodity parity, the strategic moat shifts from model quality to raw compute — and AI companies that accumulated massive GPU clusters may end up as generic compute providers, the same way AWS emerged from Amazon's need to run its own infrastructure.

**Quotable:**
> "The final cathedral of the modern corporation was designed by Jeff in IT as much as it was by Silicon Valley's storied founders." — *on why internal enterprise teams, not AI labs, will determine how AI actually reshapes work*

### Clouded Judgement 5.8.26 - When Machines Out-Eat Humans 📌
**Clouded Judgement by Jamin Ball** · email · 8 mins

1. **A quantitative AGI definition: when machines out-eat humans on energy.** AGI arrives when the marginal unit of energy is more valuable sent to a GPU than to a human — i.e., when total compute energy consumption exceeds total human energy consumption. Every other AGI definition is philosophical; this one yields a specific year, 2033, by running the math on current and projected data center capacity vs. US energy usage.

2. **US data centers currently consume ~1% of what humans do — a long way from parity.** US data center capacity sits around 40 GW; at 80% utilization that produces ~280 TWh per year. The moderate estimate of US human energy consumption (residential + personal transport + commercial buildings + consumer goods) is ~20,500 TWh. The Colossus 1 facility that xAI rented to Anthropic — 300 MW, the largest known single site — would consume 2.1 TWh annually at 80% utilization, illustrating how far individual facilities are from moving the needle on their own.

3. **OpenAI's 2GW→30GW expansion by 2030 changes the calculus dramatically.** If OpenAI scales from 2 GW to 30 GW (a 15x increase in five years) and the rest of the industry grows at the same rate, total US data center power reaches roughly 600 GW by 2030, producing ~12,600 TWh — nearly 40% of the ~21,440 TWh projected for human consumption that day. Continuing both curves at the same compounding rates puts machines at ~3 TW (~22,000 TWh) and humans at ~22,000 TWh simultaneously in 2033.

4. **Public SaaS valuations remain compressed, with a steep growth premium.** The overall median EV/NTM revenue multiple for public cloud software sits at 3.2x, while the top-5 companies trade at 21.1x. The growth bifurcation is stark: companies growing >22% NTM command 12.6x multiples vs. 2.5x for those growing <15%. Operating fundamentals: median NTM growth 13%, gross margin 76%, FCF margin 20%, net retention 110%, CAC payback 36 months.

**Quotable:**
> "I think we'll hit AGI (or we can claim AGI) when as a society we decide the marginal unit of energy is better spent on a GPU than on a human." — *Jamin Ball proposing an energy-parity definition of AGI*

### Google's GCC Warning Signal 📎
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. At Cloud Next 2026, Google stopped selling AI copilots and launched the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform — a full stack for creating, deploying, supervising, and auditing autonomous agents that act on their own across company systems for hours or days without human prompting. Microsoft, Amazon, and Salesforce are racing to build identical stacks. The competitive question is no longer whose model is smartest but who gives enterprises the best tools to deploy and control AI agent armies reliably.

2. The control layer — the governance rules that decide which agents can act, on whose behalf, and with what audit trail — is now more valuable than the underlying AI models themselves, which are commoditizing. The next bottleneck is not AI capability but enterprise context: messy data, weak metadata, and no shared semantic layer for agents to interpret what data actually means. Whoever owns that data and governance layer owns the economic value.

3. AI pricing is shifting from cost-per-prompt to cost-per-completed-task, which breaks every pricing model built on human time. To accelerate enterprise adoption, Google created a $750 million partner fund and embedded its own engineers alongside Accenture, Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, Capgemini, Cognizant, TCS, and HCL Tech to wire these agents directly into client workflows — meaning the integrators, not the enterprises, will own the agent architecture layer.

4. India's 2,000+ GCCs employ close to two million people across finance, technology, and customer operations — a sector built on the equation that skilled Indian workers cost less than skilled workers abroad. Nasscom President Rajesh Nambiar described GCCs as "AI-first" hubs that now "own a broader problem for their enterprise," but Google's shift from labour-arbitrage to cost-per-task automation directly threatens that value proposition. Unless Indian GCCs quickly capture AI oversight, enterprise data governance, and decision-making roles, they risk being reduced from strategic hubs to execution back offices as global parents and consulting firms seize control of the agent architecture layer.

**Quotable:**
> "The new GCCs have been positioned as AI-first GCCs, which means that they've been able to do a lot more interesting work, own a broader problem for their enterprise, represent much more than probably some of the service providers." — *Nasscom President Rajesh Nambiar, speaking to The Core before Google's agent platform announcement*

## Finance & Capital Markets

### Investor Relations Goes Retail 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. Small retail shareholders are a net cost to most public companies, not a constituency worth courting. A holder of 10 shares at $50 each contributes minimal capital but creates real liabilities — they may demand a printed annual report or proxy materials by mail, and Broadridge charges surprisingly high fees just to send that letter. IR rules were written when commissions were high and information was expensive to find; the cost structure has inverted, but the obligations haven't.

2. Institutional IR is a bilateral intelligence operation, not just disclosure. Companies use investor meetings to extract alternative data they can't buy directly — crossover funds triangulate cell tower pings with credit card transactions to benchmark a retailer's conversion rates against competitors, then offer that flattering comparison to win deal access. Management learns what multiple the market will pay for different capital allocation moves; investors learn what's happening inside the business. Retail shareholders offer none of this.

3. Retail attention is volatile and nearly impossible to systematize — but occasionally enormous in scale. A penny stock exiting the karaoke business, pivoting to AI logistics, and issuing a press release shifted $17 billion in market cap (mostly via declines at other logistics companies). GameStop's pre-Ryan Cohen management treated the meme frenzy as a "Risk Factor" in their 10-K, with an apologetic footnote noting that a $100 investment worth $19 in January 2020 became $1,607 a year later. Cohen arrived and immediately understood the playbook the old team had fumbled.

4. AMC turned retail populism into a capital-raising mechanism using consumer marketing psychology. CEO Adam Aron publicly declared he was "following the apes," offered free popcorn to shareholders during summer 2021, bought a stake in a gold mine, and — when shareholders rejected more share issuance — issued preferred shares with equivalent economic rights anyway, tickered APE. The popcorn wasn't a perk; it was a direct-marketing hook: "Act now! For a limited time, you'll also get a FREE..." The capital was real; so were the destructive allocation decisions that followed.

5. SpaceX is hiring a dedicated retail IR person with two of five listed job responsibilities explicitly referencing retail investors — signaling a deliberate strategy, not a footnote. As Elon's largest eventual public company, SpaceX is the primary funding vehicle for the entire Elon complex, giving him structural incentive to maximize retail enthusiasm and minimize the risk premium retail holders demand. The upside: if retail shareholders accept lower returns because holding SpaceX is "entertaining," they're voluntarily subsidizing the company. The downside, as AMC demonstrated, is that shareholder populism pressures management toward decisions designed to please rather than decisions designed to work.

**Quotable:**
> "if shareholders demand a lower risk premium to hold SpaceX, because it's entertaining to hold SpaceX, then they subsidize the company. But they're fine with that being part of the deal!" — *on the structural logic of retail IR for spectacle-driven companies*

## Business & Consumer Tech

### Titans of gaming say meh to AI 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 7 mins

1. Sony and Nintendo stocks are having a historically bad year, driven by a memory chip supply crunch that's squeezing the hardware economics both companies depend on to get consoles into consumers' hands. The big money in gaming comes from software, but you need hardware in the market first — and skyrocketing component costs are making that harder and more expensive for both firms simultaneously.

2. Both companies are actually performing well underneath the hardware pain. Nintendo launched surprise hit *Pokémon Pokopia* in March and *The Super Mario Galaxy Movie* in April, and its games consistently score at the top of review sites — last year's *Donkey Kong Bananza* earned multiple 10/10 ratings. Sony pivoted early to de-emphasize hardware in favor of IP (music publishing rights, film, video game franchises), and its operating profit rose 13% in the fiscal year ending March.

3. The market forgives Big Tech's AI spending (Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft are collectively spending past $700 billion annually on AI with questionable profit trajectories) but punishes gaming companies for having no AI narrative at all. Investors absorb a day or two of distress over massive AI capex and then rush back in — while Sony and Nintendo get no such halo for running solid businesses.

4. Gamers are actively hostile to AI: studios that deploy it face reflexive backlash over concerns ranging from devaluing human creativity to underwhelming output quality. This leaves Sony and Nintendo squeezed — the AI story that would soothe investors would antagonize their core audience. Nintendo has stayed almost entirely silent on AI; Sony on its latest earnings call announced a TSMC joint venture for next-generation image sensors and started using the phrase "physical AI," but it reads as an uncomfortable stretch for a company that would rather be tinkering with its next product.

**Quotable:**
> "If you care to remain on the good side of a gaming audience today, it's preferable not to beat your chest like Donkey Kong about how much you love AI." — *Vlad Savov, Bloomberg News tech editor, on the impossible position facing Sony and Nintendo*
