## Today's Top 3

### An Interview with Michael Morton About E-Commerce in the Age of AI ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 42 mins

1. The AI bear case for e-commerce stocks is structurally unfalsifiable — you can't kill it with a quarter or two of data the way you can kill a gross-margin-compression thesis. Stocks in Morton's coverage (e-commerce and marketplaces at MoffettNathanson) have had multiples compress 30% YTD, driven not by deteriorating fundamentals but by the unresolvable question of terminal growth rates in an AI world.

2. The bear case is also amorphous, shifting shape across consecutive investor meetings: one meeting says AI will automate all grocery and consumer-staples replenishment; the next says AI agents will replace marketplace apps for Uber and pizza; the next says Shopify will be vibe-coded into irrelevance. Each variant is unfalsifiable, making it impossible to clear the market's concern.

3. AI product discovery is measurably favoring brand.com over legacy marketplaces by 4-8x. In Google Search, brand.com already outpaced Amazon/Wayfair/Chewy/eBay/Etsy by roughly 2x. When Morton's team aggregated traffic sent from AI platforms (including AI Mode) to those same destinations, brand.com outpaced legacy marketplaces by 4-8x — a meaningful acceleration of an existing trend.

4. Shopify's merchant infrastructure is effectively impossible to replicate via vibe-coding. Its negotiating leverage with Stripe yields better fee structures than any individual merchant could secure; its one-click Meta CAPI (Conversions API) integration was personally brokered by Tobi Lütke and Mark Zuckerberg; and multi-channel sync across TikTok, Etsy, Amazon, and Walmart Marketplace involves dozens of API dependencies that, when they break, can destroy a merchant's entire top line. Even with unlimited engineers and a blank check from Anthropic, you could not recreate this stack at lower cost.

5. Tobi Lütke launched the Shopify Catalog to pre-empt AI discoverability frictions: it lets merchants pipe product feeds directly into AI platforms, bypassing unreliable raw HTML scraping. Google's comparable solution is StoreBot — a bot that places fake orders with fake credit cards on e-commerce sites to verify delivery speeds and inventory. Shopify is now extending Catalog access to non-Shopify merchants, positioning it as general e-commerce infrastructure.

6. ChatGPT Checkout failed because ChatGPT needed Amazon more than Amazon needed ChatGPT. Consumers quickly recognized incomplete inventory; ChatGPT was simultaneously attempting too many product directions at once. The lesson: retraining consumers into incomplete or parallel checkout flows is extraordinarily hard — Amazon literally patented one-click checkout to establish the baseline.

7. AI is settling into a referral channel rather than a distributor model, and this is actually the strategically favorable outcome for incumbent consumer-internet companies. Distributor = AI owns the checkout and reduces marketplaces to API calls; referral = AI sends higher-intent, further-down-funnel traffic to merchants who still own the customer relationship. Both Google and Meta already learned through failed experiments that owning checkout is less profitable and less scalable than owning the referral surface — OpenAI was about to relearn the same lesson.

8. Traffic growth from AI platforms to e-commerce, which was "vertical, up and to the right" as of November 2025, has since slowed. Amazon actively blocks most AI discovery platforms, contributing to the deceleration. Morton's interpretation is that a larger share of AI-referred clicks is going to brand.com rather than marketplaces — good for Shopify, but surprising given that models consistently ranked Amazon and Walmart first on trust, reviews, and price.

9. The historical record on automated pantry replenishment is uniformly discouraging for the "AI orders everything" thesis. In the early 1960s — pre-Walmart, smaller refrigerators, lower car ownership — only 30% of US milk consumption went through milkman delivery, even though milk is the most predictable, high-frequency grocery purchase imaginable. That share declined to zero as friction was removed. Amazon Subscribe & Save, back-calculated from Amazon's own "$1B saved" press release, accounts for just 1-3% of Amazon's 2023 GMV.

10. Piecemeal automation fails because the average US household purchases 250-300 distinct items per year. An agent that handles 10 items adds a layer of complexity without solving the overall problem — you still need to manage the remaining 240-290 items through other channels. The same logic applies to pizza-ordering via an AI agent: you still need to open a separate app to track the ETA.

11. Etsy earns over 100% of EBIT from on-site ads (per MoffettNathanson estimates), with no physical fulfillment layer. The Shopify Catalog now gives large Etsy merchants a viable exit: direct-to-AI discoverability at a fraction of Etsy's ~25% take rate. E-commerce businesses that aggregated fragmented supply and monetize purely through ads — without time-sensitive fulfillment to lock in merchants — are the most structurally exposed to AI disintermediation.

12. Amazon's on-site ad business is more resilient than feared, for two reasons. First, Mercado Libre was the first marketplace to deploy LLMs broadly across product search and reported that higher conversion rates and CPCs offset the decline in total clicks. Second, the pace of consumer adoption of AI search has been slower than Morton expected, giving Amazon time to embed sponsored prompts directly into its AI Mode results. Amazon's implicit compact with consumers — ads in exchange for near-magical delivery speeds — appears to be holding.

13. Amazon achieved price parity with Walmart in grocery essentially overnight. Morton's basket-pricing study (six large suburban US markets, running since ~2022) showed Amazon as mid-pack on grocery pricing for years. After Amazon expanded same-day grocery to 2,000+ US cities and refrigerated its fulfillment network, the latest pricing data shows full parity with Walmart. Amazon separately reported ~40x YoY growth in grocery in 2025 YTD.

14. Amazon's grocery price parity was achieved by reducing SKU depth — and that is also the ceiling of its grocery ambition. The average grocery store carries 40,000-50,000 SKUs. Walmart tried a SKU reduction years ago and reversed course after losing sales. Families with dietary restrictions, allergies, or long-tail needs cannot be served by Amazon's curated grocery layer; Morton still believes the marketplace model (DoorDash, Instacart) wins for the full household basket.

15. DoorDash built two-thirds of US restaurant delivery market share by doing the opposite of its competitors: starting in suburban markets with larger-ticket orders while Uber Eats and Grubhub burned money in cities. DoorDash is now applying the same operational playbook to grocery, growing 30-40% annually. It has built 100+ DashMarts (micro-warehouses for brand inventory) and DashLink (warehouse space rented to merchants, with Dashers running 70-package neighborhood routes) — effectively building a local FBA 2.0.

16. Same-day and next-day delivery has crossed a psychological threshold that structurally disadvantages bricks-and-mortar. Multiple operators (Walmart, Ocado, DoorDash) have independently reported conversion rate explosions when they offer same-day or next-day windows. The mechanism: once a consumer believes an item arrives in hours, the mental calculation against going to a store closes permanently, and FOMO ("better order now so I don't forget") accelerates the shift.

17. In the 18-24 month window, Waymo has effectively won the AV race in the US. The Waymo-Uber partnership has broken down; Uber is testing Nuro-Lucid (SF, year-end 2026) and Wayve (UK trials) as alternative AV partners. Waymo in mature markets like SF and LA is approaching high-20s daily trips per vehicle — Morton's model puts EBIT-per-mile breakeven at low-30s to mid-30s, meaning Waymo is close to or at unit economics in its strongest markets.

18. Waymo is importing Zeekr-built "Ojai" vans at roughly 300 per month despite 100% US tariffs on Chinese autos — a fact Morton discovered by analyzing ship bills of lading rather than VIN numbers. At that rate, Waymo's fleet roughly doubles in 12 months and mid-single-digit US rideshare market share comes into view. Waymo's willingness to absorb 100% tariffs signals that even at 2x the landed cost, the Zeekr platform makes economic sense.

19. Over a 3-5 year horizon, AV will likely follow every prior transportation-industry pattern: overbuild of operators, consolidation, price compression, volume explosion. Miles-driven data shows multiple non-US AV operators (predominantly Chinese, likely barred from the US market) running significant real-world mileage — indicating Waymo's tech stack is not the only viable one. LinkedIn scraping of former Waymo employees shows they have dispersed broadly across competitors including Zoox.

20. Waymo's internal strategic ambiguity — co-CEOs (Tekedra Mawakana and Dmitri Dolgov) apparently with divergent views; public statements simultaneously saying "we don't want to change tires" and announcing new 1P cities — has not hurt it because its lead is large enough that it doesn't yet need to make the licensing-vs.-vertical-integration choice. The Uber tension became visible when Uber chose San Francisco (Waymo's home market) for its Nuro-Lucid pilot and publicly highlighted Waymo failures during its earnings call.

21. Tesla's Cybercab at ~$30,000 is the wildcard that could render the entire AV competitive landscape moot. OEM production lines won't dedicate capacity to low-volume AV orders; every non-Tesla AV operator is constrained in vehicle supply. Tesla can produce purpose-built robotaxis at mass-production volumes and at a fraction of any competitor's cost per unit. If the technology actually works at scale, "it's game over for a lot of people."

**Quotable:**
> "In traditional Google Search, brand.com would outpace Amazon, Wayfair, Chewy, eBay, Etsy — the marketplaces — sometimes by about 2x. When we aggregate the traffic coming from AI platforms to the different marketplaces and brand.com, brand.com is outpacing what I'll call the legacy marketplaces 4-8x." — *Morton on the data behind his Shopify upgrade*

### Jeremy Grantham points to a watershed moment for big tech ⚡
**Bloomberg Odd Lots** · email · 9 mins

1. Jeremy Grantham — co-founder of GMO, with correct bubble calls on 1980s Japanese assets, early-2000s dot-com stocks, and 2008 subprime — argues that the Magnificent Seven previously operated as "near monopolies" in separate, parallel lanes with durable moats and strong pricing power: Apple owned consumer electronics, Microsoft enterprise software, Amazon retail, Google/Alphabet search and ads, Meta social networking, Tesla EVs, and Nvidia chips.

2. All seven have now converged on the same frontier: competing directly in AI. Apple is embedding AI into its device ecosystem; Microsoft is monetizing via Azure and Copilot; Amazon through AWS; Alphabet with Gemini plugged into search; Meta with Llama across social; Tesla repositioning itself as an AI and robotics company; and Nvidia supplying the chips powering all of it. The companies have gone from adjacent players to direct, existential competitors in one market.

3. Grantham's "dogfight" framing is the core valuation challenge: first-mover economics in a winner-take-most market mean all seven companies know the payoff is enormous, yet there can only be one winner. Seven cash-rich companies with deep understanding of monopoly value are now all fighting for the same prize simultaneously — a dynamic the market currently appears to be pricing as though multiple winners are possible.

4. The Grantham question markets haven't confronted: regardless of whether AI is a bubble, should the Magnificent Seven be valued as if their legacy moats still apply? The competitive insulation — the pricing power, the earnings predictability — that justified premium valuations was a product of those parallel, non-competing lanes. If those lanes have merged, the valuation premise has changed even if the cash flows haven't yet.

5. Kevin Warsh's debut FOMC press conference (June 2026) signaled a possible end to post-GFC communication norms: he declined to discuss the economy or inflation drivers, announced task forces instead, and nine members indicated preference for higher rates. The dot plot and press-conference format were innovations introduced by Bernanke after 2009 to address a specific problem — a market that didn't believe the Fed's commitment to keeping rates low amid economic slack. That problem no longer exists, and Warsh, a long-standing critic of forward guidance, may be reverting toward pre-GFC opacity, aided by a broader cultural shift away from the social-media-era expectation of institutional "backstage" transparency.

**Quotable:**
> "Just imagine 10 years from now looking back and saying you couldn't see the difference between seven easy monopolies and a dogfight of seven vicious, rich companies, huge cash flow, huge understanding of the virtues of being dominant, all deciding at the same time to fight out in one market." — *Jeremy Grantham on the Magnificent Seven's convergence onto AI*

### The Mom-and-Pop SaaS era has arrived ⚡
**Lenny's Newsletter** · email · 8 mins

1. **AI's biggest impact isn't developer productivity — it's economic participation.** For decades, software creation required access to a small, highly specialized group concentrated in a handful of tech hubs and funded by VC. That meant only ideas large enough to justify those costs ever got built. As AI collapses the cost of building, thousands of niches that could never justify a 5-figure offshore dev project — local soccer clubs, orthodontists, wedding photographers — finally can.

2. **Jevons Paradox predicts more software, not less.** When British steam engines became more efficient, coal usage tripled rather than fell. Fuel-efficient cars led people to drive farther; LED lights got put on everything. The same dynamic applies here: making software creation dramatically cheaper won't shrink the software market — it will expand it by enabling entirely new categories that previously couldn't exist.

3. **Lovable's platform data confirms the shift is already happening.** A study of builders on Lovable found 80% come from non-technical backgrounds — something that was simply impossible before. More striking: 55% of builders have more than 11 years of professional experience, suggesting domain expertise is replacing technical expertise as the primary input to software creation.

4. **The "translation layer" problem is solved when the expert becomes the builder.** Traditional software development required a subject matter expert to explain their world to a developer, who then imperfectly translated it into code. The mismatch produced mostly poor software. When the person closest to the problem builds the solution directly — a recruiter building recruiting software, a property manager building property management software — that failure mode disappears entirely.

5. **The winning opportunity sits between hobby and unicorn.** 80% of builders on Lovable intend to monetize; 35% are already generating revenue. The sweet spot is above what any individual user finds worth building themselves, but below what large labs and enterprise vendors can scalably implement. A highly profitable niche SaaS doesn't need to become Salesforce any more than a neighborhood restaurant needs to become McDonald's — and it faces essentially no competition in that specific domain.

**Quotable:**
> "Instead of a world powered by a few thousand software companies, we'll have a world powered by millions of builders. Millions of experts. Millions of mom and pop SaaS businesses." — *Elena Verna, on the scale of the coming shift*

### Google and Microsoft Team Up to Beat Back Anthropic and OpenAI 📌
**Applied AI** · email · 6 mins

1. Google, Microsoft, Salesforce, Snowflake, ServiceNow, Hugging Face, and Databricks announced a new backend protocol called Agentic Resource Discovery (ARD) on June 18, 2026. The idea: when an employee asks GitHub Copilot, Google Gemini, or a Salesforce CRM app to complete a task, the app automatically discovers and accesses any other ARD-compatible AI features or agents the company uses — a one-stop shop. Anthropic and OpenAI are conspicuously absent from the supporter list, and as insurgents without existing enterprise app portfolios, they may simply ignore it.

2. ARD is the established players' counter-move to Anthropic and OpenAI's "superagent" strategy. Anthropic wants Claude and OpenAI wants ChatGPT to be the primary interface through which enterprise workers access all their tools — the same one-stop-shop goal, but with the AI company at the center rather than the existing enterprise app vendor. ARD lets Microsoft, Google, and Salesforce position their existing apps (which enterprises already pay for) as the gateway, keeping the AI upstarts as back-end commodities rather than front-end relationship owners.

3. Microsoft has a concrete near-term incentive beyond competitive positioning: ARD would reduce GitHub Copilot's compute costs. Today, when Copilot needs to figure out which apps or agents to call for a task, it burns significant processing power on that discovery step. With ARD, participating apps broadcast their capabilities directly, so Copilot skips the search — a cost savings Microsoft called out explicitly to appeal to budget-conscious IT executives.

4. Cursor announced "Origin," a git hosting and code storage product launching in fall 2026, directly attacking GitHub's core repository business. Currently most developers using non-Copilot AI coding tools — including Claude Code and Cursor — still store code on GitHub, especially enterprise customers who need a single unified repository. GitHub's recent spate of outages has frustrated customers who previously told reporters they'd leave if a viable alternative existed; Origin may be that alternative. OpenAI is also privately exploring a similar product tied to its Codex agent.

**Quotable:**
> "If you [have] a price for everything, it becomes complicated. What is the cost of creating a PowerPoint? What is the cost of asking a basic question and so on and so forth? Instead, we say how much time it took the agent to take care of the question asked." — *Jigar Thakkar, VP of Agentic AI, AWS, on why Quick charges by agent-hours ($20–$40/month for 4–8 hours) rather than tokens*

### The Megacap Era 📎
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 8 mins

1. Today's megacaps are genuinely unique entities, not just scaled-up versions of smaller peers. At the 1973, 1987, and 2000 market peaks, the most valuable company was essentially a bet on endless hardware demand (IBM, Cisco). Exxon at the 2007 peak was the older model — a very large but otherwise typical company with only directional competitive advantages. Today's top five each hold a distinct superlative: fastest parallel compute (Nvidia), dominant search with positive feedback loops (Google), a smartphone maker whose margins are a multiple of peers' (Apple), the lingua franca for high-paid white-collar work (Microsoft), and a rocket company. Remove any one and the world is materially different.

2. Consumer staples brand "differentiation" was never intrinsic to the products — it was a structural accident. Coke's durable margins over RC Cola came from national transportation networks and national advertising enabling category monopolies, grocery slotting-fee economics that rewarded brands with TV/radio spend, and a one-time shift to suburbia and supermarkets that locked in habits. The differentiation was historically contingent, which is why those margins have been "durable-until-recently" — the infrastructure that created them is no longer novel.

3. If OpenAI and Anthropic both go public, they'll be the first pair of top-few-dozen-by-market-cap companies that are in essentially the same business — both sell tokens, via subscription and API. Among any randomly-selected pair of top-20 companies, it's hard to find two less likely to do business with each other. They could diverge (OpenAI as applications, Anthropic as infrastructure) or replicate CPG's historically-contingent duopoly by partitioning distribution platforms, but the overlap itself is unprecedented in megacap history.

4. Pure-play AI labs are in permanent sudden-death mode: high fixed costs, short model half-lives, and no fallback revenue stream mean one major miss can kill a company even after it reaches profitability. Companies with a money-printing social network, search engine, or Elon can absorb errors of omission; independent labs cannot. Investors have been desensitized to paying 100x sales for frontier AI companies, but they're probably unprepared for the scenario where a frontier lab or neocloud becomes *expensive* at 2x earnings.

**Quotable:**
> "A company that owns a money-printing social network, search engine, or Elon can afford more missteps, or errors of omission, but the other labs are in sudden-death mode all the time." — *on the existential risk asymmetry between hyperscaler-backed and pure-play AI labs*

## Markets & Finance

### Money Stuff: Synthetic IPO Pops 📌
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg Money Stuff)** · rss · 14 mins

1. SpaceX's $75B IPO at $135/share drew over $350B in demand — roughly 4.7x oversubscribed. Retail investors requested $100B and received only $15B worth of shares. The stock surged ~50% in its first two days of trading, making the day-one flip a near-perfect trade — except that nobody lets you do it: bankers screened out short-term flippers in favor of long-only holders, and retail brokerages penalize IPO flipping by cutting access to future offerings.

2. The ETF creation/redemption mechanism provides a backdoor into IPO pops. Cathie Wood's ARKK ETF — which received ~1.7 million SpaceX shares at IPO — saw a record $4.6B inflow the day before trading began, followed immediately by a record $6.2B outflow the next session. Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Athanasios Psarofagis identified this as an "IPO arbitrage" strategy: pour cash into a fund expected to receive IPO shares, extract it after the pop.

3. The sophisticated version of this trade uses in-kind creations and redemptions, not exchange-traded ARKK shares. A large institution borrows the stocks in ARKK's underlying portfolio, delivers them to ARKK in exchange for ARKK shares, waits one day for the IPO pop to flow through NAV, then redeems those ARKK shares back for the updated underlying portfolio — which now includes SpaceX at its post-pop price. Because ARKK's holdings are public, the other positions can be shorted as a hedge, leaving pure SpaceX IPO exposure.

4. Baron Capital offered a blunter version: its Baron Partners ($17B) and Baron Asset ($3.7B) funds held SpaceX at $105/share as of March 31, well below the $135 IPO price. When Baron publicly announced it would reprice SpaceX on June 4 (the IPO day), the funds jumped ~7–8% that day. Morningstar analyst Jeffrey Ptak estimated Baron Partners received $1.8B in inflows from June 1–11, as investors bought in at $105 to capture the gap to $135 (and the anticipated ~$160 post-pop price).

5. Both the ARKK and Baron strategies dilute existing long-term holders. Arbitrageurs flowing into ARKK reduce each holder's proportionate share of the IPO pop. In Baron's case, new investors buying in at stale $105 NAV when SpaceX was actually worth $135+ effectively extract value from existing shareholders — the mirror image of private-credit funds where investors redeeming at inflated NAV hurt remaining holders. ERShares responded by blocking new creations to its $2.4B XOVR ETF and imposing a 2% redemption fee ahead of the SpaceX offering.

6. The multistrategy hedge fund world is converging on a "gig economy" model for portfolio managers. Citadel, Point72, and now Chris Rokos's Rokos Capital are exploring buy-side alpha capture programs — paying external managers for trade ideas rather than hiring them as employees. Goldman Sachs estimates more than 70% of multimanager platforms allocated capital externally in 2025, up from 54% in 2022. Brevan Howard is also scaling external equity allocations through its Alpha Strategies fund. As one framing has it: this is the pod-shop model evolving back toward fund-of-funds.

7. Betting on college football teams favored by 20–25 points (money-line, not spread) produced 12.24% average annual returns with a 94.95% win rate across 376 games over six seasons in the early 2010s, per unpublished research by Mike Wohl. The structural edge comes from longshot bias: bettors overpay for underdog tickets, so the party on the other side — selling them — earns a positive expected return. This has the economic profile of bond-like income from writing insurance, similar to BOXX (option box spreads) or CAIE (autocallables). A prediction-market or sports-gambling ETF packaging this as a yield product is likely within a few years if the SEC opens the door.

**Quotable:**
> "Investors appear to be using ARKK as a piggyback vehicle for access to opportunities they may not be able to easily buy directly. That trend has become more common in ARKK around major IPOs." — *Bloomberg Intelligence ETF analyst Athanasios Psarofagis on the SpaceX IPO arb flows*

### India Isn't Ready For El Niño 📎
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. **NOAA confirmed a developing El Niño on June 11 that could strengthen to "very strong" — and India's government response came weeks too late.** The Ministry of Agriculture launched its "Khet Bachao Abhiyan" (Save the Farm Campaign) only on June 1, after El Niño warnings had been definitive since April. By then, farmers across India had already begun sowing in May using pre-monsoon showers, meaning any advice to delay planting or switch to drought-resistant crops arrived after the decision window had closed.

2. **The government's emergency response is both belated and unknown to the people it's supposed to help.** The ministry identified 197 vulnerable districts on June 1, then revised that upward to 326 districts across 12 states by June 16 — a rapid expansion that suggests the initial assessment was rushed. Farmer leader Balbir Singh Rajewal of the Bharat Kisan Union told The Core he had never heard of the "Kheti Bachao Abhiyan," meaning the awareness campaign had not reached farmers on the ground at all.

3. **India's agricultural structure is systematically misaligned with climate resilience.** GV Ramanjaneyulu, executive director of the Centre for Sustainable Agriculture, notes that crop planning is driven by government procurement policy, not by local soil, water, or environmental conditions. Half of Punjab is unsuitable for paddy cultivation, yet paddy dominates because the central government buys it — leaving farmers locked into water-intensive crops precisely where drought risk is highest.

4. **The safety net for rural workers has been dismantled at the worst possible moment.** The demand-driven job guarantee scheme MGNREGS — which gave rural households income during agricultural shocks — has been replaced by VB-G RAM G, a scheme that is not demand-driven and is scheduled to begin only in July. This eliminates coverage for April through June, the quarter that accounted for 40% of MGNREGS employment in FY25, and the exact window when a weak monsoon does its economic damage.

5. **The water crisis is already materializing: Mumbai's seven reservoir lakes sit at 10.35% capacity, leaving its 13 million residents with only 40 days of water.** Maharashtra has received 75% below-average rainfall this month — the city's driest June in over a decade. Authorities have already cut industrial water usage by 20% and disconnected construction sites, with the monsoon arriving weeks later than its usual early-June schedule. El Niño's impact this year may be compounded by the fact that it follows 2025, one of the warmest years on record, giving the agricultural system no recovery buffer.

**Quotable:**
> "Resilience comes from local conditions, but we don't plan agricultural output based on soil, water and environmental conditions. Our policy is what the government thinks is right. Half of Punjab is not suitable for paddy, and except for basmati, most of the paddy produced is not locally consumed either, but it is grown because the central government procures it." — *GV Ramanjaneyulu, executive director, Centre for Sustainable Agriculture*

### The Morning: At the Supreme Court 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. The Supreme Court has 19 cases left to decide before its summer break, with several expected on June 18. Reporters in the Court's press room get a rough headcount of incoming opinions by watching staff wheel in gray boxes — each holds about two opinions — then scramble to flip through paper booklets the moment a buzzer signals the justices have taken the bench. Opinion announcements are not livestreamed; audio is piped only into the press room, and dissenting justices occasionally read summaries aloud to register strong disapproval.

2. The Court's six-member conservative majority has already defied Trump once this term — three Republican-appointed justices joined the three liberal justices in February to strike down his sweeping tariffs. The remaining major cases include Trump's bid to oust Fed Board member Lisa Cook and his attempt to end birthright citizenship for U.S.-born children of undocumented immigrants; oral arguments suggest the Court is likely to rule against the administration on both. The one executive-power case Trump appears set to win involves the FTC: a ruling in his favor would expand presidential authority to fire independent agency heads across the federal bureaucracy, overriding congressional protections.

3. A U.S.-Iran preliminary peace deal was released June 17, days after Trump announced the end of the war. Key terms: a ceasefire covering Lebanon as well (Israel says it is not a party), reopening the Strait of Hormuz for 60 days with Iran using "best efforts" for safe commercial passage, a $300 billion reconstruction plan for Iran with no specified payer, lifting of oil-export restrictions and release of frozen Iranian assets, and a 60-day window to negotiate the hardest remaining issue — Iran's nuclear program. Trump reserved the right to resume bombing ("We're going to bomb the hell out of them if they violate the agreement") and U.S. officials privately doubt the two sides can resolve the nuclear question within 60 days.

Three other high-profile Supreme Court cases remain undecided: two Second Amendment challenges (one to Hawaii's gun control law, one on whether illegal drug users can be barred from possessing guns) and a dispute over Mississippi accepting mail-in ballots postmarked by Election Day but received up to five days late. A challenge to West Virginia and Idaho laws banning transgender women from girls' and women's sports teams is also pending; oral arguments signal the Court is likely to uphold those bans, which would set a precedent for the 25 other states with similar restrictions.
