### Xi's Japan diatribe ⚡
**FirstFT Asia** · email · 5 mins

1. During Xi Jinping's two-day summit with Donald Trump in Beijing earlier this month, Xi launched an intense, unsolicited diatribe against Japanese PM Sanae Takaichi over Japan's "remilitarisation" — a verbal attack that seven people familiar with the meeting described as the most heated exchange of the entire summit. ### My Local Pizza Shop is Using AI ⚡
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 10 mins

1. **A pizza shop in Naples, Florida is using an AI phone agent that cloned the owner's daughter's voice.** Gustafson called Bocci's Pizza on a Friday and had a natural-sounding phone exchange with what he assumed was a person — until the AI repeated a suspiciously precise "22 minutes" estimate verbatim on callback rather than rounding to twenty like a human would. The voice turned out to be a clone of the owner's daughter's voice, trained on recordings of her actual calls, including her specific sign-off phrase "Cool, see you soon."

2. **The vendor flew from Europe to Naples, spent eight hours recording the shop's operations, and charged $12,000 upfront plus $500/month.** A European company pitched owner Tony for "a few rounds" before he agreed; they then recorded the menu, the daughter's call cadence, and the busy-evening rhythm on-site, and used all of it to train the AI. Tony now runs the entire phone operation on one line instead of six, with zero teenage part-time staff.

3. **The payback period was under one month — faster than almost any B2B SaaS deal Gustafson sees.** Total cost of ownership is $12k upfront plus $6k/year. Tony recouped it immediately through eliminated labor costs (high schoolers who were, in his words, "transient and semi-dependable on a good day"), fewer order remakes from mistakes, and no sick-day coverage problems on Friday rush nights. The savings flow directly to operating margin with no multi-quarter lag.

4. **The AI speaks 16 languages, answering calls in Spanish and Portuguese for customers who prefer not to order in English.** Tony, who notes he "speaks Italian pretty good," is outclassed by his own system on multilingual capability. In Naples, Florida — a market where, per Gustafson, half the restaurants don't use DoorDash — this is a competitive capability gap that a human phone staff of two high schoolers could never close.

5. **The same unit economics apply to nail salons, physical therapy offices, independent insurance brokers, and dry cleaners — not just VC-backed companies.** The dominant AI-in-finance conversation focuses on PE portfolio margin expansion and B2B SaaS customer support cost takeout. But the pizza shop case shows the identical tool (low upfront cost, near-instant payback) works across the entire small-business economy — businesses that are definitionally not venture-backable but collectively constitute most of daily economic life. The labor displacement is real (two phone jobs gone), but Tony notes he was always struggling to fill those spots anyway.

**Quotable:**
> "Twelve thousand upfront. Five hundred a month... Paid for itself in the first month." — *Tony, owner of Bocci's Pizza, on his AI phone agent*

### An Apple Watch shake-up ⚡
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 9 mins

1. The Apple Watch — launched in 2015, generating an estimated $100 billion in lifetime sales — is losing momentum just as competition intensifies. Consumer preferences are shifting away from screen-heavy smartwatches toward screenless bands and rings that prioritize passive health tracking, longer battery life, and AI-driven insights. Whoop ($10 billion valuation, raised $575M heading toward an IPO) and Oura (valued at $11 billion, filed confidentially for a US IPO) have built multi-billion-dollar businesses on exactly this model — and Google just launched the $100 screenless Fitbit Air to chase the same trend.

2. Apple's Health app is the core liability: it feels "clinical," produces poor actionable insights, and is outclassed by Whoop's and Oura's coaching platforms. Eddy Cue — who personally uses products from both Oura and Whoop — has pushed for broader changes to Apple's health strategy after taking over the health group. An ambitious AI health coaching project internally called "Mulberry" was recently scaled back under Cue, with no features expected until later in the iOS 27 update cycle.

3. Apple is experiencing a simultaneous leadership exodus from its health and wearables organization. Former COO Jeff Williams, who oversaw health initiatives, retired last year. Fitness+ leader Jay Blahnik is departing amid litigation tied to his management conduct. Health and Apple Watch marketing chief Stan Ng recently retired, senior watch marketing manager Eric Charles left this month, and the company has steadily lost health and hardware talent to Oura. Marketing responsibilities for the watch, health, and home are now shifting to Kaiann Drance — an iPhone marketing executive — signaling that the Apple Watch remains an iPhone accessory rather than a standalone product with its own dedicated team.

4. Apple's institutional caution — the same risk aversion that caused it to miss generative AI — is now a direct liability in health wearables. The company detects sleep apnea and hypertension but still tells users to "consult their doctor" rather than offering continuous monitoring, real-time coaching, or predictive insights. Rivals are moving toward real-time blood pressure readings and continuous AI-driven coaching while Apple hesitates over accuracy, regulation, and lawsuit exposure. watchOS 27 will focus on stability and smaller refinements rather than any major new capabilities.

5. Two signals suggest sales pressure and a potential breakthrough. Apple has been offering unusually aggressive discounts on the Watch at Amazon and Best Buy — rare for Apple hardware — and added it to the education store for the first time. On the technology side, oversight of the long-running noninvasive glucose monitoring project (conceived during the Steve Jobs era) recently transferred from platform architecture chief Tim Millet to Zongjian Chen, head of the Advanced Technologies Group — widely seen as a sign the project may be nearing a stage where Chen, known as an execution-focused leader, can ramp it toward a consumer product.

**Quotable:**
> "Apple's health software often feels less like a modern consumer platform and more like the experience of reviewing charts in a waiting room. There's nothing fun about it, and competing apps are in a different league." — *Gurman on Apple's Health app vs. Whoop and Oura*

## AI & The Workplace

### The AI paradox: More automation, more humans, more work | Dan Shipper 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 6 mins

1. **Automation is a lie — Every doubled its headcount this year despite being maximally AI-forward.** Every person you hire to run an automated workflow spends their time ensuring the automation keeps working, catching the edge cases benchmarks never measure, and reframing the problem at a higher level. AI benchmarks only score problems that humans have already framed and can already evaluate; there's always a meta-level above them that requires a human. Dan Shipper's company of ~30 people expanded because more automation created more surface area requiring human oversight, not less.

2. **The personal-agent model is dead; every company will converge on one "super-agent" maintained by a single forward deployed engineer.** Shipper initially predicted an AI shadow org chart — every employee with their own personal work agent — but flipped completely after observing that unmaintained personal agents become useless fast. The winning pattern, already live at Shopify (their agent is called "River") and Viktor, is one deeply technical person who maintains a company-wide agent that trickles into specialized team agents as models mature. This new role — the forward deployed engineer — is distinct from traditional software building and is becoming the most essential hire at AI-native companies.

3. **SaaS is not dying; agents will supercharge SaaS demand and improve SaaS margins simultaneously.** When employees bring AI via Codex or Claude Code to use SaaS tools, the *user* pays for inference tokens, not the SaaS vendor — improving vendor margins. Meanwhile, Every's SaaS spend is up year-over-year because agents need their own seats and make billions of API calls. Shipper's contrarian call: "I would buy SaaS stocks right now," because the agent layer increases, not replaces, software usage volume.

**Quotable:**
> "Models make yesterday's human competence cheap... but because everyone uses the same models, it all looks the same if you use it the default way; it becomes commoditized slop. The key: ride the models — use them for everything you do, try new models when they drop, keep turning over rocks." — *Dan Shipper on how to stay relevant as AI commoditizes baseline skill*

### The Forecast: Why boo AI? 📎
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 6 mins

1. **Gen Z Americans are the most AI-skeptical young people on Earth, which is historically anomalous.** A Pew survey across 19 countries found that Gen Z Americans are more concerned about AI than young people anywhere else surveyed — even as youth globally tend to be more enthusiastic about new technology than older generations. American students are booing AI at graduation speeches and forming "Luddite clubs" on campus, per Bloomberg Businessweek reporters Victor Swezey and Jo Constantz. Notably, Gen Z Americans are still slightly *less* pessimistic than older Americans — the entire US is unusually negative on AI.

2. **Four theories compete to explain the backlash, none fully accounts for American exceptionalism.** Theory 1 (correct fear): AI has demonstrably hit entry-level jobs hard, and many experts expect displacement to worsen. Theory 2 (misplaced fear): the weak entry-level market is largely *not* AI-driven per NY Fed research, and younger workers are actually best positioned to adapt. Theory 3 (generational resentment): Harvard economist David Deming points to the condescension dynamic — job-seekers don't want to be lectured by those who've already secured careers. Theory 4 (broader malaise): the AI backlash may be a symptom of deepening Gen Z American unhappiness, which the World Happiness Report attributes heavily to smartphones and social media — and having watched the internet sour under light regulation, they discount utopian AI promises accordingly.

3. **The newsletter's predictions sidebar sketches the near-term AI landscape beyond the campus protests.** The FDA could approve its first AI-designed drug within a couple of years — an AI-developed severe asthma treatment just entered late-stage trials, and while the drug itself isn't novel, the milestone of AI producing a medicine people will actually use matters. Barclays projects China could have roughly 24 million humanoid robots installed by 2035, potentially offsetting 60% of the economic impact from its population decline. Veteran commodities strategist Jeff Currie argues the AI buildout will collide with chronic underinvestment in energy and materials to produce a decade-long commodities bull run.

**Quotable:**
> "Young people who haven't yet found jobs may not be so interested in hearing an older person who has already run their race telling them to embrace AI." — *David Deming, Harvard economist, on why graduation speeches about AI get booed*

## Markets & Economics

### The Fine Print Of Opportunity 📌
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. **India's FDI decline is a confidence problem, not just a currency problem.** Ex-IMF economist Surjit Bhalla pinpoints a sharp fall in net FDI — not the rupee — as the clearest warning signal. Around 2015, India began assuming foreign investors needed India more than India needed them; the result was bilateral investment treaty rewrites, retrospective taxation fears, and quality control orders that spooked capital. Vietnam and Bangladesh captured China Plus One supply chain shifts while India talked about raising manufacturing's GDP share without building the policy architecture to achieve it.

2. **The rupee's slide was baked in before the Iran-West Asia conflict hit.** IGIDR's Dr. Rajeswari Sengupta notes India was already dealing with expensive energy, foreign capital outflows, a hard-to-finance current account deficit, and global investors chasing AI-led bets elsewhere. A falling rupee is not automatically a crisis — sometimes depreciation is the price adjustment an economy needs — and the bigger danger is panic-driven policy that suppresses market signals rather than addressing them.

3. **India's compliance burden is a hidden tax on enterprise growth.** Rishi Agrawal of TeamLease Regtech argues that India's aspiration for more manufacturing, MSME growth, and FDI collides with a dense web of rules, filings, and criminal penalties for business errors. Decriminalisation, Jan Vishwas, labour law reform, and digitisation are incremental steps, but what's needed is a "UPI moment" for compliance — something as simple, trusted, and scalable as the payments revolution — which hasn't arrived yet.

4. **Former RBI Deputy Governor Rakesh Mohan says the oil shock exposed pre-existing fractures.** India entered the West Asia disruption already carrying weaknesses: sluggish merchandise exports, underperforming industrial production, weak private investment, and a real exchange rate that made exports uncompetitive. Mohan's prescription is to look east — attract investment into garments, footwear, furniture, and light manufacturing, then underpin it with dedicated freight corridors to fix logistics, sectors where India has lost ground to regional competitors for years.

5. **SIPs are disciplined, not magical — and India's market cycle is running on one-and-a-half engines.** DSP Mutual Fund's Sahil Kapoor warns that the Nifty's ~12% CAGR over 25 years does not accrue to investors who exit during 40% drawdowns and miss the recovery. His current read: India is off euphoric valuations but not cheap; large caps beat small and mid caps; private banks, beaten-down IT, select FMCG and chemicals offer value pockets. Corporate balance sheets are clean and cycle-ready, but weak consumption and shaky exports mean demand — not capital — is the missing engine.

6. **India is Claude's second-largest global user base, and its AI usage skews heavily technical.** Anthropic's Irina Ghose reports that 45% of Indian Claude usage is for technical and mathematical tasks, with over half the user base being technical professionals. Her enterprise advice: don't launch 50 pilots — identify three or four high-stakes "big rocks" over 6–18 months, bring governance in early, and build AI fluency across teams. Quest Global's Ajit Prabhu adds that the next AI wave is industrial: data centres are driving power demand, smarter products need more chips, and defence, aerospace, and automotive sectors are becoming software-led — rewarding engineers who think one level above their assigned role.

**Quotable:**
> "Companies are not failing to invest because they lack patriotism or ambition. They are responding rationally to incentives." — *Surjit Bhalla, ex-IMF economist, on why private investment lags in India*

### Free Lunch: Are Big Tech IPOs the market top? 📎
**Tej Parikh** · email · 4 mins

1. SpaceX, Anthropic, and OpenAI together carry a potential combined valuation of ~$4tn — roughly 6% of the entire US public equity market — comparable in scale to the equity expansion of the late-1990s dotcom bubble. BCA Research's long-run data shows the S&P 500 reliably underperforms after major IPOs, because a surge in stock supply absorbs capital near market highs, leaving fewer funds to sustain the broader rally. For context, all US IPOs combined between 1980 and 2025 totaled only ~$12.5tn on an inflation-adjusted basis.

2. For over two decades, "de-equitisation" — delistings, buybacks, M&A, and the growth of private markets — has steadily shrunk the US equity base to ~7% below its mid-2000s level, with the number of listed US companies more than halving since 1995. This structural scarcity, amplified by QE-driven liquidity after 2008 and Covid, is a primary reason markets climbed through rising rates, trade wars, and geopolitical shocks, according to Robert Buckland, former chief global equity strategist at Citigroup. The mega-IPOs, alongside potential issuance from Stripe and Databricks, plus Big Tech share sales to fund AI capex that already exceeds the 19th-century railroad build-out as a share of GDP, could end this tailwind.

3. The IPO-market-top correlation may not be causal — Matt King of Satori Insights notes that "IPO booms tend to come with strong markets and hype" and that insider selling via IPOs and PE exits is a symptom of an expensive market, not the cause of its decline. The real signal to watch is the post-IPO lock-up expiry (typically six months), when insiders gain the ability to sell at scale. Bullish offsets remain: AI breakthroughs, index reweighting as new names enter, and reinvestment of large corporate cash piles could sustain upward momentum.

**Quotable:**
> "Equitisation plays a part in killing off every bull market. We haven't seen that yet, but these IPOs may be the beginnings." — *Robert Buckland, former chief global equity strategist at Citigroup*
