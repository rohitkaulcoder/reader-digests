Anthropic can likely acquire the needed compute eventually, but it will cost considerably more than proactive planning would have.

6. The growth rate is what actually matters more than the absolute run rate — Anthropic is growing massively regardless of accounting treatment. Meanwhile, OpenAI's all-in enterprise bet risks ceding the consumer AI space to Google and Meta, who can actually monetize it through advertising.

### Not all AI agents are created equal ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 18 mins

1. Teams stall on agent prioritization not from lack of ideas but from comparing architecturally incompatible systems. One "agent" can ship in 6 weeks for $500/month; another requires a dedicated ML team and could generate a six-figure annual LLM bill. Putting a customer support assistant and a voice-enabled shopping agent on the same impact-vs.-effort spreadsheet is comparing apples, oranges, and jet engines.

2. Every agent initiative falls into one of three architectural categories, and the category determines build complexity, required skills, timeline, operating cost, and success metrics. Category 1 (deterministic automation) is your explicit flowchart with LLM nodes — built in n8n, Zapier, or Make.com. Category 2 (ReAct agents) lets the LLM decide which tools to call next, built in LangGraph, CrewAI, or AutoGen. Category 3 (multi-agent networks) has multiple specialized agents delegating tasks to each other, coordinated via Google's A2A protocol and enterprise orchestration like Temporal or Kafka.

3. Category 1 covers 60–70% of real agent opportunities and should be the starting point for almost every team. If the process can be mapped as a flowchart with fewer than 15–20 branches and completes in seconds to minutes, it's Category 1. A SaaS email support agent that reads, classifies, drafts, and routes — while sounding sophisticated — is entirely deterministic once mapped step by step.

4. A real Category 1 email support agent hit 52% completion rate at week 1, 78% at week 4, and 87% at week 8 (production-ready). The end result: 3,000 support emails/month automated, 2.5 FTE hours/day freed, and $18K/month in savings. When completion rate stabilizes and cost trends down, the workflow is working; if manual intervention stays high, the process isn't deterministic enough for this category.

5. Category 2 is correct for 25–30% of opportunities — specifically when the same user request triggers different action sequences depending on context. A voice + image shopping assistant can't be pre-mapped: a customer uploading a photo of too-small shoes and asking for Thursday delivery requires the agent to chain visual_search(), check_inventory(), get_delivery_options(), and place_order() — but dynamically reroute if any step fails (out of stock → suggest alternatives; no Thursday delivery → propose pickup).

6. A real Category 2 voice + image shopping assistant for a home goods retailer went from 71% task completion and $0.12/session in month 1 to 86% completion and $0.08/session by month 4. Image identification accuracy improved from 76% to 91%, conversion lift rose from +8% to +22%, and CSAT climbed from 4.0 to 4.5. Improving task completion while simultaneously reducing conversation length, tool calls, and cost per session is the signal that ReAct reasoning is paying off.

7. Category 3 multi-agent networks are currently used by fewer than 5–10% of teams and should almost never be the starting point. The trigger is organizational, not technical: multiple teams need to independently own specialized agents (customer service, inventory, logistics, quality monitoring) that must coordinate — e.g., the shopping agent asking the inventory agent "check all warehouses and suggest alternatives," which in turn asks the logistics agent about warehouse B delivery by Thursday. No single team can build or own all of that.

8. A large Fortune 500 retailer's Category 3 network went from 72% cross-agent success rate and $1.85/task in early deployment to 91% success and $1.92/task (stable despite more agents being added) once stabilized. Team velocity to add a new agent dropped from 6–8 weeks to 2–3 weeks. The key signal: cross-agent success rates and latency percentiles hold steady as new agents are added; if integration time and failure rates spike with each new agent, the system is over-coordinated.

9. Production systems at scale are layered hybrids, not pure-category builds. Claude Code runs a ReAct reasoning loop at its core, but adds a deterministic hooks system that fires scripts at lifecycle events (file saves, shell commands) without touching the LLM, and spawns isolated sub-agents when a task exceeds one context window. The design decision isn't which architecture to pick — it's knowing when a problem has outgrown one layer and needs the next.

**Quotable:**
> "Better to start with a Category 1 automation that ships in six weeks and delivers measurable value than to launch a Category 3 multi-agent system that's still '90% done' after six months." — *closing framework advice*

### The World: Hungary's populist paradox 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. Viktor Orban, Hungary's prime minister for 16 years and the global godfather of "illiberal democracy," lost the April 13, 2026 election primarily due to economic collapse and pervasive corruption. Hungary's GDP growth in 2025 was just 0.4%, unemployment is at a 10-year high, and Hungary ranks as the most corrupt country in the EU according to Transparency International (tied with Bulgaria). Peter Magyar, a former Orban ally who flipped to the opposition, won by promising a clean break from that corruption.

2. The economic damage is structural, not cyclical. Hungarian productivity growth averaged 2% in the decade before Orban; dropped to 1% during his first decade; and has averaged only 0.2% since 2020, per EU statistics. The mechanism: government contracts were awarded by political loyalty rather than competence, multinationals were favored over domestic firms to prevent independent economic power centers, and loyalist companies captured public tenders. Orban's son-in-law Istvan Tiborcz built one of Hungary's largest private equity and real estate empires on state-backed financing. Economist Krisztian Orban (no relation) estimates that Orban allies now control roughly one-fifth of Hungary's entire GDP.

3. This is a documented pattern called the "populist paradox": leaders win elections by promising to fight corruption, then dismantle the very institutions — independent courts, free media, procurement oversight — that constrain it, using corruption instead to entrench their rule. The EU began freezing development funds to Hungary in 2022 citing systemic procurement failures, which voters felt directly and which accelerated Orban's downfall.

4. Orban's governing model was an explicit template for Project 2025, the Heritage Foundation blueprint for restructuring the U.S. government, and Trump has implemented several of its core ideas, including seizing control of independent federal agencies. Political scientist Ivan Krastev notes the pattern has also played out under Bolsonaro in Brazil and Duterte in the Philippines. Whether the U.S.'s larger economy delays or dampens the same dynamics is an open question.

**Quotable:**
> "These companies aren't cost efficient, they aren't providing good services, they're not good at what they do — they're only good at getting along with Viktor Orban." — *Krisztian Orban, economist, on the crony firms that now control ~20% of Hungary's GDP*

## Finance & Markets

### Money Stuff: Banks Aren't Worried About Private Credit 📌
**Matt Levine** · email · 13 mins

1. Banks aren't competing with private credit — they're embedded inside it through a "re-tranching" model. Private credit funds borrow ~60% of their capital from banks and fund the rest from investors, meaning banks hold a senior claim against a pooled portfolio. When loans default, private credit investors absorb the first ~40% of losses before banks feel anything.

2. The big banks have enormous exposure but see almost no risk: JPMorgan has ~$50B, Wells Fargo $36.2B, and Citigroup $22B — with Citi reporting zero losses over the entire lifetime of that portfolio. Jamie Dimon said: "You have to have very large losses in private credit before at least it looks like banks are going to get hit. I'm not particularly worried about it." For over 98% of Wells Fargo's deals, the bank can also demand more collateral if credit quality deteriorates.

3. Two genuine risks exist in the re-tranching structure, even if banks downplay them. First, the model enables *more* lending than banks would do alone — $60 of bank exposure becomes $100 of loans to businesses, which may mean riskier borrowers or higher leverage. Second, concentration risk: Wells Fargo noted that 17% of its private credit collateral is in software companies, raising the tail scenario that a single macro shock (e.g., AI making enterprise SaaS worthless) could wipe out the entire asset class simultaneously.

4. Non-traded Business Development Companies (BDCs) are experiencing a quiet run. BDCs offer quarterly redemptions capped at 5% of shares at net asset value (NAV); this quarter, holders of more than 5% in nearly every major BDC have requested redemption simultaneously. The underlying problem: traded BDC equivalents price at ~20% discounts to NAV, so investors rationally tender non-traded BDC shares at the stated (inflated) NAV before the fund can revalue downward.

5. The structural fix for locked-up private fund investors is a secondary market at market-clearing prices — but the funds resist it because it reveals the gap between stated NAV and real value. French property funds provide a live example: Praemia REIM France opened a secondary market after halting redemptions, and the first trade cleared at a 60% discount to subscription price. Perial Asset Management's Perial O2 fund drew only one buyer, at a 70% discount. Levine's point: if more people want out than in, a secondary market is the honest solution — the gap just means the NAV was wrong.

6. Microsoft is pausing its carbon removal financing program — and the pause reveals that corporate carbon markets were never primarily about climate math. BloombergNEF estimated Microsoft's 2025 purchases represented 96% of the entire carbon removal credit market. Microsoft's emissions have risen sharply due to AI data center buildout; simultaneously, the PR benefit of "carbon neutral" branding has become politically toxic in the current US environment. When the two incentive models that justify carbon spending (actual climate benefit to the company + PR value) both weaken, the spending disappears.

7. Prediction markets exhibit a hidden form of correlation that makes them resemble financial markets more than sports books. A 28-year-old Minneapolis trader named Foster McCoy spotted Eric Trump's Instagram showing Barron Trump in a suit with siblings, correctly predicted a cascade of events (Barron would attend the State of the Union, Trump would shout out his kids, which specific words Trump would use) and netted ~$10,000. A 2004 Steven Levitt paper found bookmakers don't match buyers and sellers — they take positions and exploit bettor biases. The modern wrinkle: any given prediction market bet correlates to ~10 other bettable events, requiring traders to model correlated information sets just like equity investors model macro factors.

**Quotable:**
> "You have to have very large losses in private credit before at least it looks like banks are going to get hit. It doesn't mean you won't feel some stress and strain and you might have to do something about it. But I'm not particularly worried about it." — *Jamie Dimon on JPMorgan's ~$50B private credit exposure*

## Operations & Business Strategy

### How Superhuman Structures Its Analytics Team 📎
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 12 mins

1. **There are three canonical homes for analytics — all of them have fatal flaws.** Reporting to finance gives authority but turns the team into a perceived auditor; the product team at Heroku kept their distance from Chris Byington's data team because they felt like "data-leaning finance people." Reporting to ops works only if the COO isn't really a CRO in disguise — otherwise you get a sales bias and nobody wants to surface data showing their own pipeline is lagging. Reporting to engineering optimizes for speed but risks chasing what's interesting over what's impactful; at Grammarly it makes sense because 30-40% of the data team's work powers end-user product features.

2. **The real variable isn't org chart placement — it's finding one executive with the backbone to apply data to daily decisions.** Finance, ops, and engineering are "different fonts of the same mission." At Superhuman, Byington's team sidesteps the question entirely: analytics is a standalone function with its own seat at the exec table, no dotted lines, no intermediaries.

3. **The hub-and-spoke model has three layers: a data platform, a BI/enablement layer targeting 70% self-serve, and embedded vertical data scientists.** The data platform connects Zendesk, Amplitude, and Salesforce so a question like "how many support tickets did this account submit before renewal?" is actually answerable. The BI layer exists so that layer handles 70% of questions without any ticket to the data team — if you're at 20-30%, that's where to invest first. The embedded spokes are strict: 80% of a data scientist's energy goes to their assigned business unit; they attend offsites and know the team's problems before they're surfaced.

4. **Owning data, OKRs, and the forecast in one team eliminates the "come back next planning cycle" failure mode.** At most companies, analytics surfaces an insight, brings it to leadership, and gets told it's not on the roadmap; by next cycle the moment is gone. When the same team that found the insight also sets the goals and owns the forecast, there's no negotiation about relevance — they can act immediately. It also replaces the usual goal-setting theater (CEO throws out a board-friendly number, CFO anchors to last year + reasonable growth, both sides negotiate until mildly unhappy) with a single question: what does the data say is actually achievable?

5. **"Calling your shot" — pre-committing to what action you'll take before running an analysis — is the discipline that makes data-driven decisions real.** Before starting, you decide: if activation drop-off is biggest at the payment stage, we focus there; if not, we change our minds. This pre-commitment also pressure-tests whether the analysis is worth running at all — if you're unwilling to change behavior based on findings, don't run it. The discipline is most powerful when the team enforcing it also owns the goals and the forecast.

6. **Superhuman's 2024 hero metric was NRR for teams; it was deliberately below 100 and that was the point.** Individual users can only be retained or lost — no seat to add, no tier to upgrade into. Teams are different: land 5 seats at a 100-person company and there are 95 seats of runway. NRR anchored the entire year's work around fixing that bucket — deconstructing it into retention, expansion, contraction, and churn and tracking the input metric for each lever. By 2025, NRR improved enough that the hero metric shifted to year-over-year growth of teams. The hero metric also makes prioritization easier: anything not contributing goes to the back of the line without relitigating priorities.

7. **Three mechanisms for saying no: goal-gating, self-serve infrastructure, and "yes to the person."** If a request doesn't connect to a company goal, the burden of proof flips back on the requester — most stop asking. If 70% of questions are answerable in the BI tool, 70% of requests never arrive. When a no is unavoidable, the answer always starts with yes to the person: here's what I'm working on, here's the queue, here's what we can do instead. Byington's team runs weekly open office hours — a time-boxed hour where anyone can bring questions and learn to self-serve — as a pressure release valve that keeps relationships intact without blowing up the roadmap.

8. **The "pull test" is the most honest measure of whether an embedded data scientist is actually embedded.** Tell each business unit leader you're pulling their data scientist back to the central team. If they say "okay, thanks," the model wasn't working. If they offer to fund the role from their own budget, it is. The other two metrics: an annual one-question self-serve survey ("can you confidently make good decisions with data day-to-day?" on a 1-5 scale) and tracking the two or three truly company-shaping decisions per year to verify analytics was in the room before — not after — the decision was made.

**Quotable:**
> "Few problems survive their thorough articulation." — *Chris Byington on why he responds to every data request by asking "what are you actually trying to decide?" before touching any tool*
