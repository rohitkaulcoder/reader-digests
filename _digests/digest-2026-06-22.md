## Today's Top 3

### Net Dollar Retention Benchmarks: Where'd All the 130s Go? ⚡
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 10 mins

1. **The 130% NDR club went from 18 members to 2.** Gustafson analyzed 1,500+ quarterly NDR disclosures across 95 public software companies from 2020 to today. In 2021, 18 companies cleared the 130% threshold — now only Palantir and Figma remain. The median has compressed 13 percentage points, from 123% in Q4 2021 to 110% today.

2. **The collapse was not K-shaped — every quartile fell in lockstep.** The popular "strong get stronger, weak get crushed" narrative predicted bifurcation. The data shows the opposite: P75 dropped 14 points (130% → 116%), P50 dropped 13 points, and P25 dropped 10 points (113% → 103%), with the interquartile spread actually *narrowing* from 18-20 points to 10-13. The top-end penthouse above 130% was simply wiped out.

3. **The published median flatters the industry — the real compression is nearly double what's reported.** Companies that quietly exited NDR disclosure were mostly below-median performers: Fastly's last quarterly DBNR was 121% before switching to annual-only; CrowdStrike was above 120% before pivoting to qualitative language like "slightly below our benchmark" (later disclosed as 115% then 112%); DocuSign peaked at ~125%, cratered to ~99%, then stopped quarterly disclosure. Adding conservative estimates for these dropouts pushes the true median 6-8 points lower and total compression to 19-21 points, not 13.

4. **Business model determined who bled the most.** Consumption-priced companies (Snowflake, Twilio) had the highest peaks but fell furthest because customers could throttle usage immediately — no contract renegotiation required. Snowflake's 53-point drop from 177% to 124% is the most extreme NDR compression in public software history, averaging 4 points of decline per quarter for three straight years. Productivity tools tied to dev teams (Asana, Monday) got hit by 2022-2023 tech layoffs, while SMB-facing marketplaces (BILL, Toast) stayed depressed alongside their customers.

5. **Every 10 points of NDR is worth roughly $250M in enterprise value for a mid-size SaaS company.** One point of NDR maps to ~0.07x forward revenue multiple at slow growth rates and ~0.18x at fast ones. For a $250M NTM-revenue business at 8x, a 10-point NDR improvement re-rates them from 8x to 9x — $250M of immediate upside. If the higher NDR causes analysts to raise out-year growth assumptions (which it typically does), that compounds to ~$500M of value over 18 months. This is precisely why companies hiding NDR are making a valuation decision: there is no scenario in which you conceal a number that's been improving.

**Quotable:**
> "While median is mathematically correct, it is the median of the survivors. If you add back conservative estimates for the dropouts, the real median is probably 6 to 8 points lower than what gets passed around." — *on why published NDR benchmarks are systematically optimistic*

### Is AI talking about your product in the right way? ⚡
**Kyle Poyar** · email · 8 mins

1. **Structural prominence on your website directly shapes what LLMs say about you.** Profound data shows ChatGPT referral traffic jumped >60% starting May 7, 2026 and has held there, with homepages rising from 3.5% to ~24% of all ChatGPT referrals — meaning what you put in your nav, headers, and footer is being read by models and surfaced to buyers. When Casey Hill tested Clay's tactic of linking top customer case studies directly from its footer, 100% of ChatGPT's sources for "What notable brands use Clay?" pointed back to Clay.com; competing SaaS companies with buried customer stories (HubSpot, DocuSign, Typeform) were cited far less consistently from their own domains. Josh Blyskal of Profound confirms that Google AI Mode literally extracts headers, summarizes the text under each, and only themes that win across many pages make it into bold answer sections.

2. **Your product now competes against Claude, not just direct rivals — and you need a page that says so.** Buyers are asking AI whether to build their own solution with tools like Claude Code rather than buy yours, making "build vs. buy" a live competitive front for most SaaS. Framer published a "Framer vs. Claude Code" comparison article and within 30 days it ranked prominently for Framer's top keywords. The formula: make it feel balanced and authoritative, use structured comparison tables (which LLMs prefer to cite over prose), and anchor it with customer reviews — the goal is to own the canonical answer when AI fields the "should I just build this myself?" query.

3. **"Summarize with AI" buttons let you control the prompt that shapes your brand's LLM narrative — and may persist in users' local context.** Companies including Wispr Flow, Glean, and Galileo now embed buttons that fire a pre-written prompt ("As a [persona] trying to solve [use case], explain how [company] solves this using [homepage URL]"), giving them editorial control over how a third-party LLM characterizes the product. Click-through rates are low (<1% of homepage traffic) but James Norquay of Prosperity Media reported 950% LLM traffic increases for clients using a variation that instructs the model to remember the brand for future citations. The secondary mechanism: LLMs retain context from prior queries, so injecting your brand early may compound over future sessions.

4. **Own the canonical URL for every piece of news about your company, and structured glossary/FAQ content functions as raw material for AI answers.** Rippling published the Deel corporate espionage story first on its own blog; media outlets then linked back to Rippling's domain, concentrating SEO authority and giving Rippling control over the framing AI models learned from. Lovable extends this logic to pricing FAQs by pairing screenshots with comparison tables (prompt → work done → credit cost), a format that simultaneously explains credits to humans and gives LLMs machine-readable, citable structure. Ahrefs' CMO Tim Soulo reports that free vibe-coded micro-tools have driven 76 million site visits and ~20,000 signups — each free tool also generates user-created social content that reinforces LLM citations back to Ahrefs.

**Quotable:**
> "It's time to enable LLMs to become your best sales reps." — *closing thesis on why website structure now determines AI brand positioning*

### Building the most AI-pilled engineering team in the world | Fiona Fung (Manager of the Claude Code and Cowork Team…) ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 5 mins

1. Anthropic engineers now ship 8x more code per quarter than they did across the entire 2021–2025 period — a number that was flat for four years and then spiked this year. That throughput shift has moved the primary bottleneck from building to verification: the hardest open problem is no longer generating code but confirming the resulting experience matches intent. Fiona's team uses a "bad vs. sad" framework to triage this — "bad" is an unrecoverable error (a crash), "sad" is a recoverable pain point (flickering, dropped conversation) — and tracks both per surface area to catch regressions fast.

2. Role boundaries are dissolving: PMs are checking in code, engineers are owning design and content end-to-end, and the most valuable people are now generalists with product sense and the agency to ship solo. To keep learning flowing as engineers increasingly work independently with their own agent fleets, Fiona's team invented "pairwise programming lunches" — engineers work side by side not on shared tasks but just close enough to observe each other's Claude Code patterns and absorb new techniques.

3. Fiona has replaced six-month roadmaps with monthly planning on a plain spreadsheet after discovering her team had barely referenced the longer plans after she joined. She also automated her own morning ritual — previously reading feedback channels over coffee and hand-picking fixes to assign — into a Claude "routine" that runs every morning, spawning agents that analyze multiple feedback channels, surface themes, and generate PRs. Her prediction: managerial work is shifting from synchronous prompting to asynchronous agent management.

**Quotable:**
> "What's better than me doing it? Having Claude do it." — *Fiona Fung's core operating principle, which she admits she still has to remind herself to apply after 25+ years of shipping software by hand*

## Design & Hardware

### Making Apple cool again 📌
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 11 mins

1. Apple's industrial design studio has gone from the strategic center of the company to a service function. Under Jobs and Ive, engineers, marketers, and executives came to the ID studio to be inspired and align behind its vision — design sat at the center of everything. Today it is "a place where other teams come to get what they need — computer-aided design, prototyping, materials expertise and color choices — and get out." The team no longer has a seat at the executive table and has less influence than at any point in decades.

2. The decline traces directly to the 2015 "promotion" of Jony Ive to Chief Design Officer, which was actually cover for him stepping away from day-to-day management. When Ive fully departed at the end of 2019 to form LoveFrom (now working with OpenAI), Tim Cook handed oversight of design to COO Jeff Williams — the company's top supply chain executive and Cook's closest ally — despite Williams having no design background. Williams was chosen because the ID team already worked with his operations org and he'd led the Apple Watch project, but the structural signal was unmistakable: Apple had replaced the most influential designer in its history with a financial operator.

3. Ive's successor Evans Hankey was deliberately excluded from the executive team — reporting to Williams rather than sitting alongside the heads of hardware, software, and marketing. When Hankey left, Williams took direct control of the ID group himself rather than elevate any designer or recruit from outside, fearing a new wave of departures. The exits accelerated anyway, with nearly every Ive-era designer defecting to LoveFrom, starting their own firms, or retiring. Hankey herself went on to co-found an AI hardware startup with Ive that OpenAI acquired for $6.5 billion.

4. The talent wreckage left Apple with no credible internal candidates. Richard Howarth, the most senior remaining designer and an Apple VP, relocated to the Chicago area and works remotely — almost unheard of for a top exec — and had already failed in a prior management stint that forced Ive to temporarily re-engage. Williams ultimately installed Molly Anderson, known for Apple Watch design work but with zero prior management experience. Alan Dye, who ran UI design and was one of the few plausible future design leaders, was then poached by Meta; several software designers followed him there. Steve Lemay now leads software design, but the team's depth is similarly hollow.

5. The design vacuum is legible in the products. The iPhone looked essentially the same for roughly five years until the Air launched last year. The Apple Watch, AirPods, and Mac have retained the same industrial designs for approximately a decade, with narrow exceptions like the Watch Ultra and the MacBook Neo. In the Jobs/Ive era, a single product going that long without a significant redesign would have been unthinkable, let alone an entire portfolio.

6. John Ternus, who takes over as CEO in September 2026, is the first leader since Cook to come from Apple's product engineering and design culture rather than operations or finance. He has already begun spending time with the ID group — a contrast to Cook's historically limited presence — and told employees in a recent internal meeting: "Apple's brought truly incredible design to more people than any company in history. We're going to make sure that stays the case." His mandate, per Gurman's sources, is to find a true design leader who gets an executive table seat, authority to rebuild the organization, and a mandate to deliver breakthrough new designs.

7. Ternus inherits what Gurman calls the most prolific product pipeline in Apple's history, giving him a near-term tailwind while the structural rebuild happens. September 2026 brings the iPhone 18 Pro, Pro Max, and a foldable ("iPhone Ultra"), plus Watch Series 12 and Ultra 4. Also in 2026: multiple Macs, an entry-level iPad with Apple Intelligence, likely a smart home hub, an OLED iPad mini, and new Apple TV and HomePod mini. Spring 2027 brings a second-gen iPhone Air with improved battery and a second camera. The 20th anniversary fall 2027 line — presumably iPhone 20 Pro and Pro Max — ships alongside a second-gen foldable, camera-equipped AirPods that feed data to Siri, and Apple's first smart glasses (slipped from late 2026). A tabletop home robot with a moving arm follows in late 2027 or 2028.

**Quotable:**
> "Rather than being the group that defines products and creates iconic designs, it is now a place where other teams come to get what they need — computer-aided design, prototyping, materials expertise and color choices — and get out." — *on the current state of Apple's industrial design studio*

## AI & Society

### The Forecast: Parents vs. AI 📌
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 6 mins

1. Just over half of US teens already use chatbots for homework, yet 4 in 10 parents haven't had a single conversation about the technology with their kids, per a recent Pew survey. England's children's commissioner, in a guide for parents, put it bluntly: "The gap between parents' understanding and children's experience is widening." Millennials — the last generation with smartphone-free childhoods — are particularly sensitized to what frictionless technology costs, having already watched social media hollow out social connection and emotional resilience.

2. Jenny Anderson, journalist and author of *The Disengaged Teen*, frames AI as a repeat of the social media mistake and calls for deliberate overcorrection: parents should "over-index on caution this time around, waiting for evidence these tools actually help kids think better — or, more likely, offer to think for them." Where social media promised connection and delivered isolation, AI can reason and compose, making the substitution of the child's own cognition far more complete and harder to detect.

3. AI academic Ethan Mollick identifies the core mechanism as "cognitive surrender" — researchers' term for adopting AI outputs with minimal scrutiny, bypassing intuition and deliberation. His prescription is not avoidance but intentionality: make a conscious choice each time about what to hand over and what to keep, rather than reflexive dependence or reflexive rejection. The practical implication for parenting: deliberately reintroduce friction — require children to struggle, draft, and reason before reaching for the tool.

**Quotable:**
> "Parents feel burned by social media, which promised to connect people and ended up isolating and harming too many kids. Now comes AI, which can think and reason." — *Jenny Anderson, author of* The Disengaged Teen

**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

## Business & Economics

### The Demand For Durability 📎
**The Core** · email · 6 mins

1. **India's FDI problem is operational, not philosophical.** Policy liberalization has opened most sectors, yet long-term capital isn't flowing in at scale. Ketan Dalal (Katalyst Advisors) and Sudhir Kapadia (ex-National Tax Leader, EY India) identify the actual blockers: land acquisition friction, logistics gaps, high industrial power costs, slow dispute resolution, and tax unpredictability. Their prescription is a sector-led strategy — semiconductors, green energy, AI-linked infrastructure, manufacturing, and auto — where India can realistically slot into global value chains rather than competing as a generic destination. Foreign multinationals tend to first maximize domestic capacity before looking abroad, so India must actively outcompete that inertia.

2. **Large forex reserves can defend the rupee's day-to-day moves but cannot fix a structurally weak external account.** Economist Renu Kohli argues that India's currency vulnerability is not simply a function of oil prices or geopolitical shocks — it reflects chronic dependence on foreign portfolio flows rather than durable FDI, combined with a current account that has limited export horsepower. The RBI can intervene in spot and forward markets to smooth volatility and buy time, but without a genuine shift toward productive long-term capital inflows, every external shock re-exposes the same underlying imbalance.

3. **In banking, the durable competitive moat is the customer journey, not the product catalogue.** Pratik Shah (EY India) notes that in 2026, bank products, pricing, and offers are near-instantly copyable. What is hard to replicate is how a bank handles customers across onboarding, servicing, complaints, closures, branches, apps, chatbots, and call centres simultaneously. Physical branches are not made obsolete by digital — they remain essential for trust, deposit relationships, MSMEs, and older customers. The banks that win will be those that make digital and physical function as a single integrated system, using data and AI to anticipate needs before complaints surface.

**Quotable:**
> "AI may reduce the cost of making content, but it increases the premium on taste, trust, and human imperfection." — *Roshan Abbas (Kommune), on the creator economy*
