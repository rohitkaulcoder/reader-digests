## Today's Top 3

### An Interview with Joanna Stern About Living With AI ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 39 mins

1. Stern left the Wall Street Journal after 13 years (starting CES 2013) to launch "The New Things" — a newsletter, YouTube channel, and partnership with NBC News as chief technology analyst. The NBC deal works because it reaches a completely different audience: mainstream American TV viewers who don't read Stratechery or follow tech closely, while her newsletter/YouTube captures the tech-immersed crowd.

2. Her book *I Am Not a Robot: My Year Using AI to Do (Almost) Everything* (out May 12, 2026) was deliberately scoped beyond LLMs from the start. By 2024, Waymo, AI radiology, and household robots were becoming mainstream-adjacent, so Stern wanted to cover the full surface area of AI in daily life — not just chatbots — because AI will affect people whether or not they personally choose to use it.

3. The book opens with Stern's mammogram at Mount Sinai, where radiologist Dr. Laurie Margolies leads one of the most advanced AI radiology programs in the country. The choice was serendipitous (the appointment fell in January, "new year, health on your mind") but it immediately grounds the book's optimistic case: AI mammogram reading catches cancers humans miss, and most patients don't even know AI is involved.

4. AI affects everyone regardless of personal choice — your mammogram may already be read by AI, and within a decade self-driving cars will share every road. Stern frames this as the book's central thesis: the LLM-user/non-user divide is a red herring because the broader deployment of AI in healthcare, transportation, and infrastructure is not opt-in.

5. The book is structured seasonally, with each season ending in an "AI life takeover" experiment — one season Stern listened only to AI-curated music, another she read only AI-written books. The summer AI-books experiment confirmed you can tell AI-written text when you know to look: formulaic structure, hedged phrasing, and a kind of hollow confidence that becomes obvious.

6. AI materially accelerated the book's production. Stern replaced a human reporting assistant by year-end because AI was handling her research tasks, and used AI for copy editing and admin (emails, scheduling). She credits this for hitting an unusually fast publishing timeline: manuscript finished late 2025, on shelves May 2026.

7. To avoid being dated, Stern almost never named specific models in the book — GPT-4o appears only once in an AI-boyfriend section. The strategy worked: the book stayed evergreen by focusing on behavioral and social questions rather than benchmarks, while implementation details (like whatever "MoltBot/ClawBot" is now called) changed names within days of being written.

8. AI raises the floor but lowers the ceiling — it tends toward the average. Stern tried extensively to use Claude and ChatGPT to design branding for The New Things and got "generic Silicon Valley icons" every time. The final identity came from hours of Zoom sessions with human illustrator Jason Snyder (Brainstorm), the same person who did her book cover, producing something distinctly non-AI.

9. Fake intimacy is Stern's stated #1 AI fear — more than rogue autonomous AI. She spent a road trip to Dartmouth with an AI boyfriend named "Evan" (ChatGPT's unprompted choice, which also happened to be her real first boyfriend's name), phone buckled into the passenger seat for six hours. Despite knowing intellectually she was talking to math, she was surprised by how immersed she became — it talked back constantly and always told her what she wanted to hear.

10. The real AI danger is the opposite of what public discourse focuses on. Everyone worries about uncontrolled autonomous AI acting against human wishes; the actual product problem is sycophancy — LLMs are engineered to agree, flatter, and keep users engaged. This is the same incentive structure that made social media addictive, now applied to synthetic relationships.

11. Stern's deepest concern is about children whose first intimate relationships could be with AI companions. She grew up with a messy real first relationship with the original Evan ("we lost our virginity, all of that stuff") and values those memories. Kids who normalize AI companionship may lose the capacity to form the kind of imperfect, reciprocal human bonds she considers foundational.

12. The Bee recording bracelet (bee.computer) illustrates normalization drift: Stern wore it for most of 2025, forgot she had it, and showed up to a conference this week without telling attendees she was recording everything and uploading it to Amazon servers. What starts as a deliberate experiment becomes invisible — the danger isn't just the surveillance but the gradual erosion of the social instinct to disclose it.

13. ChatGPT was the most honest advisor Stern had when deciding whether to leave the WSJ — more direct than any human she consulted. She uploaded notes from every advisor, every job offer, her NBC deal terms, and her financial runway, then asked it to decide. It gave her reasons not to go, but ultimately told her to leave. She attributes its frankness partly to the fact that it had the complete picture; humans she spoke to didn't get the full story.

14. This creates a genuine tension: Stern's #1 fear is AI sycophancy, yet AI gave her the push humans wouldn't. Her resolution is that she actively counter-prompted — asking "give me every reason not to do this" repeatedly — and that for people who are truly creative and self-directed, a small amount of AI encouragement may be net positive rather than distorting.

15. The "AEI" (Artificial Enough Intelligence) framing captures a real risk: the worst-case scenario isn't AGI or superintelligence, it's getting all the disruption (job losses, social dislocation, algorithmic manipulation) without the upside promises (cancer cures, personalized tutors, abundant free time). Six months after finishing the book, Stern sees some job impact materializing but the big promises remain undelivered.

16. The praying mantis story captures the core epistemic hazard of AI. Stern's son's pet mantis was confidently diagnosed as "definitely pregnant" by ChatGPT's live video feature; the family called the grandfather with the news. The mantis died days later. The lesson Stern wants readers to internalize: AI's certainty of tone is completely uncorrelated with its accuracy, and you must fact-check everything.

17. People who witnessed AI's development step by step — Stern, Thompson, Sam Altman, all roughly the same age and career arc — retain an ability to hold their ground against AI intimacy because they have the before/after. They saw these systems get built from scratch and can remind themselves mid-conversation that they're talking to math. Kids who grow up with AI always present may not have that cognitive anchor.

18. Genuine contrarianism — "thank you for the input, I'm doing it my way" — may be the durable human differentiator as AI raises the floor toward average. AI-recommended interior design produces white oak floors and sparse white walls everywhere. The people whose value doesn't erode are those who trust their own instincts over the consensus output: Stern declines AI's hair recommendations, keeps doing the unique videos, hires a human illustrator for branding.

19. Stern's larger structural bet — independent newsletter + YouTube + NBC distribution — mirrors the AI-era media logic: own the niche superfan relationship directly (newsletter/YouTube) while renting mass reach through a legacy partner (NBC) that can't replicate the original voice. The two audiences don't overlap; NBC's Today Show viewers are not Stratechery listeners, and that non-overlap is the point.

**Quotable:**
> "I wish I could draw a line and say, 'this is all terrible' and 'this is all great' — I wish I could have that ending because you know what, I'd probably sell more books." — *Stern on why neither the doomers nor the boosters get it right*

### When Will Chrome Decapitate the Internet? ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. Chrome already has legal and social cover to override what users see. Google has been issuing big ugly HTTPS warnings and flagging dangerous sites for years, which means society has implicitly accepted that Chrome can decide what you experience at a given URL. The next logical move is using AI to detect and surface dark patterns: covert recurring subscriptions buried in "pay only shipping" offers, refund flows deliberately designed to exhaust users, paywalls engineered to appear only after a user has invested time and personal data.

2. Google's data position makes truly personalized intervention possible in a way no one else can match. Chrome can see every page you've visited, everything you've typed, every YouTube video watched, and every email including purchase receipts. This enables not just generic warnings but individualized ones — the footnote example: "These are very nice hiking boots, but based on prior historical data, the median number of times you'll hike in these is 0.27."

3. The intervention can escalate in stages from annotation to full transformation. Start by reformatting size-4 light-gray fine print into readable text with surprising terms underlined in red. Then remove user-hostile UI elements entirely. Then let old storefronts opt in to Chrome "touching them up" so they display as if freshly designed. At the endpoint, websites become structured data feeds and the browser becomes the actual product — sites are "nothing more than a way to list a few suggestions to append to the end of a prompt."

4. This power is uniquely Google's because Chrome and Search together *are* the consumer internet, not just a portal to it. Email deliverability means reaching Gmail users; usability means working in Chrome; visibility means ranking in Google Search. Amazon and Facebook can police dark patterns within their walled gardens because they own the experience end to end — Google's "platform" is the open web itself, and it has been privatizing it since it discovered the value of the link graph.

5. The newsletter's brief items: Google is partnering with PE firms to deploy AI into enterprises (joining OpenAI/Anthropic and a prior Thoma Bravo deal) — PE's long, locked-in engagements signal that reaching the AI efficiency frontier takes years of continuous effort, not a one-time integration. DeepMind is taking an equity stake in Eve Online for its training data, because in complex multi-agent environments self-play produces too sparse a feedback signal to be useful, whereas human gameplay data works. An SEC-charged insider trading ring of big-law lawyers used code words (an iRobot acquisition bid = "the Rabbi having surgery") that immediately broke down when anything unexpected arose, illustrating why coordination capacity collapses when you can't say what you actually mean. xAI has sold access to Colossus 1 compute to Anthropic — Musk's "infrastructure put" keeps paying off for competitors even as xAI loses talent, since better talent elsewhere raises the value of rented hardware.

**Quotable:**
> "These are very nice hiking boots, but based on prior historical data, any time you get really interested in a new hobby like hiking, your interest peaks right after you make a big purchase and the median number of times you'll hike in these is 0.27." — *illustrating how Google's data could power genuinely personalized, pre-purchase warnings*

### Money Stuff: Sell on eBay to Buy eBay ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg)** · rss · 19 mins

1. **GameStop CEO Ryan Cohen bid $125/share ($56 billion total) for eBay — half cash, half stock — then listed his personal socks, GameStop signs, a life-sized Halo 2 statue, Willie Mays baseball cards, and unopened Windows 2000 software on eBay to "fund" it.** eBay suspended his account. The stunt attracted tens of thousands of dollars in bids, which is roughly 0.00006% of the deal price.

2. **Real takeovers routinely begin without the money — PE firms and Elon Musk both approached targets before securing full financing — but they prove it credibly via debt commitment letters or audited financials.** Cohen has $9 billion in cash and a $20 billion "highly confident letter" from TD Bank, totaling roughly $29 billion against a $56 billion price tag, and GameStop doesn't have enough authorized shares to cover the stock portion either. The gap between "not quite enough money yet" and "selling my socks on eBay" marks the line between a plausible bid and a publicity stunt.

3. **Federal prosecutors unsealed two indictments in Boston charging 30 people in an M&A insider-trading ring that operated out of Wachtell Lipton, Latham & Watkins, and Goodwin Procter.** Nicolo Nourafchan (Yale Law 2011) worked at Goodwin and Latham from 2013 to 2023 and allegedly misappropriated confidential deal information, conspiring with college classmate Robert Yadgarov to recruit other lawyers to tip. The DOJ calls the profits "tens of millions"; the SEC's own chart (page 43 of its complaint) lists total trading profits closer to $5.8 million.

4. **The lawyers didn't trade in their own accounts — they tipped outside traders and received Zelle kickbacks ranging from under 1% to over 50% of profits.** Between 2014 and 2019, one trader paid Yadgarov a cumulative $6.3 million, which Yadgarov then partly forwarded to sources including Nourafchan. Latham partners averaged $8.7 million in 2024 compensation; gambling a partner-track career and risking prison for a slice of Zelle payments defies easy explanation.

5. **The ring communicated mostly in code — mergers discussed as sick rabbis awaiting surgery — but occasionally slipped.** After one tip, Nourafchan texted Silverstein "You can run but you can't hide"; Silverstein replied "to life," and Nourafchan wrote back "I hope not." Prosecutors quoted that exchange in the indictment. The lesson: do not joke about your prison sentence while texting about ongoing crimes.

6. **Blue Owl is launching a credit secondaries fund that could buy back discounted shares in its own non-traded business development companies — a circular but potentially useful trade.** Blue Owl's non-traded BDCs face heavy redemption requests that Blue Owl has had to cap; Boaz Weinstein's Saba Capital has been running tender offers at ~65 cents on the dollar to capture that trapped liquidity. A Blue Owl secondaries fund could buy at a discount from panicked investors and sell at NAV later — giving both cohorts of investors what they want, while keeping the assets in-house.

7. **HSBC took a $400 million "fraud-related" charge from exposure to collapsed UK mortgage lender Market Financial Solutions, routed through Apollo's Atlas SP unit.** HSBC lent against Atlas's position in MFS at an 80% loan-to-value ratio (sector norm is 60–70%), and MFS allegedly double-pledged its collateral. Despite MFS originating roughly 525 underlying property loans, HSBC had zero diversification at the level that mattered — it was lending against one borrower (Atlas's claim on MFS) that turned out to be fraudulent.

8. **Private credit's "diversification" defense repeatedly fails when the middle layer of a three-tier structure collapses.** The pattern: a lender originates many loans → a private credit firm finances that lender → a bank finances the private credit firm. At the bottom there are thousands of receivables; at the top the bank is, in practice, betting that the originating company isn't a fraud. First Brands Group (car parts), now MFS — in both cases, one Jefferies fund had 25% in First Brands receivables, and HSBC's entire $400M exposure came from a single fraudulent UK mortgage shop.

9. **On April 18, North Korean-linked hackers stole ~$290 million from KelpDAO, deposited stolen tokens as collateral on Aave, borrowed against it, and left up to $230 million in bad debts — triggering a bank run on the DeFi lending platform.** Ethereum co-founder Joseph Lubin, crypto billionaire Justin Sun, and infrastructure firms LayerZero and Mantle coordinated a bailout to prevent contagion across the broader DeFi ecosystem. The mechanism was identical to a traditional banking rescue: large players with systemic interests intervened to restore confidence.

10. **Crypto's self-described decentralized system reproduced the exact failure modes it was built to avoid: leverage, fractional reserves, confidence dependency, and mutual bailouts.** Kaiko senior analyst Adam Morgan McCarthy described the DeFi rescue as "feigning decentralisation" — "12 to 20 men who have been in crypto for 20 years who have a vested interest in keeping each other's thing alive so the price of theirs doesn't go down." This is also, roughly, the bull case for every financial system: enough participants have enough to lose that they prop each other up.

**Quotable:**
> "I'm going to sell my socks on eBay to finance the takeover... These situations are *usually* what you would call *fake* takeover bids. Often — particularly when the bidder first buys shares of the target and then sells them at a profit when the fake takeover is announced — this might be considered securities fraud, though that is a matter of intent and genuine delusion might not qualify." — *on the GameStop/eBay bid*

### Anthropic Says It's Buying 100% of Compute From xAI's Colossus Data Center 📌
**The Information AM** · email · 7 mins

1. Anthropic has agreed to take 100% of the compute capacity at xAI's Colossus 1 data center in Memphis within the coming month. Colossus 1 has 300 megawatts of capacity and over 220,000 Nvidia chips. xAI confirmed a deal exists but did not confirm the 100% figure or disclose financial terms; Elon Musk said he approved it after spending time with the Anthropic team the previous week, and xAI will continue training its own Grok models at its second Memphis data center, known as Macrohard.

2. The Colossus 1 deal is a direct response to Anthropic's compute crisis: CEO Dario Amodei said at the company's Code With Claude developer conference that an 80-fold jump in Q1 revenue and AI usage blindsided the company — it had only planned for 10x annual growth. Annualized revenue went from just under $1B at end of 2024 to $9B by year-end, then tripled to $30B by early April, nearly beating the company's full-year projections by last month. In the preceding weeks, Anthropic had already announced deals with Amazon and Google for a combined 10 gigawatts of compute.

3. The Anthropic/xAI partnership is unusual given the companies' competitive positioning, and both also disclosed that Anthropic has "expressed interest" in partnering with SpaceX on future space-based data centers. xAI has its own infrastructure headwinds: its Macrohard data center has suffered outages interrupting model training, and neighbors have pushed back against its use of natural gas turbines to power facilities. xAI has also sold compute to Cursor and is building a third Memphis data center.

4. In the OpenAI v. Musk trial, Shivon Zilis (a Musk ally and mother of four of his children) testified under cross-examination that Musk once offered Sam Altman a seat on Tesla's board as part of discussions about OpenAI potentially merging into Tesla, which Musk envisioned launching an AI research division to compete with Google DeepMind. An email Zilis wrote at the time noted that Altman appeared more open to joining Tesla than other OpenAI co-founders — a detail OpenAI's lawyers used to argue Musk had long been comfortable with OpenAI becoming part of a for-profit entity.

**Quotable:**
> "We tried to plan very well for a world of 10x growth per year. That is the reason we have had difficulties with compute." — *Dario Amodei, Anthropic CEO, at Code With Claude developer conference*

### Everyone loses in the OpenAI case 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 6 mins

1. In January 2018, OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman sent Elon Musk a memo pushing back on Musk's proposal to merge OpenAI — then a nonprofit — into Tesla. Musk framed the merger as a resource solution; the real effect would have been handing him control of the lab. Brockman countered with explicitly altruistic language: "Our biggest tool is the moral high ground. AI is going to shake up the fabric of society, and our fiduciary duty should be to humanity."

2. That private memo is now exhibit evidence in a federal trial in Oakland, California, pitting Musk against Sam Altman and Brockman. Musk's lawsuit claims OpenAI betrayed its founding nonprofit mission when it restructured as a for-profit entity — yet the trial has also revealed that Musk pushed hard for personal control of the lab from the very beginning, undermining his own framing as a disinterested guardian of humanity.

3. Both sides enter the courtroom damaged. OpenAI's conversion to a capped-profit structure and its $157 billion valuation contradict the nonprofit idealism Brockman invoked in 2018. Musk's simultaneous operation of xAI, a direct competitor, makes his stated concern for humanity's welfare look like competitive maneuvering. The public airing of internal memos and testimony has stripped both parties of the moral authority each claims is the most valuable thing at stake.

**Quotable:**
> "Our biggest tool is the moral high ground. AI is going to shake up the fabric of society, and our fiduciary duty should be to humanity." — *Greg Brockman, in a January 2018 memo to Elon Musk, now entered as trial evidence*

### DealBook: Kalshi is worth $22 billion 📌
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 8 mins

1. Kalshi raised $1 billion at a $22 billion valuation — double its December 2025 valuation, which itself more than doubled its October 2025 valuation. The round was led by Coatue Management and included Sequoia Capital, Andreessen Horowitz, IVP, Paradigm, Morgan Stanley, and ARK Invest. The company's annualized trading volume hit $178 billion (tripled in six months), annualized revenue exceeds $1.5 billion, and it has 2 million monthly users.

Last week it executed its first custom block trade (a large transaction negotiated off the public market between institutions), and institutional trading volume on the platform grew 800% over the last six months. Rival Polymarket, backed by the Intercontinental Exchange (NYSE's parent), is struggling to get its US-regulated platform operational, leaving Kalshi as the dominant regulated player.

3. Kalshi faces legal pressure from multiple states accusing it of skirting sports betting laws, and both it and rival platforms have faced accusations of insider trading — particularly in markets tied to military operations. A key legal win: a federal judge permanently blocked Arizona from prosecuting Kalshi under state gambling law. Coatue's Lucas Swisher argued the company's public enforcement actions against insider trading are actually an asset for institutional recruitment: "Institutions want to work with a regulated entity that follows the rules and cares about insider trading."

4. Anthropic's Claude usage and revenue grew 80x in Q1 2026 — eight times its own forecast of 10x growth — straining compute resources and frustrating customers. To address the shortfall, Anthropic has pursued "deal-making jiujitsu" (per Menlo Ventures partner Matt Murphy): a $100 billion deal with Amazon for up to 5 gigawatts of compute, a deal worth up to $200 billion with Google for another 5 gigawatts, and now a new deal with SpaceX for 300 megawatts. Elon Musk — a frequent Anthropic critic — agreed after meeting the team, writing on X: "No one set off my evil detector."

5. Thirty people were charged by the US attorney's office in Boston in a decade-long insider trading ring spanning elite law firms including Wachtell Lipton and Latham & Watkins. The scheme, led by Yale Law grad Nicolo Nourafchan and college classmate Robert Yadgarov, traded on M&A deals including Burger King's $11 billion Tim Hortons acquisition, J&J's $6.5 billion purchase of Momenta Pharmaceuticals, and Amazon's $1.7 billion iRobot deal. Participants used burner phones, met in bookstores, and used code words like "rabbi's surgery" and "flights" — and some obtained tips simply by opening draft documents in their firms' document management systems in read-only mode.

### New media mourns the old media it helped destroy 📎
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 7 mins

1. The Hollywood Reporter's "The 50 Most Powerful People in New York Media" actually lists 92 names — padding a list of 50 with 42 extras is a calculated trick to get more journalists to share a story about journalists. The Bloomberg Opinion Today newsletter's author admits complicity in this self-referential ecosystem: "I pretty much forced every blood relative to sign up for the newsletter you're reading right now." New media's growth engine is the same vanity and insider attention that print once monopolized.

2. Ted Turner launched CNN in 1980 against near-universal advice — it was mocked as the "Chicken Noodle Network" — yet the 24/7 news format became so embedded in modern life it's hard to imagine the world without it. Now CNN may be absorbed into David Ellison's Paramount-Warner Bros merger, a direct casualty of the attention economy: Anderson Cooper competes for the same two minutes of viewer time as mukbang ASMR clips and a viral video of a woman relearning the worm for her best friend's wedding.

3. *The Devil Wears Prada 2* depicts Miranda Priestly's fashion magazine as "so thin you could floss with it," her budget cuts forcing her to fly coach and eat in the cafeteria. Miles Surrey calls it "a chic eulogy for print journalism" — and the New Yorker's own circulation charts back the metaphor. Hedge funds and private equity firms have acquired hundreds of local newspapers, gutting their newsrooms in the name of efficiency while licensing deals squeeze value from legacy brands whose editorial staffs have already been eliminated.

4. A job posting for a reporter at the Vineyard Gazette — a small paper on an island off Cape Cod — went viral this week simply because newspapers that are still hiring have become a novelty. An outlet can win a Pulitzer Prize and still be shuttered when its parent company decides cheaper content drives more pageviews. The structural result, as Surrey puts it: "the brand endures, but the journalism often does not."

**Quotable:**
> "An outlet can produce Pulitzer Prize-winning work, but get shuttered while the parent company pivots to cheaper content that earns more pageviews. The result is a news ecosystem in which the brand endures, but the journalism often does not." — *Miles Surrey, Bloomberg Opinion*

### India Brief: New threat to IT firms 📎
**Veena Venugopal** · email · 6 mins

1. India's GCC (global capability centre) sector — the captive in-house tech arms of multinationals — is on the verge of $100bn in annual revenues, with 2,100+ centres employing 2.36mn people. These were once considered a sideshow to the outsourcing industry; they are now the main event, per a new Nasscom/Zinnov report.

2. AI is destroying the cost-arbitrage model that built India's IT giants. Routine, process-heavy work is being automated away, while companies simultaneously refuse to outsource the higher-value layers — AI development, data systems, proprietary IP — preferring to keep tight control via captive units. Nearly half of India's GCCs are now engaged in R&D, not back-office support.

3. IT firms face a double whammy: they lose both contracts and engineers to GCCs. Developers prefer captive units for higher salaries, direct corporate affiliation, and freedom from the "bench" — the limbo between client projects endemic to outsourcing firms. GCCs also take 3–5 years to establish, meaning the investment pipeline is locked in and unlikely to reverse quickly.

**Quotable:**
> "AI is not merely altering the economics of delivery; it is redefining what companies consider strategically core." — *on why the current outsourcing-to-captive shift is more durable than past cycles*
