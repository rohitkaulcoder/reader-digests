### Money Stuff: Private Credit Has Some Debt

**Author:** Matt Levine
**Source:** RSS
**Reading Time:** 14 min

#### Key Insights

1. The GAV-vs-NAV distinction is critical. A 10% discount on loans creates a 20% discount on the fund due to leverage. If a fund owns $200 of loans worth 90 cents with $100 of debt, reported NAV is $100 but real NAV is $80. Leverage doubles the marking error.

2. Withdrawals mechanically increase leverage in a self-reinforcing loop. A fund that gives back 5% to investors goes from 1x to 1.1x leverage — each redemption wave makes remaining investors riskier.

3. The "anti-PE" trade is real. American Operator helps employees buy businesses from retiring owners while running a "Locally Owned and Operated" certification program. Levine predicts "No PE Here" certification will become valuable the way ESG labels did.

4. The Tricolor auto lending bankruptcy reveals a paradox: borrowers still legally owe money on loans even though the company defrauded its own creditors. About half of ~40,000 borrowers are already in default.

#### Notable Quotes

> "A 10% discount on the loans — a 10% gap between the marked value of the loans and their actual market value — creates a 20% discount on the fund."

> "It is partly true — it is half-true — that private credit is funded by long-term locked-up equity investors."

> "The moon emoji is securities fraud, but the poop emoji is not."

#### One-Line Summary

Private credit's "safe funding model" narrative is only half true — funds are typically 1x levered, and that leverage magnifies NAV discount problems and creates a feedback loop with every redemption.

### The Great India Under-Allocation

**Author:** Quantum Advisors (qasl.com)
**Source:** https://www.qasl.com/post/the-great-india-under-allocation
**Reading Time:** 9 min

#### Key Insights

1. The EM bucket is the structural trap. Annual foreign portfolio flows to EM equities have fallen from ~$100B to below $50B — for a group representing 50% of global GDP. India, bucketed within EM, can only capture its share of a shrinking pie.

2. India's outperformance paradoxically triggers outflows. As India outperforms other EMs within fixed-allocation mandates, fund managers rebalance by selling India to buy cheaper laggards. India returned 10.1% annualized over 25 years vs. 7.4% for EM — yet this causes mechanical selling.

3. Domestic investors have replaced foreign investors as the marginal buyer. Since CY2021, Indian mutual funds bought ~$145B while foreign investors sold $6B net. SIPs from retail investors now provide more consistent capital than global allocators.

4. The under-allocation is quantifiable: over $1 trillion. Foreign holdings at $858B represent less than 1% of ~$120 trillion global equity allocation. On GDP-weighted basis, the gap exceeds $4 trillion.

5. Only ~20% of foreign holdings in India are through dedicated India mandates. The other 80% flows through global, EM, or Asia funds whose allocation is determined by bucket-level decisions, not India-specific analysis.

#### Notable Quotes

> "The most common response to our reach outs to prospective Global Investors is 'We do not make single country allocations.' The fact that they do not invest in single country is of course not true. Global investors have standalone allocations to the United States."

> "Foreign Investors have invested less than 0.3% of Global Financial AuM in Indian equities."

#### One-Line Summary

Foreign investors hold less than 0.3% of global AUM in Indian equities despite India's consistent outperformance, structurally constrained by declining flows to the "emerging markets" bucket.
