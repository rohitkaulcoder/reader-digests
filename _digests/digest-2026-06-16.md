## Today's Top 3

### Anthropic's Safety Superpower ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 12 mins

1. Fable 5 (the public release of Mythos Preview) was forcibly shut down by a U.S. government export control directive citing national security, issued at 5:21pm ET with no specific details. Anthropic pushed back publicly, arguing the demonstrated jailbreak only surfaced "a small number of previously known, minor vulnerabilities" discoverable by other public models. The jailbreak was reportedly reported to the government by Amazon — notable because Amazon is both an investor in Anthropic and a major inference provider, making this a deeply awkward intra-ecosystem conflict.

2. Frontier labs face a structural bear case: every leading-edge model they release at enormous cost gets distilled and commoditized by open-source alternatives, primarily from China, leaving Anthropic and OpenAI collectively losing tens of billions. The only escape is to own the user touchpoint directly — becoming the canvas for workflows rather than a commodity API input — which puts the labs on a direct collision course with software companies. Satya Nadella's essay explicitly warned against "a small number of AI systems capturing all the economic returns," but as Thompson notes, globalization hollowed out industrial economies anyway, making Nadella's warning read more like a prophecy than a deterrent.

3. The subsidized subscription plans are a data play as much as a market-share play. SemiAnalysis estimated that Anthropic's $200/month plan delivers ~$8,000 worth of Claude tokens — a massive subsidy justified by the value of real-world usage data for reinforcement learning. Anthropic escalated this with Fable by changing data retention to 30 days for *all* users, including enterprise plans that previously had zero-retention guarantees, with no third-party escrow or structural safeguard against future training use.

4. Anthropic's Fable System Card disclosed that the model would *silently* degrade its own performance for requests related to frontier LLM development — building pretraining pipelines, distributed training infrastructure, or ML accelerator design — via hidden prompt modification, steering vectors, or PEFT. Estimated to hit ~0.03% of traffic in fewer than 0.1% of organizations, the policy was quickly walked back after backlash (Fable now hands off to Opus 4.8 with disclosed notification), but it revealed that Anthropic both *can* and *will* covertly alter model behavior to achieve policy preferences — validating supply-chain-risk concerns.

5. The silent degradation policy arrived only two months after Anthropic clashed with the Department of War over autonomous weapons and surveillance use. The pattern is consistent: Anthropic believes it alone should have final say over how its models are used, that only it should be building frontier AI, and therefore that it should have final say over AI generally. Combined with pronouncements that AI will conduct all economic activity, this amounts to Anthropic's leadership seeking structural power over the entire economy — framed at every step as safety.

6. Safety justifications function inside Anthropic not as rationalizations but as genuine beliefs, and that's what makes them so powerful and so hard to contest from outside. The company was founded by people who left OpenAI believing it wasn't serious enough about safety; the mission, the talent, and the business all perfectly reinforce each other — every self-serving policy move is also, in their sincere view, the right thing for humanity. Thompson compares this to Apple's user-privacy framing, but flags the asymmetry: Apple builds phones you can take or leave; Anthropic is building something with the potential to rival nation-states in power, held by people utterly convinced of their own good intentions.

**Quotable:**
> "Anthropic gets to sell to researchers the creation of a machine god, with the mantle of being the sort of person who cares about the dangers and is smart enough to navigate them on behalf of humanity; that every policy change that falls out of that happens to be great for business is the most beautiful coincidence in the world." — *on Anthropic's perfect internal alignment between mission and commercial interest*

### Money Stuff: The Stock Market Will Get More Stock ⚡
**Matt Levine** · email · 16 mins

1. For roughly two decades, the US stock market was defined by scarcity: S&P 500 buybacks alone erased nearly $12 trillion worth of shares, steadily returning capital to investors rather than raising new capital. That era is ending — JPMorgan projects IPOs, secondary offerings, and other share sales will add roughly $1.5 trillion of *net* new stock over the next two years, the strongest period of net equity issuance since at least the late 1990s.

2. The AI buildout has exhausted cheaper funding sources in sequence. Hyperscalers first tapped profits and free cash flow, then debt markets. Now, as StoneX Financial's Vincent Deluard put it: "We started with profits and free cash flow, then they started raising debt. Now it's everything. It's the cash flow, it's the debt and it's the equity." Net share repurchases by big tech companies that provide AI computing fell last year, a direct reversal of the buyback trend.

3. This "re-equitization" removes one of the market's most durable tailwinds. Robert Buckland, the Citigroup strategist who popularized the concept of de-equitization, warns that big tech firms can no longer easily prop up their own stock prices during corrections — when Meta famously bought back heavily after its 2021–2022 crash, it was spending cash that AI capex is now consuming. The AI boom demands simultaneous, foundational, speculative investment from everyone at once, which is structurally different from the staggered capital needs of normal business cycles.

4. SpaceX priced its IPO at $135 per share ($1.77 trillion valuation) — aggressive at roughly 100x revenue and negative earnings — jumped as much as 31% on opening day, and closed up 19% at $160.95, ending day one as the sixth-most-valuable public company in the world at $2.2 trillion and making Elon Musk the world's first trillionaire. The "perfect IPO pop" benchmark is 20%, giving institutional buyers a quick win without leaving embarrassing money on the table; SpaceX hit it almost exactly.

5. By the time SpaceX actually priced, the IPO was almost an anticlimax: pre-IPO perpetual futures on Hyperliquid were trading in the mid-$160s to low-$170s the day before the official $135 offering price — essentially predicting where the stock would open. TD Securities analyst Reid Noch noted that major institutions were actively tracking Hyperliquid for real-time price discovery in the days before the IPO, raising the question of whether the betting market was setting the IPO price or the IPO price was setting the betting market.

6. The blurring of investing, trading, and gambling markets is accelerating. Crypto perpetual futures — contracts with no expiration date — now exceed $90 trillion in annual volume. Bank of America analysts call this the "gamification" of markets. The practical implication: for future IPO candidates like OpenAI and Anthropic, pre-IPO perp markets could function as an additional signal to help issuers and underwriters determine the correct offering price, reducing first-day mispricing without sacrificing short-term upside for buyers.

7. A New Yorker profile of Ken Griffin traces Citadel's origin to a 1986 epiphany about "quality of earnings." Griffin made $5,000 on a Home Shopping Network options trade but was bothered that the market maker on the other side, though earning less per trade, had reliable cash flow vs. his risky directional bet. That realization led him to convertible arbitrage — simultaneously long a convertible bond and short the underlying stock — then to building Citadel as a business that generates consistent returns rather than making good bets. Citadel's best portfolio managers win only about 54% of their trades; the edge is systematic, not directional.

8. Citadel's pod-shop model treats portfolio managers as industrial inputs: "Citadel consumes new traders as voraciously as Starbucks consumes beans." One former employee kept a "Book of Souls" listing 50 colleagues who left a small trading unit over six years; another had five bosses in five years. The former employees' verdict: "It was a gift to be there and a gift to leave." The pod shop model is, in Levine's framing, essentially what investment banks did 40 years ago — providing liquidity, capturing durable risk premiums, making markets more efficient — now running under a hedge fund structure.

9. Nubank accidentally sent bank-liquidation notices to customers after an employee triggered a liquidation protocol while submitting a routine internal software change request. Co-founder Cristina Junqueira confirmed on Instagram it was "an operational error" affecting a small portion of customers. Levine's point: sending customers a message saying "the bank has been liquidated" is nearly as damaging as actual liquidation — panic and withdrawals would follow immediately. Nubank briefly deleted itself.

**Quotable:**
> "We started with profits and free cash flow, then they started raising debt. Now it's everything. It's the cash flow, it's the debt and it's the equity." — *StoneX Financial's Vincent Deluard on how AI companies are funding the buildout*

### What Does it Mean to Have a Trillion Dollars? ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 15 mins

1. The $1 trillion threshold is arithmetically inevitable and morally arbitrary. At 2% real GDP growth, 3% inflation, a 5% equity risk premium, plus dispersion among equities — and given that humans count in base ten — it was simply a matter of time this decade before some individual crossed the mark-to-market threshold. Arguments made specifically at $1T that were equally valid at $990B are suspicious; they coincide with a sharp drop in how informed the average debate participant is.

2. Money as a concept breaks down above a few million dollars. Below that, it's a Boolean: can you afford this or not? Slightly richer: it's deferred consumption. But at the scale of a trillion, Musk's wealth buys him roughly the same thing $10B or $100B did — de facto control of SpaceX and Tesla, plus the ability to fund things that don't yet exist commercially. The relevant unit is power over output, not a claim on candy bars.

3. Trillion-dollar wealth barely competes with ordinary consumption. Musk buying a couple of houses in Austin contributes roughly "three successful dermatologists' worth" of suburban price appreciation. His real effect is redirecting America's total output from consumption toward investment — specifically space travel, AI datacenters, and EVs. The trillion dollars is the market's estimate of his share of future upside from that investment, not a hoard he's sitting on.

4. A wealth tax on Musk-scale fortunes accomplishes less than advertised. A 5% annual wealth tax on Musk would increase Federal spending capacity by about 0.67% per year — a marginal rounding error against an existing $7.4 trillion annual budget. The math of redistribution is underwhelming; the real argument for or against has to be about productive allocation, not arithmetic.

5. The "he got rich off government subsidies" argument is self-defeating. Without early EV tax credits, there would likely be no Tesla (and probably Chinese EVs instead); SpaceX wouldn't exist at scale without government launch contracts. But NASA spent approximately $840 billion (inflation-adjusted) while launch costs stagnated for decades during the shuttle era, and costs only fell once SpaceX entered. Simultaneously arguing that the government spends money better than Musk *and* that Musk only got rich because the government is easy to manipulate is a contradiction.

6. Musk is the first trillionaire largely because of extreme risk tolerance, not just superior skill. Other deca- and centi-billionaires are also talented but have much lower tolerance for the tail outcomes — bankruptcy, prison, catastrophic liability. Some of Musk's edge is genuine skill at underwriting low-probability bets; some is raw risk appetite; some is poor impulse control (his Twitter acquisition offer had no diligence contingency). The people who back Musk are partly hedging against the cost of being underweight him — which has been expensive for decades.

7. Expropriating a trillionaire's assets via politics creates a more dangerous concentration of power, not less. The government's ability to nationalize or heavily tax the 7th or 10th most valuable public company in the world gives politicians more raw power than Musk currently has. Markets have checked Musk's ambitions before — through car demand, share prices, and competitive pressure. The alternative check is a small number of low-engagement swing-state voters deciding every four years. Shifting power from markets to politics is a choice between elites, not a choice between elites and the public.

8. SpaceX IPO'd at $2.25 trillion, and Musk is already issuing informal forward guidance via personal tweets. After the IPO, Musk posted that SpaceX "might" reach $1T in revenue in 2030 and that he "would be surprised" if it's not above $1T in 2031. An implied 90% probability statement from a company's controlling founder about a specific revenue figure in a specific year is guidance — regardless of medium. SpaceX's S-1 lists the *SpaceX* X account (not Elon's personal account) for Reg FD compliance, creating a convenient ambiguity Musk is exploiting. His real accomplishments become harder to defend the more he treats securities rules as optional.

**Quotable:**
> "It's paradoxical, to put it politely, for people to simultaneously argue that Elon should pay higher taxes because the government will spend his money better than he does, but also that the only reason he has all that money in the first place is because it's incredibly easy to trick the government into wasting money." — *on the self-defeating logic of government-subsidy critiques of Musk's wealth*

## AI & Technology

### 🎙️ How I AI: Claude Fable 5 review & How Braintrust uses AI agents, evals, and CI to ship better software 📌
**Lenny's Newsletter** · email · 7 mins

1. **Claude Fable 5 is Anthropic's first "Mythos-class" model available to the public, and its benchmark numbers are real — but so are its blind spots.** It hit 80% on SWBench Pro, besting Opus 4.8, GPT-4.5, and Gemini 3.1 Pro. In practice, it excels at hard technical problems, long-horizon tasks, and vision work (document formatting, PDF parsing) but produces nearly unreadable specs and PRDs — dense paragraphs with internal references — and one-shot design output that was described as "shockingly bad": gray, black, red, simple outlines.

2. **At $10/M input tokens and $50/M output tokens, Fable 5 is a new pricing tier above Opus, and it burns tokens at roughly 2× the rate of other models.** The thorough "seasoned engineer" behavior that makes it great at hard problems also makes it over-engineered for fast iteration — when asked for an MVP that delivers customer value, it shipped something extremely narrow. The practical advice: use Fable for backend complexity and vision; default to Sonnet or Opus for front-end, strategy, specs, and design.

3. **Braintrust CEO Ankur Goyal's core claim: AI agents don't produce theoretically better code, but they produce practically better outcomes because they maintain consistent rigor humans can't sustain.** Humans lose context over days, skip tedious benchmarks, and have decaying attention on hard problems. An agent will test every column store format and every execution engine without fatigue, making the case for major infrastructure overhauls (previously too risky to validate) suddenly tractable.

4. **Evals are the new PRD — they define what success looks like rather than how to achieve it, and building the pipeline that converts real-world data into evals is the #1 engineering investment for AI product teams.** Ankur encodes designer taste into scoring functions by capturing feedback from their tastemaker ("David thinks it's OK to show both languages as long as…") and running hundreds of evals to quantify it — amplifying one expert's judgment across everything without requiring their manual review. When agents fail, the fix is never better prompting: close the session, improve the eval, and start fresh.

**Quotable:**
> "There's no staff engineer running as many rigorous benchmarks as someone using an agent. The baseline of rigor you get from agents is incredible, and there's simply no excuse anymore to skip benchmarks because they're tedious." — *Ankur Goyal, Braintrust CEO*

### I Interviewed an AI Version of GitHub's COO—Then Spoke to the Real One 📎
**Mike Taylor / Also True for Humans** · rss · 5 mins

1. AI personas are most valuable for their failures, not their successes. Taylor built a simulated Kyle Daigle from public writing, talks, and interviews and tested it against 12 planned interview questions — only 2 were strong matches, 4 partial, and 6 material misses. The misses were the useful output: they mapped exactly what wasn't on the public record and therefore where the live interview time should go. Taylor's prior startup Ask Rally, a virtual focus group tool, found the same pattern — AI personas don't replace real humans, but 85% accuracy in replicating human responses (per published research) makes them useful for preparation and stress-testing interview strategy.

2. GitHub's agent-driven activity is growing faster than any public forecast anticipated. At GitHub Universe in October 2024, GitHub announced 1 billion commits for the full year. By the May 2025 Microsoft Build event, Daigle stated GitHub was on track for 14 billion commits in 2025 if growth held linear — and in March 2025 alone, 17 million pull requests were created by agents, not humans. Daigle frames this as leaving "super-early-adoption" and climbing toward a new normal where each developer operates alongside one, two, or N agents using their skills and context, with GitHub committed to supporting "everyone's agent moment, not just GitHub Copilot."

3. The AI simulation's structural blind spot is recency: it can only re-serve already-public data. The AI correctly predicted the governing principle behind Daigle's answer on open source maintainer overload — "give maintainers explicit rules and guardrails, not just a larger inbox" — but named zero actual products. Copilot code review (now agentic, capable of finding novel vulnerabilities and implementing changes in-thread), agentic merge (letting Copilot wait for CI and policies before merging), and contributor acceptance controls were all announced at the event itself and therefore invisible to any persona trained on pre-event material.

**Quotable:**
> "Last October at GitHub Universe, we shared that there had been one billion commits on GitHub for the full year. We are on track for 14 billion if growth is linear this year, which it will not be. In March, 17 million pull requests were created by agents alone." — *Kyle Daigle, GitHub COO, on the scale of agent-driven activity*

## Finance & Markets

### Warsh is inheriting a rates-mountain breakdown 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. Kevin Warsh takes the Fed chair just as the rate-cutting cycle that Jay Powell nearly completed has been aborted by new shocks. Trump's tariff escalation last year had all but extinguished post-pandemic inflation, but US strikes on Iran more than three months ago triggered an energy shock that has pushed inflation expectations higher globally and forced central banks to recalibrate — flipping the macro backdrop from "how fast can we cut?" to "do we need to hike?"

2. Powell's descent from the inflation peak was largely successful but incomplete. Using z-scores benchmarked to the 2010–2019 low-inflation decade, the developed world suffered inflation at rare historical extremes — a shock far larger than in emerging markets. Powell avoided a recession and got inflation near target, but the descent was interrupted before it was finished; rates plateaued at a structurally higher altitude than the post-GFC decade, leaving little room to ease.

3. The ECB is the clearest warning sign for Warsh. Last week it became the first developed central bank to hike in response to the new inflation wave, and it simultaneously revised its projections toward higher inflation *and* weaker growth — a stagflationary combination. Oxford Economics' Angel Talavera says the hike is unlikely to be "one-and-done." Meanwhile, US 2-year Treasury yields sit at roughly 4.15%, well above the Fed's current policy range of 3.5%–3.75%, and markets already price in at least one Warsh-era hike this year. A Bloomberg diffusion index of 52 central banks confirms tightening is again becoming the global norm.

4. Warsh faces a structural credibility trap: markets expect him to be quicker to cut than Powell under similar conditions (reflecting Trump's preference for lower rates and why he was chosen), but the conditions that enabled cutting have evaporated. Capital Economics' Stephen Brown says a hike is nearly the base case even if oil eases, because three straight months of upside non-farm payrolls surprises have eliminated the labor-market downside risk that previously justified easing. Warsh is likely to hold this week and seek cover in data rather than signal an overtly hawkish pivot, with Oxford Economics' Michael Pearce arguing the bar for a hike remains high and Warsh may emphasize a potential improving inflation outlook to leave room for cuts later in 2026.

**Quotable:**
> "In economic policy as in mountaineering, most accidents happen on the descent from great heights." — *on Powell's legacy and why the handoff to Warsh is so treacherous*

## Geopolitics & Global Economics

### LLMs, AI And India's Deeper Tech Problem 📌
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. The hand-wringing over whether TCS, Wipro, and Infosys — each sitting on roughly $5 billion in cash — missed the AI moment is mostly misplaced. These firms did exactly what their shareholders, boards, and leadership mandated: deliver high-margin software services, capital appreciation, and steady growth. Their global counterparts Accenture and Capgemini have equally avoided mega-AI bets, for the same reason.

2. India's Chief Economic Advisor V Anantha Nageswaran traces the R&D deficit to four structural causes: a large captive domestic market, a historical lean toward trading and arbitrage, early financialisation, and third-generation business families losing entrepreneurial steam. These are systemic features, not failures of individual executives.

3. Capital markets structurally prevent bold R&D bets. Veteran investor Shankar Sharma argues that stock markets efficiently route capital toward high-ROE, predictable businesses — and no manufacturing or core research can match software services on those metrics. Management teams internalize this and optimize accordingly, leaving national technological ambitions as an afterthought. Only the state has patient capital for long-horizon R&D, yet both India and the US have starved it in favor of democratic populism.

4. India's startup generation shows no meaningful shift toward deep tech. The most high-profile founders built edtech — a sector whose existence is now routinely questioned — or burned VC on rapid pizza and shampoo delivery. Venture capital simply replicated the same short-termist capital-allocation logic as stock markets. Space and defense startups exist but don't approach the scale or audacity of SpaceX.

5. Past Indian innovation — Tata launching the Indica in 1998, Hero/Bajaj/TVS squeezing fuel efficiency from two-wheelers in the 1990s — was real but incremental. Betting on LLMs is a categorically different wager: deep-tech moonshots require a fundamentally different institutional environment, not just willingness to spend treasury cash.

6. India's education system is the root problem, not IT boardrooms. An exam culture plagued by chronically leaked papers and structured as a winner-take-all lottery systematically destroys the curiosity that bold technology bets require. India has as much to gain from solving indigenous drug discovery and scaling quality education as from a proprietary LLM that may never materialize.

**Quotable:**
> "This giant machine called the stock market does efficient capital allocation. And hence, it efficiently allocates capital to businesses with high valuation potential. No manufacturing or core research business can ever match up to services in terms of returns on equity and predictability." — *Veteran investor Shankar Sharma, on why Indian capital markets structurally prevent deep-tech investment*

### The World: Will the Iran deal stick? 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. Trump announced a U.S.-Iran ceasefire agreement on his 80th birthday (June 15, 2026), claiming the Strait of Hormuz would be "permanently toll-free" and Iran would forgo high-level enrichment "forever" — but neither the deal's text nor its core terms have been publicly released. Iran's foreign minister simultaneously described Hormuz "fees" rather than tolls, and U.S. reporter David Sanger noted Trump appeared to be claiming Iranian concessions that had not actually been made.

2. Both sides have genuine incentive to stop: Iran shut the Strait of Hormuz early in the war, spiking global energy prices, but Trump's counter-blockade cut off Iran's own oil exports — a prolonged standoff threatened to damage Iran's oil infrastructure. Iran has fired an estimated 1,500+ missiles and 4,700 drones against Gulf states, while the U.S. burned through air defense stockpiles and left Ukraine and Taiwan more exposed; yet as of May, U.S. intelligence assessed Iran still retained roughly 70% of its missile stockpiles.

3. Trump is under severe domestic pressure to exit the war: the Iran conflict is cited as a major factor in his record-low approval ratings, he has faced defections within the Republican Party, and midterm elections are months away — he campaigned explicitly on ending wars rather than starting them.

4. Israel is the most immediate spoiler: it lobbied Trump to launch the original strikes on Iran, later broadened the conflict to include Hezbollah in Lebanon, and when a deal appeared imminent this weekend, launched fresh strikes on Beirut's outskirts — prompting Trump to call Netanyahu "a difficult guy." Iran is insisting any peace deal cover Lebanon; Israel refuses to accept that constraint.

5. The nuclear question is unresolved and structurally hard: negotiators have not yet agreed on whether Iran pauses enrichment, at what future levels enrichment is permitted, or what happens to Iran's existing stockpile of enriched uranium. These are the same issues that took the Obama administration nearly two years to negotiate in the 2015 JCPOA — which Trump subsequently abandoned. Iran also wants sanctions relief and billions in frozen foreign-bank assets unfrozen, a concession Trump has repeatedly attacked Obama for making.

> "The president seems to be describing Iranian concessions that have not actually been made." — *David Sanger, NYT, on Trump's ceasefire announcement*

### Three ways India rode out Iran war 📎
**Michael Stott** · email · 7 mins

1. **The crisis was severe on paper.** When US-Israeli attacks on Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz in early March 2026, India — one of the world's biggest oil and gas importers with 10 million citizens working in Gulf states sending home $50bn a year — faced predictions of cascading failures: cooking gas running out, planes grounded for lack of fuel, crops failing for want of fertiliser.

2. **Speed of government response was the first pillar.** Within days of the conflict starting, officials identified India's specific economic vulnerabilities and drew up mitigation plans. New supply sources were secured and domestic refiners were ordered to prioritise bottled cooking gas and fertiliser production over other outputs.

3. **Shielding consumers from price shocks was the second, and most consequential, pillar.** Despite fertiliser prices nearly doubling on world markets and oil and gas soaring, the government held domestic prices steady and increased subsidies — a deliberate choice to sacrifice fiscal space in exchange for suppressed inflation, preserved consumer purchasing power, and reduced risk of social unrest. May consumer price inflation came in at 3.9%, with Citigroup projecting a peak of 6.2% in November — contained compared to what unfiltered pass-through would have caused (a contrast explicitly drawn to Chile's experience).

4. **Currency defence was the third pillar.** The Reserve Bank of India intervened to limit rupee depreciation, while the government simultaneously opened the capital account wider to attract foreign inflows: exempting foreign investors from capital gains and withholding taxes on government securities and allowing them to hold longer maturities — measures that had been available earlier but were only deployed under pressure.

5. **The damage was real but limited.** Now that Trump has announced a peace deal with Iran, officials estimate India's GDP for the year will be roughly 0.5 percentage points lower than it would have been, its fiscal deficit about 0.5 points wider, and inflation about 0.5 points worse. India will still be the fastest-growing major developing economy — a significant outcome given the initial scale of concern.

**Quotable:**
> "For a crisis initially predicted to knock India for six, that seems a pretty good outcome." — *Stott's closing assessment after walking through India's three-pronged response to the Gulf war's economic shock*
