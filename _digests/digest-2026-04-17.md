## Today's Top 3

### An Interview with F1 Driver and Venture Capitalist Nico Rosberg About the Drive to Win ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 39 mins

1. Rosberg was born in 1985 to Keke Rosberg, the 1982 F1 world champion, and was in a go-kart with a helmet by age three. His Plan B, if racing didn't work out, was already arranged: a reserved spot at Imperial College London to study aeronautics and become an aerodynamicist.

2. At 13, Rosberg and Lewis Hamilton were the only two drivers on a McLaren Mercedes-funded go-kart team — best friends who vacationed together and dreamed of one day being F1 teammates. Hamilton won more often than not even then; 15 years later, both were at Mercedes fighting for world championships.

3. Rosberg describes himself as more insecure and less self-confident than Hamilton — and credits that insecurity as a primary driver of his success. Lacking natural certainty forced extreme preparation: "When you don't have that self-confidence, you just fight so hard to prepare to the best of your abilities all the time."

4. After his difficult first year at Williams in 2006 (a bad car, nearly lost his seat for year two), Rosberg started working with a psychologist/philosopher — 2 hours every 2 days throughout each winter for 10 years. The work focused on meditation, visualization, the power of repetition, and understanding the root causes of emotions like fear, anxiety, and jealousy.

5. Using sports psychology was stigmatized in F1 at the time — drivers couldn't admit it without appearing weak, exactly as startup founders feel about admitting fear of failure. Rosberg calls it his "superpower." Lando Norris sought similar help mid-2025 season when his championship was slipping, made enormous progress, and won the title — validating the approach publicly.

6. Rosberg messaged Norris on Instagram (unreplied) with a specific critique: publicly saying "I messed that corner up" over and over — even when technically accurate — is destructive through repetition. Saying "that was nearly the best lap of my life" would be equally accurate, but the negative framing ingrained a failure identity. The lesson applies to founders who publicly self-flagellate about their company's struggles.

7. Rosberg was one of the early movers on YouTube, uploading his first F1 video in 2011 when most people barely knew the platform existed. His channel now has 1.46M subscribers. The strategy was deliberately multi-layered: fan connection, but also building a brand that makes teams choose you over an equally fast driver who lacks commercial appeal.

8. Netflix's Drive to Survive triggered a 2–3x increase in F1 sponsorship fees versus six or seven years earlier by making the sport relevant in the US and drawing in the tech industry. Mercedes now generates $400M/year in sponsorship revenue alone, over $600M including TV rights, while the budget cap keeps spending under $300M — making top teams extraordinarily profitable. The Mercedes team was valued at $6 billion; CrowdStrike CEO George Kurtz just paid $300M for a 5% stake.

9. Rosberg openly admits he missed the window to own a stake in an F1 team — an asset class that barely existed as an investment when he retired. He now casually brokers F1 sponsorships for tech companies, including currently connecting a multi-billion dollar dev-tools company with a team, paid by Mercedes in race tickets rather than euros.

10. Retiring at 31 immediately after winning the 2016 world championship was a rational decision: he had a baby at home, had given "a thousand percent" for years, and recognized he couldn't improve on the result of beating "the greatest of all time" in the best car. The decision analogizes to a founder's $10–50B exit: the perfect moment to leave on top rather than grind toward diminishing returns.

11. The marginal-gains campaign for that championship year was obsessive. Working with Harvard sleep professor Steven Lockley (who later founded the Timeshifter app), Rosberg eliminated jet lag entirely for the full 2016 season: maximum 1.5-hour daily time shift, blackout glasses 2 hours before sleep, 10,000-lux light immediately upon waking. He also stripped paint from his helmet to save 80 grams, arguing every gram matters.

12. The Yale Endowment was Rosberg's investment model inspiration. David Swensen allocated 20% of Yale's endowment (~$8B) to venture capital, achieving a 21% IRR — by far the best-performing asset class in the portfolio. Rosberg decided to replicate Swensen's approach with his own capital, then expanded to managing ~$200M AUM through Rosberg Ventures.

13. Rosberg Ventures runs a two-sided strategy: raise German/European family-office capital (which lacks access to US VC) and deploy it into the top 10 US VC funds and top 10 US growth-stage startups. Europeans know the value being created in US venture but historically haven't had the access — Rosberg's F1 network provides the trust to raise and the connections to deploy.

14. The fund-of-funds structure was a deliberate Trojan horse — a way to get positioned inside the US VC ecosystem, see everything, and identify the breakout startups and "generational founders" before moving to direct investments. Proximity to deal flow at the fund level accelerates the pipeline for direct bets.

15. Rosberg's unique arbitrage: he curates F1 race-weekend dinners mixing startup founders with CIOs of German large-cap companies (BMW, Mercedes, other legacy manufacturers) who are desperately seeking AI and sustainability transformation but are slow adopters. The value creation happens in the room — a CIO looking for exactly the product a founder is selling.

16. Dreamer — a vibe-coding AI agents startup founded by ex-Stripe CTO David Singleton and Hugo Barra (ex-Google/Meta/Xiaomi) — was immediately acquired by Meta after Rosberg met the team in San Francisco. The acquisition happened before Thompson could even write about the product, illustrating how fast the current M&A cycle is absorbing promising AI startups.

17. Rosberg is optimistic on AI but flags a specific structural risk: Anthropic has entered vertical application markets (e.g., legal tech) in ways that threaten established players like Harvey and Legora. The pattern — a frontier lab releasing a product that undercuts application-layer startups — is recurring across sectors, making frontier lab and "neo lab" investments feel structurally safer than pure-play vertical apps.

18. ElevenLabs (European, global voice AI leader) is Rosberg's example of a defensible application-layer bet: they build their own models, making them a frontier lab themselves, while also winning on platform and distribution. Even if voice models commoditize, their platform position means they aren't straightforwardly killed by OpenAI or Anthropic.

19. Europe's startup ecosystem faces a structural chicken-and-egg: cross-border scaling is expensive (different languages, different regulatory frameworks per country), which historically limited liquidity and funding depth, which limited the number of breakout companies, which limited LP enthusiasm for European VC. Lovable (Swedish, vibe coding for prosumers) and ElevenLabs are genuine bright spots, and EU-wide startup regulatory harmonization is in planning stages.

20. Rosberg's through-line across racing, YouTube, Sky Sports commentary, and VC is the same: identify every domain that influences the outcome and optimize it, whether that's jet lag, helmet weight, social media following, or F1 paddock access. He explicitly describes applying the same marginal-gains methodology to his VC career — not just picking good companies, but engineering every input that affects deal flow, relationships, and portfolio support.

> "I tried to perfect every single possible marginal gain possible... the secret was eliminating jet lag for the whole year because jet lag is a disaster. As an athlete, the difference between 99% focus and 100% focus is the difference between coming first and second." — *on his obsessive preparation in the 2016 championship year, working with a Harvard sleep professor to achieve 0.0 jet lag across an entire season*

### Building the Biotech Router ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 10 mins

1. No AI lab's model leadership is permanent, so durable competitive advantage comes from what you build *while* you're best. The winning playbook: find industry verticals that generate massive proprietary token flows (investment banks, pharma companies), lock in partnerships that yield domain-specific training data, push AI adoption downstream to partners' clients, and bundle AI services with cloud infrastructure (storage, observability, hosting) to increase switching costs.

2. Amazon's Bio Discovery closes the loop between computational and wet-lab drug research. ML models identify promising molecules; Bio Discovery connects that output directly to synthesis and testing pipelines, caching and reusing workflows so each experiment feeds richer priors into the next. The compounding effect is the point: every result makes experiment selection marginally better, raising the ROI on the entire research pipeline, not just individual runs.

3. Demis Hassabis's bet with Isomorphic Labs is that AI drug discovery eventually makes parts of the traditional development process obsolete. If enough AI-discovered candidates clear the full FDA process — and the FDA can backtest model predictions against outcomes — regulators may trust model outputs enough to waive animal testing and compress human dosage escalation timelines. The FDA has already announced fast-track programs that appear to be working, and today's political environment is erratic but not uniformly hostile.

4. The real strategic frame is Mechanists vs. Shapers — a cyberpunk dichotomy mapped onto AI investment. Mechanist AI (coding assistants, language models) captures enormous value from the token economy. But Shaper AI — modeling the human body accurately enough to target individual cancer cells, generate on-demand vaccines, or engineer sensory experiences — has a fundamentally larger TAM: all consumption ever forgone because people were too sick to earn it, enjoy it, or alive to want it.

5. In a world of AI-driven deflationary abundance, the only two things left to spend money on are positional goods (scarce by definition) and healthspan (the capacity to enjoy everything else). Biology has orders of magnitude more potentially observable data than human-generated text, which is why AlphaFold — not a coding assistant — was among the first headline AI breakthroughs. By process of elimination, biology is where AI scaling eventually must go, and Amazon is positioning AWS as the infrastructure layer for that transition.

> "AI has been incredibly good at refining the interface between humans and the outside world, but it doesn't have to be skin-deep." — *on why biotech, not just software, is the terminal AI frontier*

### Money Stuff: Tax Alpha Needs Some Real Alpha ⚡
**msprvs1=20566thzS7r5D=bounces-280172-1781@bounce.news.bloomberg.com** · rss · 17 mins

1. **Tax-loss harvesting is mechanically easy, making "tax alpha" a mass-market product rather than a scarce hedge-fund specialty.** The core trade: invest $100, buy $200 of diversified longs, short $100 of diversified stocks — you earn the market return on $100 of net exposure while generating tax losses on $300 of gross exposure. Winners go up unrealized (no taxes); losers get sold to realize losses that offset capital gains elsewhere. This doesn't require genius stock-picking; any reasonably constructed diversified basket produces it.

2. **AQR has added $47 billion since March 2024, and Quantinno $39 billion since January 2025, from tax-aware funds — nearly a third of AQR's total AUM is now in these strategies.** These flows dwarf typical hedge fund growth ($628 billion industry-wide last year per HFR). "It's the hottest product in Greenwich," per one Wall Street source, with suburban fathers at youth sports games discussing AQR's Delphi Plus fund specifically. The newer funds go further than capital losses by using swaps to generate ordinary income losses — more valuable because they offset earned income, not just capital gains.

3. **The IRS is the central hidden risk: tax-only trades lack "economic substance," so firms must maintain a credible fiction of pre-tax stock-picking alpha.** IRS doctrine requires a non-tax business purpose for transactions to receive tax treatment. A pure index-grossing strategy — buy $200 of random index stocks, short $100 of random index stocks — would be too explicit. The solution: firms like AQR and Two Sigma claim they are actually long good stocks and short bad ones, and sometimes the longs go down and they harvest losses. AQR's official line is "tax-aware strategies must be built on credible pre-tax alpha." The word "credible," as Matt Levine notes in a footnote, is doing a lot of work — it may mean "IRS-placating" rather than "actual."

4. **Tax-loss harvesting doesn't eliminate taxes — it defers them, and the deferral's value compounds on two mechanisms: time value of money and the step-up-in-basis loophole at death.** Selling losers now creates losses that offset gains now; but the unsold winners eventually create taxable gains when sold — without offsetting losses, so the total tax bill is higher later. The strategy pays off because (a) taxes in 20 years are worth less in present value than taxes today, and (b) if you die holding the winners, heirs receive a stepped-up cost basis and the deferred gains are never taxed at all.

5. **World Liberty Financial, a Trump family crypto venture, collapsed to an all-time low below $0.08 after backer Justin Sun accused insiders of secretly building controls to freeze token holders' funds.** Sun, who invested tens of millions of dollars when the project launched in 2024 — and whose SEC fraud suit was dropped after Trump took office — called the project "a trap masquerading as a door." WLFI tokens dropped more than 50% after a portion of the supply was unlocked for trading. The situation echoes a 2021 pattern: investors paid real money for blockchain receipts representing no ongoing economic claim on the underlying project.

6. **Exxon Mobil implemented a "retail shareholder voting" program letting individual investors permanently delegate their proxy votes to the board — essentially converting retail holders into a captive bloc against activist institutional investors like BlackRock.** The New York City Comptroller filed a 2026 shareholder proposal demanding Exxon offer "multiple independent options," including an "against management" standing instruction. Exxon's board responded that offering anti-management voting options would be "inconsistent with state law and the Board's fiduciary duties" — i.e., the board argued it is legally required to advocate only for itself. This represents a sharp reversal from the 2000s governance norm of maximizing shareholder oversight over boards.

7. **SpaceX purchased 1,279 Cybertrucks — over 18% of all 7,071 US Cybertruck registrations in Q4 2024 — propping up sales of a vehicle struggling to attract ordinary buyers.** Photos show long rows of idle Cybertrucks on SpaceX's Texas property; some are used as security vehicles. The lead Cybertruck engineer announced in October that SpaceX was replacing gas-powered support vehicles with the trucks, and Electrek reported SpaceX could ultimately buy ~2,000. Musk's companies — SpaceX, xAI, and others — collectively purchased another 60 vehicles in the same period, representing a related-party sales channel with no arm's-length market test.

8. **Allbirds announced a rebrand to "NewBird AI" and a $5 million investor commitment (marketed as $50 million, with the remaining $45 million at the investor's option), causing the stock to surge 582% in one day before falling 31% the next.** Nearly 300 million shares traded on the surge day versus a daily average of 20 million. At the time of writing, shares were still up ~375% from pre-announcement levels despite the company having no demonstrated AI capability. A parallel micro-cap, Myseum (Nasdaq: MYSE), renamed itself Myseum.AI on April 15 and rose 125% the same day. The pattern: "cynical AI pivots get rewarded and presumably someone is making money off of this — probably the investor who committed the $5 million."

> "The deepest and most fundamental risk is that the IRS catches wind of this." — *anonymous financial adviser, on tax-aware long/short hedge fund strategies*

## Finance & Markets

### The World: Bitcoin's greatest mystery 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 9 mins

1. Bitcoin was introduced in October 2008 via a white paper posted to an obscure corner of the internet by "Satoshi Nakamoto" — a pseudonym. The paper described a decentralized electronic currency requiring no banks, no fees, and no government oversight. Several hundred million people use it today, making the mystery of its creator one of the most consequential unsolved questions in modern finance.

2. NYT investigative reporter John Carreyrou — who broke the Theranos scandal — spent a year and a half investigating and concluded that Satoshi is British computer scientist Adam Back. Back has been on the suspect list for years but always denied it. Carreyrou puts his confidence at "between 99.5 percent and 100 percent."

3. The key forensic evidence is a shared writing tic: both Back and Nakamoto are "pathologically incapable of using hyphens correctly," inserting them where unnecessary and omitting them where grammar requires them. Carreyrou confirmed this pattern across their writings using AI-assisted linguistic analysis. Back called the similarities a series of coincidences.

4. Back in the 1990s was a member of the cypherpunk movement — privacy-minded anarchists who feared governments would use digitized financial records to surveil citizens and wanted to build "electronic cash" to circumvent that. He also openly wrote back then about the need to operate under an alter ego to avoid government suspicion, suggesting the Satoshi persona was long premeditated.

5. Satoshi mined 1.1 million bitcoins in the first 16 months of Bitcoin's existence. At current prices that fortune is worth approximately $82 billion, placing him among the wealthiest people on earth — coins that have never moved. He used a pseudonym partly out of fear of legal retaliation from governments, and partly to position Bitcoin as a leaderless "discovery" (like digital gold) rather than a product with a CEO, since a named founder would undermine the decentralized mythology the community depends on.

> "I think Satoshi also didn't want people to treat Bitcoin as just another company with a product and a C.E.O. He wants Bitcoin to feel like a discovery, like digital gold — a digital commodity that was discovered and is mined from the ground of the internet." — *John Carreyrou, explaining Nakamoto's strategic anonymity*

### Iran's so far away, stocks can hit a record 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 have hit all-time highs — the Nasdaq's first in six months — despite an ongoing US-Iran war that has shut down oil flow through the Strait of Hormuz. Since March 30, the S&P is up 10.7% and the Nasdaq 14.2%. Since 1950, only 21 other S&P rallies have achieved 10% this quickly. The only historical parallel at comparable valuations is a two-week window in March 2000 — just before the dot-com bubble burst. Earnings estimates for S&P 500 Q1 have actually *fallen* during this rally, making the move harder to justify on fundamentals.

2. Valuations are stretched by every measure: the 10-year Treasury sits at 4.3%, higher than at any point during the entire 13-year post-GFC bull run. Robert Shiller's "excess CAPE yield" — earnings yield minus bond yield — is deeply negative, a level that has historically predicted poor equity returns over the following decade. Previous rapid 10% rallies all occurred either deep into bear markets (1974, 1975, 1982) or after major crises (LTCM 1998, GFC 2009, Covid 2020); this one came from near-record levels.

3. Trader conditioning from last April's "TACO" (Trump Always Chickens Out) episode explains the rally mechanics. When Trump postponed his tariffs after Liberation Day, momentum traders who had sold short were crushed. This time, knowing that pattern, they refused to sell aggressively on bad news and bought heavily on any hint of resolution. The March 31 pivot came on the slim signal that Iran's president said he was "ready to end the war" but needed concessions first — fear of missing out did the rest. Victor Haghani of Elm Wealth adds that modern markets now require a third investor type beyond the traditional rational- and momentum-trader duopoly: "static investors" (passive 401(k) holders) and "buy the dip" retail cohorts who mechanically pull others upward regardless of fundamentals.

4. The Strait of Hormuz blockade poses a slow-burn food inflation threat that markets are largely ignoring. Persian Gulf countries account for at least 30% of global fertilizer supply, are the largest regional exporters of urea and ammonia, and the second-largest of diammonium phosphate; LNG from the region is also a key fertilizer input for India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Turkey. The effect will only become visible at harvest time — months away — but a Farm Bureau survey already finds over 70% of US farmers cannot afford all the fertilizer they need, and Arthur Erickson of Hylio reports input costs have doubled since the pandemic. The Ukraine-Russia war's closure of the Black Sea offers a precedent for how badly food prices can spike.

5. The Powell-Fed saga continues but markets are discounting it on two grounds. The DOJ has made unannounced visits to the Fed pursuing a probe over building renovation overruns; Trump has threatened to fire Powell if he stays past May 15; Republican Senator Thom Tillis is blocking Kevin Warsh's confirmation until the prosecution ends. Markets are unfazed because (a) the Iran war has made rate decisions moot — no one expects any move, and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent implicitly accepts this; and (b) Polymarket gives only 60% odds Powell stays through May, with markets treating a Trump firing as unlikely bluster. If Trump did elevate ultra-dovish Governor Stephen Miran as temporary chair, it could destabilize things — but the assumption is he won't.

> "The momentum traders got crushed last April. This time throughout the entire war, on bad news you'd sell a little and on good news you'd buy a lot. There was this fear of being caught too short when the reversal happened." — *Jim Masturzo, Research Affiliates, explaining the rally's behavioral driver*
