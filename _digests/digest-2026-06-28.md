### Token Costs Threaten Frontier Lab Dominance ⚡
**Contrary Research** · email · 5 mins

1. **Open-source and Chinese models are undercutting US frontier labs by an order of magnitude.** OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol costs $5 input / $30 output per million tokens — already half the price of Fable 5 ($10/$50) — but DeepSeek V4 Flash undercuts both at $0.14 input / $0.28 output, roughly 36x cheaper than GPT-5.5 on input and over 100x cheaper on output. China's Z.ai released GLM-5.2, which costs about 1/8th of Claude Opus 4.8 for certain tasks and is now the third most-used AI system globally.

2. **State-backed Chinese competitors with no profit motive are structurally harder to outcompete on price.** Six of the ten models on the leading AI leaderboard were developed in China, meaning US frontier labs face subsidized rivals who can price at cost or below. Uber's situation illustrates the stakes: the company burned through its entire annual AI budget in four months and had to implement spending tiers — yet enterprise AI usage overall still runs ~95% on frontier models, meaning the cost-driven migration away from frontier labs has barely begun.

3. **The newsletter's broader news roundup surfaces a cluster of competitive pressure on US AI dominance.** Anthropic accused Alibaba of the largest known model-distillation attack on Claude — 28.8 million exchanges via ~25,000 fake accounts to extract reasoning and coding capabilities — following three earlier campaigns attributed to DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax. Separately, China retook the world's fastest supercomputer title for the first time since 2017 with Shenzhen's LineShine hitting 2.198 exaflops using a CPU-only design built entirely on Chinese-designed processors, with the TOP500 organizer noting US export controls give China "a strong incentive to develop domestic alternatives."

### The Bifurcation of Capital is Inevitable ⚡
**Kyle Harrison from Investing 101** · email · 9 mins

1. **Size success is a one-way trap.** In 2020, Tracy Britt Cool — Warren Buffett's protégé who ran Berkshire subsidiaries including Pampered Chef and Benjamin Moore — left to found Kanbrick, a firm explicitly targeting businesses "too small for Berkshire": family-led companies doing $5M–$50M in earnings. She didn't leave because Berkshire failed; she left because it succeeded so completely that a whole tier of excellent businesses became mechanically invisible to it. The most telling thing about any capital-allocation machine is the set of deals it can no longer afford to do.

2. **The fat-wallet problem is arithmetic, not philosophy.** By 1994, Berkshire required a minimum $100M deployment per position, shrinking its investable universe dramatically — and that was when net worth was $11.9B. At $1 trillion today, a company generating $8M in earnings is a rounding error regardless of its quality. Buffett wrote it plainly: "Though there are as many good businesses as ever, it is useless for us to make purchases that are inconsequential in relation to Berkshire's capital." Scale doesn't reveal new opportunities; it buries existing ones under a minimum-check-size floor.

3. **Venture's version of the curse: Capital Agglomerators vs. Cottage Keepers.** a16z raised a $15B fund in January 2026 and now manages over $90B in AUM — capturing 18% of all US venture dollars in 2025. In Q1 2026, approximately 73% of LP capital went to just five firms. For a fund at that scale, a $1B exit is meaningless; they need outcomes "north of $50B," despite fewer than 50 public tech companies ever reaching that mark. The agglomerators have climbed so far upmarket that their own investable universe has, in Buffett's phrase, "shrunk dramatically."

4. **The last Cottage Keeper broke ranks.** Benchmark long represented the durable counterweight — the firm that kept fund size as strategy, with ~$4.8B under management. In June 2026, it raised a $2B growth vehicle, its first meaningful scale-up since 1999. The resulting gap is an 18-to-19x chasm between Benchmark and a16z. The middle of venture is being squeezed out: the structural incentive of LP capital and fund economics push every firm toward agglomeration over time.

5. **PE/VC funds are structurally forced to play gin rummy.** Berkshire's structural genius is the ability to never sell — Buffett explicitly refused "gin rummy managerial behavior (discard your least promising business at each turn)." See's Candies, purchased on just $25M of net worth, sent more than $410M in pre-tax profits to Berkshire to be redeployed wherever returns were highest — ultimately into BNSF railroad. Conventional 7-to-10-year PE/VC funds are contractually obligated to harvest and return capital, regardless of whether selling is the right call.

6. **The unanswered question: what is the modern equivalent of insurance float?** Berkshire's permanent capital came from insurance float — a near-zero-cost, perpetual capital base that freed Buffett from ever being a forced seller. AI-native HoldCos have Buffett's temperament (buy good businesses, never play gin rummy, route cash to the best pasture) but not his structure — they still answer to LPs who eventually want their money back. The firm that solves for a modern float equivalent will be structurally positioned to serve the long tail of startups that megafunds' scale forces them to abandon, a tail that only grows larger as capital concentrates at the top.

**Quotable:**
> "The size that makes you powerful is the same size that makes whole categories of opportunity invisible to you." — *on the curse of scale that affects every capital-allocation machine from Berkshire to a16z*

### "My uncle used Claude to write my Nana's obituary" ⚡
**The Substack Post** · email · 11 mins

This is Substack's weekly Weekender digest — a curated roundup of essays, interviews, and personal writing. The titular piece is an essay by Daisy Cashin; three other substantial pieces follow.

1. Daisy Cashin learns mid-rage-walk on Virginia's Huckleberry Trail that her uncle used Claude to write her Nana's obituary. She doesn't blow up the family function or fire off drunk texts — not because AI-generated grief writing is defensible, but because her Nana's soul is "sturdier than that." The restraint is itself the argument: some violations can't be corrected, only witnessed.

2. Obituaries and eulogies exist for the living, not the dead — they are the mechanism by which survivors connect, keep going, and make pain slightly more bearable. Cashin frames grief as proof of life: "the pain means that there are feelings in you, and that they are worth expressing." Outsourcing that expression to a language model hollows out the function the words were meant to serve.

3. In lieu of the AI obituary, Cashin writes her own from memory — granular, embodied, untransferable. Her Nana busted U-turns on I-81 to get a better look at a red-tailed hawk, plucked black snakes off porch pillars bare-handed, kept apple slices in her bag at all times, stood in the driveway waving until the car disappeared, and was singing "Country Roads" until she died. These details are the obituary. No prompt could have produced them.

4. On June 16, Snap CEO Evan Spiegel unveiled the Specs — $2,195 AR glasses with a 4-hour battery — at the Augmented World Expo, pitching them as the "post-smartphone era." By end of keynote, Snap's stock had dropped 10% and the company had lost over $1 billion in market cap. The comparison is brutal: Ray-Ban Meta sells for $350, has moved 2 million units since September 2023, and commands 76% of global smart glasses shipments. Snap entered the same market at 6x the price with no story to justify the gap.

5. Barbara de Vries revived Calvin Klein Sport in the early 1990s — repositioning it away from "Missy" department store lines toward a DKNY-adjacent slot, with Kate Moss as the catalyst. Her ousting came not from poor performance but from a licensing executive named Barb Warner who stormed into her office after de Vries independently pitched a CK sneaker to Calvin. Warner had Calvin's ear and his friendship; de Vries had kept her team deliberately insulated from company politics — isolation that protected her creative output but left her blind to the alliance forming against her.

6. At age six, Blakely Spoor graduated college in Nashville and avoided New York (where "90% of peers were headed"), instead spending a summer at her family's cabin in the San Juan Islands. She never planned to stay. One morning, after her neighbors asked when she was leaving and she had no answer, she thought: "Oh, so I live here now." The island population drops below 50 full-time residents in the off-season, most of them 30+ years her senior; she built her social life through baked-good drop-offs to elderly neighbors who became her lifeline through windstorms and king tides.

**Quotable:**
> "What I remember of my Nana is her heart, her mind, her smile, her hands, the way she'd pull a weed or waddle out to the porch with a pitcher of lemonade and a plate of BLTs, that she was always the last person at the dinner table, that her favorite jokes were ones about white horses in mud, that she'd pluck a blacksnake off the pillars on the porch without hesitation and set it free in the pines with a smile." — *Daisy Cashin, writing the obituary her uncle outsourced to Claude*

### DealBook: A.I. eyeballs 📎
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 8 mins

1. The 25-year-old search-click-traffic contract that powered digital advertising is functionally dead. Google now serves AI-generated summaries before organic search results, and consumers increasingly get answers directly from chatbots like ChatGPT — bypassing the entire click-funnel that Fortune 500 marketing budgets were built around. Publishers lose referral traffic, brands lose discoverability, and political strategists lose visibility into donor behavior.

2. OpenAI is monetizing the vacuum it helped create: at Cannes Lions 2026, the company pitched advertisers on placing ads inside ChatGPT, with projections of $100M annually by 2030. Google simultaneously showcased ads embedded in its AI search summaries. The platforms that disrupted the old model are now selling the replacement infrastructure — at prices and terms they control entirely.

3. A new "AEO" (AI Engine Optimization) industry has exploded almost overnight. Profound — which tracks how ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini describe and cite brands — reached unicorn status this year after raising $96M at a $1B valuation, just two years after founding, and logged ~300 meetings in one week at Cannes. Adobe paid $1.9B to acquire SEO firm Semrush specifically to reposition it for AI visibility. Startups IV.AI and Jasper set up on "Yacht Row" selling language-optimization and content-generation services aimed at making brands legible to LLMs.

4. Brands are discovering that optimizing content for AI readability actively degrades the human experience — forcing a dual-content strategy. Knotch (Anda Gansca, CEO) built an "agentic content engine" that produced separate machine-readable content versions for clients including Zillow and Ally Financial after noticing that AI-optimized pages repelled human readers. No best practices exist yet: Zoom CMO Kim Storin described a monthly CMO roundtable inventing norms in real time.

5. Nobel economist Al Roth (Stanford, 2012 prize) reframes prediction-market "insider trading" concerns: securities markets ban insider trading because it threatens their price-discovery function, but prediction markets don't perform that same economic role, so the same logic doesn't cleanly apply. The real problem, per Roth, is when presidential associates bet on Truth Social posts — a public-private corruption issue, not a market-design indictment. His proposed fix for gambling addiction risk: require apps to let users pre-set loss limits ("when I've lost $100, shut me down"), analogous to bartender cutoff rules.

**Quotable:**
> "It's not like best practices exist right now. We're creating the best practice ourselves." — *Kim Storin, CMO of Zoom, on brands navigating AI discoverability with no established playbook*

## Finance & Markets

### 🧠 Community Wisdom: Beating a career slump, adding more structure to an established team, questions for new-team … 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 16 mins

1. Career flatness in a slow, red-tape-heavy job that "barely touches AI" is often not burnout but environmental mismatch — the environment is hollowing you out, not you running out of fuel. Cindy Cohen distinguishes the two sharply: lacking motivation vs. lacking a compelling reason to be motivated. Clarity about what's missing usually comes before motivation returns, not after.

2. The best use of a motivational lull is administrative debt, not self-improvement. Steven Jones recommends using the downtime for things you'll never be excited about — taxes, files, car maintenance — so you have a clear plate when drive returns. Fighting the lull by forcing productivity on big goals usually fails; working with it does not.

3. Mentorship is a productivity hack disguised as altruism. It forces you to articulate what you've learned (sharpening your own thinking), exposes you to people whose motivation is contagious, and looks good on a resume — three returns from one low-energy investment. It also redirects career skills toward community orgs and nonprofits if the day job is too constrictive.

4. Aaron Nichols, who went through the same slump and made a significant career direction change, warns against hiring a coach to push through feelings — hire one to understand what those feelings are signaling. A somatic coach helped him recognize that the "not motivated to lead here" signal was real and pointing toward an exit, not an obstacle to overcome.

5. The single highest-value question in a new-team 1:1 is one almost nobody asks: "Who's the one person you'd recommend I talk to that no one else has probably mentioned?" (Joni Hoadley). This surfaces informal power brokers, trusted operators without fancy titles, and the people who know "where the bodies are buried" — none of whom appear on any org chart.

6. Matthew Stublefield's protocol for onboarding to a new team rejects the standard "drop a meeting with no agenda" approach entirely. He pre-researches the org, builds light dossiers on each key contact, messages them in week 2 (not week 1, when context is too thin), and sends structured agendas with the meeting request — turning introductory 1:1s into targeted knowledge transfers rather than awkward get-to-know-yous.

7. Resistant teams that skip user stories and refinement almost always fall into one of two camps: they don't know what structure to use (fixable with lightweight templates and LLM-assisted tooling like NotebookLM), or they genuinely don't believe it creates value (not fixable by adding more process). Tibi Lorga's diagnostic: get 1:1 time with each person and ask "it doesn't seem like we're getting much value out of these stories — do you feel the same?" Let them diagnose. If they agree, you've earned trust; if not, you've learned the real constraint.

8. Josh (Lenny's community) reframes chronic process problems as likely org structure problems: wrong team composition causing cross-team dependency, strategy mismatch where the team's manager is incentivized at cross-purposes to organizational goals, or the team being stuck at the wrong "stage" of work (executing specs when they should be involved in problem identification). No amount of Jira hygiene fixes a structural misalignment.

9. The half of a growth job that consisted of operational reps — sending outbound, drafting posts, pulling reports, running experiments — is compressing fast to AI agents. What remains is deciding what's worth doing, defining "good," and catching when the system is confidently wrong. Bal Sieber's reframe: growth practitioners are shifting from operators to directors, speccing plays tightly enough that agents can run them, then editing output with taste.

10. The trap in AI-enabled growth work is that "building systems" becomes its own form of busywork — elaborate workflows that feel productive and move nothing. The old discipline still applies: be ruthless about what actually moves a number; just point that discipline at which systems to build rather than which experiments to run. Independent growth practitioners find the most traction with founder-led B2B SaaS and early-stage companies; post-PMF and enterprise teams typically want a hire, not a fractional partner.

**Quotable:**
> "Are you lacking motivation, or lacking a compelling reason to be motivated?" — *Cindy Cohen, on the distinction between burnout and outgrowing your environment*

### Why Indians Hate Carpooling 📌
**Kudrat Wadhwa** · email · 3 mins

1. **Safety distrust makes carpooling a hard sell for anyone except solo young men.** Arif, a 39-year-old video producer, booked a BlaBlaCar ride from a small town in Uttar Pradesh to Delhi and found the driver wasn't the listed person, the car was overcrowded, and he was charged extra — with no useful response from BlaBlaCar customer support. Despite most of his experiences being fine, he won't recommend the platform to women or non-solo travelers, a threshold that excludes a large share of potential users.

2. **Convenience beats cost savings once a reliable alternative exists.** Raja Chatterjee, a Mumbai-based media professional, carpooled for years through WhatsApp groups — saving money and even making friends — but switched to Cityflo premium buses, which cost more. The reason: no more hunting for rides, no uncertainty about whether someone on his route would be traveling that day. When the marginal cost of reliability drops (Cityflo offers scheduled, guaranteed seats), people pay for predictability rather than tolerate the coordination friction of carpooling.

3. **Carpooling is a collective-action problem: it benefits society but not necessarily the individual who does it.** Fewer cars means less congestion, lower emissions, and reduced fuel consumption — but those gains are diffuse and shared. The individual absorbs all the inconvenience (schedule inflexibility, stranger anxiety, coordination overhead) while capturing only a fraction of the societal upside. This asymmetry explains why carpooling surges in conversation whenever fuel prices spike or pollution alarms the public, yet never becomes mainstream: the incentive math only briefly tips in its favor.

**Quotable:**
> "I'd only recommend it to a young man travelling alone. For women and other travellers, I'd hesitate." — *Arif, 39-year-old video producer, on BlaBlaCar after a ride with the wrong driver, an overcrowded car, and a surprise surcharge*

### The Morning: Natural selection 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Serena Williams, who "retired" from tennis at age 40 in 2022 after 23 Grand Slam singles titles and 319 weeks ranked No. 1, declined to use the word "retirement" in her farewell Vogue essay — instead calling her departure an "evolution away" from tennis toward family and business. Four years later, Wimbledon announced she would play singles again, making the linguistic framing look less like euphemism and more like genuine self-knowledge.

2. Calling a departure an "evolution" rather than a "retirement" keeps a door open without committing to any outcome. Williams didn't script a comeback — but by refusing to define her exit as permanent, she preserved optionality for herself and avoided a narrative she'd have to publicly reverse. The framing protected her from a story of failure if she returned and from a story of regret if she didn't.

3. The newsletter uses Williams' example to push back on the human preference for declarative finality in life choices. "Away/Toward" lists — itemizing what you're moving away from and what you're moving toward — reframe difficult exits as directional movement rather than rejection, reducing the rancor that often accompanies endings. Williams herself said "I don't want it to be over, but at the same time I'm ready for what's next" — treating those two feelings as coexistent rather than contradictory.

4. The broader news in this June 27 newsletter: Venezuela earthquakes killed at least 920 people and injured 3,300+, with the U.S. earmarking $150 million in relief and only three functioning ambulances serving greater Caracas. John Bolton pleaded guilty to mishandling classified information, facing up to five years. A record Sotheby's auction in London brought in £393.4 million (~$521M) across 25 works, led by Modigliani's 1917 "Seated Nude With Necklace" at £48.2 million. Lin-Manuel Miranda's "Warriors" — his first musical since "Hamilton" — is set to open on Broadway next spring, based on the 1979 gang film.

**Quotable:**
> "Maybe the best word to describe what I'm up to is evolution. I'm here to tell you that I'm evolving away from tennis, toward other things that are important to me." — *Serena Williams, in her 2022 Vogue farewell essay*

### What I learned from watching Andy Burnham for a decade 📎
**FT Weekend** · email · 5 mins

1. Andy Burnham is positioned as the UK's next prime minister, and Jennifer Williams — former award-winning political editor of the Manchester Evening News, now FT northern England correspondent — spent a decade embedded close enough to trace exactly how he got there. Her conclusion: Burnham transformed himself "with spectacular success" repeatedly, but the constant was his ability to spot "the story" and use it as the route to "the prize." A decade ago, the prize was escaping opposition backbench obscurity by making his name as Mayor of Greater Manchester; now it is Downing Street.

2. The newsletter's lead essay, by historian Edmund Fawcett, poses a structural question about post-Trump American conservatism: if Trump's second term ends with tamed inflation, manageable deficits, and peace in Ukraine and the Middle East, the Republican hard right can claim "performance legitimacy" — i.e., illiberal but effective — making it very difficult for Democrats or liberal Republicans to dislodge. Conversely, if those wins don't materialise, the opposition has its opening. The piece frames Trumpism's durability as entirely contingent on delivery, not ideology.

3. Malcolm Moore's Lunch with the FT profiles Blackwater founder Erik Prince — described variously as "modern warlord," "soldier-entrepreneur," and "mercenary-in-chief" — at a £1,200-a-night hotel, where the conversation spans the privatisation of war. The piece functions as a Rorschach test: readers' outrage tends to land on the bottled water bill rather than the subject matter, illustrating how format shapes reception of even the most charged figures.

**Quotable:**
> "Time and again I watched Burnham transform himself with spectacular success. But on one thing he was constant. He never lost his eye for the story — or the prize; the first was always his route to the second." — *Jennifer Williams, FT northern England correspondent, on a decade covering Andy Burnham*
