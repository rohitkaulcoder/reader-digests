### 🧠 Community Wisdom: What great product leaders do differently, Claude vs. ChatGPT, receiving hard feedback two we… ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 20 mins

1. **Good product strategy is a filter, not a vision — if it can't say no without you in the room, it's useless.** Joni Hoadley points out that AI has made everyone (sales, support, engineers) capable of spinning up a prototype or drafting a PRD over lunch, so the volume of "have you considered" requests hitting PMs is "orders of magnitude higher than it was two years ago." Without a sharp strategy, every request gets debated on its merits; with one, most get answered in seconds: "Not on strategy, here's why."

2. **Great product leaders make their own tradeoff reasoning legible and kill their own pet projects first.** Joni Hoadley's list: publish reasoning clearly enough that PMs can predict your decisions before you make them; back PMs publicly when they use the strategy to say no to a loud VP; and apply the strategy to your own ideas first — if you don't, nobody else will. Ian McAllister's implementation: investment "themes" with key metrics, and any project that doesn't move a theme metric stays on the backlog.

3. **Leadership is knowing when to stop climbing one hill and find another entirely.** Barada's hierarchy: execution = hill-climbing; discovery = hill-climbing + experimentation; strategy = choosing which search space gets your scarce attention; leadership = deciding when the current hill is a local maximum and it's time to switch. Most product debates collapse the last two steps, treating "which hill" as a given.

4. **For non-technical AI learners, a direct conversation with Claude beats any course or newsletter.** Community member rahul ajit pasted the question directly to Claude, which replied that newsletters assume a baseline vocabulary (APIs, tokens, RAG, fine-tuning) and pile unfamiliar terms on unfamiliar terms — conversation sidesteps this entirely by meeting users where they are. The suggested on-ramp: "I'm not technical. I run [type of business]. I want to understand how AI could save me time, but explain things simply." Ethan Mollick's *Co-Intelligence* (book) and his *One Useful Thing* Substack were the community's top picks for supplementary reading.

5. **Vanilla Claude Code (Opus 4.7) loses to ChatGPT defaults; a customized harness with extra tools flips that result.** Community member Barada drew the distinction clearly: out-of-the-box, ChatGPT's defaults are stronger for reasoning-heavy non-technical work; with a custom harness (extra tools, context, thinking mode, skills), Claude wins. Most practitioners in the thread run a two-to-three-tool stack: ChatGPT for planning/writing, Claude for coding and workflows, with Codex or Perplexity handling research and code audits to avoid burning Claude tokens.

6. **Intercom doubled engineering velocity in 9 months by adopting Claude Code organization-wide.** Brian Scanlan's podcast episode (referenced without details) claims a 2× improvement in engineering output across Intercom within nine months — one of the most concrete AI productivity numbers cited in the thread and a data point for the "customized harness" argument above.

7. **Pre-PMF frameworks and books are permission structures, not answers — 20-30 customer conversations are the actual work.** Lennybot synthesized the recommended reading list: *The Mom Test* (Rob Fitzpatrick, teaches non-leading customer interviews), *Continuous Discovery Habits* (Teresa Torres, repeatable learning system), *Running Lean* (Ash Maurya, lean canvas), and Reforge's PMF course (forces thinking through four types of fit). But the bot's closing line: "content and frameworks are just permission structures. What actually matters is talking to 20-30 people who could be customers."

8. **B2B2C pre-PMF founders over-engineer marketplace balance when they should pick one side and ignore the other until the core exchange works.** For B2B2C specifically, Lennybot's rule: pick supply or demand, focus hard on one, use shortcuts for the other, and don't try to balance both until you've proven the core exchange. Joshua Herzig-Marx diagnosed the specific case (a premium consumer health-tech startup with B2B demand running ahead of DTC traction) as "technology in search of a buyer" — needing all three things simultaneously: a market that values the product, a mechanism to reach it, and a monetization mechanism.

9. **The first PM earns the right to push for strategic focus through total tactical reliability and visibly holding company-level anxiety.** Joshua Herzig-Marx's two rules for earning influence at a pre-PMF startup: (1) own the unglamorous implementation work — project management, running meetings, answering the phone — with complete reliability; (2) demonstrate that you understand what the company is trying to achieve and that everything you do advances that goal, not just your workstream. Niching down feels like killing the founder's dream, but framing it as "the first step in a long-term strategy toward world domination of better health" changes the conversation.

10. **Blanket negative feedback delivered to a 2.5-week employee based on secondhand complaints, with zero prior warnings, is a toxicity signal — not a new-hire failure.** An anonymous PM received a message from a CTO/cofounder reading: "I've seen very little good from you here so far. Your mocks were garbage… you constantly push for more meetings… you've detracted from the project." The PM had received only positive feedback from all three direct collaborators, led praised customer discovery calls, and built a working prototype in week one. The "committee" was a design engineer's optional weekly sync; the "crappy takehome" was what got the PM hired. The PM quit, citing $300K TC and 0.075% equity at a unicorn they'd already walked away from to take this role.

## Product & Leadership

### The product skill you must now master: Reinvention 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 10 mins

1. The 20-year PM playbook — start hands-on, prove you can build, then move up to help others build — has not been updated; it has been reversed. The most in-demand product people today are opinionated, hands-on, high-agency builders who stayed close to the craft. PMs who climbed into pure facilitation, stakeholder alignment, and information-moving roles are discovering that AI now does exactly those things better and cheaper.

2. Waiting for the market to "settle" before reinventing is a compounding trap. Every quarter spent on the sideline steepens the re-entry hill, and the catch-up cost grows faster than any clarity gained by waiting. Nikhyl Singhal calls the hardest-hit cohort the "Shadows of Superpowers": A-students of the old game — the ones who built the brand, earned the promotions, got the big-company logos — are the most disoriented precisely because the old game rewarded them so well, insulating them from noticing it changed.

3. The builder-vs-manager diagnostic is the single most important career question right now. Open PM roles are at a multi-year high and comp is up for hands-on builders; the executive builder — hands-on craft plus the ability to shape direction and communicate directly to CEOs without translation layers — is in the best market of their career. The pure coordinator (whose primary skill is alignment, process, and cross-functional translation) is being structurally absorbed, not cyclically laid off.

4. Ageism in tech is real but often misdiagnosed. The actual question is not age but appetite for reinvention — Singhal observes plenty of senior leaders reliving early-career intensity, diving into AI tools, and compounding their experience into the current toolset like "fine wine." The crossroads is for those who find the idea of climbing another hill genuinely exhausting; that's a legitimate choice, but it should be made consciously rather than disguised as a market complaint.

5. Non-traditional backgrounds — a decade in enterprise sales before PM, or deep roots in HR, finance, logistics, or healthcare — have flipped from liabilities to wedges. The combination of function depth + product mindset + AI fluency produces what Singhal calls the "sales builder" or "HR builder": someone who can systematically rebuild a legacy function from the inside, the hottest internal mandate at companies right now. AI removed the historical limiter (these people couldn't ship); what remains — deep functional understanding plus strong opinions — is exactly what's scarce.

6. Prolonged deliberation (coaching vs. consulting vs. founding vs. traditional role) is itself the blocker for many long-tenured PMs. Singhal's prescription: take a 6–12 month bridge role, even slightly beneath you, even without certainty it's the long-term fit — get current with how product is actually built right now and let the work generate conviction that reflection never will. Coaching as a full-time career is "romantic" but "a long and disappointing road" for anyone outside the top 1% whose skill LLMs cannot yet replicate.

**Quotable:**
> "The question isn't whether you can compete with 'real' product people. It's whether you can recognize that your background is suddenly an asset worth leading with." — *on PMs who came from sales, HR, finance, or domain-specific fields*

### Intel Earnings, Intel's Differentiation?, Whither Terafab 📌
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 9 mins

1. Intel's strong Q1 earnings and blowout Q2 guidance ($13.8–$14.8B vs. analyst estimate of $13B) are primarily driven by a structural AI shift, not CEO Lip-Bu Tan's turnaround alone. The CPU-to-GPU deployment ratio has moved from 1:8 to 1:4 and is trending toward parity, because inference and agentic workloads require far more CPU-based orchestration than training did.

2. Tan twice dodged analyst questions about Intel's competitive differentiation against AMD and Arm (including Nvidia's upcoming Vera CPU and AWS Graviton). This silence is telling: the demand for CPUs is so overwhelming that Intel sold "de-specced" and shelved legacy products it had written off — by definition the least differentiated chips in its portfolio — and found willing buyers for all of them.

3. Intel's real long-run advantage may be fab self-sufficiency, not product differentiation. The original thesis for Intel Foundry was that external customers were needed to fund leading-edge capacity; but if CPU demand is insatiable, Intel may consume its own fabs entirely — and uniquely avoid the TSMC capacity constraints that bind every competing CPU maker.

4. The foundry model's hidden benefit: even if Intel ends up being its own primary customer, running Foundry as a standalone entity forced brutal benchmarking of efficiency and yields, which are now the main lever for near-term supply increases. Tan's operational improvements are real, but they were unlocked by the discipline the foundry restructuring imposed.

5. The Terafab announcement — Tesla spending ~$3B on a research fab at Giga Texas, using Intel's 14A process — is best read as a speculative process-licensing agreement rather than a concrete revenue opportunity. The real payment Intel expects is not money but access to whatever novel low-cost manufacturing innovations Musk's team develops; Tan signaled as much while conspicuously declining to confirm any revenue commitment.

### How to use Claude for GTM and pricing analysis 📌
**Kyle Poyar** · email · 9 mins

1. Claude "skills" are reusable `.md` files that encode GTM frameworks and get loaded into Claude Chat or the desktop app via Customize → Skills. Rather than writing them by hand, Poyar built his by pointing Claude at existing source documents and running a single prompt — the skill file essentially builds itself. Once installed, skills trigger automatically when a relevant prompt is detected, or can be invoked explicitly with a `/skill-name` tag.

2. The `/deep-gtm-research` skill replicates a strategy consultant's workflow: gather context first, propose a research plan for sign-off, prioritize primary sources, then deliver a pyramid-structured report with in-text citations and a source table. Poyar used it to analyze all 50 companies on the Forbes 2026 AI 50 list — finding that 52% offer a free plan, 56% publish pricing publicly (opacity was concentrated in legal, healthcare, robotics, and enterprise infra), and 10% have no commercial product yet. Research that used to take a couple of hours now runs in minutes.

3. The `/pricing-teardown` skill scores pricing pages across 10 dimensions: 7 covering the human buyer experience (value prop, plan clarity, cognitive load, trust, behavioral psychology, transparency) and 3 covering AI agent readiness (machine-readable pricing, FAQ coverage, per-tier depth). The AI-readiness layer matters because LLMs increasingly recommend tools to buyers, and opaque or image-heavy pricing pages can leave a company invisible or misrepresented in those recommendations.

4. When Poyar tested the pricing teardown on Linear's page, Claude gave it a D — verdict: *"Exceptionally clean and low-friction for human buyers, but dangerously thin on value framing and AI agent readability — the page looks great and says almost nothing."* The skill initially docked points for missing social proof, which was a bug (Claude dropped the image-based logos and OpenAI quote). Catching and correcting these errors iteratively is how skills improve — the fix is to give Claude feedback and ask it to update the `.md` file.

5. The `/icp-sharpener` skill interviews you across six questions about best customers, pre-purchase commonalities, and churn reasons, then synthesizes distinct segments with firmographic profiles, technographic signals, trigger events, and messaging hooks. For Poyar's solo consulting practice, Claude identified three priority segments: agentic AI companies trapped on seat-based pricing, established companies under pressure to monetize AI features, and early-stage AI companies shipping faster than their pricing strategy. Critically, the skill's output feeds directly into the next one.

6. The `/gtm-plays-brainstorm` skill selects 5–8 plays from a library of 25+, ranks them by fit to your product/ICP/stage, and specifies the trigger signal, automation mechanics, enabling tools, and a recommended first play. Intent-based outbound was the #2 channel where GTM leaders planned to increase investment in 2026 (per Poyar's October 2025 State of B2B GTM report). For Poyar's own consulting pipeline, Claude's top recommendation was exporting LinkedIn connections, enriching via Clay for ICP fit, and sending Tier 1 matches a short personalized message — not a pitch.

**Quotable:**
> "Exceptionally clean and low-friction for human buyers, but dangerously thin on value framing and AI agent readability — the page looks great and says almost nothing." — *Claude's verdict on Linear's pricing page after running the /pricing-teardown skill*

### Chip supply chains risk being next casualty of war 📎
**John Authers** · email · 7 mins

1. Propylene glycol methyl ether acetate (PGMEA) — a photoresist solvent derived from naphtha and essential to semiconductor lithography — has become harder to source since the Iran war disrupted Middle East shipping two months ago. The gap between a naphtha cargo and a finished chip spans months and dozens of processing steps, so feedstock disruptions take a long time to surface in production data and are very hard to reverse once they do.

2. Japan's largest chemical company, Shin-Etsu Chemical, has already withheld its full-year earnings forecast, citing supply constraints on naphtha-derived products from the war. Its most recent quarterly operating income of ~$860 million was down 13% year-over-year and missed estimates — a concrete early signal that the disruption is hitting real financials, not just supply-chain theory.

3. The immediate fallout is likely manageable but not zero. BCG's Ramiro Palma estimates photoresist materials account for ~15% of chip production costs, giving the value chain enough cushion to absorb a price spike without halting output. Morningstar's Brian Colello flags helium — a third of global supply comes from Qatar — as a parallel watch item, though current inventory covers months and alternative sources exist.

4. Markets are not pricing any of this downside. Bloomberg's chip supply-chain equity index shows nothing amiss, US stocks have largely recovered on the assumption the worst is past, and emerging-market chipmakers have regained their losses. The MSCI EAFE (non-US developed markets) has not recovered — it is more exposed to Strait of Hormuz disruption and lacks the chipmaker concentration that is sustaining investor confidence elsewhere.

**Quotable:**
> "The distance between a naphtha cargo and a finished semiconductor is measured in months and dozens of intermediate steps, so disruptions to the feedstock take a while to register and are very hard to reverse." — *Sohil Sinha, FirstPost, on why PGMEA shortages are a slow-burning risk*
