## Today's Top 3

### Reddit as a Reinforcement Learning Gym for Persuasion ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 10 mins

1. **RL gyms work by specifying a reward function — and the hard part is making that function manipulation-proof.** An RL gym is a simulated environment where an agent is given tasks (e.g., find the cheapest 3-bedroom Airbnb listing for given dates) and continuously updates toward good outcomes. The classic failure mode is reward-hacking: investment bank traders are the historical archetype, having found the optimal way to maximize expected bonus in any given year while systematically elevating the probability of wiping out their employer — and in markets this is pathological because everyone converges on the same strategies and they all crash simultaneously.

2. **Ad clicks are a real but badly-specified eval for AI-driven advertising.** Click-through rate tells you whether a model placed an ad well, but last-click attribution starves brand advertising, PR, and product quality of credit, systematically over-allocating budget to performance marketing. Worse, ads optimized purely for clicks become decoupled from the broader business — eroding brand value and creating future negative PR even as short-term numbers look good.

3. **The true upstream variable for ad effectiveness is beliefs, not behavior — and beliefs are nearly unmeasurable.** An ad for crystals that block 5G and neutralize Bill Gates' nanoprobes works only if someone else has already built a conspiracy-minded audience; the ad's performance is borrowed from prior belief-formation work, not its own. Polls are noisy, and academic persuasion studies are systematically vulnerable to data fabrication. What actually happens is that most persuasion produces minor view shifts that revert to equilibrium, punctuated by rare catastrophic worldview flips — tech Twitter between June 2020 and November 2024 being the illustrative data set.

4. **Reddit upvotes are a surprisingly high-fidelity instrument for measuring belief change at scale.** At the individual comment level, upvotes mostly measure whether someone is saying what the audience already believes in a flattering way. But a more sophisticated approach is possible: baseline the sentiment distribution in a thread, inject a new comment, then measure how downstream sentiment shifts. Done at scale, this gives brands and political actors a training signal for astroturfing — a genuine RL gym with real humans as the environment and sentiment shift as the reward signal.

5. **The same capability that makes AI more persuasive could be used to make it less so — and Reddit has a structural incentive to pursue the defensive version.** GPT-4o was demonstrably too good at validating users' delusions about math discoveries and Trilateral Commission mind-control podcasts. Calibrating persuasion downward — making models responsive to users without reshaping their beliefs — requires the same training data as maximizing persuasion. Reddit values its corpus of largely human-generated tokens as a training data asset, which means it needs to get extremely good at AI detection even as AI improves at sounding human. Controlled introduction of AI into the platform — studying what it looks like and measuring its impact — is the most direct path to that capability.

**Quotable:**
> "We really want to believe that if we just said the right words in the right order, we'd be able to totally change someone's mind, when what really happens is that most of this talk produces minor shifts in views, many of which snap back to equilibrium, followed by occasional extreme swings in someone's entire worldview." — *on why measuring persuasion is so hard, and why Reddit upvotes are a rare real signal*

### Could humans become "Sun-eaters" in the future? ⚡
**Big Think** · email · 11 mins

1. Humans are heterotrophs — we must kill to eat — but plants and algae are autotrophs that bootstrap biomass from sunlight, water, and CO₂ without killing anything. Every calorie humans consume is ultimately traceable to photosynthesis, which means a generations-long dream has emerged: to cut out the food chain entirely and get energy directly from the Sun, becoming "stellivores."

2. That dream is now commercially active. Startup Savor makes butter by replicating chemistry found near deep-sea hydrothermal vents, with no agriculture involved. Solar Foods sells "Solein®," an edible protein grown by feeding microbes electricity and CO₂ — "food out of thin air" — eerily presaged by mycologist E.C. Large's 1937 novel *Sugar in the Air*, whose fictional "Sunsap" syrup was synthesized from light and air in the same way.

3. The scientific underpinning built up over centuries. Jan Ingenhousz discovered photosynthesis in 1776. Then in 1828, Friedrich Wöhler synthesized urea — an organic compound — from purely inorganic materials, shattering the assumption that only living organisms could make organic chemistry. Chemist Marcellin Berthelot extrapolated this in an 1894 interview: cornfields and farms would disappear; "coal will no longer be dug" except "with the object of transforming it into bread or meat."

4. Thermodynamics (1850s onward) reframed the stakes. Ludwig Boltzmann (1886) described life as a "struggle for entropy" flowing from the hot Sun to cold Earth. Physicists began mourning the vast "flood" of sunlight radiating into empty space as wealth "forever wasted." By 1923, science writers were arguing that "direct exploitation of solar energy" would render Earth "habitable to many billions more people than it now contains." Russian geochemist Vladimir Vernadsky followed in 1925 with his essay "Human Autotrophy," arguing civilization must become autotrophic — like photosynthesizers, "dependent only on themselves for their nutrition" — to stop being a destructive, potentially suicidal force on the biosphere.

5. The moral case for synthetic food has a surprisingly long pedigree. Mary Shelley's monster in *Frankenstein* (1818) survived on acorns and berries, signaling that a synthetic being need not "destroy the lamb." William Winwood Reade wrote bluntly in 1872 that "our enlightened posterity will look back upon us who eat oxen and sheep just as we look back upon cannibals." French socialist Charles Fourier (1822) fantasized about bioengineered "anti-lions" pacifistically powering railways instead of eating prey.

6. At the largest scales, the idea becomes cosmological. Georges Gamow calculated in 1963 that the heavy hydrogen in Earth's oceans (~0.01% of oceanic hydrogen, or 1.65×10¹⁷ tons) could fuel fusion reactions generating enough calories (6×10²⁹) to feed humanity for 60 billion years. Freeman Dyson went further in 1960, proposing that an advanced civilization might dismantle Jupiter to build a shell of solar panels enclosing the entire Sun — a "Dyson sphere" — to capture its full energy output. SETI researchers now actively scan for such structures as thermodynamically unavoidable signatures of advanced civilizations, calling them "stellivores." The implication: becoming a Sun-eater may be a universal attractor for any sufficiently intelligent species, not merely a human fantasy.

**Quotable:**
> "Our enlightened posterity will look back upon us who eat oxen and sheep just as we look back upon cannibals." — *William Winwood Reade, 1872, on the inevitable future of synthetic food*

### Going Private: Pimco's secret Gulf debt deals show private placement power ⚡
**Bloomberg** · email · 11 mins

1. Since the US-Iran war erupted on Feb. 28, 2026, Gulf states effectively frozen out of public bond markets have turned to private placements for emergency financing. Pimco — the $2.27 trillion asset manager — has lent roughly $10 billion to Abu Dhabi, Qatar, Kuwait, and Qatar National Bank via secretly negotiated bonds. The private placement market rewards buyers with premium coupons: Qatar's privately placed bond carried a 4.8% coupon, about 0.3 percentage points above where its publicly traded bonds implied.

2. The scale of the market shift is stark in the data: Gulf borrowers raised $50 billion from public bond offerings in January–February 2026 alone, but only $13.8 billion via private placements from Feb. 28 through April 23 — Pimco's role made the latter possible at all. Bloomberg Economics chief EM economist Ziad Daoud noted it's telling that only the three Gulf states with the strongest balance sheets (Abu Dhabi, Qatar, Kuwait) could access capital at reasonable rates under geopolitical stress.

3. Pimco simultaneously used its balance sheet to reopen the frozen BDC (business development company) bond market: it bought a $400 million, 2.5-year bond from Blue Owl Capital Corp (OBDC) at 6.5% yield — 2.7 percentage points above equivalent Treasuries — ending a complete shutdown in BDC issuance. The signal carried immediately: the next day Goldman Sachs' direct lending fund raised $750 million (above its $500 million target) with nearly $3 billion in orders, and Blackstone's BCRED drew $4.3 billion in demand for an $850 million sale at 2.3 points above Treasuries. BDC bonds had hit a record $38 billion issued in 2025, up from $5 billion six years earlier, but retail inflows into non-listed BDCs collapsed from $12 billion in Q1 2025 to $4.9 billion in Q1 2026.

4. JPMorgan Asset Management is building a private credit operation despite Jamie Dimon's public skepticism toward the industry. The firm has earmarked $50+ billion of its $4.9 trillion balance sheet for direct lending through its commercial and investment bank and holds another ~$50 billion in "back leverage" loans to private credit funds. JPMAM hired Jeff Bracchitta — formerly co-head of direct lending at JPMorgan's commercial and investment bank — nearly a year ago; his team sits in fixed income rather than alternatives, reflecting a house view that public and private credit markets will converge. The structure will likely mirror Citi-Apollo and Wells Fargo-Centerbridge bank-manager partnerships.

5. The Alternative Investment Management Association (representing Blackstone, Apollo, and others) is lobbying the SEC to lift the current ban on cross-trading assets between affiliated funds — a change that would directly address the liquidity crisis hitting semi-liquid private credit funds overwhelmed by retail redemption requests. Current rules restrict cross-trading to securities with "readily available market quotations" and require trades at "independent current market price," making it nearly impossible to move private assets or illiquid fixed income between affiliated vehicles. The SEC has placed the cross-trading rule on its regulatory flexibility agenda, and commissioners have been meeting regularly with industry participants; the Department of Labor simultaneously released draft guidance to reduce class-action risk for employers offering private credit in 401(k) plans.

6. Trey Parker, co-founder and CIO of Sycamore Tree Capital Partners, argues the private credit market needs a controlled purge rather than a bailout: he expects 3%–5% default rates to persist "on both a soft and hard basis for the last two or three years," and warns that companies carrying sub-1.5x interest coverage and over 7x leverage "are going to really struggle to get refinanced." Firms built under zero-interest-rate conditions can no longer afford reinvestment once debt service consumes cash flow. Parker sees credit secondaries as the release valve — projecting volumes growing from $20 billion in 2025 to $35 billion+ in 2026 and doubling or tripling beyond that — and Sycamore Tree launched its own credit secondaries platform this month.

**Quotable:**
> "Unless you believe in a greater fools theory, those businesses that have sub 1.5x interest coverage and north of 7x leverage are going to really struggle to get refinanced." — *Trey Parker, CIO, Sycamore Tree Capital Partners, on the private credit reckoning ahead*

## AI's Impact on Work & Tools

### Clouded Judgement 4.24.26 - The AI-Driven Employment Explosion 📌
**Clouded Judgement by Jamin Ball** · email · 9 mins

1. **The correct unit of AI disruption is the task, not the job.** Jensen Huang's framing: a software engineer's *job* is building high-quality software; *writing code in an IDE* is merely one task of that job. AI automating the task of code-writing doesn't eliminate the job — understanding systems, architecting solutions, debugging unexpected failures remain entirely human. Conflating task with job is the category error behind most AI unemployment predictions.

2. **Even if coding is 60-70% of an engineer's time, compression multiplies output rather than headcount.** Compress 70% of the time and you don't get rid of the engineer — you get that engineer shipping 3-4x more. Software demand is nowhere near saturated; the market has been supply-constrained, not demand-constrained. Easier supply means more software built, which means more engineers needed, not fewer.

3. **ATMs arrived in the late 1960s and US bank teller employment doubled by the mid-1980s.** Before ATMs, branches required ~21 tellers; after saturation, ~13 — making each branch cheaper to run. Cheaper branches led to more branches, and more branches led to more tellers in aggregate. Tellers stopped being cash-in/cash-out machines (the automated task) and became relationship bankers who cross-sold financial products (the job), making the role more valuable, not obsolete.

4. **Legal tech automated the most time-consuming lawyer tasks, yet lawyer employment grew through the 70s, 80s, and 90s.** Legal research once required days or weeks in physical law libraries; Westlaw (1975) and LexisNexis (late 70s) compressed it to hours. Word processors then let one lawyer produce 5-10x the document output. The pattern held: automating the tasks (research, drafting) expanded the profession rather than contracting it.

5. **Geoffrey Hinton predicted in 2016 that AI would eliminate radiologists; instead, the field grew.** A radiologist's *job* is patient care; *reading and interpreting scans* is the task. AI improved dramatically at scan interpretation, but demand for imaging exploded simultaneously — more scan types, more screened conditions, an aging population — and radiologists' work evolved toward complex interpretation, interventional procedures, and clinical consultation. Automating the task raised the ceiling on the job.

**Quotable:**
> "The hard part of being a software engineer was never the typing. It was never the syntax. It's understanding the problem deeply enough to know what to build." — *on why automating code-writing doesn't automate software engineering*

### How do you make a chart? 📌
**Benn Stancil** · email · 8 mins

1. Making a chart in AI-era software spans 8 rungs of abstraction — from asking an image model (ChatGPT Images) to render a picture, through Excel, charting libraries (Matplotlib/Plotly/d3), raw JavaScript, C, a bespoke domain-specific language, all the way to "grow corn to feed the workers who build the mine that digs the silicon…that draws the chart." The escalating absurdity is the point: the entire software supply chain — programming languages, operating systems, databases, consumer apps like Excel — lives between those poles and supports thousands of companies and millions of jobs.

2. AI's growing capability turns the comedy into a genuine economic question: how far down that ladder can/should AI go before intermediate layers become unnecessary? MotherDuck (a cloud data warehouse) answered by releasing "Dives" — AI agents that generate interactive JavaScript visualizations directly from natural language on top of warehouse data, bypassing dedicated BI tools entirely. Fabi's pitch makes the same claim: Python dashboards take "minutes vs. weeks" vs. traditional tools, and Claude, when asked by MotherDuck, agreed that "the drag-and-drop dashboard becomes a curiosity, like a fax machine with a particularly nice interface."

3. The counter: software isn't just a shorthand — it solves the hard problems you don't know exist until you encounter them. Overlapping axis labels, misplaced legends, colliding colors, the Rubik's-cube cascade where fixing one thing breaks another — these are problems Tableau spent twenty years solving. If LLMs really can navigate all of that, though, the logic doesn't stop at charts: they could also bypass the hosting infrastructure, the database layer, everything. The honest answer is that no one knows where the floor is, because every ceiling we set for LLMs has eventually been breached.

4. A separate escape route: maybe the future of analytics isn't better charts at all, but "vibes." An Omni customer support leader spent 75 pages of AI conversation identifying 10 categories of rep mistakes from support logs, with cited examples per rep and concrete remediation suggestions — no dashboard required. Crucially, vibes have no edge cases: there are no overlapping axes in how something *feels*. The slipperiest part of the whole chart problem disappears when you stop making charts.

5. SpaceX is reportedly in talks to acquire Cursor (the AI coding assistant) for $60 billion — framed as SpaceX needing Cursor's training data and user base, while Cursor needs SpaceX's massive GPU cluster. The punchline: a company whose stated mission is to build a city on Mars is pitching investors on its enormous technological ambitions by buying a SaaS product that helps people write websites. AI coding agents may or may not be a technological singularity, but they are unambiguously the attention singularity.

**Quotable:**
> "I don't know [if] AI coding agents are a technological singularity, but they are definitely the attention singularity." — *closing line, on SpaceX's $60B Cursor acquisition as proof of the moment*

## Healthcare & Social Impact

### Could robot health care be an improvement? 📎
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 6 mins

1. US eldercare is so poor that robots are a serious alternative. The author watched his father deteriorate from final-stage Parkinson's in a care facility where near-daily incidents of staff carelessness — including rough handling that left bruises — were the norm. The question of whether a self-learning AI robot could handle a dying person more gently than trained human professionals is not rhetorical.

2. Japan is the live test case, and so far robots are failing to win over patients. Bloomberg's Catherine Thorbecke visited Tokyo nursing homes and observed "plushie robots the size of human babies" being handed to elderly residents and prototype conversational dolls meant to fill gaps when family and human care fall short. Despite Japan's acute crisis — a rapidly aging population combined with a shrinking workforce — the elderly have not warmed to robotic companions.

3. The real danger is that robots become a cost-cutting cover story, not a care solution. One expert cited by Thorbecke warns that robot healthcare may actually require *more* intervention from the already-scarce human staff, not less. Rapid AI deployment could change those economics, but the likelier outcome in profit-oriented private equity-owned facilities is a "techno-bureaucratic fix" that lets owners avoid the harder structural investment — what Thorbecke calls treating care work as essential infrastructure.

**Quotable:**
> "They may have a place in the future of care, but the bigger risk is that governments and companies will use them to dodge the harder fixes required to treat care work as essential infrastructure." — *Catherine Thorbecke, on plushie and conversational robots in Japanese nursing homes*

### The Morning: Youth suicides drop 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Since the July 2020 launch of 988 — the national suicide prevention hotline that replaced a 10-digit number, backed by a $1.5 billion federal investment — youth suicide rates have fallen 11 percent below projections. Researchers estimate roughly 4,400 adolescents and young adults are alive as a result. The effect was even larger in states where 988 usage was highest.

2. Researchers isolated the 988 effect by testing two control groups: people over 65 (far less likely to call 988) showed no comparable drop, and England — which had no equivalent hotline change — showed no drop either. This rules out broader societal trends as the primary cause and pins the decline specifically to 988 uptake.

3. Youth suicides peaked in 2021 after years of increases. Dr. Laura Erickson-Schroth of the JED Foundation attributes part of the decline to regression to the mean — the peak cohort was the first generation to encounter social media in adolescence, then uniquely devastated by pandemic shutdowns. The 988 effect is real, but sits atop a broader statistical correction.

4. The Trump administration last summer eliminated the "Press 3" option for LGBTQ+ callers — a line that had been handling nearly 10 percent of all 988 calls. This week HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. announced Press 3 will be restored. The flip-flop illustrates the political vulnerability of a program that has bipartisan origins and clear empirical results.

5. A secondary story in the newsletter: 1,100 Afghan allies held at a U.S. military base in Qatar — sent there with a promise of U.S. resettlement after helping American forces — now face a forced choice between returning to Taliban-controlled Afghanistan and being relocated to conflict-wracked Congo. Lawmakers in both parties oppose the plan; Rep. Don Bacon (R-NE) said, "We made promises to those fighting by our side to bring them to the U.S. We should keep our promises."

**Quotable:**
> "You search for the threads of their life that they can hold onto, the kindling. All you have to do is listen." — *journalist Jason Cherkis, who worked on a 988 hotline for about a year, describing calls from lonely young men, overwhelmed caregivers, and people with nowhere else to turn*
