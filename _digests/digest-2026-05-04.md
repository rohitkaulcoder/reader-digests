## Today's Top 3

### What's working right now in AI search ⚡
**Kyle Poyar** · email · 10 mins

1. AEO is now the #1 channel B2B marketers are increasing investment in, with AI searches representing roughly one-third of all searches — AI Overviews cover 21% of Google keywords and ChatGPT alone handles about one-fifth of Google's query volume. The fundamental shift: AI engines recommend products rather than listing links, meaning buyers shortlist (or decide) before ever visiting your site. Salesforce going "headless" is an early signal that AI interfaces may eventually bypass brand websites entirely.

2. Visibility metrics are no longer enough — the new goal is pipeline influence. Beehiiv CMO Darren Chait shifted from tracking LLM visibility to measuring LLM-attributed signups; after rewriting pages the models were actually citing (like their enterprise landing page), they saw significant growth in attributed leads and signups with no change in raw visibility. The two metrics that matter now: *comprehension* (how accurately AI describes your product vs. competitors) and *pipeline influence* (direct referral traffic plus self-reported or manual attribution).

3. Brand messaging inconsistency is the biggest AEO vulnerability. Reply had 500+ blog posts describing itself differently; aligning everything around one idea — "sales engagement and AI SDR" — got them into the top 3 and visible in ~25% of 300+ tracked prompts. Webflow's AEO lead Vivian Hoang frames it as a cross-functional problem: AI pulls from external publications, review sites, and community discussions, not just owned content — making PR effectively the new AEO.

4. Structural prominence on your site signals importance to LLMs. Casey Hill of DoWhatWorks found that what brands put in headers, top nav, and footers directly affects citation rates — Replit's "Vibe Coding 101" footer link increased categorical association between Replit and vibe coding queries; Clay and Sundial saw higher customer citation rates after linking top use cases in their footers. Adding a "last updated" date to pages drives an average 15% citation uplift (Kevin Indig, Growth Memo), but only if the page is genuinely refreshed with new content.

5. AI queries average 60 words (vs. 3.4 words on Google), making everything effectively long-tail and personalized. Air published dedicated use-case pages for audiences like creative ops and immediately saw visibility gains for tracked prompts in Profound, with attributed conversions from users who hit those pages before buying. Eli Schwartz's rule: skip top-of-funnel visibility ("best family cars") and target mid-funnel buying intent ("where do I buy a 2025 used Kia Sorento near me") — that's where AI-educated buyers are actually ready to convert.

6. Discovery call transcripts are the new keyword research tool for finding mid-funnel queries that don't appear in traditional search analytics. Skio's head of marketing Allen Finn scrapes every demo and discovery call via MCP, uses DataForSEO APIs + Claude Code to map questions to keywords and content clusters, then pushes to AirOps for production — the result was Skio's best 28-day traffic window in four years. A secondary benefit: a library of hyper-relevant content SDRs can use in outbound and AEs in follow-up.

7. YouTube has surpassed Reddit as the leading AEO channel, now cited in 16% of LLM answers vs. 10% for Reddit. ClickUp leaned into video and now sees its content in 20-40% of relevant AI Overviews; LLM-driven workspace acquisition scaled 22x over the same window. Growth advisor Holly Chen reports a new tactic: acquiring small but established YouTube channels (real engagement history matters, not scale) and using AI to produce niche content across multiple sub-topic channels — LLM citations start within two months.

8. Mass AI-generated content is not a viable AEO strategy. Less than 20% of articles cited by ChatGPT are AI-generated; 80% are human-written (Ethan Smith, Graphite). Help Scout — despite being outspent on brand and funding by competitors flooding the zone with AI content — remains the most mentioned and cited brand in AI Overviews for customer service software by doing original research and producing genuinely useful content.

> "Qualified buyers don't ask top-of-funnel questions. They ask esoteric ones, specific to their business and the problems they're facing. So we scrape every demo and discovery call for real questions and pain points, use various DataForSEO APIs + Claude Code to tie them to keywords and build content clusters, then push the results to AirOps to write 'em. Per Search Console, we just had Skio's best 28-day traffic window in four years." — *Allen Finn, head of marketing at Skio*

### The World: DeepSeek's sequel ⚡
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. DeepSeek's January 2025 announcement — that it had built an AI model rivaling ChatGPT at a fraction of the chip cost — sent tech stocks plummeting globally and became the most-downloaded free app in the U.S. Analysts called it "AI's Sputnik moment" and predicted it would force American companies to become more efficient with compute. That prediction turned out to be wrong about what actually mattered.

2. DeepSeek's real disruption wasn't efficiency — it was open source. Unlike OpenAI and Anthropic, which guard their model architectures as trade secrets, DeepSeek published full technical details for anyone to use and build on. In the months after the initial release, Chinese tech companies released dozens of additional open-source models; by end of 2025, roughly one-third of all global AI usage involved Chinese open-source models.

3. For developers in Nigeria, Malaysia, Brazil, and elsewhere, building on Chinese open-source models costs more than 90% less than building with OpenAI's technology — making China's AI output function like a geopolitical soft-power asset. One investor likened it to the global cultural reach of Hollywood films and Big Macs. Chinese officials, who originally turned to open source as a practical shortcut for catching up, now see it as a deliberate strategy to cement China's image as an AI superpower.

4. The openness is constrained by politics: all models must comply with Chinese censorship and content regulations regardless of who builds on them, and government agencies in South Korea, Taiwan, and Australia have banned employees from using DeepSeek over data-security concerns. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has framed the competition as "democratic AI vs. authoritarian AI" — but the newest DeepSeek model, released in late April 2026, lags only OpenAI and Anthropic on coding benchmarks. For most global users, "good enough and cheap enough" is beating "best but expensive."

> "Maybe coders around the world don't feel as though they need the best. They just need good enough — and cheap enough." — *Meaghan Tobin, on why DeepSeek's latest model, despite ranking third behind OpenAI and Anthropic, is winning global adoption*

## Business & Finance

### Banks in danger of 'choking' on AI debt 📌
**FirstFT Asia** · email · 5 mins

1. The sheer scale of AI infrastructure financing is straining global banks' balance sheets to the breaking point. JPMorgan Chase, Morgan Stanley, SMBC, and MUFG are actively exploring private stake sales and so-called "risk transfers" — off-balance-sheet deals that redistribute exposure to a wider pool of investors — specifically to free up capacity for more lending. The mechanism is familiar from real estate and leveraged finance, but the dollar amounts are unprecedented for a single sector.

2. Oracle and CoreWeave, two data centre operators underpinning AI labs' compute needs, have borrowed hundreds of billions of dollars combined to build sites across the US. As one measure of the jam: JPMorgan and MUFG have spent more than six months trying to distribute just one deal — $38bn in construction debt tied to a single data centre project leased to Oracle in Texas and Wisconsin — and are still working through it.

3. The capacity crunch is structural, not cyclical. Banks set internal lending limits relative to a borrower's size; data centre debt tied to a handful of hyperscale operators is concentrating risk in a way the credit system was not designed to handle, forcing lenders to invent new distribution channels rather than simply raising limits.

> "The sizes we're talking about . . . they're out of scale to anything we've thought about, ever. Banks very quickly start choking." — *Matthew Moniot, co-head of credit risk sharing at Man Group*

### Ternus hints at his plans. 📌
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 9 mins

1. **Apple has quietly ended its "net cash neutral" policy — a signal that Ternus will prioritize investment flexibility over shareholder returns.** Since 2018, Apple had formally targeted keeping its cash and debt in balance, which in practice meant returning cash to shareholders rather than accumulating it. On its quarterly earnings call, CFO Kevan Parekh announced the policy is gone: "We are no longer providing net cash neutral as a formal target, and we will independently evaluate cash and debt." Apple still authorized a fresh $100 billion buyback, so the old strategy hasn't disappeared — but retiring the framework is itself the signal.

2. **The shift positions Ternus — who comes from the product side — to spend more like Jobs and less like Cook.** Cook's era returned over $1 trillion to shareholders and grew Apple into a $4 trillion company, but internal engineers and product designers have long pushed to hold more cash for major acquisitions and R&D. Apple's largest-ever deal remains the $3 billion Beats Electronics purchase in 2014, tiny relative to peers. Evercore analyst Amit Daryanani reads the move plainly: "Now they have the optionality to do it less. This is a sign they want to do more deals and invest cash differently."

3. **iOS 27 (code-named "Rave") is a focused, stability-first release with two priorities: performance and AI.** The overhaul skips broad feature additions in favor of a "Snow Leopard"-style update. Siri gets redesigned as a standalone chatbot app with third-party agent support via the App Store, multi-command handling, and a chat-thread interface competing directly with ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude. Visual Intelligence moves from a bolt-on feature into the Camera app itself as a dedicated Siri mode, gaining the ability to extract nutrition info from food packaging and capture contact details from anything the camera sees.

4. **Apple's AI robotics team — working on the tabletop robot internally dubbed J595 — is hemorrhaging senior talent to Google DeepMind and Meta.** The team's prior leader left for Meta last year. Senior executive Libo Meyers (on the J595 project) retired last month. Senior engineering manager Benoit Landry just departed for Google DeepMind, which also recently picked up Johnnie Manzari from Apple. The J595 is being built in Vietnam alongside the upcoming HomePod with screen.

5. **Vision Pro is selling worse than a year ago, and Apple has effectively paused development of the current enclosed headset form factor.** The $3,499 price is unchanged, the device hasn't gotten lighter, and content remains thin. In-store demos have slowed and consumer demand has softened. The product's original creator is reportedly considering leaving the company. Apple has shelved further iteration on the enclosed headset to focus on smart glasses and AR spectacles instead.

> "This is the most exciting time in my 25-year career at Apple to be building products and services. There are so many opportunities before us, and I couldn't be more optimistic about what's to come." — *John Ternus, first public remarks as Apple's CEO-in-waiting, declining to share any product specifics*

## AI & Enterprise Software

### The Briefing: Palantir's AI Pricing Power 📌
**Laura Bratton** · email · 6 mins

1. Palantir stock has fallen nearly 20% in 2026 while the Nasdaq is up 8%, driven by the same fear suppressing Salesforce, ServiceNow, SAP, and HubSpot: that generative AI from Anthropic and OpenAI will erode demand for enterprise software. But Palantir's core product — software that consolidates data across all of a company's systems, then lets engineers build custom applications and AI agents on top — is precisely the infrastructure layer that makes those AI tools usable. Competitors like Salesforce and ServiceNow have only recently begun building that capability.

2. Palantir's U.S. commercial business is the key signal to watch: in the most recent quarter it grew 140% year-over-year to $507M, outpacing its more-celebrated U.S. government segment ($570M, +66%). For full-year 2026, Palantir projects U.S. commercial revenue of $3.14B — 115% growth — accelerating from 109% in 2025 and 54% in 2024. Analysts expect Q1 2026 results (reported Monday) to show total revenue of $1.54B (+74%) and EPS of $0.24 (+200%).

3. Palantir is already running the outcome-based pricing model that Salesforce, HubSpot, and Adobe have only recently announced. It charges some customers a fee only when predefined business milestones are hit — for example, when profit margins rise by a set amount post-installation, or when an industrial customer's machinery data is successfully aggregated and monitored. Other customers pay a flat annual base fee plus usage-based surcharges. The company also embeds "forward-deployed engineers" on-site to build custom software — a white-glove model that OpenAI and Anthropic have begun copying to help enterprises actually operationalize AI.

> "Palantir is arguably ahead of other software firms just now scrambling to change their pricing models to tie software costs to usage or business outcomes." — *on Palantir's outcome-based pricing, which it has quietly offered before Salesforce and HubSpot announced their own versions*

### Why cultivating agency matters more than cultivating skills in the AI era | Max Schoening (Head of Product, Notion) 📎
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 5 mins

1. **Agency, not skills, is the real differentiator when AI closes skill gaps.** Skills can be delegated to a model; the willingness to treat the world as malleable and change things cannot. At Notion, designers Brian Levin and Eric Liu didn't wait for permission to start building prototypes in code — they moved from PRDs to Figma to terminal on their own initiative. The people who thrive aren't the ones who learn the most syntax; they're the ones who act as if obstacles are removable.

2. **The first 10% of every project is now free — the last 10% is still 90% of the work.** Spinning up 10 agents to explore 10 janky versions costs nothing, making early exploration essentially free (GitHub's old mantra: "demos, not memos"). But quality, reliability, and actually shipping to real users haven't gotten cheaper. The entire value of AI acceleration concentrates in exploration; execution discipline is unchanged.

3. **Software quantity has exploded; quality has not — and that gap is the opportunity.** Max: "I don't feel like the quality of software has increased all that much in the last 12 months. The amount of software has, but it's very, very hard to find software that is reliable." Even top labs' own tools regress every two weeks and still can't render a TUI at a reasonable frame rate. The SaaSpocalypse is overstated for the same reason: most people don't want to maintain their own software. "Software is like a garden. You need to tend to it. The thing you pay for in the as-a-service is the maintenance." Anthropic itself, of all companies, runs on Slack.

> "I don't feel like the quality of software has increased all that much in the last 12 months. The amount of software has, but it's very, very hard to find software that is reliable." — *Max Schoening on the vibe-coding era's real output*

### The Forecast: How gerrymandering ends 📎
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 7 mins

1. The Great Redistricting War of 2025–2026 has escalated faster than anyone predicted. Florida adopted a new map designed to flip four additional US House seats to Republicans. The same week, the Supreme Court gutted the Voting Rights Act's power to prevent racial majorities from being gerrymandered out of fair representation, prompting Louisiana and Tennessee to draw new maps, while a Virginia Democratic map got tangled in court.

2. The current arms race emerged from both parties abandoning historical restraint. For most of the past 60 years, maps were rarely neutral but were built to survive a decade of change and often reflected bipartisan incumbent protection. Now Democratic states like California and Virginia have torn up their own redistricting reform safeguards to retaliate — even though Democratic voters are geometrically disadvantaged, being "inefficiently concentrated" in dense districts, meaning Democrats can lead the national generic congressional ballot by ~6 points (per RealClearPolitics) and still lose seats.

3. The core moral dilemma — whether retaliatory gerrymandering is ethically justified — has no clean answer, and political philosopher Gianni Sarra of King's College London argues it may be the wrong question. Both parties claim the other started it (Republicans point to Illinois and Maryland; Democrats point to Trump's demand for five additional Texas Republican seats between censuses). Sarra's position: asymmetric gerrymandering, where one side restrains itself and the other doesn't, is actually *worse* than universal gerrymandering. The lesser evil is mutual escalation, not unilateral disarmament.

4. There is no visible off-ramp because the system is self-reinforcing. Incumbents elected under gerrymandered maps have every incentive to preserve those maps. Sarra's 2022 paper predicted retaliatory gerrymandering would follow the 2030 census; instead it arrived years early and more chaotically. His conclusion: "the costs from initially minor infractions will grow and grow" — and there is nothing in the current political incentive structure to stop them.

> "Eventually a lot of incumbents will be elected on the current system, and so why would they want to destroy it?" — *Gianni Sarra, political philosopher at King's College London*

### Bw Reads: The other autism controversy 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 6 mins

1. ABA (Applied Behavior Analysis) is the dominant US autism treatment — endorsed by the US surgeon general, the American Psychological Association, and the American Academy of Pediatrics as the "gold standard." Children are routinely prescribed up to 40 hours per week of it, compared with a few hours for speech or occupational therapy, and all 50 states legally require insurers to cover it. That mandated coverage has made it a magnet for private equity, which has spent the past decade buying up ABA clinics and chains across the country.

2. The case against ABA is both ethical and empirical. Critics — including therapists, social scientists, and autistic adults — argue that its core mechanism (rewarding compliance, withholding rewards for non-compliance) prioritizes neurotypical conformity over accommodation, and compares it to conversion therapy. One recent study found children who received ABA were 30% more likely to be hospitalized for mental health issues than a control group. Autism diagnosis rates have soared from 1 in 150 children in 2000 to 1 in 31 in 2022, making the stakes of this debate enormous.

3. ABA proponents and critics talk past each other partly because the treatment's meaning has shifted. Kaelynn Partlow, 29 — an autistic Netflix reality star who became a certified ABA practitioner — describes her own treatment in the 1990s as including punitive components ("they would dump books on the floor and tell her to pick them up") while conceding "I don't think they would operate in that way now." Advocates argue modern ABA is gentler and more individualized; critics say the behavioral framework itself is the problem regardless of implementation. Europe largely sidesteps the debate by neither legally recognizing nor regulating ABA at all.

> "It gets downright NASTY every time, from both sides" — *Kaelynn Partlow, autistic ABA practitioner, describing online reactions whenever she posts about the therapy*
