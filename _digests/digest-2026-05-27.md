### Money Stuff: Index Funds Can't Say No to SpaceX ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg)** · rss · 17 mins

1. **SpaceX's upcoming IPO has three structural problems for any rational investor.** Its governance eliminates all standard checks on management — no meaningful shareholder voting, no fiduciary duties — which large public pensions have called "the most management-favorable governance structure ever brought to the US public markets at this scale." Its valuation is ~100x revenue despite losing $4.9 billion on $18.7 billion in revenue last year. And its float is tiny: only ~5% of shares will be sold in the IPO, with the rest locked up until at least August (Musk himself faces a 366-day lockup).

2. **Index providers have historically used quality filters to protect passive investors from exactly these situations.** From 2017 to 2023, major index providers excluded new dual-class share companies (bad governance). The S&P 500 maintains profitability requirements that kept Tesla out for years. Most indexes require minimum float and a 12-month "seasoning period" after an IPO before inclusion. These rules exist precisely to prevent index funds from being forced to buy stocks that are hard to trade, unprofitable, or ungoverned.

3. **S&P Dow Jones Indices is now proposing to waive all three of these protections for sufficiently large companies.** In a "Consultation on Treatment of MegaCap Companies," S&P defines a MegaCap as a company with market cap at least equal to the 100th largest company in the S&P Total Market Index. For MegaCaps, S&P proposes cutting the seasoning period from 12 to 6 months and waiving float minimums and profitability requirements. Other index providers are planning similar waivers and will let SpaceX in even faster.

4. **The core problem for passive investors: with only 5% float and trillions of ETF dollars chasing it, index inclusion will be frantic and expensive.** Todd Sohn, chief ETF strategist at Strategas, put it plainly: "If SpaceX is up 100 per cent the week after the IPO, and they have to buy it, they have to buy it. They have to take that price . . . They can't discriminate." Index fund managers who care deeply about governance and valuation are structurally unable to act on those views — they must buy whatever the index mandates.

5. **This is a collective action problem that the index consultation process could theoretically solve — but won't.** If all institutional investors told S&P DJI "don't waive the rules for SpaceX," the index would hold firm and SpaceX would have to revise its IPO structure to be more investor-friendly. Instead, active buyers are enthusiastic enough about SpaceX to drive a high price, which forces the index to include it, which forces passive investors to buy regardless of their governance views. The market's collective enthusiasm overrides any individual investor's objection.

6. **Carbon credits are uniquely fraud-prone because buyers need the certificate, not the underlying reality.** Unlike jet fuel (a functional input: fake jet fuel means planes don't fly), carbon credits are only inputs to environmental disclosures. A CEO can check the "Removed Some Carbon" box with fake credits just as easily as real ones. Bloomberg's Petra Sorge and Natasha White found Chinese projects in Europe's government-run UER scheme claiming to avoid nearly 120,000 tons of CO2e that, per drone footage and satellite imagery, did not appear to exist.

7. **German regulators found 45 suspicious Chinese carbon-offset projects sold to major energy companies; none of the buyers face penalties.** Companies including BP, EDF, Exxon, Shell, TotalEnergies, MOL Group, and Vitol bought the fraudulent credits. Because they purchased "in good faith," Germany's General Customs Directorate confirmed they keep the environmental offset benefits even though the underlying reductions never happened. The system worked exactly as designed: the incentives reward buying a certificate, not verifying the certificate represents anything real.

8. **Prediction markets are structurally spoiling pre-taped reality TV because outcomes are known but not yet official.** On CBS's Survivor 50, a trader placed a $45,500 bet on Aubry Bracco winning on February 28 — just days after the premiere — earning a $4,500 profit. By the May 20 finale, $32.7 million in wagers had pushed her odds to 97%. Kalshi also correctly predicted the Masked Singer winner at 91% confidence three months before the finale, and spoiled Next Level Chef. The gap between filming and airing creates a window where insiders can trade on certain knowledge.

9. **Fernando Tatis Jr. lost his lawsuit against Big League Advance Fund for procedural reasons, validating that "buy stock in people" contracts are enforceable.** As a minor leaguer, Tatis sold 10% of his future MLB earnings for $2 million; he then signed a $340 million Padres contract. BLA now claims a multiple of $2M in return — with an implied interest rate Tatis's lawyers calculated at 161.54% on one $3.2 million payment, potentially illegal under usury laws. The court dismissed his case on grounds he had waived his right to object to arbitration. Enforceability is established; whether the deals are equitable remains unresolved.

10. **A Fidelity customer's accounts — including a Roth IRA set up at age 16 — were accidentally deleted, with Fidelity's own reps telling her she had no accounts there.** Her online statements and tax documents vanished simultaneously, leaving her unable to even provide account numbers. When she called the fraud department, they declined to help because with no account on record, there could be no fraud to investigate. She eventually recovered her funds, but the incident illustrates the crypto community's structural point: all financial assets are just entries in for-profit databases that you have no direct control over.

**Quotable:**
> "If SpaceX is up 100 per cent the week after the IPO, and they have to buy it, they have to buy it. They have to take that price . . . They can't discriminate." — *Todd Sohn, chief ETF strategist at Strategas, on the bind facing passive index funds at SpaceX's IPO*

### Nvidia Earnings, The AI Stack, Nvidia's New Reporting ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 10 mins

1. Nvidia's Q1 FY2027 results were record-breaking: $81.6B in sales (up 85% YoY, beating the $78.9B analyst consensus), $58.3B in net income (3x YoY, beating $42.9B estimates by 36.5%), and networking revenue alone tripled to $14.8B. The company announced an $80B share buyback and raised its quarterly dividend from $0.01 to $0.25 per share, committing to return 50% of free cash flow to shareholders.

2. Anthropic's shift to Nvidia is an underappreciated earnings tailwind. Until recently, Nvidia had "largely zero" coverage of Anthropic because Google and Amazon secured Anthropic's workloads on TPUs and Trainium in exchange for investment. That is now changing rapidly — Nvidia is securing capacity for Anthropic across Azure, AWS, and CoreWeave — meaning Nvidia's recent growth happened *without* the fastest-growing frontier lab, and that lab is now being added on top.

3. Nvidia's three moats are CUDA lock-in (binding developers to its chips), supply-chain priority access (first-in-line at TSMC for logic, packaging, and memory), and vertical integration across chips, networking, and systems. Of these, CUDA matters less as developers abstract above it — Anthropic already proved a frontier model can be trained and served on non-Nvidia silicon. Supply priority and systems integration are the durable near-term moats.

4. Nvidia's new reporting framework splits Data Center into two submarkets: "Hyperscale" (public cloud giants and large consumer internet companies, currently ~50% of Data Center revenue) and "ACIE" (AI Clouds, Industrial, Enterprise — neoclouds, sovereign AI deployments, enterprises, and industrial operators). The key distinction: hyperscalers want to commoditize Nvidia by rolling their own chips and networking; ACIE customers buy complete Nvidia systems and have neither the capability nor the incentive to design around Nvidia.

5. ACIE is Nvidia's long-run lock-in play. These customers — regional AI clouds, 250,000 enterprises, chip fabs that can't pipe data to a remote cloud — buy entire integrated systems, not just chips, and can't plausibly build alternatives. Their share of Nvidia's revenue has grown over the last nine quarters. A 50/50 revenue split almost certainly masks that ACIE customers pay more per unit (buying full systems) while hyperscalers dominate in raw unit count, making ACIE disproportionately valuable for margin and lock-in.

6. The new Vera CPU targets ACIE, not hyperscalers. Nvidia claims $20B in CPU revenue visibility this year, with Vera delivering 1.5x faster performance per core, 2x performance per watt, and 4x density per rack vs. x86. While Kress mentions hyperscaler partnerships, the real buyers are neoclouds already all-in on Nvidia — hyperscalers have their own custom CPU programs and relationships with AMD and Intel, making them far less likely to standardize on Vera.

**Quotable:**
> "A random data center or enterprise customer is not going to make their own chip or roll their own networking; they're just going to buy entire systems from Nvidia, and Nvidia really doesn't have any competition in this space." — *on why ACIE is Nvidia's most strategically valuable customer segment*

### The Rocketship 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 13 mins

1. SpaceX's S-1 reveals two structurally different businesses bundled into one offering: a mature rocket/Starlink segment that generated $15.5bn in revenue and $7.8bn adjusted EBITDA in 2025, and a nascent AI segment (xAI + X) that lost $1.2bn on adjusted EBITDA despite $3.1bn in revenue, with a $6.4bn operating loss once depreciation is included. The S-1 carefully limits its "stellar track record of capital allocation" claim to Space and Connectivity only — the emphasis is deliberate, not accidental.

2. A $15bn/year Anthropic compute deal ramping up May 2026 and running through 2029 will cosmetically transform SpaceX's near-term financials, potentially making it look more like a neocloud than a rocket company by revenue for its first few public quarters. The deal carries termination risk — either party can exit with 90 days notice — but assuming it holds, it provides step-function revenue growth that will flatter early public-market comparables.

3. SpaceX's core competitive advantage isn't rockets or satellites; it's compressing the timeline between capital deployment and return. SpaceX brought COLOSSUS (its Memphis data center) online in 122 days and COLOSSUS II in 91 days, versus a two-year industry benchmark for a 100MW greenfield data center. The same compression logic applies to orbit: launch costs per kilogram are down 92% from pre-SpaceX levels, with Starship targeting 99%+ reduction.

4. At a $1.25tr proposed valuation, SpaceX would trade at roughly 25x run-rate revenue for a business with significant fixed costs and capitalized depreciation on every satellite, launch, and fuel burn. The $75bn IPO, if completed, would represent 60% of all the capital Musk has ever deployed through SpaceX — meaning the IPO isn't just one raise among many, it's the dominant event in the company's financial history.

5. Buying SpaceX is structurally a bet on Musk's continuing ability to raise capital at low cost, not a direct bet on rockets or AI. The mechanism: if SpaceX supports a $1tr+ valuation, its cost of capital drops below most private alternatives, which means marginally positive-NPV frontier projects (return 7%, cost of capital 6%) get funded that otherwise wouldn't. Musk's eccentricities and governance failures matter less than whether public investors remain willing to extend him the benefit of the doubt.

6. The "Theory of Elon" problem is that his track record forces you to hold a consistent model: every skill you downgrade requires upgrading another to explain the outcomes. If he's not technical, he recruits extraordinary engineers; if he has bad judgment, he has supernatural persuasive ability. The S-1 codifies "The Algorithm" — 5 steps: eliminate dumb requirements, delete parts, optimize remaining parts, accelerate cycle time, automate last — as the operational explanation for how Musk companies outperform on execution while often missing on timelines.

7. Three short-take arguments from the newsletter's "Elsewhere" section: (a) DeepSeek has made its 75%-off introductory AI pricing permanent, a bet that token-price commoditization benefits whoever controls the cost curve; (b) AI coding tools (per the makers of Pi/OpenClaw in the WSJ) are creating technical debt faster than organizations can manage it, favoring teams that build microservice architectures before customer load hits; (c) Waymo's benefits to disabled riders illustrate a recurring pattern — new technologies raise quality variance at the top but reduce it at the bottom by substituting for variable human skills.

### The pope speaks up for humans, not humanoids 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. Factory workers in countries like India are being fitted with smart glasses that record their hand movements during stitching and product packing. That data becomes training material for robots designed to replace those exact workers — a dynamic captured by Ashish Narayan, one such worker, who said "it feels like working in your own grave, while you make your own casket."

2. Pope Leo XIV's new encyclical warns that AI-driven decisions on employment, credit, and access to public services risk being "fully delegated to automated systems" that lack "compassion, mercy, forgiveness, and above all, the hope that people are able to change." He calls for AI to be "disarmed" — stripping away the assumption that technical power automatically confers the right to govern. The reference to Stalin's taunt ("how many divisions has the pope?") and the Soviet Union's eventual fate is invoked to argue papal moral authority has real geopolitical weight.

3. Morgan Stanley research finds that AI diffusion is already occurring at roughly twice the pace of the internet era, yet has not so far triggered large-scale unemployment. The greatest risk is diffusion moving too fast — rapid adoption can create a skill mismatch that eventually overwhelms labor market flexibility. The Global X Robotics & AI ETF is rallying, though the sector ran far harder relative to the broader market during the pandemic wave.

4. Barclays analyst Zornitsa Todorova identifies the real engineering chokepoint in humanoid robots as "brawn" — specifically actuators that convert energy into movement. Actuators account for roughly 50% of total humanoid production costs, versus 35% for brains (chips/software) and 15% for batteries. With roughly 8 million factories and 2.8 billion buildings globally, it is cheaper to adapt robots to human environments than to rebuild the physical world around them — making humanoid form a practical industrial bet, not a novelty.

5. Noah Ramos of Alpine Macro maps a bifurcated AI race: the US leads in "brain" (advanced semiconductors, LLMs, where compute capex is decisive), while China dominates the "body" (manufacturing, actuators, and the magnetic rare earths that underpin them). China's rare-earth control combined with its manufacturing depth is hard to challenge near-term — helping explain Washington's escalating chip export restrictions and the recent surge in Chinese IT stocks, which have now outperformed even the US tech sector under Trump 2.0. Nvidia's Jensen Huang has called physical AI "the next frontier," but the companies best positioned to capture it may not be American.

> "It feels like working in your own grave, while you make your own casket." — *Ashish Narayan, Indian factory worker whose daily hand movements are recorded by smart glasses to train the robots that may replace him*

## Business & Markets

### How helping your rivals makes you harder to beat 📎
**Big Think Business** · email · 8 mins

1. On May 23, 1925, a 6.8-magnitude earthquake struck Kinosaki, Japan, killing 283 people and burning every building to rubble. The surviving ryokan (inn) owners — all competitors — held over 100 meetings and agreed to treat the entire town as a single inn: each ryokan a "room," the streets its hallways, the bathhouses shared amenities. They posted their guiding philosophy throughout the village: *kyozon-kyoei* — coexistence and co-prosperity. Kinosaki is still thriving 100 years later.

2. Japan is home to over 33,000 companies operating for more than a century, and more than 140 running for over 500 years (*shinise* — venerable establishments). Meanwhile, S&P 500 company lifespans have collapsed from over 60 years in the mid-20th century to roughly 15–20 years today. Western zero-sum competitive culture — "disruption," "killer apps," "winner-take-all" — is producing companies that are better at winning and worse at lasting.

3. In 1997, Ryuzaburo Kaku, honorary chairman of Canon, published "The Path of Kyosei" in the *Harvard Business Review*. Kaku survived the Nagasaki atomic bomb at 19 while working as a conscripted laborer and spent three days hiding underground. He later built Canon from a small camera company into a global technology manufacturer, and introduced *kyōsei* (combining the characters for "together" and "life") as Canon's philosophy: "a spirit of cooperation in which individuals and organizations live and work together for the common good."

4. Kaku's five-stage framework moves outward like concentric ripples. Stage 1 is economic survival — secure the foundation, because "even companies in the later stages of kyōsei must increase profits." Stage 2 is cooperating with labor: Canon eliminated the distinction between salaried and hourly workers as early as 1943, introduced a five-day workweek before any other major Japanese company, committed never to fire a domestic employee, and had zero strikes in 60 years at Canon Tokyo.

5. Stage 3 — cooperating with competitors — is where the framework becomes counterintuitive. Canon supplied the laser-printer engines that powered HP's products, despite being fierce rivals in the printer market; both companies profited. Stages 4 and 5 extend further: global companies address structural imbalances (building factories in trade-deficit countries, training local workers) and advocate for systemic policy reform. Most executives never reach Stage 5 because short-term pressure crowds out systems-level thinking.

6. Kyōsei is structurally different from trade associations, which pool resources to protect insiders by lobbying against customer-friendly rules and blocking new entrants. Trade groups always need an enemy. Kyōsei needs none — the Kinosaki innkeepers weren't organizing against anyone; they were rebuilding what they all shared. Critically, cooperation requires strength, not weakness: Canon could only practice kyōsei with competitors because it had the patents and technology to bargain from a position of power.

**Quotable:**
> "Your competitor's survival is not a threat to yours. It is a *condition* of yours. If the town dies, every inn dies with it." — *on the Kinosaki ryokan owners' post-earthquake decision*

### What does Trump want with Cuba? 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 6 mins

1. Trump's endgame is turning Cuba into a US economic dependent — repeating the pattern of Soviet and then Venezuelan patronage, but with Washington as the patron. With Nicolás Maduro captured in a January 3 raid in Caracas, Cuba lost its principal oil supplier and patron state. Trump and Secretary of State Marco Rubio (born in Florida to Cuban parents who fled before the 1959 revolution) are now squeezing Havana with a de facto oil blockade, expanded sanctions targeting foreign companies operating on the island, and a CIA director visit demanding "radical change" before any pressure is lifted.

2. The humanitarian squeeze is severe and accelerating. Cuba's 10 million people face rolling blackouts, food rations running short, and families cooking with wood and charcoal — the only functioning businesses are private ones, which Fidel Castro banned but the government re-legalized in 2021. The pressure campaign forced out Sherritt International Corp., a Canadian nickel miner and one of the island's largest private investors. A former Trump adviser then emerged as a potential white knight to acquire Sherritt's Cuban joint ventures, illustrating how regime change and connected dealmaking are intertwined.

3. Military signaling is backing the economic strangulation. The Pentagon deployed the USS Nimitz carrier group to the Caribbean on the same day the US unsealed murder charges against former Cuban president Raúl Castro (tied to a 1996 shootdown). US Marines flew the Stars and Stripes over Caracas during weekend military exercises — a pointed demonstration to Havana of what followed Maduro's capture. Cuba's President Miguel Díaz-Canel says he'll negotiate but will not cede sovereignty or the existing system of government, leaving the two sides at an impasse even as a Supreme Court ruling revived thousands of Cuba property claims worth $9 billion, adding another legal overhang for any would-be investors.

**Quotable:**
> "Trump wants to change that by making the island as dependent on the US as it was previously on Venezuela and, before that, the Soviet Union." — *on Trump's Cuba strategy*
