## Today's Top 3

### Amazon's Durability ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 14 mins

1. Amazon's May 2026 launch of Amazon Supply Chain Services (ASCS) — packaging air/ocean freight, trucking, and last-mile delivery into one suite for businesses like P&G and 3M — confirms a decade-old prediction about Amazon's core strategy: build infrastructure for internal use, then sell it to the world. The pattern played out first with AWS (2006), then with third-party logistics, and ASCS is the official capstone of that logistics arc.

2. Amazon converts marginal costs into capital costs, then leverages those capital costs by selling them to others. AWS did this structurally with Nitro (a custom chip that offloads hypervisor, network, and storage management from expensive Intel/AMD servers), enabling more VMs per physical machine and creating a structural cost advantage over rivals who pay full margins on commodity hardware.

3. The Graviton ARM chip playbook shows how this compounds: Graviton's early generations were inferior to Intel/AMD, but Amazon used them internally to power PaaS services like RDS — meaning customers who didn't explicitly choose Graviton were already on it. AWS double-dipped: higher PaaS margins on top, cheaper silicon underneath. Graviton is now competitive on performance while still cheaper, but the internal-use moat precedes any third-party adoption by years.

4. AWS was genuinely disadvantaged in the AI training era (2023). SemiAnalysis documented the two weaknesses: Amazon's proprietary EFA networking couldn't match Nvidia's NVLink for chaining thousands of GPUs together in training clusters, and Nvidia deprioritized H100 allocations to AWS because Amazon was visibly moving workloads away from Nvidia silicon — so Amazon got a meaningfully smaller share of GPU supply relative to its cloud market share.

5. The shift from training to inference has reversed AWS's position. Inference doesn't require networking thousands of chips — a model's parameters fit in a single server. Agentic workloads (the third AI inflection, post-reasoning models) are CPU-heavy and require routing between heterogeneous compute resources, which is exactly what Nitro's disaggregated-resource abstraction was built for. Jensen Huang's argument that tokens-per-watt justifies Nvidia margins over power investment may not hold for a company that can buy more power upstream and then spend less downstream on Nvidia margins.

6. Amazon bought Annapurna Labs in 2015, shipped its first AI chip in 2019, and Trainium 3 (2025-era) is now rated as a credible competitor by SemiAnalysis. Bedrock, Amazon's AI platform, already runs on Trainium behind the scenes — repeating the Graviton playbook where customers consume the cheap internal silicon through an abstraction layer before they know or care what chip they're on.

7. Amazon's Anthropic investment (multi-billion dollars) gave AWS exclusive early access to a frontier model that runs on all major clouds — unlike OpenAI's former Azure exclusivity, which Microsoft ultimately had to relax because it was hurting Azure's enterprise appeal. Meanwhile, Amazon's physical-world core business (retail + data centers) means it faces no internal pressure to divert customer compute to self-serve AI needs — unlike Microsoft, which visibly missed Azure growth targets in early 2026 by prioritizing its own AI workloads, or Google, which faces existential AI pressure on search.

8. Amazon Leo (satellite broadband) follows the identical formula: capital-intensive upfront, Amazon as first-best customer (drone delivery announced 13 years ago requires reliable satellite connectivity), and eventual third-party sales to businesses like Apple. Long-term AI vulnerability — and long-term AI investment incentive — correlates tightly with how much of a company's core business touches the physical world: Apple and Amazon need only access to frontier models, not ownership; Google and Meta must lead in AI because aggregator businesses face one-click competition; Microsoft sits uncomfortably in between.

**Quotable:**
> "Amazon, more than any other company, actually operates with decade-long timeframes, consistently making real-world investments at massive scale that (1) convert their marginal costs into capital costs and (2) gain leverage on those capital costs by selling them to other businesses." — *on why the ASCS launch and AWS's AI position are the same story*

### Money Stuff: Investment Banking Without a License ⚡
**Matt Levine** · email · 14 mins

1. **Morgan Stanley's Budapest analysts were doing licensed investment banking work without licenses.** The WSJ reports FINRA opened an inquiry after a whistleblower alleged that front-office analysts in Budapest — doing the same pitch decks and live-deal work as peers in New York and London — lacked the Finra registrations US law requires. A 2024 Morgan Stanley memo acknowledged the analysts "are not licensed to undertake any regulated activities," yet the rules weren't enforced: analysts appeared on pitchbook "Our Team" pages with US and UK flags, and at least one traveled to meet a European client in person.

2. **The Budapest operation was essentially labor arbitrage on regulated work, and it backfired on the workers.** Budapest analysts worked 1 pm to 7 am local time to service New York, were relatively poorly paid, and saw transfer promises to home offices quietly walked back. The whistleblower's decision makes sense in that light: becoming an unlicensed-investment-banking whistleblower turned out to be a better deal than remaining an unlicensed investment banker.

3. **The practical licensing rule is probably: if you appear on the working group list, you need to be licensed.** Formatting spreadsheets anonymously in a back office is almost certainly fine. But once a client might call you for advice — because your name and phone number appear in a pitchbook — you are advising someone, and you need to be registered. Appearing on the "Our Team" page under a US flag while working from Budapest is the kind of thing that crosses that line.

4. **Kalshi's "sports bets are CFTC-regulated event contracts" argument creates a legal trap for state sportsbooks.** Kalshi successfully argued that sports bets are commodity futures, federally regulated by the CFTC and preempting state gaming laws. The obvious corollary — not yet enforced but noted in a Lexicon Labs CFTC comment letter — is that state-licensed sportsbooks are trading swaps with retail customers on unregistered exchanges, in violation of Section 2(e) of the Commodity Exchange Act, which requires retail swap trading to occur on a CFTC-designated contract market.

5. **Sports betting has gone from a state-by-state question to entirely outside state jurisdiction — in both directions.** States can no longer ban it (Kalshi's federal preemption argument wins) and arguably cannot license it either (their sportsbooks would be illegal unregistered swaps exchanges). The 2018 Supreme Court ruling that "each State is free to act on its own" has been effectively reversed by regulatory interpretation, not by any act of Congress.

6. **Deutsche Bank trader James Vorley's lawsuit illustrates the tension between formal compliance training and informal desk culture.** Vorley, convicted of wire fraud for spoofing gold and silver futures between 2008 and 2013 and sentenced to 12 months and a day in prison, is now suing Deutsche Bank for £12 million, claiming senior traders explicitly taught him the spoofing strategy. Deutsche Bank denies formal endorsement but acknowledges informal teaching may have occurred "without the bank's knowledge." The reality of trading desks is that the head of your desk training you to spoof carries more authority than a muted compliance video.

7. **The SEC's $1.5 million Musk settlement — down from a sought $200+ million — effectively prices insider-trading-adjacent disclosure violations at about 1%.** Musk crossed Twitter's 5% disclosure threshold in late March 2022 but didn't file for 21 days (the rule requires 10, now tightened to 5 business days after Musk's trades), buying more stock secretly and saving roughly $150 million at Twitter shareholders' expense. The Trump-era SEC settled for $1.5 million, paid by a Musk revocable trust with no admission of wrongdoing — prompting observers to note that well-connected activist investors now have little financial reason to file 13Ds on time.

### Why SaaS freemium playbooks don't work in AI, and what to do instead ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 12 mins

1. **AI free tiers create a self-defeating paradox that SaaS never faced.** Every time a free user hits Enter, GPUs fire and cash burns — there is no near-zero marginal cost like in traditional SaaS. Google's Gemini free tier became so capable it could "write flawless Python, draft perfect marketing copy, and summarize long email threads instantly," causing users to ask why they'd pay $20/month. The classic SaaS move of gating features like Deep Research or live audio would have blocked users from ever experiencing the magic needed to build a habit and desire to return — while also not necessarily targeting the biggest compute costs.

2. **Gate usage intensity, not model quality — that's the real monetization lever.** Google's original single "Gemini Advanced" tier gated model quality, but power users who upgraded consumed such massive compute that unit economics became "terrifying." The redesign into Plus/Pro/Ultra tiers maps each tier to a specific usage intensity level and context window size (up to 1 million tokens), giving users predictable prepaid pricing instead of AWS-style bill shock. Midjourney executes the same logic with "Fast Mode" (limited monthly GPU hours, priced for instant access) vs. "Relax Mode" (unlimited but queued) — users pay for priority, not better images.

3. **Gate outcomes, not just outputs — sell hours of labor saved, not answers.** LLMs have transitioned from passive text generators to autonomous agents that reason, plan, use tools, and retain memory. Google made Chrome auto-browse — an AI agent that autonomously navigates websites, fills forms, compares products, adds items to carts, and books appointments — exclusive to higher-tier subscribers, framing the paywall around employee labor eliminated rather than answer quality. Intercom's Fin AI agent is the gold standard: it charges $0.99 per resolved support ticket, free to attempt but only billed on confirmed success.

4. **Gate your heaviest compute modalities as the ultimate top-tier unlock.** Google's Genie 3 (a real-time interactive "world model") was so compute-intensive that engineers joked TPUs were "melting on every prompt," and serving it at the 100K+ QPS needed for hundreds of millions of DAUs was physically impossible. Rather than a liability, Google turned it into an upgrade incentive by restricting it to the highest subscription tier. Consumers intrinsically understand that a cinematic, photorealistic real-time 3D environment is premium in a way they don't accept for a 500-word email summary — so the paywall doesn't feel punitive.

5. **Fire upgrade prompts only at high-intent behavioral signals, not on a timer.** Three conversion triggers have proven effective for Google AI subscriptions: (1) the "multi-turn metric" — a user who submits 5+ follow-up refinement prompts in a single session is doing real work and has high willingness to pay at that exact moment; (2) the "cross-platform trigger" — accessing the product on both desktop and mobile within a 48-hour window signals daily-life integration; (3) "continue this chat" — when a shared Gemini conversation link requires a Pro-tier model or 1M-token context window, the recipient hits a soft paywall that demonstrates premium value through a real-world use case.

6. **Bundle AI with sticky, non-AI utilities to survive the "episodic usage" churn trap.** AI churn is structurally higher than SaaS because usage is naturally episodic — a user might write 10 reports in October and zero in November, then cancel. Bundling Google AI subscriptions with Google One cloud storage was the "masterstroke": users almost never cancel cloud storage because losing photos and emails is too painful, so they automatically reach month two of AI usage and have time to build a habit. Cursor achieves the same lock-in by embedding AI into the IDE and indexing the developer's local codebase — churning requires actively tearing down a customized environment that's expensive to rebuild.

7. **Route prompts to the cheapest capable model invisibly to protect gross margins.** Serving your largest frontier model for every free-tier query is financially unsustainable — even users who type "please" and "thank you" to their LLMs are, in aggregate, costing millions in compute. Semantic routing at the input layer sends simple queries ("What is the capital of France?") to hyper-cheap micro-models like Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite or an open-source 8B parameter model, while complex logic puzzles route to heavier reasoning models with token metering. Amazon Bedrock's Intelligent Prompt Routing offers a cloud-native version of this, dynamically predicting response quality per request and routing accordingly.

8. **Avoid three structural anti-patterns that turn power users into margin killers.** First, hiding cancel flows breeds distrust — offer a frictionless one-click pause that preserves custom instructions, so a temporary project lull doesn't become a permanent exit. Second, locking tier boundaries in stone is fatal: the frontier model commanding a premium today will be commoditized within months, and companies have bled by giving away "$5,000 of compute for a $200 subscription" when costs shifted. Third, flat 24/7 pricing incentivizes heavy workloads during weekday peak when GPU costs are highest and cloud providers may charge surge pricing; "usage multipliers" or compute "happy hours" that double credit value on weekends smooth server load and monetize otherwise idle infrastructure.

**Quotable:**
> "We stopped selling 'answers' and started selling 'hours.'" — *on reframing the Gemini Advanced value proposition around agentic productivity after Chrome auto-browse launched*

### Deployment 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 8 mins

1. The AI industry structurally mirrors China's manufacturing buildout: capex-constrained, demand assumed to materialize, and costs falling as volume rises. That model produces middle-income growth but stalls before getting rich — China had to move beyond cheap labor, and AI labs similarly need more than cheap tokens to capture lasting value.

2. Both OpenAI ($10bn) and Anthropic ($1.5bn JV with PE firms) are building *sogo shosha*-style intermediaries — the Japanese trading conglomerates that plugged into raw material supply on one end and end consumers on the other, letting Japan control demand without owning every downstream business. The labs are doing the same: use the consulting vehicle to manage where and how fast token consumption grows, rather than passively waiting for adoption.

3. The five strategic benefits of a semi-captive consulting arm: (a) generate proprietary domain-specific training data via client feedback loops; (b) shift capacity toward high revenue-per-token work when inference is scarce and cheap volume when GPUs sit idle; (c) hedge against commoditization at different capability tiers; (d) segment investor pools — ASI believers shouldn't subsidize EBITDA-expansion bets and vice versa; (e) create a second hiring track where output isn't purely raw-brainpower-bounded, letting labs take more risks on talent.

4. The labs entering the PE/consulting space won't crowd out incumbents much, because the labs have a built-in conflict of interest: their incentive to push their own inference is visible, and at least some clients will discount that. Traditional PE "just add AI" deals — like the Amex Global Business Travel transaction — remain credible precisely because they lack that conflict. The median enterprise is at "VCR blinking 12:00" with AI adoption; the runway is long, and reasonably competent incumbents keep being the default beneficiaries.

**Elsewhere in the issue:** Amazon opened its logistics network as an API (mirroring Salesforce's recent move), positioning it to capture AI-agent-driven shipping spend as bots take over procurement. The Trump administration is mulling pre-release government approval for frontier AI models — less a new constraint than a change in who plays enforcer, since labs are already cautious. YC owns ~0.6% of OpenAI (founded under YC Research as a nonprofit), likely its highest-dollar return ever, edging out Stripe — a textbook power-law outcome from maximizing surface area. Meta is using ML to infer users' ages from photos without facial recognition, illustrating that companies routinely know more than they reveal and self-censor by choice — a trust relationship that tightens as users spell out more of their thinking token by token. Record US buybacks in 2026 reflect that AI capex from a handful of hyperscalers flows as high-margin revenue to the broader public market, partially recycling rather than destroying cash.

**Quotable:**
> "The median company is probably at about that stage with respect to AI, and the business model that maximizes dollar upside is buying companies and improving them. It's notable that both labs are partnering with existing PE firms — again and again, reasonably competent incumbents are the default beneficiaries of AI." — *on why traditional PE still wins even as OpenAI and Anthropic enter consulting*

## Finance & Operations

### How Samsara Matured it's Forecasting from Private to Public 📌
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 9 mins

1. When Dominic Phillips became CFO of Samsara, the company had over $100M in revenue and no annual planning process, no budget-versus-actuals reviews, and C-suite members who had never held a budget envelope. Phillips came from ServiceNow, where he ran IR, Corporate Development, and FP&A under Mike Scarpelli (later Snowflake's CFO) — one of the most rigorous forecasting environments in public-company software. He spent two years building that discipline at Samsara before the IPO, explicitly crediting the pre-IPO runway as essential to doing it right.

2. Samsara's capital allocation is explicit and deliberate: ~high-30% of revenue goes to Sales & Marketing (consistent with its top-down, direct sales motion modeled after ServiceNow), high teens to 20% to R&D, and the remainder to G&A, which has generated increasing leverage at scale. The R&D bucket uses an investment horizon framework: 70% of annual R&D funds target this year's product roadmap, 30% goes to medium- and long-term product bets — preventing the short-termism that plagues growth-stage R&D without sacrificing near-term output.

3. Annual planning starts with a 1,000-day (3-year) strategy, then strips away years 2 and 3 to go deep on year 1. Operating principles set the outer bounds first — demonstrate year-over-year leverage, maintain Rule of 40 or better — and within those guardrails Samsara tries to grow as fast as possible. Top-line targets are triangulated between Finance, Sales, and R&D using internal data (pipeline capacity, rep ramp rates, rep productivity) plus external benchmarks against publicly traded peers, then adjusted for macro signals like competitive dynamics and economic shifts.

4. Budgeting methodology is a deliberate hybrid: mature functions use full zero-based budgeting from scratch each year, while others are pushed to fund new initiatives from run-rate savings before incremental dollars are deployed. FP&A analysts are embedded throughout the org so Finance knows which approach each function actually requires — avoiding the failure mode of running a rigorous zero-based process on a department where most costs just need to roll forward. The draft is locked by end of Q3; Q4 is reserved for tweaks based on the final quarter's emerging data.

5. Reforecasting follows a "Three, Two, One" accuracy standard: after month one of a quarter's actuals, the forecast for the remaining two months should land within 3% of what actually happens; after month two, within 2%; after the final forecast update, within 1% across all forecastable line items. This runs at both levels simultaneously — quarterly reforecasts of the remainder of the fiscal year, and monthly reforecasts of the current quarter — so the plan is continuously refreshed as real data arrives.

6. The feedback loop is the mechanism that makes accuracy improve over time. FP&A functions fail when they extract inputs from business leaders for models but never report back on how those inputs performed. Samsara treats FP&A analysts as embedded business partners — present in staff meetings alongside HR business partners — so the relationship is bidirectional: Finance tells the business what missed and why, which generates better inputs in the next cycle. Phillips frames this as a living-organism model: a company gets better at pacing itself only when information flows both ways.

**Quotable:**
> "It's important to understand and appreciate where your org is today from a maturity standpoint, but also have a really strong view of, where does this need to go and what are you building toward?" — *Dominic Phillips, CFO of Samsara, on inheriting a company with $100M+ revenue and no annual planning process*

## Markets & Geopolitics

### Iran draws a line and puts markets on notice 📎
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. Iran's missile strike on the UAE — launched after Trump offered "safe passage" to neutral ships through the Strait of Hormuz — signals that the month-old ceasefire is fraying. Analysts initially dismissed the attack as perfunctory, but prediction markets now give less than 50-50 odds that Hormuz traffic returns to normal by end of June; two weeks ago that probability was 90%. Brent crude futures for year-end have broken above $90/barrel, surpassing the previous conflict peak set when Iran hit a Qatari LNG facility.

2. The most likely resolution is a return to something resembling the 2015 JCPOA — Iran caps its nuclear program in exchange for restored oil exports — yet there is little urgency to get there. Macquarie's Viktor Shvets calls a JCPOA-like deal the "base case" since "it is not clear whether there are any other even modestly rational answers." The catch: BCA Research's Marko Papic argues the absence of a recession removes all pressure on both sides, leaving the impasse stable and self-reinforcing. Two-year inflation swaps have risen above the Fed's 3% upper target, their highest since 2022, tightening the political squeeze ahead of midterm elections.

3. AI-driven earnings are insulating US stocks from the oil shock: the Magnificent Seven (only Nvidia yet to report) delivered robust results, keeping the S&P 500 near all-time highs even as energy costs surge. But the reported strength is partly an accounting artifact — companies buying GPUs and data-center hardware book those outlays as capex, so aggregate reported profits rise while aggregate cash flows are unchanged, as BCA's Peter Berezin explains. The "tell" is that share buybacks have receded to ~4% per annum while operating cash flow runs at ~20% to fund the buildout, compressing dividend and buyback yields to levels last seen at the peak of the dot-com bubble.

4. The equity risk premium has turned negative: subtracting the 10-year Treasury yield from the S&P 500 earnings yield now yields **-88 basis points**, meaning investors are paying a penalty to hold stocks over bonds with no margin for error. Wall Street projects hyperscalers' free cash flow to fall more than 70% from its peak by end of 2026 even as earnings climb, so generous shareholder distributions remain distant. The real tail risk isn't that AI proves to be worthless, but that the economy transitions to AI-empowered operation that no longer requires trillions in ongoing spending — the internet analogy, where data transmission keeps growing but capex eventually plateaus.

**Quotable:**
> "The alarmist view has been proven incorrect. We can muddle through. There's no recession around the corner. And that unfortunately means there's no pressure on either side to come to a deal." — *Marko Papic, BCA Research, on why the Iran oil-supply standoff has no near-term resolution*

### The World: Can Trump strong-arm Iran? 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. Trump has cycled through four escalating tactics — a late-March ultimatum demanding Iran "FULLY OPEN" the Strait of Hormuz under threat of destroying its power plants, a threat to wipe out "a whole civilization," a naval blockade on Iranian shipping, and now a vague plan to escort stranded ships through the strait. None has worked: Iran responded to the escort plan with missiles and drones, and most tankers are still declining to transit. As of early May, roughly 1,600 ships with ~20,000 seafarers remain stranded in the strait.

2. Trump's core bet — that the blockade will quickly force Iran to stop pumping oil, damaging its wells — rests on a miscalculation. Iran exported 1.81 million barrels per day in April, but during Trump's first term it drew production down to ~200,000 barrels/day without significant infrastructure damage. It can also store oil in idle tankers (capacity ~2 million barrels each) and route exports by road and rail to Pakistan. Brett Erickson of Obsidian Risk Advisors says Iran is "not particularly close to even starting" a forced well shutdown; even if the war ended today, normal flows would take "multiple months" to restore.

3. Iran's decision-making is structurally immune to the economic leverage Trump is applying. Suzanne Maloney of the Brookings Institution notes Iran "has withstood more pressure than any other economy in history" without regime collapse or policy concession. Sanam Vakil of Chatham House adds that Iran does not make strategic decisions based on GDP — otherwise it would have cut a deal years ago. The country's authoritarian structure means the domestic political feedback loop that would force compromise in a democracy simply doesn't exist.

4. The asymmetry of stakes runs against the U.S. For Iran, reopening the Strait under coercion would be an existential humiliation — the regime's survival narrative depends on resisting American pressure. For Trump, rising global energy and fertilizer prices are already felt by American consumers, and he faces midterm elections in the fall. Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group captures the pattern: each time pressure fails, Trump reaches for "a new tool of coercion which he believed would magically conjure victory," convinced he is "one little turn of the screw away" — a cycle that has yet to break Iran's position.

**Quotable:**
> "They don't make decisions based on their G.D.P., because if so, they would have done a deal years ago." — *Sanam Vakil, director of the Middle East and North Africa program at Chatham House, on why economic pressure alone cannot force Iran's hand*
