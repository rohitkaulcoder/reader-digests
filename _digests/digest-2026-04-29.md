## Today's Top 3

### An Interview with OpenAI CEO Sam Altman and AWS CEO Matt Garman About Bedrock Managed Agents ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 38 mins

1. Microsoft amended its agreement with OpenAI on April 27, 2026, ending Azure's exclusivity on OpenAI models. The new terms: Azure remains "primary" but non-exclusive; Microsoft's license runs through 2032 regardless of AGI progress (the AGI clause was scrapped); Microsoft stops paying revenue share to OpenAI; OpenAI continues paying revenue share to Microsoft through 2030 at the same percentage but subject to a total cap; Microsoft remains a major shareholder.

2. Azure's exclusivity was actively damaging Microsoft's investment in OpenAI: enterprises overwhelmingly want AI models on their existing cloud of choice, not a new one. Anthropic had a concrete competitive advantage over OpenAI in enterprise deals precisely because it was multi-cloud. Microsoft traded differentiation for a healthier OpenAI P&L — no longer paying revenue share also improves Azure's own financials.

3. The flagship product is **Bedrock Managed Agents, powered by OpenAI**: OpenAI's frontier models packaged inside a fully AWS-native agent runtime — identity, permissions, state, logging, governance, and deployment — all running within the customer's own VPC. Customer data never leaves AWS; OpenAI does not see it. Support is handled by AWS frontline, with OpenAI as escalation for model-layer bugs.

4. The model and the harness are no longer separable. Altman says that watching Codex he genuinely cannot tell how much credit goes to the model versus the scaffolding around it. Tool-calling began as a bolted-on afterthought and was progressively baked into training; Altman expects model and harness to converge further, and pre-training and post-training to eventually converge as well.

5. Codex launched as a local agent — not cloud — because local gives you security, environment setup, and file access "for free," dramatically lowering activation energy. The long-term destination is cloud-based agents, but local was the practical path to a working product. Garman draws the analogy: every computing era has maintained a client layer; local and cloud will eventually bridge rather than one replacing the other.

6. Agent identity is a fundamental unsolved primitive. Altman poses the open question: should an agent use the employee's own account (flagged as "agent"), a separate account, or something else entirely when, say, 10 agents are operating simultaneously under one person? There is currently no industry-standard construct for this, and Altman estimates there are "50 other things like that" that will have to be reinvented as agents join enterprise workforces.

7. AgentCore is AWS's low-level set of agent primitives — memory, safe execution environments, permissioning — available today with any model, including OpenAI via direct API call. Bedrock Managed Agents is a higher-level managed product co-built by AWS and OpenAI engineering teams on top of AgentCore, bundling those primitives with OpenAI models into a single integrated experience. AgentCore is the DIY toolkit; Bedrock Managed Agents is the managed service.

8. Running OpenAI models on AWS will use a mix of Trainium (AWS's custom AI chip) and Nvidia GPUs, shifting toward more Trainium over time. Altman says OpenAI is "quite excited" to run models on Trainium. Garman notes the naming confusion — Trainium handles both training and inference — but argues that virtually all customers interact with chips only through abstractions: they're talking to a model interface, never the silicon.

9. Token-based pricing is obsolete. GPT-5.5 costs more per token than GPT-5.4 but requires far fewer tokens to complete the same task, meaning the per-token metric is misleading. Altman reframes OpenAI as an "intelligence factory": customers want the best unit of intelligence at the lowest price with unlimited capacity — whether produced by a large model on few tokens, a small model on many, Trainium, GPUs, or anything else is irrelevant to the buyer.

10. Demand is currently concentrated at the absolute frontier, which surprised Altman. More customers are telling OpenAI "give me more capacity at any price" than are negotiating on price. This mirrors the trajectory of compute: cost per cycle has dropped orders of magnitude over 30 years, yet more compute is sold today than ever before in absolute terms.

11. Intelligence demand may be the first genuinely uncapped utility. Unlike water (marginal utility drops quickly) or electricity (moderate elasticity), Altman argues there is no natural saturation point for intelligence: "I just want more, I'll just use more as long as the price is low enough." Garman draws the parallel to compute cycles, which went from astronomically expensive in 1986 to essentially free — and demand never stopped growing.

12. Enterprises are converging on a remarkably consistent wishlist: an agent runtime environment, a management layer connecting their data to agents, token-spend oversight, and a workspace product for employees (like Codex). This package is not yet fully built by anyone. Thompson posits a two-tier architecture — a lower "data spelunking" agent layer that continuously indexes SaaS and database sources, topped by a user-facing agent layer.

13. That two-tier architecture may be temporary. As models become powerful enough to hold more context and execute more autonomously, Altman expects a full re-architecture: "At some point, you may say, 'Actually, we have such incredible capabilities, let's re-architect the whole thing.'" The current structure reflects today's model capability ceiling, not a durable design.

14. AWS deliberately chose neutrality over vertical integration — the opposite of Google Cloud's "fully integrated stack" approach (chip → model → agent layer, as described by Thomas Kurian). Garman: "We view our success as if the partners are successful and they're building on top of us." AWS owns the lower-layer infrastructure (there is "one S3"), but moves up the stack by partnering rather than building first-party everything.

15. The most common startup stack today is AWS for infrastructure + OpenAI API for AI, making Bedrock Managed Agents a natural consolidation play. Garman confirms this pattern is widespread. AWS maintains startup dominance through scale, reliability, partner ecosystem, credits, and direct advisory relationships — Garman personally visits Silicon Valley once a quarter to meet founders.

16. Altman identifies four great startup-enabling platform shifts: Internet, cloud, mobile, AI. YC launched in 2005, AWS in 2006 — the two were "incredibly hand-in-hand." Cloud made YC's model viable: before AWS, "the server costs more than" YC's few-tens-of-thousands-of-dollars seed checks. AI startups today are scaling revenue within a single batch cycle in ways that never happened before, with Altman noting YC's expected revenue benchmarks are "changing every month."

17. Current agent UX is still in a "Homebrew Computer Club" era. Altman says that watching users interact with ChatGPT — copy-pasting between windows, managing complex prompt chains — makes him "want to pull my hair out at how much pain and suffering they're going through to get anything to work at all." He views this as fixable and imminent, not a permanent condition.

18. ChatGPT is "the first really large-scale new consumer product since Facebook." The Microsoft/Bing collaboration from the 2023 interview did not reshape search as Altman anticipated, but ChatGPT vastly exceeded his expectations as a standalone consumer product. OpenAI's API and Codex in particular have also grown significantly, though those weren't what Altman was envisioning in early 2023.

19. The AWS-OpenAI partnership is exclusive for this managed agent product — not just technically (it uses AWS-native APIs) but by explicit intent: Altman says "spiritually, we want to do this as a joint effort between our companies." Garman declines to confirm whether AWS will offer similar managed agent products for other model providers, saying only "the fullness of time is a long time."

**Quotable:**
> "If you think about intelligence as a utility, there's no other utility I know of that I'm just like, 'I just want more, I'll just use more as long as the price is low enough, I'll just use more.'" — *Sam Altman, on why demand for AI is structurally unlike any prior utility*

### Money Stuff: The Robots Make the Predictions ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg)** · rss · 16 mins

1. **Polymarket is a negative-sum game dominated by automated bots.** A Bloomberg analysis of every Polymarket wallet active since January 2025 found over 100,000 accounts lost at least $1,000 — nearly twice the number that made that much. A tiny slice of what appear to be automated, high-frequency trading bots captured the majority of winning-side profits, while everyone else collectively lost $131 million. Unlike stocks (where retail investors lose to HFT firms *and still* make money via economic growth), prediction markets have no positive-sum layer — every dollar won is a dollar lost by someone else.

2. **Individual hobbyists, not just institutions, are building these bots.** A computational hydrologist named Darri Eythorsson described building an automated prediction-market trading system in six days using Claude Code — "I described what I wanted in plain language; the AI wrote the code." Thousands of people are independently deploying such agents across the same markets, with no reporting obligations, no compliance departments, no capital requirements, and no circuit breakers. These amateur bots are entirely invisible to existing monitoring frameworks.

3. **AI-homogenized retail behavior creates a new kind of systemic correlation risk.** When retail investors increasingly delegate decisions to the same small family of frontier AI models (Claude, GPT, etc.), market behavior converges on those models' biases and blind spots. If every agentic retail system is trained to "buy the dip," hedge funds can anticipate billions in predictable agentic demand after any 2%+ drop. This is a qualitatively new form of concentration risk that nobody designed and nobody governs.

4. **Boaz Weinstein's tender offer for OBDC II at 65% of NAV attracted less than 1% of shareholders.** Blue Owl Capital Corp. II, a non-traded BDC that halted normal redemptions, was offered a buyout at a steep discount by Saba Capital and Cox Capital Partners. The near-total rejection reflects both a price gap (Blue Owl's public BDC, OBDC, trades at ~76% of its $14.81 NAV) and a structural psychology: non-traded BDC investors were sold an instrument that "doesn't fluctuate," so they prefer waiting years for 100 cents on the dollar over taking 65 cents now.

5. **The non-traded BDC structure insulates investors from market reality in ways that cut both ways.** Non-traded BDC holders never see a quoted market price below NAV, so discount is psychologically invisible to them. When OBDC II itself tried to merge into the publicly traded OBDC last year, shareholders revolted rather than immediately realize a 20%+ discount — even though OBDC shares are freely tradable. Weinstein's firm Saba is now expanding bids targeting 30–40%+ discounts across public and private BDCs and interval funds.

6. **Rivian replaced its worthless 2021 CEO compensation package with a fresh moonshot grant.** Scaringe's 2021 options (exercise price $21.72, targets at $110–$295) were cancelled because the goals were "unlikely to be attained" — the stock now trades at $16.72, down from a post-IPO peak of $172. The new package grants 36.5 million options worth up to $4.6 billion if targets from $40 (roughly half the IPO price) to $140 are hit over the next decade. The accounting treatment makes his 2025 "pay" appear as $402.6 million, largely a paper figure from the new grant's valuation.

7. **Repricing underwater executive options solves a motivation problem but creates a commitment problem.** If the market expects that missing targets leads to easier targets, the original performance incentive dissolves. Rivian's board acknowledged this tension by framing the reset as a one-time fix for goals that were "never realistic," but the new lowest target of $40 — barely above where the stock got to briefly post-IPO — still represents a 2.4× return from today's price, a more achievable moonshot by design.

8. **World Liberty Financial used retroactively imposed KYC requirements to freeze Justin Sun's token unlock.** Sun invested in WLF tokens in late 2024 when KYC verification was loose; when 20% unlocks were announced in August 2025, his tokens were blocked. WLF then published a new "Token Unlock Agreement" granting itself power to "in its sole discretion, decline to unlock, restrict access to, or freeze any wallet" — terms not in the original purchase agreement. Sun's lawsuit argues this is post-hoc rulemaking; this fits a pattern where scruffy crypto projects raise money anonymously, then use respectability requirements to selectively withhold returns.

**Quotable:**
> "The barrier that once separated retail hobbyists from institutional-grade trading infrastructure has collapsed. The dynamics I am describing scale directly — as will the risks on a global scale." — *Darri Eythorsson, computational hydrologist who built an automated prediction-market trading bot in six days using Claude Code*

### What a Disney Vacation Taught me About Pricing and Packaging ⚡
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 9 mins

1. Disney prices a 3-day ticket lower per day than a 2-day ticket — and sometimes cheaper in absolute dollars — because the ticket is essentially a loss leader. That third day means another hotel night, two more in-park meals, more souvenirs, and another round of $6 water bottles in 94-degree Orlando heat. The model is seat-based plus consumption: discount the seat to maximize everything spent inside the ecosystem.

2. The $42 MagicBand is functionally Apple Pay on your wrist — it removes friction from every purchase decision for three days, and people spend more when payment is invisible (Amazon built the same insight into one-click checkout in 1997). The extra wrinkle is that people actually *want* to wear it; kids refused to take theirs off four days after returning home, which means the $42 feels like a souvenir rather than a toll. Consumption-based models live or die on how easy it is to consume.

3. Lightning Lane (skip-the-line) uses dynamic pricing rather than a fixed price: ~$300 per ticket on peak days, ~$150 on off-peak. Disney nudges anxious planners to lock in seven days early at a "better price," but day-of buyers often get cheaper inventory at park entry — both segments feel like they won. The architecture captures maximum willingness-to-pay across buyer types without either group feeling gouged.

4. Kids under 3 eat free at buffets, but the character breakfast runs $65 per adult — a ~$200 check for a family, even after the free kids' meals. Free tiers only work if the paid side is priced for the full value of the moment, not the cost of the food. Meanwhile, Disney's "jumbo" pizza is a normal large pie cut into 4 slices instead of 8, sold at $10–$12 per slice ($42 for the whole thing) — and tourists walked the boardwalk bragging about the enormous slices. Repackaging something you already offer, while doing less work, is a legitimate pricing lever.

5. Every major ride deposits you directly into a themed gift shop at peak emotional high — Pirates of the Caribbean swords sold at a ~3.2x markup over Amazon price, yet families buy them because they're paying to memorialize a moment, not to own a sword. Most companies time upsells to the contract renewal cycle; Disney puts the gift shop right after the ride. The broader moat is the closed ecosystem: no third-party food vendors, no outside Lightning Lane equivalent, even Lyft pickups are Disney-branded — customers have no price-comparison escape valve, and every touchpoint (including an app opened dozens of times per day for directions, food orders, and wait times) doubles as a transaction layer.

**Quotable:**
> "Every company wants pricing power. But they usually think about it as a single product to be sold. Disney does a world better — an ecosystem where the customer never has a chance to price shop. And one where every moment (and purchase) is interconnected to the next." — *closing summary*

### Libraries and Language Culture When AI Writes the Code 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 8 mins

1. The great 2000s programming language wars — C vs. Lisp vs. Python — turned out to be arguing about the wrong thing. Python was nobody's favorite on elegance or performance grounds, but it won because it accumulated better libraries. Reddit's 2005 switch from Lisp to Python was driven not by language quality but by ecosystem depth: Lisp had a library for basically every task, but rarely more than one, and those libraries were poorly documented and lightly used.

2. Python's library dominance traces directly to its origins as a teaching language (descended from ABC), which made it legible to non-programmers — particularly academics who built domain-specific tools. Those tools compounded: `numpy` begat `pandas`, and the growing stack made switching costs prohibitive. The lesson is that language adoption is a network-effects problem, not a language-quality problem.

3. The library-for-everything model embeds a structural vulnerability: a project is only as trustworthy as its least trustworthy import. The XZ utility library attack is the exemplar — one attacker spent two years cultivating trust with the maintainer, became a co-maintainer, then introduced a vulnerability chain that would give full system control. Similar attacks on crypto users are increasingly common because payoff is immediate and easy to monetize.

4. LLMs make this attack surface larger and more dangerous. Vibe-coded apps reflexively import libraries, and models were trained on codebases written when that habit was relatively safe — they carry forward that training bias into a world where it's increasingly unsafe. More code is being written than ever, but a smaller proportion of it is being read; the ratio of unreviewed imports to reviewed ones is rising fast.

5. LLMs also offer a partial escape hatch: you can ask an LLM to reimplement a suspicious library's specific function rather than importing it. The catch is that the most-used, hardest-to-socially-engineer libraries are the heavily maintained ones — the same ones that are worth attacking. Anthropic's Mythos project (proactively patching known software vulnerabilities) is a direct response: exploits chain together, so breaking one link before it's weaponized reduces the blast radius.

**Quotable:**
> "There just aren't as many shoulders on which to stand." — *Reddit's engineering team, explaining the 2005 switch from Lisp to Python*

### The power grid is breaking. Can it fix itself? 📌
**Big Think** · email · 9 mins

1. **The December 2025 Colorado outage illustrates the grid's core fragility.** Xcel Energy deliberately cut power to nearly 50,000 people along the Front Range for multiple days after wind gusts exceeded 100 mph — a precaution justified by the 2021 Marshall Fire, which a similar windstorm sparked and which destroyed 1,084 homes. A grid that must be preemptively shut down for days to prevent catastrophe is not a resilient grid; it's one that trades one type of disaster for another.

2. **Demand is exploding while aging infrastructure creaks under the load.** U.S. electricity demand grew 2.1% in 2025 and is projected to grow ~2% annually through 2030, with roughly half of new demand expected from data centers alone. Yet over 10,000 generation projects were already stuck waiting for grid interconnection at end of 2024, and a DOE study projects transmission capacity must double or triple by 2050. Meanwhile, more than 50% of transformers are over 33 years old and approaching end of life; 70% of transmission lines are 25+ years old.

3. **The renewable transition and cyberattacks add two new stress vectors simultaneously.** Solar and wind introduce variable, hard-to-predict supply — and rooftop solar/batteries are turning the historically one-way grid into a two-way flow that legacy infrastructure wasn't designed to handle. On the digital side, a typical utility experienced more than 1,500 cyberattacks per week in 2024 (per IEA data), on top of physical attacks like Russia's ongoing strikes against Ukraine's energy grid.

4. **Self-healing grids can detect faults and reroute power automatically in seconds, not hours.** Massoud Amin (University of Minnesota, "father of the smart grid") defines them as systems that use distributed sensors, communications, and controls to "prevent small disturbances from turning into large outages." The traditional model requires human operators to diagnose conditions with incomplete information — a process taking minutes to hours. Duke Energy Florida reported that self-healing investments helped avoid 280,000 extended outages and over 300,000 customer-hours of outages in 2025 alone.

5. **Dynamic line ratings can unlock 10–40% more transmission capacity without building new lines.** In March 2026, PJM Interconnection became the first U.S. grid operator to comply with a FERC order updating transmission capacity limits using real-time weather data — hourly instead of static conservative ratings. DOE's Idaho National Laboratory research estimates this approach increases usable capacity by roughly 10–40%, offering a faster near-term capacity gain than permitting and constructing new infrastructure.

6. **AI accelerates operator decision-making but remains a copilot, not autopilot.** NREL published "eGridGPT" in 2024, a generative AI model designed to support control room operators by interpreting grid sensor data in real time. PJM is using AI for interconnection planning; NextEra Energy and Google Cloud announced a collaboration for AI-driven grid optimization. But grid experts unanimously hold that AI will not become the ultimate operator — its primary value is speed, helping humans process the data torrent from modern sensor networks fast enough to act before cascading failures take hold.

**Quotable:**
> "When you consider the complexity of the task of operating an electric grid, the many different crises that can happen, and the paucity of data traditionally available, it's a miracle that the lights stay on at all." — *Alexandra "Sascha" von Meier, former Director of the Electric Grid Research program, California Institute for Energy and Environment*

### The World: Dirty gold 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Gold is now trading near $5,000 an ounce, the peak of a 25-year frenzy driven by media figures like Tucker Carlson (who is now personally selling gold) and institutional buyers — central banks, hedge funds — seeking refuge from war, sanctions, and economic instability. The deep irony: buying gold to escape violence directly funds violence. Gold finances Sudan's civil war, Russia's Ukraine invasion, and helps Venezuela and Iran blunt the effects of financial sanctions.

2. Colombia's largest drug cartel, the Clan del Golfo, has controlled the La Mandinga mine for the past eight years. Every miner on the property pays a cartel "tax" (the cartel's own word, issued in a post-publication statement), and the proceeds fund the cartel's territorial control through murders and bombings. Illegal miners there also use mercury to extract gold, poisoning the surrounding environment.

3. The U.S. Mint is legally required to make its investor-grade coins from U.S.-mined gold only, yet for decades it has processed foreign gold from dubious sources: Mexican and Peruvian pawn shops, a Congolese mine co-owned by the Chinese government, and a Honduran company that excavated an Indigenous graveyard for the ore beneath it. The Mint enables this through a definitional loophole — foreign gold is reclassified as "U.S. gold" so long as the supplier also purchases some American gold.

4. The Royal Canadian Mint, which publicly claims cutting-edge gold-tracing technology, has been caught in the same supply chain — it, too, buys cartel gold. The structural reason neither mint can clean up its supply chain is economic: gold is a low-margin commodity, miners receive roughly 90% of the world spot price, and every link in the chain — Colombian exporter, Texas middleman, refiner — earns only a thin slice. With no pricing power, the only way to make money is volume, so no participant has a financial incentive to reject even suspect ore.

**Quotable:**
> "Wealthy people and governments buy gold to seek refuge from violence and instability — and in doing so, they fuel more violence and more instability." — *Justin Scheck, summing up the central irony of the global gold market*

### Did the UAE just kick off OPEC's farewell tour? 📎
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 7 mins

1. The UAE's exit from OPEC — announced Tuesday, effective May 1 — is categorically different from the four departures that preceded it (Indonesia 2016, Qatar 2019, Ecuador 2020, Angola 2023). Those were peripheral members; the UAE is one of the cartel's largest producers with the geological reserves, sovereign wealth, and capital investment capacity to unilaterally reshape global oil supply. Bloomberg Opinion columnist Javier Blas calls it "the biggest existential crisis the group has faced since its establishment more than half a century ago."

2. The real driver is a decade-long cold war between Abu Dhabi and Riyadh over production strategy, not the ongoing Iran conflict despite the convenient timing. The UAE has consistently pushed to pump more oil even at the cost of lower prices, while Saudi Arabia — allied with Russia — fought to keep prices near $100/barrel by capping output. The Iran war simply gave the UAE political cover to formalize what had been a simmering strategic rupture.

3. With the Strait of Hormuz currently blocked, the global economy is drawing down strategic and commercial oil reserves. When the conflict ends, the UAE will be free of OPEC's production ceiling and positioned to flood the market — but Iranian mines scattered across shipping routes must first be cleared, a task requiring sustained multinational coordination that remains politically fraught.

4. The UAE and Saudi Arabia are now split on both oil policy and war policy: Riyadh wants to prevent further escalation to protect regional stability, while Abu Dhabi is prepared to endure escalation in hopes of forcing regime change in Tehran. The UAE's exit could also trigger contagion — other members watching Abu Dhabi go solo may recalculate their own allegiances, with OPEC's collective discipline eroding member by member.

**Quotable:**
> "Its move to quit OPEC has little to do with the Islamic Republic... the UAE wanted to produce more, even at the risk of lower prices; the Saudis, allied with Russia, wanted to keep oil as close to $100-a-barrel as possible, even if that meant the group had to curb output." — *Javier Blas on the real cause of the UAE's OPEC exit*
