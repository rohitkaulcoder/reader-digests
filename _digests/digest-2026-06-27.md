### America Invents, China Applies ⚡
**'The Generalist' via PubsforSubs** · email · 10 mins

1. China's Ministry of Commerce quietly released a 17-point plan — *Implementation Opinions on Accelerating the Development of 'AI+Consumption'* — that received almost no coverage in Western press. It mandates AI rollout across retail, clinics, classrooms, elder care, tourism, and catering, with specific targets like "tens of millions of households and tens of millions of shops," plus fully integrated AI tourism chains covering itinerary planning, ticketing, hotel booking, and simultaneous translation.

2. The "vehicle-road-cloud integration" plank is a case where central planning holds a structural advantage: full self-driving benefits only fully materialize when every vehicle on every road shares real-time position and routing data. No private-market actor can mandate that — China's political will, state muscle, and vast consumer data banks make it the only plausible entity to pull it off.

3. China's playbook here is proven, not aspirational. Beijing designated EVs and solar as strategic industries and delivered: China now makes 75% of the world's electric cars, holds 80%+ share across every manufacturing stage of solar panels, and sold 90% of humanoid robots shipped globally last year — backed by $230 billion in EV subsidies (2009–2023) and $50 billion in new solar capacity. The AI consumption push follows the same template.

4. Chinese frontier models are competitive on benchmarks and drastically cheaper at the token level. Zhipu's GLM 5.2 — released the same week Trump reportedly pressured Anthropic — costs less than a tenth of Fable 5. American AI-executive-assistant startup Lindy evaluated DeepSeek V4 Flash for inbox management and calendar tasks and found it performed equivalently to Anthropic's model at one-tenth the price, saving the firm millions in months.

5. Three genuine reasons the US may still lead: (a) A Georgia Tech study found DeepSeek used 26.3× more tokens than its OpenAI rival to accomplish equivalent tasks, meaning per-task cost — not per-token price — makes Chinese models *more* expensive on efficiency benchmarks than GPT 5.5 or Opus 4.7. (b) Nvidia chips still sell on China's black market at 2× US retail, signaling domestic chip quality remains insufficient. (c) The Chinese government has never intervened to suppress or restrict its AI labs — unusual given the CCP's ultracautious posture — which may be the most credible signal that Beijing privately believes the US still leads on frontier capability.

6. Google lost ~$240 billion in market cap after Noam Shazeer (co-head of Gemini) departed for OpenAI and Nobel laureate John Jumper (AlphaFold, DeepMind) announced plans to join Anthropic — with Jonas Adler and Alexander Pritzel also heading to Anthropic. The pull factors: Anthropic and OpenAI are pre-IPO, making their equity "ripe," while Google's stock is mature; and access to cutting-edge models is described by insiders as "intoxicating," like "surfers chasing the perfect wave."

7. US data center buildout is bottlenecked not by capital but by hardware: of ~12 gigawatts of capacity promised to come online this year, only ~5 gigawatts are under active construction. The constraint is high-voltage transformers, switchgear, and grid-scale batteries — unglamorous, 12+-month-lead-time hardware — while the four largest tech companies target $700 billion in combined capex this year. Meanwhile, China controls ~70% of global indium supply, the mineral used in the optical transceivers that wire GPU clusters together, and has introduced a permission-by-permission export licensing regime — keeping revenue flowing while retaining the ability to throttle individual customers.

> "The US has no strategy for applying AI. Yes, smart people are working hard to come up with new products people might actually want to use. The US market is great, but is it definitely greater than a China-wide Manhattan Project for getting AI into people's lives? *Definitely*?" — *on the structural asymmetry between US market-driven AI adoption and China's state-directed rollout*

### Clouded Judgement 6.26.26 - Time to Power ⚡
**Clouded Judgement by Jamin Ball** · email · 11 mins

1. "Time to Power" has become the single gating factor for data center financing. Lenders financing billion-dollar data center builds — most heavily debt-financed — don't start the clock on repayment until tokens are being generated and revenue is flowing. No power means no tokens, no revenue, no loan. Customers (neoclouds' offtakers) won't commit capacity agreements to a site they don't believe will actually receive power on a viable timeline. Both sides of the equation — debt and demand — require proof of power before committing a dollar.

2. The US has ~1,200–1,400 GW of installed, grid-connected capacity, but 2,300–2,600 GW is stuck in interconnection queues — nearly two full grids' worth of power waiting to be connected. Only 10–20% of queued capacity historically ever reaches commercial operation; projects drop out when wait times drag on or connection costs balloon. Even in a record year like 2026, roughly 86 GW is being added against a 2,500 GW queue. The problem is not that electrons don't exist — it's that they can't reach where they're needed.

3. The real bottleneck is transmission, not generation. Moving power from a plant to a data hall requires high-voltage transmission lines, substations, and transformers — each a separate multi-year sequential project requiring its own financing and regulatory approval. PJM (the grid operator covering much of the mid-Atlantic) shows projects spending ~3 years just to reach an interconnection agreement, then another ~4 years before coming online. Transformer lead times alone now exceed 160 weeks — meaning ordering the equipment that steps down voltage to usable levels takes over three years.

4. Rack power density is exploding, making the last-mile delivery problem far worse. Power per rack went from ~40 kW in the H100 era to ~120 kW with GB200, and Nvidia's Rubin Ultra racks arriving in 2027 will require ~1 MW per rack. At those densities, a single 1 MW rack fed via the traditional AC-to-DC conversion chain requires ~200 kg of copper busbar; a gigawatt-scale data center would need ~200,000 kg of copper just in rack busbars. Each conversion step — transformers, switchgear, rectifiers, distribution gear — bleeds energy as heat, and at gigawatt scale those losses become a significant share of the total power budget.

5. Nvidia's push to 800V DC distribution is a direct response: convert AC to DC once at the facility perimeter, distribute high-voltage DC across the building, and step down to chip-level voltage (~1V) only inside the rack. This eliminates up to four conversion stages compared to traditional architecture, cuts copper use ~45%, and reduces total cost of ownership by ~30% (Nvidia's figures, directionally credible). When power is the scarce input, efficiency in the last mile becomes a hard competitive edge on both cost and capacity.

6. The most sophisticated hyperscalers — Meta, Amazon, Google, xAI, Crusoe — have concluded the interconnection queue is too broken to wait for, and are going "behind the meter": building their own generation on-site to bypass the grid entirely. This is a meaningful structural signal. The largest and most well-resourced buyers in the market have decided it is faster to become power producers than to remain grid customers. It points to a systemic mismatch: the US electric grid was designed for slow, predictable load growth from many small consumers, not for single offtakers requesting hundreds of megawatts or gigawatts at a time — requests historically the size of entire small cities.

**Quotable:**
> "The winners of this next build won't just be the ones with the most chips or the cheapest capital. They'll be the ones who figured out, early, that the scarcest input was never compute — it was time to power." — *closing argument*

### The frontier fails the Turing test ⚡
**Benn Stancil** · email · 9 mins

1. The general-vs-specialized model taxonomy is dissolving into meaninglessness. Engram just raised $98M to retrain general-purpose models for specific tasks (Notion building custom agents, Harvey internalizing law firm knowledge); Cursor's Composer models were pretrained on the open base model Kimi K2.5 then fine-tuned via reinforcement learning to emulate the Cursor environment. These are neither purely general nor purely specialized — they're "general, ish," and the category boundary is now a spectrum, not a line.

2. Claude used to have a distinct human personality — warm, whimsical, capable of genuine prose — while ChatGPT was clinical and direct. The early tells of AI writing (the "delves," em-dashes, relentless bullet points) were obvious but benign. Recent Claude has developed entirely different tells: dense, jargon-heavy outputs full of "substrates," "nodes and edges," "orthogonal axes," abrupt pivots framed as revelations, and conclusions buried in abstraction. To demonstrate, Stancil fed Claude his own draft and inserted the generated next paragraph — it is genuinely incomprehensible, rhyming-sounding nonsense that mistakes the appearance of depth for depth itself.

3. The most plausible explanation for this shift: Anthropic has optimized Claude to be an elite software engineer — both for economic reasons (Claude Code generates enormous revenue) and strategic ones (Anthropic frames coding as the path to superintelligence). As Claude got better at talking to computers, it lost the weights that helped it talk to people. It can now solve any math problem and autonomously hack systems, but has become incapable of explaining what it did in plain language.

4. Borges' *Library of Babel* is the controlling metaphor: a near-infinite library mathematically guaranteed to contain all possible books, including "the true story of your death" and the solution to every human problem — but hopelessly unsearchable. Humanity's initial "unbounded joy" collapsed into chaos and madness, because knowing the answer exists somewhere in infinite noise is almost worse than not knowing at all. Claude's matrices contain the entire written universe; the question of whether any given cryptic output is "wondrous arcana or just a random string of statistical exhaust" is genuinely unanswerable.

5. Frontier models may now be inverting the Turing test: they can *do* everything but can no longer *pass as human* in conversation. This might actually be fine — the anthropomorphized chatbot interface was a historical accident, and AI that does more while presenting less like a person would solve real harms. But the AI industry's pivot toward enterprise and engineering buyers is also producing a new self-corruption dynamic. Early fears about AI manipulation mirrored fears about social media being hijacked by Cambridge Analytica or Russia — but social media didn't need bad actors; it became self-corrupting by design. AI optimized for engineers isn't creating sycophants; it's creating bombastic thought leaders who can't speak straight. The new problem isn't manipulation — it's learning which thoughts are worth untangling and which are riddles with no exits.

**Quotable:**
> "It is language with the punch of authority and the gloss of confident dissent, though you can never quite be sure if it does or doesn't agree with you, because it is also completely and utterly incomprehensible." — *describing the new Claude writing style*

### An Interview with Figma CEO Dylan Field About Design and AI 📌
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 36 mins

1. Figma's founding insight in late 2011 was that WebGL was far more capable than anyone recognized — the only other company that seemed to grasp it was Onshape. Co-founder Evan Wallace proved this out technically, and Field's design internship at Flipboard provided the "nail" for the WebGL "hammer": collaborative browser-based design software.

2. Real-time multiplayer collaboration was planned from day zero but nearly every user said they didn't want it — designers of the era had an agency-culture identity built around the grand reveal, showing polished work in meetings rather than working in the open. Figma shipped without multiplayer in its closed beta but realized before the 2016 launch that operating without it was intolerable: if two people were in the same doc and one made a change, the other's browser force-reloaded.

3. Figma's early customers included Coda (Shishir Mehrotra) and Notion — cloud-native, minimalist companies that matched Figma's ethos — and early differentiation came from superior vector tools and design systems with a unique component approach, not just collaboration. FigJam (whiteboarding/diagramming) was always on Field's roadmap but the team resisted until the pandemic, when the user base wrote in en masse demanding it.

4. The $20 billion Adobe acquisition (announced September 2022) collapsed after 16 months of regulatory resistance, a period Field describes as "an eternity." AI uncertainty was a genuine factor in his willingness to sell: pre-ChatGPT, he couldn't confidently model whether AI would be 1/10th or 100x for the business, making a certain large number attractive against an uncertain future.

5. Adobe's strategic problem with design was structural, not circumstantial: they acquired Macromedia and got Fireworks (the real predecessor to Sketch and Figma), but it was never a focus — their core was "creativity," while Figma's core was "design," and Field argues those were genuinely different things in 2022. Adobe's "add, add, add" legacy software culture was the opposite of rethinking from first principles.

6. Field draws a firm line between art and design: art communicates an emotion or idea to affect an audience (not necessarily problem-solving), while design is where problem-solving meets creativity. By 2026, he says design, creativity, media, advertising, and brand have merged into a single domain — the attention economy forces differentiated visual voice, and design is the differentiator in software.

7. Figma's stock went from a post-IPO peak valuation of $56.3 billion to below $10 billion — less than half of Adobe's offer — on a market narrative of "AI losers." Field treats this as an incorrect narrative rather than a verdict: he had Figma's biggest earnings beat last quarter (Q1 2026) and believes the AI-winner/loser binary is too blunt a frame for evaluating software companies.

8. AI is a tailwind for Figma via a data flywheel: the more activity users do in Figma, the better Figma can understand their needs (with permission) and serve them. Field is deliberately vague on specifics ("it's strategy"), but the mechanism is: usage data → contextual capability improvements → better outcomes → more usage. He frames data liquidity and equity as the key AI-era moat.

9. Figma keeps its AI model stack swappable by design, using a mix of open-weights models, proprietary small models, and third-party APIs. Field mentions Sakana AI's release the night before the interview as an example of the discontinuous, unpredictable pace of model development that makes lock-in dangerous. He sees local inference as a significant future story as open-weights quality rises.

10. Anthropic's launch of Claude Design created a conflict of interest: Anthropic's CPO sat on Figma's board and then left after reports he would offer a competing product. Field's response — "it's complicated" — is the most pointed thing he says in the interview.

11. "Code on the Canvas" (announced at Config 2026) treats code and design as complementary materials on the same surface, not competitors. Vectors and images offer direct manipulation and precise control; code offers full fidelity, state, logic, and production accuracy. The product lets users move between the two — pull design into code, make changes in code, reconcile them back to design — alongside new capabilities like Motion (timeline-based animation from an acquisition of Modyfi) and shader fills/effects that expose WebGL's underlying power to end users for the first time.

12. Figma's acquisition of Weavy (now rebranded Weave) is part of the Canvas expansion, connecting Figma more directly to codebases. "Make Local" lets users connect Figma Make to local codebases; an MCP integration allows pulling from Figma via external tools. The trajectory is toward Figma as the full-stack surface from ideation through production.

13. AI draws from the center of the statistical distribution of existing work; genuinely differentiated output is by definition out-of-distribution. This means teams using AI face a structural pull toward safe, consensus-driven work — the same dynamic Field attributes to committee-designed outputs in the pre-Figma agency era. The design teams that will win are those using AI as a tool they direct rather than a direction they follow.

14. Individual AI tools are creating a "tunnel vision" crisis in design teams: companies go through three adoption phases — "let's use AI," then token-maxxing leaderboards with extreme behavior, then enforced token budgets — and after phase two, designers become viscerally attached to their individual AI-generated directions. Bringing five such designers into a collaborative setting produces "design gridlock" rather than synthesis.

15. AI collaboration remains fundamentally single-player: LLMs are one-on-one experiences, and the unsolved problem for teams is divergence (generating many directions collectively) as much as convergence. Field sees this gap as a structural tailwind for Figma's canvas-as-shared-space model, which enables both.

16. Non-verifiability is a moat: the hardest problems in design — taste, aesthetics, user experience, cultural fit — cannot be evaluated with standard evals. Field's formulation: "the word of the year is evals, evals, evals — but how do you write the right evals for non-verifiability?" LLMs currently underperform on aesthetics, and the ambiguity that makes design hard to automate is also what makes it durable.

17. Field personally "vibe-maths" — uses AI to explore mathematical domains he never fully mastered in school — and treats it as a deliberate practice of empathy for vibe-coders, as well as discovery. His personal exploration of prompting strategies that elicit broader model outputs (which he deliberately doesn't call "jailbreaking") feeds back into Figma's product thinking.

18. Field sees Figma's independence post-Adobe as essential right now: the speed of the current AI environment requires organizations that can pivot quickly and update priors constantly, the opposite of the large-company integration that an Adobe acquisition would have required. Config 2026 drew 10,000 designers in person.

**Quotable:**
> "AI is trained on the distribution, and the most interesting, differentiated work will be out of distribution by definition." — *Field on why AI creates more need for human creative direction, not less*

### Court Philosophers 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. Philosophy and math are uniquely hard because they're uniquely easy — anyone who can count and ask recursive questions has started doing both. This means practitioners compete with thousands of years of precedent, and a serious burden-of-knowledge problem eats up peak fluid intelligence just reaching the frontier. The fields have consequently started selecting for conscientiousness as much as raw brilliance: someone too brilliant to read Hegel risks accidentally reinventing Hegel.

2. AI labs are hiring full-time philosophers, per a recent *Economist* piece. Philosophy majors have surprisingly strong credentials at the tail: Thiel, Karp, and Soros (whose tens of billions in returns were partly a procrastination project on a thesis extending Karl Popper) all have philosophical roots; philosophy GRE scores rank in the top third for quantitative reasoning. Median earnings aren't great, but this is a major that selects heavily at the extremes.

3. AI labs are currently functioning like early-stage consulting or investment banking — employers of generically smart people who haven't yet picked a lane. That arbitrage historically closes: either selection processes get brutal or comp comes with punishing hours. The difference at AI labs is the genuine breadth of new use cases keeps generating novel special projects and research opportunities that absorb generalist intelligence productively.

4. The practical reason labs need philosophers is that baking ethics into a model is hard in exactly the way founding a religion is hard. Every attempt to define a new moral system — or rigorously describe an existing one — tends to fail or fragment. Even the LDS church, organized relatively recently, produced a century-old splinter group over polygamy. Leibniz and Galileo held court-philosopher jobs for the Hanoverians and Medici; AI labs are just the current patron class.

5. AI companies are now effectively setting universal moral rules for billions of people — users ask models relationship questions, life decisions, and ethical dilemmas. Reasoning models make this worse: even a slight moral preference compounds across a long chain of thought. A model that's even a little bit vegan will, after enough reasoning steps, conclude dinner is a toss-up between tempeh and seitan. Labs need someone who can argue the case for building tools that let users do some bad things — not because philosophers are more moral, but because they've watched confident ethical arguments get thoroughly demolished.

6. *Elsewhere briefs:* MicroStrategy's STRC preferred stock (designed to hold $100/share, currently at $76 with an $11.50 dividend) mirrors a currency peg — like the Bank of England in 1992 or Indonesia in 1998, there's a rate where raising it further signals you're about to stop, forcing a binary outcome between recovery and collapse. AI companies being overcharged on compute actually implies real ROI is even higher than reported benchmarks, since the denominator is inflated. OpenAI may push its IPO to 2027, partly to report Q3/Q4 numbers when it can plausibly reclaim the #1-lab slot. Hedge funds are absorbing more of the treasury-bond/futures basis trade as the most balance-sheet-efficient holders. California shipped a job-loss tracking dashboard for AI-exposed sectors — the kind of tool nobody would have bothered building before Codex could do it over lunch.

**Quotable:**
> "If your model is even a little bit vegan, that tendency will compound over the course of a long reasoning session and end up telling you that dinner is a toss-up between tempeh and seitan." — *on how reasoning models amplify even slight embedded moral preferences*

### Money Stuff: There's a SpaceX Treasury Company 📌
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg)** · rss · 14 mins

1. Triller (Nasdaq: ILLR), a social-media company with ~$15M market cap that lost $174.5M on just $21.6M revenue last year, announced it is acquiring exposure to 3,917,185 SpaceX shares via a four-layer SPV structure: Triller → a wholly-owned SPV subsidiary → SAC1 (a Bahamian vehicle) → Fortune Offshore Fund – Gigafund → SpaceX. The price is $411M (~$105/share), a discount to SpaceX's current ~$155 market price because the pre-IPO shares are locked up and illiquid. Triller's stock briefly spiked from $0.77 to $5.30 on the announcement before settling around $3.18.

2. The "digital asset treasury company" (DAT) playbook — raise cash, buy crypto, watch the stock trade at a 2x premium to NAV because retail can't easily access the underlying — is now dead. Many DATs trade below NAV today, and investors have lost significant money. The SpaceX version is doubly late: SpaceX completed its IPO this month, meaning any retail investor can buy SPCX directly at market price on the same exchange as ILLR. There is no scarcity premium left to capture.

3. Triller has roughly $2M in cash but is paying $411M for the position, meaning it is borrowing essentially 100% of the purchase price with the SpaceX shares as collateral. The deal language — "the Company retains a *portion* of the position as treasury" — strongly implies the lender receives most of the upside (via a funded collar, variable-share forward, or warrants) in exchange for financing the full purchase. Triller may effectively be renting SpaceX exposure rather than owning it outright.

4. AI is replicating for boutique hedge funds what Lotus 1-2-3 did for LBO firms in the 1980s: KKR's Donald Herdrich bought an Apple IIe in 1980 and found he could "pick apart" giant companies' finances in an afternoon instead of weeks — a "$99 LBO software package" democratized analysis that had previously required armies of analysts. Now, five-person Palinuro Capital (launched 2025 by Alfonso Peccatiello) uses LLMs to parse central bank speeches from Hungary to South Korea in real time, work that previously demanded regional specialists, competing against desks of 20–50 people.

5. On a potential SpaceX-Tesla merger: Tesla shareholders face a meaningfully lower litigation bar than SpaceX shareholders. Any Tesla shareholder — even a single-share holder — can sue for being bought out at too low a price (direct harm, no ownership threshold). SpaceX shareholders would need to own 3% of the company to bring a derivative suit — a stake worth over $52 billion at current prices. Tesla also hasn't pre-emptively barred class actions the way SpaceX has, creating substantially more litigation risk on the Tesla side of any deal.

6. Non-traded BDCs and interval funds offering quarterly liquidity may paradoxically face *more* redemption pressure than daily-liquidity mutual funds. When investors can exit any business day, they tend not to — "I can always leave tomorrow." When exit windows are only quarterly, investors may preemptively redeem during uncertainty rather than risk being locked in for three more months. JPMorgan won SEC approval to offer *monthly* (instead of quarterly) redemptions on a new private credit interval fund, arguing shorter windows would actually reduce the urgency to redeem.

7. Legitimate passive income exists and it looks nothing like Instagram. Greg Keogh, a mechanical engineer, designed a lint roller nearly as wide as a paper-towel roll after a chance conversation with a dog owner, listed it on Amazon, and now personally nets $50,000–$115,000/year working two hours or less per month — seven years after the initial design. The model is: do something genuinely useful once, then collect perpetual modest returns. The Instagram version — courses teaching people to sell courses — produces no such thing.

> "The personal computer accomplished for Wall Street's buyout boutiques what the advent of gunpowder did for Mongol warriors." — *George Anders, describing KKR's adoption of VisiCalc in 1980, quoted in a 2020 IEEE paper on Michael Milken's spreadsheets*

### Going Private: Credit fund stress rises as PIK loans swell 📌
**Bloomberg** · email · 10 mins

1. Non-traded business development companies (BDCs) appear to have survived the 2025 redemption wave — exit caps are in place and cash is trickling back — but the underlying rot is deepening. Fitch recorded a new high of 6% US private credit defaults in April, and funds heavily exposed to software borrowers are using Payment in Kind (PIK) loans to convert interest payments into new debt, deferring the reckoning rather than resolving it. Len Tannenbaum (Tannenbaum Capital) warns the severity of any eventual blow-up depends on how leveraged individual funds are and "how long they've swept things under the rug."

2. PIK is a structural camouflage mechanism, not a workout tool: it lets distressed borrowers roll unpaid interest into the principal, compounding the pile while keeping the loan technically current. Retired Wall Street risk expert Victor Hong coined the acronym POOP — "Principal On Outstanding Principal" — to describe the dynamic. The problem surfaces only when liquidity dries up, at which point multiple quarters of deferred interest all crystallize simultaneously against an inflated principal balance.

3. Redemption pressure is accelerating industry-wide, forcing the biggest managers to cap withdrawals. Apollo, BlackRock, Morgan Stanley, and Blackstone all hit their 5% quarterly redemption thresholds this quarter. Ares capped its $22.6 billion Strategic Income Fund on Thursday after 14.4% of investors sought to exit — up from 11.6% in Q1. Simultaneously, advisors are steering clients toward listed BDC siblings trading at a median 24-27% discount to NAV, calling it a "no-brainer" arbitrage when the same manager runs both vehicles.

4. London hedge fund manager Lee Robinson — who turned a $20 million position into $200 million betting against US subprime mortgages in 2008 — is now amassing credit default swap positions against insurers exposed to private credit, including Lincoln National, MetLife, and Berkshire Hathaway. Net notional CDS bets on US insurers rose to $5.6 billion by May 22 from under $4.9 billion at year-end 2023. The trigger Robinson is watching for: "any single blow-up" at one stressed insurer that cascades through the sector.

5. Insurer exposure to private credit has grown fast enough to alarm regulators. Moody's found that a fifth of US life insurers' $4 trillion fixed-income portfolios sat in illiquid assets (mostly private credit) at end-2025, up from 18% the year before — with the increase particularly concentrated at insurers owned by private equity giants like KKR and Apollo. The Bank of France warned that opacity in private credit structured products could breach investor trust in a manner analogous to 2008, and JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs are now selling protection products against insurance-sector risks.

**Quotable:**
> "PIK is POOP — Principal On Outstanding Principal — and it doesn't matter how much, POOP smells. That pile of stuff builds up and when liquidity is draining, it all shows up." — *Len Tannenbaum, Tannenbaum Capital founder, on Payment in Kind loans masking credit distress*

### Why do we divide politics into left vs. right? Blame France. 📎
**Big Think** · email · 8 mins

1. On September 11, 1789, France's National Assembly voted 673 to 325 on whether to grant Louis XVI a veto over its laws. Supporters of the monarchy clustered to the chairman's right; radicals who wanted to curb royal power drifted left. That accidental seating arrangement gave the world its most durable political shorthand — one that spread from France to virtually every democracy within a century.

2. The terminology carries a structural bias that predates the Revolution by millennia. In Latin, *sinister* means left and is the root of all things foreboding; *dexter* (right) gives us "dexterity." French encodes the same slant: *gauche* means clumsy, while *droit* means both "right" and "correct." Since roughly 90% of people are right-handed, the right side has always carried positive cultural connotations — a free propaganda advantage for conservatives.

3. In 1972, French political scientist Jean-Pierre Faye coined the "political horseshoe" after observing that Communists and Nazis frequently collaborated in early-1930s Weimar Germany to undermine the liberal center. His thesis: the farther you travel from the center in either direction, the closer the two extremes bend toward each other — forming a horseshoe rather than a straight line. This helps explain why many 2016 Bernie Sanders supporters voted for Donald Trump: both campaigns voiced profound anti-establishment distrust that the linear model can't represent.

4. David Nolan, founder of the U.S. Libertarian Party, published the Nolan Chart in 1971 to fix the one-dimensional model's core flaw. He split political space into two independent axes — control over economic decisions and control over personal life — generating four quadrants. This captures distinctions the left-right line cannot: Nordic social democracies (socialist + free), the Soviet Union (socialist + unfree), Singapore (capitalist + free), and Pinochet's Chile (capitalist + unfree) are four genuinely different regimes that collapse into two positions on a simple spectrum.

5. Later two-dimensional maps (notably an internet-circulated "political diamond" from around 2000) add labels like Anarchism and Fascism at the authoritarian poles, and illuminate real distinctions — George Orwell fought *with* anarchist militias *against* Moscow-aligned communists in the Spanish Civil War, a nuance invisible on the left-right line. But each map embeds its maker's bias: Nolan placed libertarianism at the apex; the diamond soft-pedals anarchism's internal contradictions; all are Western frameworks with no space for Confucian governance, Islamic political theory, or Ubuntu. Ronald Inglehart's GAL-TAN axis (Green/Alternative/Libertarian vs. Traditional/Authoritarian/Nationalist) and his materialist-to-post-materialist value shift add a third dimension, but complexity is the enemy of the original model's one genuine virtue: simplicity.

**Quotable:**
> "The map is not the territory. As political charts grow ever more elaborate in the pursuit of completeness, they trade away the one thing that made the original so durable: simplicity." — *closing reflection on why left vs. right persists despite its flaws*

### Once upon a time, Indian soldiers dominated China 📎
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 7 mins

1. When Pete Hegseth announced the US would drop "Indo-" from "Indo-Pacific Command" and revert to the old "US Pacific Command" (PACOM), it sent a symbolic shockwave to New Delhi. The "Indo-Pacific" name was added in 2018 with deliberate fanfare as a signal that a newly aggressive China would be contained by multiple countries under US leadership — with India as a named partner. Dropping the prefix now reads, in the words of Admiral James Stavridis (former NATO supreme commander), as "a direct shot at India."

2. India's anxiety is grounded in a painful recent memory: China-India fought a month-long war in 1962 that ended with Beijing occupying more than 17% of Jammu and Kashmir — land China still holds today. This loss shapes how India frames any shift in US alliance posture, because the possibility of a future confrontation with China is not theoretical, it is territorial and ongoing.

3. The 1962 defeat obscures a longer, reversed history: Indian soldiers — recruited by British India — were instrumental in humiliating China for nearly half a century. In 1860, during the Second Opium War, thousands of British Indian troops under James Bruce (the 8th Earl of Elgin, notorious for taking the Parthenon marbles) defeated the Qing dynasty, drove the emperor inland, and burned the Old Summer Palace. In 1900, British Indian soldiers were again decisive in crushing the Boxer Rebellion, which had besieged foreign diplomatic compounds in Beijing.

4. British India's role went beyond battlefield victories: it cultivated and processed the opium that, exported to China, catastrophically hollowed out the Qing economy and government — a form of economic warfare that predated conventional military campaigns. China's current triumphalism after "cleverly weathering the Iran war" (Karishma Vaswani's phrase) suggests Beijing may have forgotten this history of vulnerability to Indian-led pressure — the kind of historical amnesia that tends to repeat.

**Quotable:**
> "The switch to 'Indo-Pacific' in 2018 was done with great fanfare and touted as a new chapter in US-Indian relations — reflected thinking that a newly aggressive China would have to be faced on multiple fronts and by multiple countries, bound together by US leadership." — *Mihir Sharma on what the name change symbolized*
