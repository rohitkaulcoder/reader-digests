## Today's Top 3

### Money Stuff: The SEC Can't Find Victims ⚡
**Bloomberg** · rss · 15 mins

1. **Elon Musk broke a clear disclosure rule and made $143 million doing it.** US securities law required him to disclose his Twitter stake within 10 days of crossing 5% (hit on March 14, 2022; deadline March 24). He filed 11 days late on April 4, meanwhile buying ~13 million additional shares at ~$39 each. When he disclosed, the stock jumped to ~$50. The math: he saved roughly $143 million (the SEC later said "at least $150 million") by secretly accumulating shares before the price reflected his interest. The SEC sued in 2025 to claw it back.

2. **The core problem: disgorgement requires victims, but stock market fraud has no identifiable victims.** Musk bought mostly from market makers — electronic trading firms that themselves bought the stock seconds earlier at ~$39 from someone else. Those intermediaries didn't "lose" $11/share in any meaningful sense; they just passed shares through. The actual sellers who might have held and benefited from a timely disclosure are untraceable, and even if found, nobody forced them to sell — they might have sold regardless for unrelated reasons (tuition bills, etc.).

3. **The same structural problem kills pump-and-dump enforcement.** In the Sripetch/Stockpalooza.com case, operators promoted at least 20 penny stocks and dumped before prices collapsed, generating $6.6 million in illicit profits. A Texas judge initially threw out charges for lacking identifiable victims; the case was overturned on appeal. Now Sripetch is before the Supreme Court arguing the 2020 ruling requiring disgorgement be "awarded for victims" means the SEC must show quantifiable pecuniary harm to specific people — not just that the fraudster profited.

4. **The SEC is sitting on $5 billion in unclaimed disgorgement, revealing the system's dysfunction.** When the SEC wins disgorgement, it typically collects money into a "fair fund" and runs a claims process — but the amount collected from fraudsters rarely matches the losses of identifiable victims. The SEC's fiscal 2025 financial report shows it holds over $5 billion in disgorgement and penalties not yet distributed. Critics argue this enriches the Treasury rather than compensating victims. The Supreme Court has cut SEC disgorgement authority three times in the past decade, and the Sripetch case could cut it further — with direct implications for the Musk suit.

5. **Private credit is countercyclical to syndicated lending, which may make it a financial stabilizer, not an amplifier.** A March 2026 paper by Hinzen, Mondini, Rintamäki, and Steffen documents that when spreads in the leveraged loan market widen and deal-making becomes harder, firms substitute into direct lending rather than foregoing debt entirely — especially sponsor-backed firms. The structural reason: private credit funds raise capital first and then deploy it, so they always have dry powder even when public credit markets freeze. Banks and bond underwriters work the opposite way (lend first, then syndicate), so they go quiet precisely when markets are stressed.

6. **The "interception trade" exploits the precise moment a rival firm's noncompete expires.** Almost every top hedge fund portfolio manager leaves on a year-long gardening leave under a noncompete. The spot market for talent is tiny — but exactly at the moment the noncompete expires, a manager is briefly available with near-zero delay, making her worth materially more than a manager who won't be free for a year. Recruiters now keep databases of placed managers and their noncompete expiration dates. As London recruiter Jason Kennedy puts it: "The less gardening leave he has, the more valuable he becomes to the buyer." The irony: the managers being poached understand optionality better than anyone.

7. **AQR's tax-aware long-short strategy empire now has a short seller targeting it.** AQR Capital Management (backed by Affiliated Managers Group, which has $813 billion in AUM) has built a large business around strategies that harvest tax losses by going long and short simultaneously on a beta-one equity portfolio. An AQR paper confirms that pre-tax alpha materially boosts after-tax returns in these strategies — not just for financial reasons but because the IRS requires "economic substance" beyond pure tax savings. Orso Partners has now shorted AMG, arguing in a letter to investors that AQR's recent growth "is built on regulatory arbitrage that faces immediate scrutiny."

**Quotable:**
> "If you do fraud in the stock market, in some intuitive sense the victim of the fraud is *the stock market*: You extracted a certain amount of money from the market, but exactly who put that money into the market is not always clear." — *on why disgorgement returns are so structurally difficult*

### Ad-Based Platforms Have Mostly Solved Optimal Taxation ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 14 mins

1. **Economists' ideal tax hierarchy runs: property > consumption > income > corporate profits/capital gains.** The canonical case is that taxes on property and consumption distort behavior least, while capital gains taxes create a perverse lock-in incentive — deferring gains functions as an interest-free government loan conditional on holding a suboptimal asset. Most real-world tax systems invert this ranking for political reasons.

2. **Property taxes are theoretically ideal precisely because they're politically toxic.** San Francisco homeowners have accumulated enormous wealth on the backs of tech-industry renters who made the city desirable, while California's Prop 13 freezes their tax basis and penalizes moving — the same distortion as a capital gains tax. Rent control in SF is effectively a housing subsidy worth roughly 20% of the city's budget, if you price the gap between controlled rents and market rents.

3. **The Harberger tax is the logical extreme of efficient property taxation: self-assess your home's value for tax purposes, but legally accept any offer at that price.** Sentimental value gets a dollar amount attached — a homeowner who loves a $200k house can declare it at $250k and pay accordingly. In practice, this means "the more happy memories you have, the higher your tax bill, enforced by letting BlackRock buy you out," which is why it has never been implemented anywhere.

4. **Online advertising accidentally implements a version of the Harberger tax's core insight — allocating scarce inventory to whoever is the highest bidder weighted by responsible use — without requiring legislative approval.** Ad auction slots go to some combination of highest bid and best fit to the user experience, exactly the multi-factor allocation mechanism that property tax theorists have fantasized about.

5. **Ad platforms tax consumption, not income, which is economists' second-preferred base.** Low-margin dropshippers in competitive categories are taxed on each transaction; brand advertisers pay to nudge someone into upgrading an iPhone or drinking a Diet Coke. The base closely tracks what people actually buy rather than what they earn, and the "tax rate" varies dynamically with competition.

6. **Quality scores — introduced in search advertising more than two decades ago — function as tax credits for positive externalities.** An ad that fits naturally into a user's Instagram feed costs the advertiser less per impression, because the platform forgoes revenue in exchange for reduced harm to the user experience and better long-term retention. This is structurally identical to a government offering a tax credit when $1 of forgone revenue produces at least $1 of public good.

7. **Platforms are forced to optimize long-term present value rather than quarterly revenue, which disciplines the "tax collector" against abuse.** Meta investing in Reels (short-term revenue hit to stay relevant against TikTok) and Google deploying generative AI into search results (COGS headwind for quality reasons) both represent platforms accepting near-term margin compression to protect the long-term franchise — the equivalent of a tax authority that can't afford to drive taxpayers offshore.

8. **Ad revenue funds a set of public goods — search, social graphs, free video hosting — that would otherwise plausibly be government services.** The internet itself began as a government project (ARPANET). Now those information-sharing and communications functions are privatized, with the platforms keeping most of the surplus and remitting a share as corporate tax. Meta's own economic contribution team estimates Meta Ads are linked to ~$548B of US economic activity annually, roughly 2% of GDP.

**Quotable:**
> "It's so straightforward to collect good information on taxpayers' willingness to pay, and it's so profitable to make them pay it, that this part of government is a private-sector initiative." — *on why ad platforms have effectively privatized the public-goods funding function that governments used to monopolize*

### An Interview with F1 Driver and Venture Capitalist Nico Rosberg About the Drive to Win ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 39 mins

1. Rosberg was karting at age 3, racing competitively at 6-7 alongside father Keke (1982 F1 world champion), and immediately winning. His academic parallel-track was so serious he had a reserved spot at Imperial College London to study aeronautics as Plan B — his route into F1 if driving didn't work.

2. At age 13, McLaren funded Rosberg and Lewis Hamilton as a two-car go-kart team — the two biggest rising stars of that era. Hamilton won more of those races. They became best friends, dreamed together about being F1 teammates in 15 years, and that exact dream materialized at Mercedes — with all the conflict that implied.

3. Rosberg describes himself as "more sensitive, more insecure, less self-belief" than Hamilton. Rather than being disabling, this drove obsessive preparation — he never felt safe enough to coast. He eventually beat someone he explicitly calls "the greatest of all time," validating the idea that self-doubt channeled into preparation can outperform natural confidence.

4. After a brutal 2005 debut at Williams (on the verge of not being re-signed), Rosberg decided that training the body 4 hours/day while ignoring the mind was irrational. He found a sports psychologist/philosopher and worked with him for 10 years — 2 hours every two days in the off-season. He describes it as harder than physical training: learning meditation, visualization, the power of repetition, and understanding the root causes of fear and anxiety.

5. In mid-2010s F1, admitting you had a "brain doctor" was seen as weakness — Rosberg kept it entirely secret. Lando Norris, mid-2025 title battle, publicly spiraled before seeking help, ultimately winning the championship. Rosberg DM'd Norris on Instagram (never got a reply) specifically to flag that narrating your failures out loud — "I always mess up that corner" — ingrains the belief neurologically, regardless of whether it's accurate.

6. The ideal founder, in Rosberg's framing, requires a paradox: extreme conviction in a non-obvious idea (enough to resist board pressure, consensus, and years without validation) combined with extreme curiosity and openness to learning. Pure conviction becomes arrogance; pure openness collapses the founding thesis. Finding both traits in one person is rare and is what Rosberg screens for.

7. Rosberg started a YouTube channel in 2011 when almost no one else in F1 was doing it — now at 1.46 million subscribers. The calculus was explicit: a large personal brand attracts sponsorship, and sponsorship interest makes a team choose you over a driver of equal speed. He applied the same maximize-every-domain logic to Sky Sports commentary post-retirement, staying visible to keep the F1 platform relevant to his VC work.

8. Netflix's Drive to Survive triggered a 2-3x increase in F1 sponsorship fees by making the sport mainstream in the US tech market. Mercedes now earns $400M/year in sponsorship revenue alone, with total revenue around $600M against a $300M budget cap — implying structural profitability. The Mercedes F1 team was valued at $6B; CrowdStrike CEO George Kurtz bought 5% in early 2026 for $300M.

9. F1 race weekends function as pre-scheduled, globally distributed networking infrastructure. Mercedes pays Rosberg in race tickets rather than euros because the access is more valuable to him than cash — he hosts the VC community there to build relationships. Atlassian CEO Mike Cannon-Brooks told Thompson the Williams sponsorship essentially eliminates scheduling friction: C-levels from major global companies are always in the paddock, making 22-24 customer meetings per year automatic.

10. The Hamilton-Rosberg rivalry lasted four years — longer than the legendary Senna-Prost pairing at McLaren, which imploded after two. Team principal Toto Wolff's solution was a contract requiring both drivers to split crash repair bills 50/50 regardless of fault. Rosberg's most expensive crash cost him $360,000 out of pocket, after which he gave Hamilton noticeably more track space.

11. Rosberg retired at 31, five days after winning the 2016 championship, with a young baby at home. The decision was partly rational: "I achieved my dream in the best possible way, I beat the greatest of all time — it's not possible for me to do better." He analogizes it to a $10-50B startup exit: total life commitment, enormous success, exit on a high, with the achievement compounding in reputation forever.

12. Rosberg's 2016 championship push involved systematic marginal gains across every variable. He worked with Harvard sleep professor Steven Lockley (who later commercialized the approach in his startup Timeshifter) to achieve 0.0 jet lag for the entire season — max 1.5-hour time-zone shift per day, blackout glasses 2 hours before sleep, 10,000-lux light immediately on waking. He also stripped the paint from his helmet to save 80 grams.

13. After retiring, Rosberg spent seven years in an identity crisis — "trying everything and nothing." The problem: an F1 driver is effectively CEO of their own performance operation, but the skill is entirely non-transferable. He had to accept starting from zero, with no guarantee of success in any new domain.

14. The investing thesis came from studying the Yale Endowment. David Swensen had allocated 20% of Yale's endowment (~$8B) to venture capital, earning 21% IRR — the best-performing asset class in the portfolio. Rosberg decided to replicate the structure with his F1 earnings and use his unique access advantages to build the same edge Swensen had.

15. Rosberg Ventures reached $200M AUM (announced January 2026). The strategy started as a fund-of-funds — indexing the top 10 US VC funds and top 10 growth-stage companies — which Rosberg describes as a "Trojan horse" that buys market visibility and deal flow insight. That visibility then enables direct startup investments, the ultimate goal.

16. European family offices have substantial capital but lack access to US venture deals and are missing the asset class's returns. Rosberg raises from them via warm F1-network introductions — never pitching directly, always asking for an introduction to the family office and letting the opportunity speak for itself. In the reverse direction, he connects German large corporates (BMW, Mercedes-Benz) with US startup products they need for digital and sustainability transformation.

17. Europe's startup ecosystem has genuine bright spots — Lovable (vibe coding for prosumers, Sweden), ElevenLabs (voice AI, European-founded, now global leader) — but structural problems suppress scale. Country-to-country expansion is expensive due to linguistic and regulatory fragmentation; historically thin VC liquidity has created a self-reinforcing funding gap. The EU is attempting a unified startup regulatory framework to reduce this friction.

18. Rosberg's AI investment stance is net-bullish but wary of frontier lab encroachment. Anthropic has already launched a legal product that threatens Harvey and Legora (the two legal AI leaders). ElevenLabs is the counter-example: defensible because they build their own voice models rather than wrapping others, and have strong platform and distribution advantages that pure commodity voice providers can't replicate.

19. Rosberg introduced Ben Thompson to Dreamer, an AI startup building vibe-coding agents founded by David Singleton (ex-Stripe CTO) and Hugo Barra (ex-Google, ex-Xiaomi, ex-Meta). Meta acquired Dreamer almost immediately after its March 2026 launch, before Thompson could write about it — Rosberg had been excited to use it at scale for Rosberg Ventures.

20. Nineteen-year-old Kimi Antonelli (Mercedes) is dominating the 2026 F1 championship. Rosberg coached him in go-karting when Antonelli was 12, and has resumed contact now — he wrote Antonelli the same mental advice that worked in 2016: take it race-by-race, optimize only for the next race, don't think about the championship. Lewis Hamilton, 37, is still competing and "driving really well" — and Rosberg notes, half-jokingly, that every additional Hamilton win makes Rosberg's 2016 title look better.

**Quotable:**
> "When you don't have that self-confidence, you just fight so hard to prepare to the best of your abilities all the time. It was nice to see that someone like me, that did not believe until the very last corner, was still able to actually win in the end." — *Rosberg on insecurity as competitive fuel*

### TSMC Earnings, New N3 Fabs, The Nvidia Ramp 📌
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 8 mins

1. TSMC raised its 2026 revenue growth outlook to above 30% (upgraded from below 30%), with capex trending toward the upper end of its $52–$56 billion range. Neither figure is a dramatic surprise, but the direction of revision matters: it signals that AI chip demand is holding up despite Middle Eastern conflict and tariff uncertainty.

2. CEO C.C. Wei's prepared remarks contained language that is genuinely unprecedented for a TSMC executive: he specifically named the shift "from generative AI and the query mode to agentic AI and the command and action mode" as a driver of step-change token consumption. TSMC executives historically deflect application questions with "not my problem" answers — Wei's specificity suggests Jensen Huang (or someone) directly convinced him that agentic AI is real and will require massive additional compute, a conviction that now shapes TSMC's investment planning.

3. TSMC is breaking a foundational rule: it is building brand-new 3nm fabs rather than repurposing older ones. Three new fabs are planned — a new fab in Tainan (volume production H1 2027), Arizona's second fab retooled for 3nm (H2 2027), and Japan's second fab moving to 3nm (2028). Historically TSMC only added capacity to a node before it hit target; adding fresh fabs to a process that first ramped in 2022 is structurally Intel-like and entirely un-TSMC.

4. These new 3nm fabs are effectively dedicated Nvidia Rubin fabs. GPUs have been reticle-limited in size since Maxwell (TSMC 28nm), meaning a single process defect destroys an entire GPU rather than 1-of-8 Apple A-series chips — so Nvidia always waits for a mature, high-yield process before committing. Rubin will be Nvidia's first 3nm chip, but Nvidia's capacity appetite is now so large it can't simply absorb Apple's hand-me-down capacity as it did in prior generations. The new fabs, built Intel-style as copy-exact duplicates of proven lines, give Nvidia the volume it needs at a node that's already mature enough to be economical.

**Quotable:**
> "Wei has been the person most worried about a bubble — for good reason! — and now he isn't just saying AI is real, but meaningfully changing TSMC's approach to capitalize." — *on C.C. Wei reversing his caution and committing to new 3nm fabs*

Two of the four prior instances (December 2008 during the GFC, August 2002 during the dot-com fallout) proved fools' rallies, with a new low following before recovery could stick.

3. The rally is hard to justify on economic fundamentals: Citi's economic surprise index has been negative since the war began, offering no support for higher equity prices. Instead, earnings expectations for S&P 500 companies *excluding* the Magnificent Seven suddenly jumped at the war's outbreak — suggesting brokers had already been marking up full-year forecasts and needed only a trigger to release the pent-up demand. Gavekal Research's Nicolas Oudin frames the resulting investor choice as leaning into rate cuts + stabilizing US-China trade + AI capex boom versus a tactical "buy the rumor, sell the fact" exit; markets stampeded toward the optimistic scenario, creating a real vulnerability if de-escalation assumptions are stress-tested.

4. Compressed valuations do, however, raise SaaS firms' cost of equity capital and gut the acquisition currency they rely on to integrate AI, making the valuation damage self-reinforcing regardless of whether the existential fear is warranted.

### Mobius: Adventurer, Anomaly, And Market Legend 📎
**The Core** · email · 6 mins

1. Mark Mobius, who died last week, partnered with Franklin Templeton in 1987 to launch an emerging markets fund from a tiny Hong Kong office with $100 million. He grew that portfolio to over $40 billion across 70 countries by venturing into economies — including early-1990s India — when foreign capital was viewed by developing nations with a mix of desperation and suspicion. He was buying Infosys and India's IT sector before most Indians understood what was brewing.

2. Mobius's credentials would get him rejected by any modern HR department: a BA in fine arts and a master's in communications (Boston University), time spent playing piano in a nightclub, Japanese learned in Kyoto, and a PhD in political science and economics from MIT (1964). Jim Rogers, who co-founded the Quantum Fund alongside George Soros, had a similarly non-standard background — degrees in history, philosophy, and politics. Both men were adventurers first and investors second, and that worldly curiosity is precisely what let them discover new markets before the excavators arrived.

3. Today's top fund managers in India and on Wall Street are predominantly MBAs, Chartered Accountants, and cost accountants — technically skilled excavators who arrive after the mine is mapped. As global market volatility makes extracting returns increasingly difficult, the scarcity is not analytical horsepower but the prospector mindset: the willingness to fly into obscure airports with outdated navigation maps, as Mobius's pilots described, and bet on the trajectory of economic liberalisation before the spreadsheet can confirm it.

**Quotable:**
> "We routinely fly into obscure airports with decrepit runways where the navigation maps were either outdated or printed in languages other than English." — *Mobius's pilot, describing what it took to reach the markets their boss was buying into*

### The looming battle over the Fed's balance sheet 📎
**Katie Martin** · email · 5 mins

1. Kevin Warsh's Fed chair nomination — his hearing was April 21, 2026 — rests on two pillars: willingness to cut rates (which earned him Trump's nod) and a desire to shrink the Fed's balance sheet, which peaked at $9tn in 2022, equivalent to 35% of GDP. The Fed had made meaningful progress via quantitative tightening, cutting its footprint by more than $2tn (~7% of GDP), but the balance sheet has since grown again by over $200bn since December 2025 through "reserve management purchases" — designed to keep reserves "ample" and maintain control of short-term rates. FOMC members remain haunted by the September 2019 "repocalypse," when a liquidity shortage caused a sudden spike in market interest rates.

2. The intellectual case for a smaller balance sheet has two threads. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent calls QE "gain of function monetary policy," framing crisis-era bond purchases as a market-distorting policy aberration. Fed governor Stephen Miran goes further: a smaller balance sheet reduces mark-to-market losses at the central bank, cuts interest payments on reserve balances (which Congress views as a banking subsidy), and keeps the Fed out of "the credit allocation game across sectors" — preserving the boundary between monetary and fiscal policy. Before the global financial crisis, the Fed's balance sheet was just 6% of GDP.

3. The operative question is *how* Warsh would shrink it: regulatory fine-tuning or active bond sales. Dallas Fed president Lorie Logan — a former manager of the Fed's System Open Market Account — argues for changing liquidity regulation to reduce banks' *demand* for reserves rather than just cutting supply, which she sees as necessary to safely shrink the balance sheet without volatility. The "full English" alternative — active asset sales — risks upward pressure on borrowing costs. Unhedged's verdict: Warsh will loudly criticize the balance sheet but stop short of anything that truly disrupts the Treasury market.

**Quotable:**
> "It is, after all, easier to cry foul over the balance sheet from a distance than to do anything about it in real life." — *Unhedged's assessment of Warsh's likely course of action*

## Geopolitics

### The World: Iran's non-nuclear deterrent 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 6 mins

1. Iran's control of the Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of the world's oil supply passes — has proven to be a more immediately usable deterrent than any nuclear weapon. The strait's shallow waters force ships to pass within miles of Iran's mountainous shore, terrain that inherently favors missiles and drones over the naval power of an adversary. The current US-Israeli war has converted this theoretical leverage into a demonstrated fact: a top US war aim is now simply restoring access to a waterway that was open before the conflict began.

2. Despite weeks of active combat, American military and intelligence officials estimate Iran retains roughly 40% of its attack drone arsenal and over 60% of its missile launchers — more than sufficient to hold Strait of Hormuz shipping hostage indefinitely. Iran previously attempted to blockade the strait in the 1980s using mines during its war with Iraq, but mines are indiscriminate and dangerous to friendly vessels; precision-guided drones and missiles have made the tactic far more controllable and credible. Danny Citrinowicz, former head of the Iran branch of Israel's military intelligence and now an Atlantic Council fellow, summarized the lesson bluntly: "Everyone now knows that if there is a conflict in the future, closing the strait will be the first thing in the Iranian textbook. You cannot beat geography."

3. The crisis has produced a tit-for-tat blockade standoff: Iran declared the waterway open on Friday (sending markets surging), then reversed course Saturday, saying the strait remained under its "strict control" until the US lifted its own blockade of Iranian ports. On Sunday, a US Navy destroyer fired on and seized an Iranian cargo ship, which Iran's armed forces called piracy and vowed to retaliate against. Diplomatic talks are nonetheless continuing — VP JD Vance is heading to Islamabad, and Iran's parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf is expected to participate in a new round of negotiations — though a two-week ceasefire expires April 21 with Iranian officials publicly skeptical of a breakthrough.

**Quotable:**
> "Iran has tested its nuclear weapons. It's called the Strait of Hormuz. Its potential is inexhaustible." — *Dmitri Medvedev, former Russian president and deputy chairman of Russia's security council, on social media*
