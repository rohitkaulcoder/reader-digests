## Today's Top 3

### Can AI make an iPhone? ⚡
**Benn Stancil** · email · 7 mins

1. **The hardest part of making the iPhone wasn't imagining it — it was refusing to un-make it.** A thought experiment: in 2004, given the brief ("an iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator") and a table full of reference devices, you probably could have conceived of something like the iPhone. In 2005, if Jony Ive ran a Crazy 8's sprint and produced 80 concepts including the actual iPhone, you might even have picked it. But in 2006, when an engineer explains that adding just two arrow keys — still 32 fewer than a Blackberry — would multiply what the device can do, would you have said no? That third question is the real one, and most people would have said yes to the buttons.

2. **Great work is the discipline of subtraction sustained across hundreds of decisions, not a single inspired choice.** Creation isn't a singular act; it's a repeated one. Most people start with something simple and elegant, then get tired, get tempted by tradeoffs, and start adding buttons, menus, and "long and unnecessary preambles." The reason nobody else invented the iPhone isn't that they couldn't imagine it — it's that nobody else had the discipline to insist it *stay* an iPhone through every pressure to compromise. Jobs's genius was less about taste at any one moment and more about the relentless, tyrant-grade enforcement of a vision over years.

3. **Taste Labs is a 2026 startup built on the premise that AI can be given genuine taste** — not the median preference of ChatGPT's mass RLHF feedback, but the judgment of a "curated community of tastemakers." Standard reinforcement learning from human feedback works by averaging millions of user votes, producing outputs optimized for the philistine middle. Taste Labs instead tries to weight expert judgment more heavily, so the model acts less like a focus group and more like a designer. The goal: make AI produce outputs that are "not just correct, but feel right."

4. **Taste Labs is caught in the same trap it's trying to solve — it can't say no.** The small demo game at the bottom of their website lets users rate designs and then, regardless of what they chose, tells them "your taste rocks." The entire company is premised on the idea that most people *don't* have taste that rocks — that's why you need curated tastemakers. But it's also a business, and people like to be told yes. This is the irreducible tension: producing great things requires a model willing to reject, refuse, and tell users their ideas are bad. Steve Jobs is known for two things — making great things and being an absolute tyrant. You probably can't have one without the other, and we don't like AI models that say no.

**Quotable:**
> "The reason nobody else invented the iPhone is because nobody else had the discipline to insist that it *stay* an iPhone." — *on why taste is about sustained refusal, not a single inspired decision*

### Going Private: A nightmare scenario for private markets ⚡
**Bloomberg** · email · 10 mins

1. The Bank of England's System-wide Exploratory Scenario (SWES) for private markets is deliberately harsher than GFC conditions in several dimensions: year one brings 30-35% equity declines and inflation rising to 7%; year two delivers -4% UK GDP and high-yield spreads peaking at 1,200 bps with total sterling borrowing costs hitting 1,800 bps; year three sees 7.5% unemployment and two-notch downgrades for rated alternative asset managers. Crucially, the scenario includes zero policymaker interventions — no bailouts, no emergency facilities — across the full five-year horizon, with findings to be published in early 2027.

2. The BOE's concern is systemic interconnection, not just sector fragility. PE-sponsored businesses account for 15% of total UK corporate debt and 10% of private sector employment (over two million jobs), making a private markets seizure a Main Street problem. The ECB has separately nearly doubled the banks under its private credit probe to 20; Deutsche Bank, Barclays, BNP Paribas, and HSBC alone account for almost two-thirds of European bank exposure to private credit, though Bloomberg Intelligence notes disclosed exposures represent just 2.3% of loans — a figure ECB Supervisory Board member Sharon Donnery says banks can't even reliably aggregate.

3. Brazil's IG4 Capital ($4.8B AUM) is simultaneously pursuing the two largest distressed corporate situations in the country's history. Petrochemical company Braskem and sugar/ethanol producer Raizen carry combined debt of 127 billion reais ($25.1B); IG4 acquired its Braskem stake by buying 21 billion reais of non-performing loans from five banks that Novonor had pledged as collateral. IG4 needs one-third of Braskem creditors to agree to an out-of-court restructuring before July debt payments — support it doesn't yet have — while simultaneously seeking a majority debt position in Raizen in what would be Brazil's largest-ever out-of-court restructuring.

4. Pretium ($67B in residential real estate AUM) survived Trump's January executive order targeting institutional single-family home buyers and is positioning to help fill a 6-million-home US shortage. The closed-end fund structure — two-year investment period, three-year harvest, no monthly subscriptions or quarterly redemptions — lets Pretium apply more leverage than evergreen vehicles, producing unlevered returns in the low teens that can rise to mid-teens with leverage. Co-president Jon Pruzan argues residential real estate has only dropped double digits twice in modern memory (Great Depression and GFC), and unlike cash-flow-backed private credit, every Pretium loan is secured by a physical house or developable land.

5. AI disruption introduces a structural new risk for private credit lenders: they cannot exit. In liquid credit, investors who misjudge whether a software company's moat will survive AI competition can sell; private credit investors hold the loan for its full life regardless. Franklin Templeton's BSP Global Investment Strategist Anant Kumar frames this as a fundamental underwriting question — not "does this borrower own too much software?" but "can this business model remain durable for the full loan duration?"

**Quotable:**
> "AI risk is no longer simply a question of whether a manager owns too much software. It is a question of whether the borrower's business model can remain durable for the full life of the loan. In liquid credit, investors can change their mind. In private credit, they must live with the consequences." — *Anant Kumar, Global Investment Strategist, Franklin Templeton's BSP*

### Generalist Intelligence: The Off Switch ⚡
**'The Generalist' via PubsforSubs** · email · 11 mins

1. **Anthropic given 90 minutes to shut down Fable.** Amazon discovered a way to bypass safety guardrails in Fable — Anthropic's "guard-railed" release of its underlying Mythos model — and alerted the US government. Washington invoked export controls on a Friday afternoon, giving Anthropic 90 minutes to comply. Because denying access only to foreign users wasn't technically feasible in time, Anthropic had to cut off everyone, including American employees.

2. **The "it's good for business" spin doesn't hold.** Some claimed the government intervention created a mystique that would attract customers, pointing to an uptick in Claude usage after Trump previously labeled Anthropic a "supply-chain risk." This misreads the situation: either Fable's guardrails failed (embarrassing), or the government arbitrarily killed a compliant product (terrifying to buyers). Neither outcome reassures enterprise customers.

3. **Dario's regulation wishlist collides with reality.** Anthropic has loudly advocated for AI oversight, but what it wanted was something like the FAA — an arms-length technical regulator. What it got was political intervention with a 90-minute clock. The irony is acute: having spent years warning about AI's dangers and calling for government constraints, Anthropic now looks surprised when a hostile administration takes him at his word.

4. **US AI export restrictions are a gift to Chinese competitors.** DeepSeek's flagship model costs 57x less per million output tokens than Fable, and Chinese models (DeepSeek V4 Pro, MiniMax, Qwen) are increasingly close to the US frontier in capability. For international companies already weighing AI vendor risk, erratic US access controls make switching to cheaper Chinese models a rational business decision, not a political one.

5. **France is the canary for European "strategic autonomy."** At the G7 in Évian-les-Bains, Emmanuel Macron said the export ban had "clarified the stakes" — warning that the US can "from one day to the next turn off the switch." France has already ejected Palantir from its domestic intelligence agency DGSI, replacing it with ChapsVision, and is planning to equip government workers with a Mistral-powered assistant. Mistral is reportedly raising a $3.5 billion round, possibly accelerated by Anthropic's troubles.

6. **Sovereign AI doesn't need to be the best — just reliable enough.** The competitive bar for European or Chinese AI is not matching Anthropic or OpenAI at the frontier. Concorde was faster, but people were fine getting from New York to London in six or seven hours on an ordinary jet. If US models are 20% better but randomly unavailable, "good enough and always on" wins the enterprise contract.

7. **This is the first skirmish, not the last.** The immediate pretext was that a foreign power could weaponize Fable. The next pretext could be that a foreign power is using some future model *too effectively* — economically or technologically. If AI is as transformative as its builders claim, governments will eventually fight to control it. Tech companies have capital; governments have power. This week demonstrated which one wins when they collide.

8. **Side stories worth noting.** Geothermal is breaking out of volcanic-zone limits: Ormat unveiled a 100MW unit for non-volcanic sites; Critical Energy (ex-SpaceX, $22M raised) plans first projects by 2027; Fervo Energy halved per-well drilling costs at its Cape Station 100MW installation. Tether — which cleared $10B+ net profit in 2025 from interest on US Treasuries backing its $186B USDT stablecoin — led a Series C of up to $1.4B in German humanoid-robot maker NEURA Robotics, alongside Amazon, NVIDIA, and Qualcomm, with the explicit goal of making USDT the settlement currency for autonomous machine labor. SpaceX's AI1 satellite-datacenter plan targets 1GW of orbital compute by 2027, but the IPO filing concedes launches start "as early as 2028" and chips are already scarce.

**Quotable:**
> "If you're an international company buying AI tools, that's a lot of risk… If the US from one day to the next can turn off the switch…" — *Emmanuel Macron at the G7, Évian-les-Bains, on the Anthropic export ban*

### How Anthropic Makes Claude More Reliable 📌
**Laura Entis / Context Window** · rss · 4 mins

1. Before Anthropic's dynamic workflows feature, getting Claude Code to reliably coordinate multiple agents required elaborate workarounds. Senior applied AI engineer Nityesh Agarwal's solution for Every's AI project manager "Claudie" involved one coordinating agent delegating to fleets of subagents that communicated via local markdown files — functional but "a little bit hacky." The core problem was that Claude, anxious about token spend, would ignore instructions to spawn separate reviewer agents and collapse them into a single subagent, degrading output quality even when given "increasingly dramatic directives" not to do so.

2. Dynamic workflows solve the reliability problem by shifting control from prompt instructions to deterministic scripts. Instead of Claude deciding each step on the fly, it writes a reusable script that enforces the coordination structure — if you specify three verifier subagents, the script generates exactly three every time. In a concrete test, Every general manager Marcus Moretti used it to convert Spiral's 11-section Figma redesign file into code: Claude spun up 11 dedicated subagents, each reviewing a section and checking results against the original file, running for a couple of hours and replacing what would have been repetitive manual review cycles.

**Quotable:**
> "I spent so many weeks building that other thing. Now it's useless. But that's the cost of being at the frontier. You need to be ahead of everybody else, and sometimes that means you need to throw away your past work." — *Nityesh Agarwal, after dynamic workflows rendered his hand-built multi-agent setup obsolete*

### Nerdy chip rivalry returns 📎
**Bloomberg Technology** · email · 5 mins

1. For most of computing history, chip companies — Nvidia vs. AMD in graphics, Intel vs. AMD in CPUs — competed by trading arcane benchmark scores that few consumers understood. These wars shaped real outcomes: AMD's multi-year CPU benchmark leadership directly drove meaningful market share gains against Intel, and Nvidia's relentless focus on graphics performance sharpened the competitive instincts that would later dominate the data center.

2. The AI boom silenced the benchmark wars by collapsing them to a single variable. Once Nvidia's GPU-based accelerators became the indispensable infrastructure for AI training, performance comparisons became irrelevant — the only metric that mattered was *how many* Nvidia GPUs you could acquire. Rivals couldn't claim superiority; they could only take pot shots at price or power consumption while Huang updated his designs annually, closing gaps before competitors could exploit them.

3. Benchmark rivalry is back because the workload is shifting from training models to running them. CPUs are having a renaissance as inference and agentic AI demand general-purpose compute, prompting Nvidia to spotlight its new Vera processor. Simultaneously, Cerebras used aggressive performance slides to reach the biggest semiconductor IPO ever; AMD released charts arguing its Epyc CPUs win on real-world system performance (not chip-level specs); and Nvidia is pushing "AgentPerf," a proprietary benchmark where its Grace Blackwell design claims a "massive step up" over its prior generation — framing the metric around the workload its next product, Vera Rubin, is designed to dominate.

**Quotable:**
> "The amount of capex that's being spent is dramatically higher than even the high end of what anyone would have thought not just a year ago but three months ago." — *Robert Schiffman, Bloomberg Intelligence senior credit analyst, on Big Tech AI infrastructure spending that has crowded out share buybacks*

## Markets & Economics

### India's Infra Boom, Running On Unpaid Bills 📌
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. **India's EPC sector is posting record order books while quietly running out of cash.** India Ratings and Research's FY26 review found revenue across its EPC portfolio grew just 2.8% — against double-digit growth management had promised — while operating margins fell to 10.2%, a multi-year low. The order book grew 14% to roughly Rs 5 lakh crore (about three times annual revenue), meaning contractors are taking on more work from clients who are paying more slowly.

2. **Only Rs 29 of every Rs 100 in operating profit actually arrived as cash — half the historical norm.** Cash collected from operations was ~29% of operating profit in FY26, against a historical average of nearly 65%. The remaining Rs 71 sat in unpaid bills, retention money, uninvoiced work, and disputed claims — and both profits and cash collections fell for the second consecutive year.

3. **The slow payer is overwhelmingly the Indian state itself.** Of India Ratings' 27% rating actions on the EPC sector, eight were downgrades or outlook cuts. Payment delays on Jal Jeevan Mission (the Centre's rural tap water programme) and state irrigation contracts crushed water-focused contractors; shrinking workloads hit highway builders; and even power transmission — the one segment with strong demand — triggered downgrades because money was trapped in unpaid bills on half-finished work.

4. **NCC Limited, one of India's largest construction firms, pulled its full-year guidance mid-FY26 and never put it back.** The company cited extended monsoons, project delays, and stretched government payment cycles. By February, head of strategy Neerad Sharma was still telling analysts: "No guidance as we speak." It is the sector's clearest signal that the cash-flow gap has become too unpredictable to model.

5. **India is separately seeking $2.5 billion from the World Bank ($1.5B) and ADB ($1B) to plug an infrastructure spending gap widened by the Iran conflict.** Oil import bills surged after the West Asia war disrupted Strait of Hormuz shipping, forcing New Delhi to spend more on fuel subsidies and widen its budget deficit. The multilateral loans — expected to be announced within two months — are meant to sustain urban infrastructure and job-creation programmes while commercial contractors wait to be paid.

**Quotable:**
> "For every Rs 100 of operating profit the sector reported in FY26, only Rs 29 arrived as cash. The remaining Rs 71 sat somewhere between the contractor's books and reality: in unpaid bills, retention money, work not yet invoiced, and disputed claims." — *India Ratings and Research FY26 EPC sector review*

## Culture & Philosophy

### Forget Stoicism. Skepticism is the ancient philosophy we need today. 📎
**Big Think** · email · 7 mins

1. Stoicism's pop revival was sparked by Ryan Holiday's 2014 book *The Obstacle Is the Way*, which brought Marcus Aurelius' *Meditations* to Silicon Valley and professional athletes. Holiday's follow-up *The Daily Stoic* newsletter and copycat books turned Stoic texts like Seneca's *Letters* and Epictetus' *Discourses* into bestsellers — but CUNY philosophy professor Massimo Pigliucci argues this pop version is a distortion, and Stoicism itself has a fundamental flaw.

2. The better alternative is Academic Skepticism, which flourished at Plato's Athens Academy from 266 to 90 B.C. Its founder Arcesilaus radicalized the Socratic tradition — where Socrates said "I know only that I know nothing," Arcesilaus went further, saying he didn't even know *that* for certain. The school treated doubt not as an endpoint but as a starting position for the deeper question: how do you live a meaningful life?

3. Skepticism is a practical shield against today's epistemic threats — conspiracy theories, AI deepfakes, and ideologically motivated reasoning — because it decouples personal identity from specific beliefs. Pigliucci: "As a Skeptic, I can admit my mistakes without being ashamed of it, because it's built into the philosophy that my personal identity doesn't depend on assuming one thing or another." This makes changing your mind rational rather than humiliating.

4. The Socratic method — asking "What do you think you should do?" rather than telling — operates through *aporia* (suspension of judgment) and reduces polarization by replacing adversarial debate with genuine dialogue. The catch: it doesn't scale. It works in small private-school classes but not with millions of people. Pigliucci calls for a "21st-century Plato" to write new dialogues on abortion, immigration, gun control, and climate policy.

5. Stoicism's core weakness is that its insistence on bearing suffering quietly and focusing only on what you can control can breed passivity toward injustice. The contrasting fates of Seneca and Socrates illustrate the difference: both were forced to commit suicide by authorities, but Seneca died having tutored the tyrant Nero (whose reign triggered Roman civil war), while Socrates died as he lived — not speaking truth to power, but *questioning* it.

**Quotable:**
> "It may be certainty — not doubt — that more often holds us back." — *on why Skepticism is more pragmatic than Stoicism in times of crisis*

### On Books: Dads deserve better 📎
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 7 mins

1. **Fatherhood books haven't caught up to fatherhood reality.** Today's American father spends four times as much time with his children as his grandfather did and is twice as likely to be a stay-at-home dad — yet the publishing industry still hands him *Dude, You're Gonna Be a Dad!* and *The Caveman's Pregnancy Companion: A Survival Guide for Expectant Fathers*. The reluctant-oaf trope isn't just tired; it leaves men navigating parenthood without substantive guidance. Kevin Maguire, writing in Bloomberg Pursuits, argues the only serious alternative is literary fiction: Ta-Nehisi Coates' *Between the World and Me*, Michael Chabon's essay collection *Pops*, Karl Ove Knausgård's *My Struggle*, and Rob Delaney's *A Heart That Works* — a memoir about his son Henry's brain tumor diagnosis and short life.

2. **W.E.B. Du Bois left the US for Ghana in 1961 with no intention of returning, and the world is finally reckoning with why.** The NAACP co-founder and first Black American to earn a Harvard PhD wrote to a friend: "I just cannot take any more of this country's treatment. We leave for Ghana October 5th and I set no date for return. Chin up, and fight on, but realize that American Negroes can't win." Six decades after his 1963 death, two films are reviving him: Rita Coburn's two-hour documentary *W.E.B. Du Bois: Rebel With a Cause* (aired PBS, May 2026) and Kahlil Joseph's experimental *BLKNWS: Terms & Conditions*, which remixes Du Bois' never-completed *Encyclopedia Africana* in visual form. A Ghanaian non-profit is simultaneously restoring Du Bois' Accra home and has catalogued roughly 1,500 of his books in a climate-controlled lab.

3. **Jonathan Haidt's smartphone-harm thesis finds an unlikely precedent in a 1978 polemic.** *The Anxious Generation*, now four years old, remains near the top of bestseller lists, using data to show that more screen time correlates with depression, insecurity, and mental illness among children. Jerry Mander's *Four Arguments for the Elimination of Television* — written by an advertising executive in 1978 — makes the same structural argument about TV: the problem is the technology itself, not the dosage. Mander contended that television desensitizes viewers, eliminates critical thought, and undermines democratic society regardless of how much one watches. The medium is the message, and the message is poison.

4. **Cornell economist Eswar Prasad has reversed his view on decentralized finance.** In his 2021 book *The Future of Money*, Prasad predicted DeFi would upend traditional financial institutions. Five years on: "None of that has really come to pass." The most successful blockchain applications have been centralized, not decentralized. Prasad has also grown wary of central bank digital currencies (CBDCs), which he now fears could let governments restrict when and how money is spent — a theme he extends in his new book *The Doom Loop: Why the World Economic Order Is Spiraling Into Disorder*, which links rising authoritarianism to fragility in global financial architecture.

**Quotable:**
> "I just cannot take any more of this country's treatment. We leave for Ghana October 5th and I set no date for return. Chin up, and fight on, but realize that American Negroes can't win." — *W.E.B. Du Bois, in a letter to a friend, departing the US permanently in 1961*
