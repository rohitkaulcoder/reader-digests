### How to build your AI GTM system ⚡
**Kyle Poyar** · email · 13 mins

1. Most AI GTM teams are stuck on a treadmill: every piece of content, every research doc, every sales asset starts from scratch. AI output isn't compounding because there's no persistent context — stale ICP files, calcified positioning, brand drift amplified across every agent-run channel simultaneously.

2. The real ceiling of Claude.ai and ChatGPT is session-boundedness. Matteo Tittarelli (founder of Genesys, co-founder of GTM Engineer School) built an early version inside a Claude project for Archive.com, manually feeding 100+ customer interviews and sales transcripts — but nothing persisted between sessions, so all effort went into rewriting and copy-pasting giant prompts. Moving to Claude Code meant building a folder architecture that persists context across every session.

3. The solution is a four-layer system: (1) **System of context** — foundational .md files on ICP, competitors, positioning, messaging, brand; (2) **System of skills** — modular skills for each GTM lane that read from context folders and write structured output to target folders; (3) **System of orchestration** — agents and hooks that decide which skills to run, when, and on what inputs (e.g., a refresh agent detects win-loss skill hasn't run in five weeks and dispatches it against latest Gong transcripts automatically); (4) **System of integrations** — MCPs pulling external data in (Exa, Granola, Gong, Apollo, GA4) and pushing outputs to live destinations out (Buffer, Google Ads, Smartlead, Klaviyo, Webflow).

4. The folder structure has two non-obvious memory files: `latest.md` (short-term memory — what changed recently) and `history.md` (long-term memory — logs, milestones). Every agent session reads these first and writes to them after any non-trivial action. The five PMM foundations folders (icp/, competitors/, positioning/, messaging/, brand/) sit above all execution workstreams — update one and every downstream output reflects it on the next run. Pivot's Series B rebrand demonstrated this: updating one brand kit HTML file propagated new fonts, colors, and gradients across three vibe-coded apps in a single Claude Code chat.

5. The four-stage execution loop is Research → Strategy → Execution → Refresh. Research skills run in a specific order (win-loss → competitor research → ICP → positioning → messaging → TOV → brand kit) using dated `MMYY-topic.md` naming so agents know when to supersede stale files. Anti-hallucination hooks enforce confidence scoring on every claim (verified / inferred / estimated / unavailable) and source attribution with URLs — because if the inputs lie, everything downstream lies. A PE-backed regulatory-intelligence platform got four confident-but-wrong messaging houses on first pass; the PMM director rejected three on sight because the model inferred a mid-market segment that wasn't theirs. Human corrections baked back into core ICP files fixed every downstream run.

6. Refresh cadence is the operating discipline that makes the system compound rather than rot. The recommended schedule: full PMM spine (win-loss → competitors → ICP → messaging) monthly; positioning quarterly. AdvisoryAI, an AI platform for UK financial advisors, added 13 new competitor analyses over two quarters as competitors entered monthly — each as a dated file superseding the last, creating a continuous repositioning arc. A good refresh reads the previous canonical file first and asks the agent to surface only what changed, not rewrite everything.

7. The GitHub repo is the team's shared brain. For Crescendo (Series C AI CX scaleup), Tittarelli packaged the GTM system as a dedicated skills repo — 17 skills plus orchestrating role-agents. When the new VP of Product Marketing joined, onboarding was a single `git pull`. A new hire clones the repo on day one and reads the same locked context every existing operator reads. This is also the cleanest freelancer handoff: deliver a working system with skills, context, and output in one repo and onboard the team into AI-native working simultaneously.

**Quotable:**
> "Velocity comes from skills, the producers. Coordination comes from agents, the coordinators. But compounding lives in the contextual spine and folder structure — the md files, the ontology, the rules, the memory, the hooks." — *closing summary*

### The State of Fable, The Jailbreak Problem, SpaceX Acquires Cursor ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 10 mins

1. The Anthropic-Trump administration standoff has deeper roots than a single phone call. Anthropic gave the administration a pre-approved list of 111 organizations for Mythos access, then ~50 additional entities received access without notification — including a South Korean telecom suspected of China ties. That breach of process, layered on top of a prior dispute with the Department of War, gave an already-skeptical White House a constituency for broad sanctions. The administration's demand: fully account for all Mythos recipients and jailbreak-proof Fable before sanctions lift.

2. The alleged Fable 5 jailbreak was almost certainly not a jailbreak in any meaningful sense. Cybersecurity expert Katie Moussouris (CEO of Luta Security, reviewing the White House report at Anthropic's request, unpaid) found that the "bypass" involved asking Fable to "fix this code" after it declined "review the code for security issues" — standard cyberdefense workflow. Moussouris concluded it was "the model working as intended," and noted GPT-5.5 has the same capabilities. Anthropic contests whether what the administration showed them even constitutes a jailbreak at all.

3. Demanding a jailbreak-free AI model reflects a category error about how probabilistic systems work. Encryption is deterministic — you can define hard boundaries in math, making it impossible to distinguish "good" from "bad" use cases. AI is probabilistic: jailbreaks work by tipping a probability function through creative prompting, not by exploiting a binary flaw. There is no version of Fable that can fix buggy code for defenders but refuse to identify vulnerabilities for attackers — the same capability is both. Restricting it via export controls leaves U.S. defenders worse off while foreign and open-weight models close the gap within months.

4. Anthropic created its own political trap by leading with dramatic danger claims without explaining the underlying mechanics. The Project Glasswing announcement described Mythos as able to "spot vulnerabilities that survived decades of human review and millions of automated security tests" — but never explained *why* a probabilistic model can do this while also being impossible to fully constrain. That omission left the administration with a picture of a terrifyingly capable weapon and no framework for understanding why a perfect safety guarantee is impossible. Dario Amodei writes sophisticated essays aimed at people already convinced of AGI's importance; he has not meaningfully communicated with those positioned to erect barriers.

5. SpaceX's $60 billion all-stock acquisition of Anysphere (Cursor) is structurally clever: it exploits a tiny post-IPO float. SpaceX shares rose 19%, 20%, and ~5% on its first three trading days, pushing market cap to ~$2.66 trillion — but with a small float, that price is artificially elevated. By paying in stock now, before most lock-ups expire (deal expected to close Q3) but after SpaceX is added to major indices, SpaceX buys Cursor's proprietary coding-interaction data and product distribution at what will likely prove a discounted dilution cost. Cursor, already using SpaceX compute, has teased a 1.5-trillion-parameter general-purpose model trained from scratch that claims to rival Opus and the latest OpenAI models in size.

**Quotable:**
> "The same holds for every capable AI model, including the foreign and open-weight systems the United States cannot reach with export controls, many of which will match Fable and Mythos capabilities within months. Will all the US based models be export controlled?" — *Katie Moussouris, CEO of Luta Security, on the futility of restricting cyberdefense-capable AI*

### Going Private: Private capital liquidity lifeline meets resistance 📌
**Bloomberg** · email · 9 mins

1. **Continuation vehicles are proliferating but losing credibility through overuse.** Private capital continuation funds hit 133 in 2025, raising $78.75 billion (Preqin), but the first five months of 2026 show a slower pace — 42 vehicles raising $32.8 billion. Jeremy Lytle of ECI Partners identified the core problem: GPs keep pitching each rolled asset as a "once in a decade" opportunity, and LPs have stopped believing it. Only about 50% of single-asset continuation transactions actually close, per Nigel Dawn of Evercore at a Coller Capital event in April.

2. **LPs are shifting scrutiny from headline fees to DPI — and the numbers are damning.** Investors are deprioritizing TVPI (what a manager *believes* their portfolio is worth) in favor of DPI (what they've actually returned). A Carta study of 2,775 VC funds closed 2017–Q1 2026 found that 2019 and 2020 vintage funds have median DPIs barely above zero, and less than half have returned any capital at all. Across the 2017–2018 vintages, fewer than 20% have reached 1x DPI — meaning LPs are sitting on paper gains with little real cash to show.

3. **Carry crystallization in continuation vehicles is the flashpoint.** Steffen Pauls (Moonfare founder) says carry is the most exposed fee because there are simply fewer gains to share. Where managers use continuation vehicles to lock in carry without adequate third-party price validation or genuine LP optionality, investors are pushing back hard — demanding reinvestment of those earnings to prove alignment. Pauls' verdict: "Where it's a GP marking its own homework, it won't [survive]."

4. **Goldman Sachs expects a private markets distribution boom by 2028, anchored by the SpaceX IPO moment.** Pete Lyon (co-head of Goldman's Capital Solutions) and Michael Brandmeyer (CIO, Goldman's External Investing Group) forecast distributions returning to 15–20% by 2028, potentially exceeding the 2021 peak within two to three years. The record-breaking SpaceX IPO — which closed June 12 at a $75 billion valuation — has reignited confidence that the IPO pipeline can reopen and provide the exit routes PE firms have been waiting for.

5. **Emerging PE managers are beating established rivals on the metric that matters most.** A Sagard Private Equity Solutions study of 184 buyout funds found specialist emerging managers hit 1x DPI roughly 15 months faster than established firms. The mechanism: smaller funds, concentrated bets, and founding partners personally committing 5–10% of fund size — giving them the same urgency to return capital that LPs have. Meanwhile, Advent International is nearing final close of a $26 billion flagship — its largest ever — defying the LP capital crunch by pointing to marquee exits like the €29.4 billion TK Elevator sale to Kone and the Innio Holding data-center IPO.

**Quotable:**
> "Where a continuation vehicle offers genuine LP choice, third-party price validation and transparent fee treatment, crystallizing carry is legitimate and will survive. Where it's a GP marking its own homework, it won't." — *Steffen Pauls, Founder & Co-CEO, Moonfare*

### The bad gnus on SpaceX 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. SpaceX debuted with a $2.75 trillion market cap, narrowly pushing Amazon ($2.65T) into sixth place, and will soon be added to Nasdaq's indexes. Whether or not it's fairly valued, its forced inclusion into passive funds creates an acute allocation problem: index investors must buy it regardless of opinion, pouring money into a company whose valuation rests on heroic assumptions.

2. Passive investing is a misnomer at this scale of concentration. Benchmark risk compels institutional managers to hold stocks they actively dislike, because being "underweight" a 7.9% S&P 500 constituent like Nvidia is a massive active bet against it. SocGen's Andrew Lapthorne notes the top 20 MSCI All-World stocks are on average 60x bigger than the average of the other 2,500 names — a ratio that exploded post-pandemic and post-ChatGPT — so fund managers herd into these names purely for self-preservation, further eroding market efficiency. This is Paul Woolley's "curse of the benchmarks" made flesh.

3. The concentration problem is worse in emerging markets than the US. The MSCI EM index's five largest stocks — TSMC, Samsung, SK Hynix, Tencent, and Alibaba — account for a full third of the 1,205-stock index. Taiwan and South Korea together represent 49% of the index despite having a combined GDP of ~$3 trillion, versus China's $21 trillion at only 21% weight. DataTrek's Nicholas Colas calls MSCI EM "captured" by two countries not because of economic size but because of AI-chip dominance.

4. The MSCI EAFE (developed markets ex-US) dodges concentration but pays a steep price: its only AI exposure is ASML at 2.85%, with the rest of the top five being HSBC, Roche, AstraZeneca, and Novartis. Earnings forecasts for chips are growing at "staggering speed" in US and EM indexes, while EAFE investors are shut out — driving EM's biggest run of outperformance over EAFE since the post-GFC period.

5. A record 80% of BofA's global fund manager survey respondents named semiconductors the world's most crowded trade — the most-crowded "most crowded" reading since the survey began. Forced buying by index trackers makes the crowd nearly impossible to disperse, and SpaceX's imminent index inclusion will force yet another round of reluctant purchases at whatever price the market demands.

**Quotable:**
> "The problem is that benchmark risk forces many institutional investors to take positions in stocks they do not even like. The top 20 stocks are on average 60x bigger than the average of the other 2500 names in MSCI AC World; being on the wrong side when they move up is painful, so you hold them, simply to keep up with the benchmark. And if more funds look like the index? Why not just buy the index." — *Andrew Lapthorne, quantitative strategist at Societe Generale*

### Volatility Drag is What's Missing from Inequality Discussions 📎
**Capital Gains** · email · 6 mins

1. The dynastic wealth puzzle reveals a paradox: if the 1900 US census millionaires had simply invested in a balanced mix of equity index funds and bonds, *The Missing Billionaires* estimates we'd have 16,000 billionaires today — yet only 10% of the Forbes 400 are descended from people on the original 1982 list, let alone 1900. John Jacob Astor owned a few percent of Manhattan (worth ~$1.74T today); compounding a 4% cap rate reinvested in bonds over 200 years should have produced a family fortune approaching $1 trillion split among hundreds of heirs. Instead, there's one Astor who's rich by UK standards and a Viscount.

2. Volatility drag, not bad luck, explains the disappearance of dynastic wealth. Most people get rich by owning a single volatile asset, and funding even a modest lifestyle from an undiversified portfolio is the equivalent of levering up a single-stock position. A backtest of leveraged ETFs to 1927 shows the effect concretely: 2x S&P 500 leverage produces a bumpy but higher net worth, while 3x leverage makes you *poorer* — the Great Depression losses are so large they're never recovered, and any fixed living costs accelerate the path to zero.

3. Bill Gates vs. Steve Ballmer illustrates the trade-off at the extreme: Gates has spent decades systematically selling Microsoft and diversifying, making him less exposed to any single outcome — and that's precisely why Ballmer, who keeps essentially all his wealth in Microsoft, is now richer. Larry Ellison, Jensen Huang, Sergey Brin, and Larry Page all make the same concentrated bet. What goes untracked is everyone who made the identical bet on a company whose stock stagnated or went to zero; those people aren't in the inequality conversation because survivorship bias ensures only the winners remain visible.

4. The inequality implications split depending on the unit of analysis. For any given wealthy individual, concentration in a single volatile asset is a self-correcting mechanism — volatility drag and drawdowns bleed the fortune over time. But for the rich *in aggregate*, concentration raises mean outcomes by giving exposure to power-law winners even while it reduces median outcomes by generating massive drawdowns. When durable wealth does persist, it's informative: it signals a previously unexploited opportunity (whether in software, real estate, or something less benign like addictive products or regulatory arbitrage) — a conclusion worth reaching case by case rather than in aggregate.

**Quotable:**
> "if the millionaires identified by the US census in 1900 had invested their money in a balanced mix of equity index funds and bonds, we'd have 16,000 billionaires today. Yet only 10% of the Forbes 400 list today either are or are descended from people who were on the original list in 1982" — *on how rarely wealth compounds dynastically*

### India's Defence Execution Test 📎
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. India's listed defence companies posted their largest-ever order books in FY26 alongside some of their worst revenue numbers in years. Bharat Dynamics (India's primary missile maker) saw revenue fall 27% even as its order book swelled; Zen Technologies (simulators and counter-drones) fell 29%; Hindustan Aeronautics grew sales just 7% despite carrying an order book of Rs 2.54 lakh crore — over seven times its annual revenue. The same pattern repeats at Mazagon Dock, BEL, and Bharat Forge's defence subsidiary: swelling inventories, tightening working capital, balance sheets diverging sharply from headlines.

2. The mechanism behind the gap is milestone-based revenue recognition: a supplier only books revenue when the Ministry of Defence formally inspects and takes delivery against a contract milestone — not when it produces the equipment. Production has been outrunning acceptances. Against Rs 6.81 lakh crore of procurement approvals issued in FY26, only Rs 2.28 lakh crore of contracts were actually signed — a gap four times the comparable figure from the previous year. The Defence Secretary himself acknowledged in March 2025 that internal timelines had become "too luxurious." Notably, component and ammunition suppliers like Solar Industries and Astra Microwave are unaffected because their products move through procurement without the same milestone-by-milestone acceptance process — the divide is platforms vs. components, not PSU vs. private.

3. The aggregate order pipeline across India's ten listed defence names exceeds Rs 4.5 lakh crore, against combined annual revenue of roughly Rs 99,000 crore — implying years of theoretical backlog. But these companies trade at price-to-earnings multiples well above their historical averages, built on the assumption that orders convert into earnings on schedule. If FY27 delivers the revenue catch-up the order books imply is due, FY26 will look like a blip; if it doesn't, the sector's premium valuations face a reckoning.

4. India's public examination system is structurally gameable, and arrests of cheaters won't fix it. In a documented 2019 case, a group of top medical students were paid by Karnataka colleges to legitimately write and top entrance exams — then decline admission, allowing colleges to reclassify the seats as management-quota positions and sell them to the highest bidder. The students broke no rules; the Enforcement Directorate found nothing to prosecute. The same dynamic is playing out now: after NEET 2026 results were cancelled for 2.3 million students following alleged paper leaks, India temporarily banned Telegram (until June 22) after cheating networks used the platform to defraud candidates — a move the Internet Freedom Foundation called a "disproportionate measure that would punish ordinary users instead of addressing the systemic source of exam leaks."

**Quotable:**
> "The gap is not between ambition and capability, it is between order and acceptance." — *on why India's defence stocks show record order books but falling revenues*

### The World: Teenagers vs. social media bans 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Australia's December 2023 law — the first national ban barring under-16s from major social media apps — has largely failed to remove existing teen users six months in. About 70% of parents report that teens aged 13–15 who were on the platforms before the ban were either never removed or quickly created new accounts, according to Australia's own regulatory body overseeing implementation.

2. Age verification is trivially defeated: teenagers openly describe drawing mustaches on their faces to fool photo-based age checks. The platforms — TikTok, YouTube, Facebook, Instagram, and Snapchat — are under investigation by the Australian regulator for failing to enforce the law, and face fines of up to AU$49.5 million (~$35 million) each, but enforcement actions have not yet been issued.

3. The ban's logic depended on critical mass — if enough teens were removed, peer pressure would flip from pulling kids onto apps to normalizing life without them. That threshold hasn't been reached, so the social dynamic is unchanged. Parents like Lauren Hillier, 42, who had hoped the law would give cover to strict households, find themselves still fighting alone: her 13-year-old is still on Instagram, her 15-year-old still on Snapchat.

4. The law's real constituency may be future cohorts, not current 13–15-year-olds. Parents of today's 12-year-olds say it has given them a legal and social hook to delay onboarding — one mother is offering her son AU$2,000 (~$1,400) to stay off social media until 18. The hope is that a generation of kids who never join forms new norms, the way an earlier generation gradually stopped smoking — but this is years away and unproven.

**Quotable:**
> "The kids all laugh about it, 'What a joke, we haven't been taken off anything.'" — *Lauren Hillier, 42, Sydney mother of a 13-year-old on Instagram and 15-year-old on Snapchat*
