### "Wine-rimed lips and voices a little too loud" ⚡
**The Substack Post** · email · 13 mins

1. Reddit's AITA subreddits (/r/AmITheAsshole, /r/AITAH, /r/AmITheJerk) collectively draw millions of weekly visitors, making them likely the most-read short fiction form today — dwarfing the combined readership of every literary magazine. Isaac Kolding classes them alongside penny dreadfuls: mass-market stories revealing the preferences of large audiences, all about middle-class manners, one of prose fiction's most durable themes.

2. The AITA genre gains its "special zing" from existing in a limbo between fact and fiction — readers suspect many posts are fabricated, and hunting for fabrications is itself a key pleasure. This places AITA in a long tradition: in the early 19th century, readers often believed novels were literally true; the Irish priest Edward Mangin (1808) listed histories and biographies under the umbrella term "novel." Reality TV, autofiction, and "Based on a True Story" films all exploit the same mechanism.

3. British alcohol consumption is now at a record low, and a cottage industry of "sober influencer" content has emerged — epitomized by figures like Neva Coleman, whose viral video "everything I would tell you about alcoholism and addiction if I wasn't afraid to hurt your feelings" frames ordinary social drinking as pathology. Hannah Williams identifies this as Temperance 2.0: the starched black dress replaced by athleisure, the sarsaparilla replaced by matcha latte, but the same haughty moralism underneath.

4. The anti-drinking turn is happening at a specific societal moment: relentless self-optimization culture, collapsed boundaries between online and offline life, and a tone that blends "poor-dears paternalism" with moral scolding. Williams's counter-claim is that alcohol, consumed socially — a pint in a beer garden, orange wine after work, cheap sangria on holiday — is a legitimate social joy, not a disorder to be corrected, and that declaring you love it shouldn't automatically signal dependency.

5. Starbucks ($112 billion market cap), which sells one of the most standardized and easily automatable products in the economy, spent years streamlining stores with fewer workers and more automation to improve thin margins — and concluded it was a mistake. CEO Brian Niccol reversed course: handwritten notes on cups, ceramic cups, and "the return of great seats" drove customers to "sit and stay," with more baristas now being hired per store. Alex Imas uses this as evidence that even commodity production hits a floor where human presence creates value automation cannot.

6. Economists David Autor and Neil Thompson argue AI won't uniformly eliminate jobs — the outcome depends on *which* part of a job gets automated. When automation removes simpler tasks (as inventory management did for warehouse work), employment expands and wages fall. When it removes harder expert tasks (as accounting software did for complex bookkeeping), the remaining work becomes more specialized, wages rise, and fewer workers qualify. Same technology, opposite labor market outcomes.

7. As material abundance rises, the scarcity that matters shifts from commodities to social and mimetic properties: relationships, status, exclusivity, and what René Girard called "mimetic desire." Marx's commodity form severed products from their makers and enabled industrial scaling — but once basic needs are saturated, people don't just want more things; they want things *from specific people*, which reintroduces human labor into the production process on entirely new terms. The post-AI "hospitality economy" is the result.

8. Aaron, now a bartender at one of New York's priciest hotel lobby bars, grew up a child of Middle Eastern immigrants in a predominantly Black, low-income East Coast neighborhood, rebelled against a strict religious household, and spent four years as a volunteer firefighter. He suffered third-degree burns on both legs entering a house fire without his protective suit — driven by what he calls a "Superman complex." At 21, doctors told him he might not walk; he recovered, spent time in an Amish community rehabilitation program, bartended his way through 43 countries and 8 countries of work, and became hooked by the "fuck you money" culture of Aspen, where guests would spend $15,000 spraying Ace of Spades without blinking.

### 🧠 Community Wisdom: What great product leaders do differently, Claude vs. ChatGPT, receiving hard feedback two we… ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 20 mins

1. A product strategy that can't filter requests without you in the room is a vision statement, not a strategy. AI has turned everyone — sales, support, random engineers — into a combo PM/designer/developer overnight, so the volume of "have you considered X" requests hitting PMs is orders of magnitude higher than two years ago. Joni Hoadley's test: a new PM should be able to use the strategy to say no on day one.

2. Great product leaders make their own tradeoff reasoning legible so PMs can predict their decisions before they're made — that's the only real way to scale yourself. James Conway's complaint: too many leaders default to "don't give me problems, give me solutions" when their actual job is to make the tradeoff explicit, not to demand the answer be pre-resolved.

3. Kill your own pet projects first using the same strategy you hand to the team; if you won't apply it to your own ideas, nobody will apply it to theirs. When a VP of Sales pushes back and the leader caves, the strategy dies on the spot — so backing PMs publicly when they use the strategy to say no is non-negotiable.

4. Product strategy can be mapped as a search problem: execution = hill-climbing, discovery = hill-climbing + experimentation, strategy = choosing which search space deserves your scarce attention, leadership = deciding when to stop climbing one hill and find another. Barada's framing implies that most strategy debates are actually debates about which hill, not about how fast to climb.

5. For a non-technical person learning AI, interactive conversation with Claude beats any newsletter or course — newsletters assume a baseline vocabulary (APIs, tokens, RAG, fine-tuning) and layer jargon on jargon with each issue. The practical starting prompt: "I'm not technical. I run [type of business]. I want to understand how AI could save me time — explain things simply and ask me questions." Ethan Mollick's *Co-Intelligence* book is the top supplementary recommendation for business people who want to read rather than converse.

6. Vanilla Claude Code without customization gives ChatGPT the edge on reasoning-heavy non-technical work; with a custom harness (extra tools, thinking mode, skills), Claude beats ChatGPT. Practitioners like Mika Reyes and Tyrone Ross have landed on a 3-tool split: ChatGPT for planning and writing prompts, Claude/Cursor for coding, Codex or Perplexity for research and audits — cheaper than a single Max plan and avoids token burn.

7. The core pre-PMF failure mode is "technology in search of a buyer": you simultaneously need a market that values the product, a mechanism for reaching that market, and a mechanism for being compensated — and most early-stage teams are missing all three at once. Joshua Herzig-Marx's prescription: niche down much further than feels comfortable, because defining the first step toward one narrow audience is not abandoning the vision, it's the only viable path toward it.

8. For B2B2C startups, pick one side of the marketplace and focus hard there while using shortcuts for the other — don't try to balance supply and demand until the core exchange is proven. Lennybot's framing: frameworks like Lean Canvas and jobs-to-be-done are "permission structures," not signal; what actually matters is 20–30 conversations with people who could be customers.

9. A first PM earns the right to push for strategic redirection by (1) owning all tactical implementation with total reliability — project management, running meetings, answering the phone — and (2) visibly holding the anxiety for company outcomes by showing every action traces back to what the company is trying to achieve. Joshua Herzig-Marx calls these the two consistent behaviors he sees in successful people at early-stage companies.

10. Blanket condemning language ("garbage," "very little good") delivered with no prior negative feedback and no attempt to understand the employee's perspective is a red flag for a toxic environment, not evidence of a PM failure. The anonymous poster in Thread 5 had received positive feedback from all three collaborators, ran customer discovery calls that account executives praised, and was hired specifically on the strength of the "crappy take-home" the CTO attacked — the eng lead was privately sending negative reports to the CTO from week one. The community consensus: quit (which the poster did), and do more due diligence on feedback culture before joining next time.

**Quotable:**
> "Write a strategy sharp enough that a new PM could use it to say no on day one. If it doesn't filter most requests without you in the room, it's a vision statement, not a strategy." — *Joni Hoadley, on why sharp product strategy is more urgent than ever in the AI era*

## Investing & Financial Markets

### DealBook: Wine's fraud problem 📌
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 9 mins

1. Wine is unusually fertile ground for financial crime right now. British con man James Wellesley was sentenced Monday to 10 years in prison for a $100 million scheme in which he told 140+ investors they were making collateralized loans to wine collectors — neither the wine nor the collectors existed. The same week, Patrick Briones, former top wine buyer at Albertsons, pleaded guilty to bribery for taking kickbacks from wine sellers, and winemaker Jeffry Hill was sentenced for a $2.5 million grape-mislabeling scam.

2. Wine fraud is structurally endemic, not a new crisis. The first big modern scandal was vintner Fred Franzia's 1993 "blessing of the loads" scheme — fraudulently passing off inferior grapes as zinfandel, costing consumers $55 million and earning his firm a $2.5 million fine. Rudy Kurniawan then ran the most prolific modern scheme, selling an estimated $150 million in counterfeit wine between 2002 and 2012 before being convicted in 2013. Around the same time, Robert Dahl killed his business investor Emad Tawfilis — chasing him through a vineyard with a gun — after Tawfilis discovered Dahl had been misappropriating funds.

3. Enforcement, not crime levels, drives the apparent ebb and flow of wine fraud. Former federal prosecutor Benjamin Kingsley told DealBook that Napa Valley's geographic and cultural isolation made investigation extremely difficult, and that a "lull" in visible scandals during the 2000s reflected a lack of dogged agents and prosecutors, not actual absence of crime. After the 2008 financial crisis refocused money and attention on Napa, prosecutions multiplied: a Whitehall Lane Winery bookkeeper was charged with embezzling $600,000 in 2015, and wine dealer John E. Fox admitted to selling $20 million in "phantom wine" futures between 2010 and 2015.

4. Wine's supply chain is uniquely opaque, making detection nearly impossible. Maureen Downey, founder of Chai Consulting and a leading wine fraud expert, describes it as "more opaque than guns or illicit drugs." Producers suppress disclosure to protect brand reputations, victims avoid coming forward to hide gullibility, and governments underfund enforcement — creating a near-perfect environment for ongoing crime.

5. The wine industry's severe economic downturn since 2022 adds pressure without being the root cause. Per the International Organization of Vine and Wine, global wine consumption is at its lowest since the 1960s; Gallup found U.S. adult alcohol consumption fell from 60% to 54% between 2023 and 2025. Silicon Valley Bank's Rob McMillan reports that wineries with "very weak" financial health have nearly tripled since 2022, with 30% of the industry struggling. But McMillan also notes: "Through all economies over the last 30 years, we've had crime" — fraud flourishes in boom and bust alike because the product's mystique and the inability of most consumers to distinguish a $20 bottle from a $2,000 one makes deception almost frictionless.

**Quotable:**
> "It's getting bigger and getting worse because nobody wants to talk about it. The producers don't want to admit that it's happening, victims don't want to come forward and governments don't want to invest time going after it." — *Maureen Downey, founder of Chai Consulting and wine fraud expert*

### Revisiting The Outcome Distortion Complex 📌
**Kyle Harrison from Investing 101** · email · 8 mins

1. Good outcomes grant moral license to the means that produced them — and bad outcomes retroactively condemn them. When Harrison read Amy Chua's *Battle Hymn of the Tiger Mother* and found her methods harsh (berating daughters into violin and piano practice), he suppressed his discomfort because her daughters turned out accomplished. The moment he learned her husband faced sexual harassment accusations, the license evaporated instantly. The same dynamic governs how investors judge companies: "maximizing shareholder wealth" functions as a catch-all that can justify any behavior, which is precisely why it's dangerous.

2. The one-click checkout space raised over $1B in funding across multiple companies on a coherent thesis — control the customer checkout relationship across merchant sites — yet the leading player never generated more than $600K in revenue and is now defunct, with another being sued by its largest customer. Terra Luna launched with a vision of making Bitcoin a payments mechanism, then lost 99.9% of its value and wiped out $40B from the broader crypto market (for context: Bitcoin processes ~4 transactions per second vs. Visa's 3,000+). These weren't random failures — they were good ideas executed under investor-fueled excess.

3. Venture investors drove founders to build for ZIRP conditions, then the same investors refused accountability when those conditions reversed. A 2022 quote (unattributed in the piece): "We collectively told companies to build in a very different culture than the culture we have today, economically... I think we collectively as investors need to acknowledge our huge role in this." In 2026, the same cycle is playing out again with AI — SaaS stocks dying while markets rip ahead — and no reckoning is on the horizon. "This time is different" is the clarion call of every major capital destruction event.

4. Uber ($152B market cap, $52B+ in revenue) and WeWork (~$3M, in bankruptcy) illustrate the asymmetric moral cover outcomes provide. Uber faced documented accusations of covering up sexual assault and harassment; WeWork wiped out ~$20B of invested capital. The success lets Uber brush nearly anything under the rug; the failure turns WeWork into a cautionary tale. Josh Wolfe said in 2022 he still believed there is "massive accounting fraud" at Tesla — but Tesla has produced more cars than any company founded after 1967, and the outcome creates pressure that silences even legitimate skepticism.

5. Katherine Boyle's "American Dynamism" thesis at a16z argues VC, for all its faults, is more effective than government at tackling national-interest sectors (aerospace, defense, public safety, housing, supply chain, manufacturing) because "professional investors, even in a bull market, care more about value creation than bureaucrats who are not measured by or compensated for their success." The prescription for individual investors: be conviction-driven — imagine the world you want to exist, then ask whether the companies you back make that world more or less likely.

**Quotable:**
> "We collectively told companies to build in a very different culture than the culture we have today, economically. I think we collectively as investors need to acknowledge our huge role in this." — *unnamed VC in 2022, cited as representative of a pattern repeating in 2026*

### Why there's no such thing as the petrodollar 📎
**FT Weekend** · email · 4 mins

> **Note:** The source file is the FT Weekend newsletter email (April 25, 2026). The petrodollar essay by Brendan Greeley is featured as the headline piece but is linked rather than reproduced in full. The digest below draws on the teaser, the essay title, and Greeley's stated thesis as framed by FT Weekend editor Janine Gibson.

1. The "petrodollar" concept — the idea that dollar hegemony is upheld by a 1970s deal to price oil exclusively in USD — is widely misunderstood. FT markets editors characterize the conventional petrodollar narrative as "nonsense" and "balderdash": the causal arrow most people assume (oil pricing → dollar demand → dollar dominance) is either backward or vastly overstated. Brendan Greeley, FT contributing editor and author of the forthcoming *The Almighty Dollar: 500 Years of the World's Most Powerful Money*, makes this the centerpiece of his essay.

2. Dollar dominance has roots going back five centuries — long predating the Nixon-era oil arrangements. The title of Greeley's book signals his argument: the dollar's power comes from deep historical foundations (financial market depth, network effects, rule of law, institutional credibility) rather than from Saudi Arabia's pricing decisions. A shift away from dollar-denominated oil contracts would not, on this view, meaningfully undermine the dollar's reserve-currency status.

### Longreads + Open Thread 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 8 mins

1. Claude Sonnet 4.7 can identify the author of a text with eerie accuracy — even for emails, old school assignments, and fiction never posted online — by pattern-matching against a large existing corpus. Hobart tested this on himself and confirms it works, though the model fabricates its reasoning when asked to explain. This doesn't end pseudonymity entirely: it means anonymity is available to the unknown, but once someone becomes famous under a pseudonym, they're permanently tied to it. A partial countermeasure is running text through AI before publishing pseudonymously, but this either produces AI-flavored prose or reveals that "pen names" are now essentially just long prompts.

2. Books are cheaper in real inflation-adjusted terms than they were in the 1960s, yet feel more expensive — explained by price anchoring set in childhood. People who grew up buying books (e.g., a $5.99 Star Wars paperback in the mid-90s) anchor to those low prices and experience any increase as a loss; people who came to books later anchor at already-higher prices. The upwardly mobile who shift media consumption toward books encounter price increases precisely when their incomes are growing fastest, compounding the distortion.

3. The overnight anomaly — where equities and other asset classes earn most of their returns during off-hours trading — gets conspiratorial treatment from some researchers, but market manipulation is a poor explanation. Sustained price-fixing conspiracies are nearly impossible to maintain when any participant can defect by simply opening a second brokerage account and trading against their co-conspirators. The only market conspiracies that do work operate on timescales so short there's no window to defect.

4. In competitive data businesses, the hardest moat to attack is full-screen incumbency — a product that lives permanently on a trader's or analyst's primary monitor is nearly impossible to displace. Challengers therefore default to API-first distribution, which brings in sophisticated early customers who provide high-quality feedback, making the product progressively more appealing. With UI-building now cheap via AI-assisted coding, serious new entrants will be born headless and either adopt a minimal default interface or piggyback on someone else's.

5. Shareholder activism — the practice of acquiring a stake and pressuring management — is a disproportionately American phenomenon compared to hostile takeovers, which occur globally. The cause is historical: the U.S. developed strong pro-shareholder norms because the state was less involved in the economy than elsewhere, making corporate governance more consequential. U.S. capital market liquidity then amplifies activism: once an activist enters, it can trigger a preference cascade that rapidly replaces a company's existing shareholder base with activist-aligned investors.

6. *Reentry* (the SpaceX book) illustrates a specific risk-tolerance pattern: committing to a launch deadline before knowing what obstacles stand between now and it. This functions as a forcing mechanism — useful for software prototypes, genuinely dangerous when the hardware is rocket-shaped and filled with explosive fuel. SpaceX's talent pipeline also complicates the "scrappy upstart vs. lazy NASA" narrative: many key people, including NASA flight director Holly Ridings (who witnessed the *Challenger* explosion in class and resolved to prevent recurrences), came directly from or through NASA. The book's counterfactual is stark — a slightly different sequence of events would have made SpaceX an expensive cautionary tale rather than a success story, suggesting that consistently beating long odds is itself a distributable skill, not just luck.

## Philosophy & Personal Development

### How to actually stop caring what people think (and why it changes everything) 📌
**The Big Think Interview** · email · 6 mins

1. The entire self-help industry optimizes for the wrong thing. Aristotle drew a line between Hedonia (pleasure, comfort, short-term dopamine) and Eudaimonia (deep purpose, a life worth living with zero regrets). Modern life — social media, consumer products, 30-day wellness fixes — is engineered to maximize Hedonia. But the research and lived experience both show it's Eudaimonia that actually drives life satisfaction. Pursuing comfort harder doesn't get you there; it actively crowds it out.

2. The Backwards Law (borrowed from Alan Watts) explains why chasing good feelings backfires structurally: the act of chasing a positive experience is itself a negative experience, while accepting a negative experience is itself positive. Every instance follows the same pattern — the harder you try to impress people, the less impressive you feel; the more love you need, the lonelier you become; the more spiritual growth you pursue, the more self-centered you grow. The self-help treadmill is a direct consequence of this law: each hit requires the next.

3. The correct question isn't "what will make me happy?" but "what am I willing to struggle for?" — or, as Manson puts it when speaking to audiences, "what kind of masochist are you?" Meaning lives in the specific pain you secretly enjoy that most people don't: the struggle you relish, the challenges you're most proud of overcoming. Happiness isn't pursued and achieved; it's a side effect that arrives automatically once you've found something worth caring about.

**Quotable:**
> "Instead of asking yourself, what's going to make me happy? Ask yourself, what am I willing to struggle for? What are the problems I actually kind of like having in my life?" — *Mark Manson, on the question that reorients everything*

### The Morning: Lights in the sky 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Karl Lemstrom, a Finnish scientist, spent the late 19th century trying to replicate the aurora borealis using a complex copper-wire apparatus meant to channel atmospheric electricity. He had the physics wrong — auroras are caused by charged particles from the solar wind interacting with Earth's magnetic field — but his wires did produce faint luminosity, likely St. Elmo's fire. He died believing he had succeeded in creating artificial auroras.

2. Scientists are still unraveling auroras in 2026: the NYT recently reported on a new 10,000-antenna radar array in Norway designed to answer specific open questions, including what drives variations in aurora density and why they move. Meanwhile, electromagnetism underlies phenomena most people never consciously register: birds navigate using Earth's magnetic field, sharks detect prey via electroreception, and the human body runs continuously on electrical signals.

3. Understanding the physics of the northern lights does nothing to account for the emotional experience of seeing them. Kirsch, who stumbled upon them accidentally while driving a rented pickup through North Dakota cornfields on an ordinary September night, describes electric-green towers and skyscrapers spanning from road to sky — and reports feeling deep gratitude for days afterward, a sense that beauty can emerge from nothingness without any effort on the observer's part.
