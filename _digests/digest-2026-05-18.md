## Today's Top 3

### Inside the brain's nightly erasure of "you" ⚡
**Big Think** · email · 4 mins

1. Every night, consciousness vanishes completely during deep non-REM sleep — not fades, but ceases. High-density EEG studies show that when slow-wave activity dominates the posterior cortical regions, subjective experience is absent. Cortical neurons fall into synchronized oscillations punctuated by silence; to be inside a slow wave is to be nowhere at all.

2. REM sleep reverses this collapse while cutting off the body: the brainstem actively paralyzes the muscles, yet fast desynchronized firing in the posterior cortex reaches activity levels that match — and in some regions exceed — waking levels. The visual cortex shows similar activation patterns as when processing a real scene, producing internally generated episodes of consciousness with no sensory input.

3. Ordinary dreaming is consciousness without metacognition — full immersion with no capacity to observe oneself from outside the moment. Lucid dreaming is the precise exception: when dreamers gain insight into their dream state, fMRI and EEG data show partial reactivation of the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, and precuneus — regions that support self-monitoring and are otherwise offline during REM. The experience itself doesn't change; only the meta-awareness of it does.

**Quotable:**
> "Dreaming is being without knowing that you are." — *on ordinary REM consciousness, stripped of metacognition*

### Why we're at the beginning of the AI hardware boom | Caitlin Kalinowski (ex–OpenAI, Meta, Apple) ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 5 mins

1. **A memory price shock is coming for robotics startups, and the window to act is now.** Memory prices are spiking—potentially doubling or more—driven by AI data center demand. Consumer robotics companies can't compete on price with data centers, which are buying in massive volume. Kalinowski is actively advising startups to pre-buy memory and stockpile components while they still can, warning: "We are in trouble as an industry."

2. **VR failed as a consumer product but succeeded as an R&D program for everything that followed.** The technologies built for headsets—SLAM (simultaneous localization and mapping), depth sensors, spatial computing—are now the foundational stack for robotics, autonomous vehicles, and military drones. The core capability VR required (understanding how an object moves through space) is identical to what a robot needs, which is why ex-VR engineers are now central to the robotics boom.

3. **The entire U.S. hardware supply chain—from raw magnets to actuators to final assembly—has been offshored, and that's a national security crisis.** Over 25 years, essentially every layer was moved to China, Japan, and Korea. The same actuator technology that spins a drone rotor also moves a robot arm, meaning adversarial control of the supply chain is adversarial control of both civilian robotics and military hardware. Kalinowski's conclusion: "We need to re-industrialize this country significantly in order to be safe in a military sense."

**Quotable:**
> "What you can do behind a keyboard with AI is going to saturate." — *On why the frontier is shifting from software to physical hardware, and why labs like OpenAI are now investing in robotics*

## AI & Tech

### Apple's Siri app 📌
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 8 mins

1. Apple's privacy-first strategy — which lets dedicated privacy teams veto features and restricts real user data collection in favor of synthetic data — has directly caused Siri to fall behind Google, Anthropic, and OpenAI. Rather than compete on capability, Apple plans to use its privacy stance as cover: positioning the trade-off as a deliberate moral choice rather than an engineering failure, which gives it a ready-made narrative for whenever the new Siri underperforms rivals.

2. iOS 27's revamped Siri quietly swaps out Apple's own models for Google's Gemini — a 1.2 trillion parameter model — while keeping the "Apple Foundation Models" branding. Apple is also leaning on Google's cloud infrastructure to run parts of the new Siri at scale, which contradicts the company's Private Cloud Compute privacy pitch; Apple has been careful not to confirm that the new Siri will use the same chips, data centers, and security protections as its current Apple Intelligence features.

3. The first-ever standalone Siri app in iOS 27 is designed to compete directly with ChatGPT and Claude: users can open a persistent chat history, enter "Search or Ask" mode via a swipe from the top center of the screen, and choose auto-deletion of conversations at 30 days, one year, or never. Unlike rivals such as Meta — which just launched an optional incognito mode — Apple's position is that privacy controls should be the default state, not a toggle users have to find.

4. Despite a two-year delay (the revamped Siri was originally promised for 2024), internal iOS 27 builds still label the new Siri as a "beta" and include a toggle to opt out. The original Siri launched as a beta in 2011 and shed that label in 2013 — meaning Apple may ship an unfinished AI assistant under a beta label for the second time, while Google is already rolling out Android 17 with Gemini Intelligence doing the app-level task completion Apple promised at WWDC 2024 but has yet to deliver. Gurman estimates Apple is one to two years behind Google on those capabilities.

**Quotable:**
> "Siri has still felt like a beta, or even rougher, for the better part of 15 years." — *on Apple potentially shipping the new Siri under a beta label again, just as the original was in 2011*

## Geopolitics & Security

### Energy crisis nears a tipping point 📌
**FirstFT Asia** · email · 6 mins

1. The blockaded Strait of Hormuz has pushed nearly 80 countries into emergency economic measures, with Brent crude trading at ~$109/barrel and global fuel stocks falling at the fastest rate on record. Aberdeen chief economist Paul Diggle is now stress-testing a scenario where Brent rockets to $180/barrel, which his team models as triggering surging inflation and recessions across Europe and Asia — not yet his base case, but one he calls "very serious."

2. Peak summer demand is arriving at the worst possible moment. Air conditioning load and holiday travel will simultaneously spike demand for crude, gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel precisely when supply is most constrained by the near-closure of Hormuz. Australia has committed $10bn to emergency fuel and fertilizer stockpiles; India has gone further, urging citizens to forgo gold purchases and foreign holidays to protect the country's foreign currency reserves.

3. The crisis is cascading into freight and industry well beyond the oil price itself. Shipping lines have abandoned the Gulf in favor of costlier alternative land routes, generating severe backlogs and surcharges for importers. EU transport commissioner Apostolos Tzitzikostas, speaking at an FT conference in Athens, put the stakes plainly: if the Hormuz Strait is not reopened "in the coming weeks," a global recession "could be on the table."

**Quotable:**
> "We are living on borrowed time." — *Paul Diggle, chief economist, Aberdeen, on the $180/barrel oil scenario*

### The World: He's forging the future of war 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 6 mins

1. **Mykhailo Fedorov, Ukraine's 35-year-old defense minister (appointed four months ago), is pursuing a strategy of attrition via autonomous drones.** The explicit goal is to raise Russian casualties from 35,000 killed and wounded per month to more than 50,000 — enough to outpace Moscow's ability to recruit replacements. Fedorov's background is digital advertising, not the military, and he is pushing for drones capable of making lethal decisions without a human in the loop.

2. **Ukraine is turning its battlefield footage into geopolitical currency.** Fedorov is managing a library of more than five million annotated drone videos — including footage of how humans behave as killer drones close in (running, hiding) — and last month opened that data to companies from allied nations on a reciprocal basis: share your AI model with Ukraine, gain access to the dataset. He is also working directly with Palantir CEO Alex Karp to integrate AI into Ukraine's military, and has met with former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, whose venture fund D3 is focused on weapons development in Ukraine.

3. **The autonomous-weapons push has opened a civilian-military fault line inside Ukraine.** Fedorov has never served in the military; an adviser from his office publicly called a Ukrainian armored-vehicle assault "a crime" after soldiers were killed — and the unit fired back that if the adviser knew better, he should enlist. Human rights groups warn that delegating lethal decisions to machines crosses a moral red line and risks unlawful civilian casualties. Fedorov's response: "The risks are not as high as you think."

**Quotable:**
> "Autonomous weapons are the new nuclear weapons. Countries that possess them will be protected." — *Mykhailo Fedorov, Ukraine's defense minister*

### Sector Refresh 📌
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 7 mins

1. Three structural shifts in the public tech market forced Gustafson to expand from 8 to 9 sector indexes: AI infrastructure companies (CoreWeave, Cerebras) IPO'd with capital structures that simply didn't exist as public comps two years ago; fintech multiples diverged sharply between B2B payments, B2C payments, and crypto exchanges; and back-office and MarTech SaaS got "carpet bombed into oblivion," making a clean generic "SaaS" bucket meaningless for benchmarking operators.

2. A brand-new "Take-Rate Platforms" sector (19 companies: Uber, Airbnb, Shopify, DoorDash, Toast, Lyft, etc.) was carved out of Vertical SaaS specifically because transaction-volume businesses earn money on GMV, not seat licenses — mixing them with pure-software Vertical SaaS was distorting the median multiples. A hospitality or commerce CFO using the old Vertical SaaS comp set was comparing their unit economics against the wrong model entirely.

3. The old 28-company FinTech bucket was split into two: "Payments & Money Movement" (11 companies — Adyen, PayPal, Block, BILL — the payment rails, volume game, utility margins) and "Consumer Fintech, Lending & Crypto" (16 companies — Coinbase, Robinhood, Klarna, Affirm — CAC-driven, marketing-heavy). These two groups have structurally different cost structures and valuation logic and should never have shared a median.

4. Seven legacy hardware and infrastructure names were cut entirely — HPE, NetApp, Cisco, Lumen, Rackspace, Newegg, BigCommerce — because they dragged sector medians and have no natural home in a software-native benchmark universe. The total tracked universe is now 138 companies across 9 sectors.

**Quotable:**

### The AI stock boom is starting to feel nostalgic 📎
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 5 mins

1. Today's AI-driven market concentration mirrors the "Nifty Fifty" bubble of the late 1960s–70s, per analyst Richard Abbey. The top 10 S&P 500 companies now make up 40% of the index's value — nearly matching the Nifty Fifty era when 50 stocks held ~45%. Polaroid, the canonical Nifty Fifty name, controlled two-thirds of the instant-camera market yet traded at 90x earnings before the bubble burst.

2. Abbey's core warning: the AI giants are building toward direct competition with each other, and head-to-head rivalry will compress margins even as it benefits consumers. The parallel to Polaroid is structural — dominant market share and sky-high multiples held right up until competing technologies eroded the moat. Business forecasters are already turning pessimistic on margins, with rising costs from energy, aluminum, copper, and freight (tied to Iran war disruptions) set to filter through slowly.

3. Elsewhere in the newsletter: GameStop, sitting on $9 billion in cash against a $10 billion market cap, bid for eBay (valued at $48 billion) — CEO Ryan Cohen proposing a $28 billion cash / $28 billion stock split. eBay rejected it. Matt Levine notes the offer's appeal was "Ryan Cohen's management and meme-stockery," drawing comparisons to Elon Musk buying Twitter and David Ellison acquiring Warner Bros. — corporate finance rewarding boldness over traditional deal logic.

**Quotable:**
> "The AI monsters now being built will ultimately compete against one another. Head-to-head competition, while good for consumers, is all but certain to damage profits." — *Richard Abbey, on why the AI rally echoes the Nifty Fifty*

### The Forecast: Are prediction markets working? 📎
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 6 mins

1. **The 2024 legal breakthrough unleashed markets the Nobel laureates never envisioned.** In 2008, 22 economists — including Nobel laureates Kenneth Arrow, Paul Milgrom, Thomas Schelling, Robert Shiller, and Vernon Smith — published a manifesto in *Science* calling prediction markets a "potent research tool" and explicitly stating CFTC-regulated markets "presumably would not include contracts on the outcomes of sports events." Their lobbying went nowhere for 16 years. Then an October 2024 appeals court ruled that Kalshi's congressional election futures were not gambling, and the boom began — only now Kalshi and Polymarket's home pages are dominated by sports bets and celebrity "mention markets" on whether a celebrity will use a particular word in a speech.

2. **Academic research now confirms most traders lose money, and a new psychological hazard called "prediction laundering" distorts public perception.** A majority of accounts are losing money on these platforms. University of Toronto computer scientist Yasaman Rohanifar identified "prediction laundering" — the mechanism by which attaching a percentage to an outcome (e.g., "72%") makes it feel like objective truth to observers, even when it reflects, as one trader put it, "just a mess of guesses and incentives." Both Kalshi and Polymarket defend this by arguing their platforms are more accessible than traditional financial markets.

3. **The one area where prediction markets deliver on the original promise is macro-forecasting — but the hedging rationale remains theoretical.** The Federal Reserve found that Kalshi contracts improve on consensus expert surveys for GDP growth and unemployment forecasts, unique value since no existing financial markets generate such predictions. The laureates' other key argument — that liquid markets would let companies hedge political risk, like buying insurance against a specific election outcome — remains "largely theoretical" and "blatantly doesn't apply to many, if not most" current contracts. Where to draw the regulatory line (markets like Strait of Hormuz reopening odds vs. sports gambling) will likely fall to the Supreme Court.

**Quotable:**
> "It's funny because once I see a number attached to a possible outcome, it stops feeling like an opinion and more like... real truth. It doesn't matter who is betting or why. You just see 72% and it feels like that is the truth, even though I know it is coming from... just a mess of guesses and incentives!" — *trader interviewed by researcher Yasaman Rohanifar on the "prediction laundering" effect*
