### The long road to $1 on-chain ⚡
**Lombard Notes** · email · 13 mins

1. **ACH is the US entry rail, and its 2-day return window poisons everything downstream.** ACH — governed by Nacha (a private industry association incorporated 1974) — handled 35.2B transactions worth ~$93T in 2025 using a proprietary format, not ISO 20022. An ACH credit entry can be returned for up to two business days after settlement, meaning a dollar "received" by Circle is not legally final until that window closes — and that single rule forces every subsequent design decision in the USDC minting stack.

2. **The US runs two competing ACH clearinghouses by deliberate design; Europe runs one.** FedACH (Federal Reserve, public) and EPN (The Clearing House, private bank consortium) each process roughly 50% of ACH volume. Europe's equivalent, STEP2, is a single entity operated by EBA Clearing. Both systems do the same thing — net the positions of participating banks — but move no actual money; clearing is reconciliation, not value transfer.

3. **Settlement lands at Fedwire ($1,148T in 2025) but Circle never touches it directly.** ACH net positions settle through the Federal Reserve's National Settlement Service into master reserve accounts at the Fed — the same ultimate layer as Fedwire ($1,148T settled in 2025 vs. ~€450T for Europe's T2, roughly 3x, reflecting the dollar's reserve currency role). Circle is a state-licensed money transmitter, ineligible for a Fed master account, so it settles through BNY Mellon: the Fed credits BNY Mellon, BNY Mellon credits Circle. In December 2025 Circle received conditional OCC approval to establish First National Digital Currency Bank (a national trust bank) to eventually access Fedwire directly — but the Fed has not formally resolved whether such charters qualify, so the BNY Mellon intermediation layer remains for now.

4. **The US has no statutory equivalent of the EU Settlement Finality Directive — ACH finality is structurally weak.** The EU's SFD protects individual payment instructions from insolvency proceedings the moment they enter a designated system. The US has no single instrument doing this: Fedwire finality operates under Regulation J (a Fed regulation, not a statute), which makes transfers irrevocable when credited but lacks the SFD's explicit insolvency protection. For ACH specifically, NSS settlement of the net operator position does not extinguish the two-day return window on underlying entries — the Fed's reserve account bookkeeping and Nacha's return rules coexist. Circle's practical response is a mandatory holding period before USDC is available for withdrawal.

5. **Travel Rule compliance replicates the EU's structural gap: the payment rail doesn't carry enough data.** FinCEN's Travel Rule threshold for money services businesses is $3,000 — three times the EU's €1,000. Above it, Circle must collect originator name, address, account number, and amount. But ACH carries originator name and account number, not address — the same field missing from EU SEPA Credit Transfers. Circle fills the gap from KYC onboarding records assembled off-chain, not from the payment message itself. The threshold is higher, the format is different, but the structural incompatibility between what payment rails carry and what compliance rules require is identical on both sides of the Atlantic.

6. **Minting creates an irreversible on-chain token against a not-yet-final fiat dollar — neither MiCA nor the GENIUS Act resolves this.** Once Circle mints USDC, the token exists on-chain: publicly verifiable, immutable, unreversible through any ACH mechanism. But the backing dollar's return window is still open. Blockchain finality is probabilistic (sufficient block confirmations), not legally recognized — no court or insolvency framework treats on-chain confirmation as equivalent to a T2-settled SEPA payment under the SFD or a Fedwire transfer under Regulation J. The GENIUS Act (signed into law July 2025) and MiCA both leave this gap open: on-chain confirmation and legal finality remain two different things.

7. **~80% of USDC reserves sit in a BlackRock-managed SEC 2a-7 money market fund — structurally different from Monerium's statutory segregation.** The Circle Reserve Fund (USDXX) holds cash, short-dated US Treasuries, and overnight Treasury repo agreements, custodied at BNY Mellon; remaining cash sits at BNY Mellon, Customers Bank, and Cross River Bank. Deloitte attests monthly. The legal contrast with EU EURe is sharp: EURe holders have a statutory claim under EMD2 Article 7 — legislated per-customer segregation that survives issuer insolvency. USDC holders have a contractual claim on a money market fund governed by securities regulation, enforceable only through general insolvency proceedings. Both tokens are backed 1:1 and redeem at par in normal conditions; the difference only becomes visible when the issuer is in distress.

8. **The US assembled its stablecoin stack from parts designed for adjacent purposes; Europe built purpose-specific law 25 years ago.** Every layer of the €1 journey sits beneath legislation written for it: the SFD for fiat finality, EMD2 for reserve protection, MiCA for the token. The US stack uses state money transmitter licenses, SEC MMF regulation, and Federal Reserve policy — all repurposed. The GENIUS Act begins constructing a federal framework, but it is newer and thinner than EMD2's 25-year foundation. USD stablecoins are 99% of all stablecoins in circulation, yet they run on the less-legally-developed road.

**Quotable:**
> "The $1 road and the €1 road are the same length. One is paved. But everyone in crypto uses the other." — *concluding the US/EU regulatory comparison*

### An Investment Banker's Kind of Investment Bank ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 10 mins

1. Full-service investment banks are structurally compromised by bundling advisory, underwriting, and fairness opinions — a bank advising on a merger while also underwriting its debt has a strong incentive to talk up a bad deal rather than kill its own fees. This is the persistent force behind boutique banks: founders capture higher expected value by narrowing focus and eliminating those conflicts.

2. Lincoln International, which recently filed an S-1, grew revenue 35% last year with 27% net margins by doing almost exclusively one thing: representing PE firms and private sellers in middle-market M&A. The 27% figure flatters them — add equity distributions back in and compensation hits 72% of revenue — but that's partly a pre-IPO accounting artifact, since executives are front-loading future pay as a lump of equity priced by the market.

3. Lincoln's actual clients aren't the companies changing hands — anonymous operations like Spartaco, Maysteel, or Broen Valve Technologies that will never transact again on any reasonable timeframe. The repeat customers are the PE firms on one or both sides of every deal. PE's structural edge over public markets in owning companies worth $50–500M means 100% of shares change hands every transaction, and Lincoln captures a spread on each one.

4. Lincoln is better understood as a slow-moving exchange than as a bank. It sits at the center of a high-latency market for private companies, accumulating proprietary data on EV/EBITDA multiples across hundreds of deal types. This positions it to sell valuation attestation services to PE firms reporting NAV to their LPs — a recurring, non-cyclical revenue stream that hedges against M&A cycle volatility. The conflict-of-interest risk is structurally contained: no single client exceeded 3% of revenue in 2025, meaning the financial gain from fudging a valuation would need to be ~30x the reputational damage for it to be rational.

5. The core investor dilemma is adverse selection: Lincoln specializes in selling companies at peak valuations, so the question is why outside investors get this opportunity at all. Goldman's IPO in May 1999 is the cautionary benchmark — the S&P spent more than half the next decade in drawdown from that date. The deeper structural problem is that investment banks are effectively owned by their star producers; if the firm takes too large a cut, those bankers leave and form their own shops. Sustaining the franchise requires the Lincoln logo to compound its value slightly faster than the individual skills of the people who earn under it — a treadmill that works well until it doesn't.

**Quotable:**
> "It's the task of any growing investment bank to make the value of its logo compound a bit faster than the skill of its top producers. Which is a nicely-aligned incentive for as long as it lasts." — *on the fundamental structural tension of boutique bank IPOs*

### AI Optimization Is Mostly Just Good Marketing 📎
**Amanda Natividad** · email · 7 mins

1. The GEO/AEO industry keeps repackaging good writing as AI magic. Ahrefs' February 2026 data found that an AI Overview correlates with a 58% lower average click-through rate for the top-ranking page — so the stakes are real. But Cyrus Shepard's analysis of 54 AI citation experiments, patents, and case studies concluded that most critical factors still align with traditional SEO: "win SEO, win AI citations, most of the time, with extra steps."

2. AI visibility breaks down into four layers, not one monolithic trick. **Retrievability** is whether crawlers can even access the page — Shepard's top-ranked factor — now complicated by different AI company crawlers and sites deploying anti-scraper blocks. **Extractability** is whether the system can parse and reuse what it finds: clear definitions, structured headings, evidence close to the claim, self-contained passages — which is just good writing. **Credibility** is whether the system has reason to trust the source over generic consensus slop; original research, first-party data, named sources, and explicit methodology all signal this. **Public evidence** is the zero-click layer: Reddit threads, YouTube, LinkedIn posts, reviews, and comparison pages that AI systems and buyers use to form impressions outside your own website.

3. Credibility cannot be manufactured by publishing "complete guides." AI search sometimes surfaces the most distinctive artifact rather than the best one — and when the public record around a brand is thin, confusing, or nonexistent, both humans and machines fill the gap with whatever stray evidence exists. The actual fix is a stronger public record: customer examples, transparent methodology, expert quotes, and a presence in the credible third-party places an audience already trusts.

4. The end goal is trustworthiness, not technical compliance. Obsessing over ideal H2 header length for LLMs, or treating "LLMs are watching videos now" as a new imperative, misses that captions and transcripts were already useful for accessibility, search, and human comprehension. The frame that actually holds: be findable, be useful, be credible, be present where your audience learns, and create evidence that outlives the click.

**Quotable:**
> "The goal is not to follow every hack to trick the robots. It's to become more trustworthy. First, by people. Then, by robots." — *on what GEO actually requires*

### The Morning: Who owns the Strait? 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Iran has choked off the Strait of Hormuz — the passage through which one-fifth of the world's oil supply once flowed — using attacks on passing vessels, while the U.S. has imposed a blockade on Iranian ports. The combined effect has trapped thousands of ships in the Persian Gulf, triggering a global economic crisis. Trump's summit with Xi Jinping this week is shadowed by this standoff: Trump wants Xi to lean on Iran to reopen the strait, while U.S.-Iran negotiations are reportedly on "life support."

2. This is a recurring crisis: during the Iran-Iraq War in the 1980s, both sides attacked shipping in the Persian Gulf, spreading the conflict to a small but deadly naval war. Iranian forces intercepted vessels bound for Iraq and its allies, killing more than 400 civilian sailors and damaging 500 commercial ships along with American warships — a pattern that U.S. military planners have never stopped worrying about.

3. The legal framework is UNCLOS (the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea), which neither the U.S. nor Iran has ratified but which still binds both as "customary international law," according to James Kraska, a professor at the U.S. Naval War College. Under it, Iran may claim a 12-nautical-mile territorial limit — but only if it respects the right of free navigation through those waters. Iran's demand to charge tolls on passing ships would explicitly violate this law.

4. The clearest precedent is the Corfu Channel Case (late 1940s): Albania fired on Royal Navy warships and mined the strait between Greece and Corfu, killing dozens of sailors. The International Court of Justice — in its very first ruling — held that Britain had the right of free passage and that Albania was obligated to clear the mines. Albania, a far weaker state than Iran, complied. Kraska notes the precedent may not extend to a nation with Iran's military and geopolitical weight.

**Quotable:**
> "Fighting there would be like a knife fight in a phone booth." — *a U.S. military commander on combat in the Strait of Hormuz*
