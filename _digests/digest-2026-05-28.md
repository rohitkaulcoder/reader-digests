## Today's Top 3

### The SpaceX IPO and Data Centers in Space ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 14 mins

1. **SpaceX's IPO financials are indefensible on current numbers — but that's not the point.** SpaceX is seeking a $2 trillion valuation on $18.67 billion in revenue and $4.9 billion in losses, with growth *slowing* from 35% to 33%. The $5.1 billion AI R&D expense (for xAI, a model now in 5th place whose founding team just left) flipped the company from small profit to massive loss. The S-1's $28.5 trillion TAM — including $26.5 trillion in AI — is a joke chart. But Tesla's valuation never made financial sense either, right up until Models 3 and Y actually worked.

2. **Musk's companies win by changing the rules through scale, not by playing the existing game better.** Starlink generated $8.7 billion in revenue and $4.4 billion in profit last year. American Airlines just announced Starlink on 500+ narrowbody aircraft starting Q1 2027; United did the same in 2024. The "carrot" is faster in-flight internet; the "stick" is that every competitor will have it, forcing airlines to upgrade on their own dime — the same pattern by which Tesla forced the auto industry into EVs.

3. **Space data centers don't need to look like Earth data centers — they look like satellites.** SpaceX's V2 Mini Direct-to-Cell satellite is 7.4m × 2.7m × 0.3m and dissipates ~25kW. Nvidia's NVL72 rack (which runs a 1-trillion-parameter FP4 model) is 2.2m × 1.1m × 0.6m and uses up to 135kW. The form factor gap is small; the power gap is a hard engineering challenge but not physically impossible. Starlink satellites already interconnect via laser links, so a fleet of rack-satellites is architecturally coherent — each with solar panels and ~200+ sq meter radiator arrays for cooling.

4. **Agentic inference is the killer use case for space compute — and it's the biggest future market.** Three AI workload types are emerging: training (needs Nvidia's full stack), answer inference (needs speed), and agentic inference (work done overnight by agents following instructions from other computers). Agentic inference tolerates latency, which means it can use slower/cheaper memory instead of HBM, and doesn't need cutting-edge GPUs. This makes it uniquely compatible with the constraints of space hardware — and unlike the other two categories, its market size scales with compute availability, not human population.

5. **Terrestrial data center buildout is hitting a hard ceiling from zoning opposition, making space a non-optional alternative.** Power was the expected constraint; zoning is the surprise. Communities that had no say when factories moved to China now hold veto power over AI infrastructure. Paying communities for permission raises costs; in the long run, demand for compute may simply exceed every viable Earth location. SpaceX is already monetizing xAI's Colossus 1 ground data center at $15 billion/year for 300MW (≈3,000 racks) — a price point that makes the space version legible once launch costs drop sufficiently via Starship reuse.

6. **This IPO matters beyond its valuation: it's a bet that retail investors can fund frontier infrastructure the way VCs fund startups.** Early SpaceX investors will exit rich; yet so much remains unbuilt (Starship at scale, rack-satellites, agentic compute markets) that there's genuine upside left. The analogy to nuclear power is stark — failure to build enough compute could deny humanity the ability to even imagine what unlimited AI could produce. A $2 trillion bet on that frontier, open to retail capital, is a feature even if the current financials are fiction.

7. **The Musk playbook: start with a dream, convert believers into capital, and use that capital to build the infrastructure that validates the dream.** Tesla stock issuances didn't dilute shareholders — they recruited more meme-propagators, funding the infrastructure that made the meme real. SpaceX repeats this: Mars, $28.5 trillion TAMs, and rack-satellites are the dream. Musk's prior track record (reusable rockets, Starlink, FSD) earns him the benefit of the doubt that the dream is at least directionally achievable, even when current financials suggest otherwise.

**Quotable:**
> "Instead of infrastructure leading to a movement, a movement, via the stock market, funded the building out of infrastructure." — *on why Tesla's stock issuances paradoxically drove the stock price up, and why the same dynamic now applies to SpaceX*

### Money Stuff: Robinhood Adds Some Agents ⚡
**Bloomberg / Matt Levine** · rss · 15 mins

1. **Robinhood launched agentic trading accounts in beta** — customers can connect a third-party AI agent via the Robinhood Trading MCP (Model Context Protocol) to an isolated account funded separately from their main portfolio. Use cases include portfolio rebalancing, thematic stock-picking (e.g., "monitor AI/semiconductor stocks for new entrants and analyst upgrades"), and automated mean-reversion strategies. It is not a simple chatbox — users must set up their own AI agent and connect it via MCP.

2. **The ETF, once the universal packager of trade ideas, is already becoming obsolete.** ETFs turned concepts like "buy stock and sell covered calls" or "borrow money to buy Bitcoin and gold" into one-click products. But there are infinite possible trades no ETF provider has thought of, and agentic AI plus a natural-language interface lets a retail investor specify any idiosyncratic strategy and execute it — no ETF ticker required.

3. **Zero-commission trading is the other half of this equation — and it changed the nature of investing, not just its cost.** In a world of $9.95-per-trade commissions, your thesis had to be "this company is good, I'll hold it." Free trading makes valid any strategy that requires dozens of daily trades: "buy stocks that went down yesterday," "buy oversold stocks and sell when they mean-revert." The combination of free execution and agentic AI enables a qualitatively different kind of retail investing.

4. **Trades are not truly free — electronic market makers collect bid/ask spreads on every Robinhood order, and agentic trading is a windfall for them.** Retail investors making constant, small, rule-based trades are the most predictable and profitable order flow for firms like Citadel or Virtu. Robinhood plans to expand agentic trading to options, crypto, event contracts, and futures — "automated retail options trades" being among the most profitable phrases in a market maker's vocabulary.

5. **Widespread agentic retail investing could either be random noise or catastrophic herding.** If each investor automates their unique ideas, the result is noise. But if agents converge on the same ~20 popular strategies — "buy the dip" being the most obvious — hedge funds will front-run the automated demand. A 2%+ S&P drop would become a mechanical signal that billions in agentic retail buying arrives the next morning.

6. **Microsoft agreed to pay $250 million to settle shareholder litigation over its $75.4 billion Activision acquisition.** A Swedish pension fund alleged that former Activision CEO Bobby Kotick, facing a sexual harassment crisis and imminent termination, tipped Microsoft about his desperation for a deal — enabling a lowball offer. The golden parachute perverse-incentive theory: a CEO about to be fired "for cause" (losing all unvested equity) has reason to sell the company at any price rather than face termination. The Delaware Chancery Court let the case proceed in October before the settlement.

7. **FTSE Russell changed its rules to add large IPOs to its indexes after just 5 trading days, down from quarterly reviews — explicitly anticipating SpaceX's IPO.** Over $30 trillion in assets globally are benchmarked to indexes now under review or already revised. Nasdaq already cut the waiting period to 15 days; S&P is weighing a reduction from 12 months to 6. At 5 days, index arbitrage desks may effectively be buying into the SpaceX IPO itself, which undermines the theory that index funds passively follow prices set by active investors — at that speed, they help set the price.

8. **Bankrupt auto-parts maker First Brands faces a $285.5 million US government claim for underpaying China tariffs via transfer pricing manipulation.** First Brands set the price its Chinese subsidiary Longkou Haimeng charged its US subsidiary Brake Parts Inc. — and allegedly reduced those prices to a fraction of market rate to minimize tariff liability. The fraud is unusually observable: identical brake parts sold by unrelated manufacturers cost First Brands roughly twice what it paid Haimeng. In January 2022, after Chinese authorities grew suspicious of Haimeng's collapsing revenues, BPI wired a $40 million "make up payment" — internally called exactly that — to shore up the subsidiary.

9. **Pseudonymous prediction market trader @PrinceHal has made $3.7 million lifetime trading inflation on Kalshi by building forecasting models that consistently beat major financial institutions.** A macro economist at a prominent Wall Street hedge fund reviewed his trades and said he was "dumbfounded" — "This guy might literally be Nostradamus." Meanwhile, Kalshi publicly claims "there is no house that wins when customers lose," while Susquehanna co-founder Jeff Yass says his firm is a major volume provider and that sharp bettors are winning at Susquehanna's expense — a direct contradiction that reveals who the actual counterparty is.

### India's Data Centres Are Going Off Grid 📌
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. India is building out 3.3 gigawatts of new data centre capacity in the AI era, and the scale of that buildout is breaking the traditional utility model. Hyperscale campuses exceed 100 MW of continuous load — far beyond what state distribution companies (discoms) can reliably deliver — and even a minute of downtime is operationally catastrophic. Per a JM Financial report, power density per rack already ranges from 5 to 50 kW, and AI workloads push that toward the upper end.

2. Andhra Pradesh has become the first state to allow hyperscale data centres to obtain private power discom licences — a privilege previously reserved for companies like Tata Power, Adani Electricity Mumbai, and Torrent Power, which supply electricity to entire geographic zones. This lets a data centre act simultaneously as a large industrial consumer and a licensed distributor, giving it control over its own dedicated feeder infrastructure, tariff structure, and renewable sourcing mix rather than depending on state utility timelines.

3. The economics create a new hybrid model: data centres must contract for large power blocks upfront, and any excess capacity they don't consume can legally be sub-distributed to other commercial and industrial consumers nearby. Ram Soni of Praxis Global Alliance identifies the four drivers — faster infrastructure augmentation, dedicated feeder infrastructure, tariff visibility, and flexible renewable sourcing — as what makes private discom status attractive beyond just reliability.

4. Becoming a licensed distributor triggers an obligation data centres would not otherwise face: under Ministry of Power mandates, all distributors must source at least 33% of their power from renewables by FY26 (the renewable purchase obligation, or RPO). Power experts consider this a net advantage rather than a burden — unlike legacy state discoms weighed down by years of stranded losses and coal commitments, a freshly licensed data centre discom can design its procurement portfolio from scratch to meet the RPO without restructuring an existing fleet.

**Quotable:**
> "Hyperscale data centres typically require over 100 MW of continuous power along with very high uptime standards, making private discoms and specialised power arrangements attractive for faster infrastructure augmentation, dedicated feeder infrastructure, tariff visibility, and flexible renewable sourcing." — *Ram Soni, practice leader, mobility energy and transportation, Praxis Global Alliance*

### Bloomberg Going Private: Private Credit Under Pressure 📎
**Bloomberg / Ellen DiMauro** · email · 7 mins

1. **Private credit trading is going mainstream, driven by AI-linked liquidity stress.** Deutsche Bank originated a $760 million private credit loan for a CoreWeave data center in Wyoming; the Related Companies borrowed the funds and CoreWeave will lease the space. Now $200 million of that loan is being shopped for sale — a sign that lenders including Apollo Global Management and KKR are actively trading out of positions to manage a liquidity squeeze, record redemption requests, and margin calls on bank leverage facilities. Buyers like Diameter Capital Partners are stepping in.

2. **The CoreWeave loan's terms reveal both AI demand and elevated risk.** The debt carries a spread of 4.25 percentage points over the benchmark rate with a stabilized debt yield of 17% and a minimum debt yield floor of 11%, plus a debt service coverage ratio of 1.35. CoreWeave has a pre-lease agreement worth $2.8 billion in contracted rent — more than three times the loan size — providing a cushion, but the sale of a $200 million slice signals lenders want to reduce concentration in AI infrastructure credit.

3. **The ECB warned that AI-linked private credit could amplify financial stress across Europe.** Its financial stability review stress-tested a "severe shock" to private credit markets and found that European insurers — not banks — would bear the heaviest losses, because they hold larger and less senior exposures to private credit and equity. Pension funds face aggregate losses across all three stress stages. Banks are more insulated due to seniority in their loans to private credit funds and relatively small positions. The ECB's finding: direct credit losses appear manageable, but second-order market valuation losses could be sizable.

4. **Apollo CEO Marc Rowan faces a governance challenge from his own LPs over political activity.** The American Federation of Teachers and American Association of University Professors — whose members have at least $29 billion in retirement assets invested in Apollo — sent a letter demanding an internal investigation into whether Rowan used Apollo staff to advance a White House compact. The compact offers universities preferential federal funding in exchange for banning DEI programs. Documents obtained by the teacher groups suggest Rowan used Apollo employees to schedule meetings and prepare materials for university leaders — a potential misuse of firm resources for political ends.

5. **Payment-in-kind interest in BDCs has doubled since pre-COVID, creating a growing mismatch between reported earnings and cash.** PIK income — where borrowers satisfy obligations with more debt rather than cash — averaged 8.1% of rated BDCs' interest and dividend income in 2025, up from roughly half that before the pandemic, according to Fitch Ratings. Because BDCs must distribute 90% of taxable income including PIK, they are paying out income they haven't actually collected in cash. Fitch's Meghan Neenan notes: the higher the PIK accrual, the larger the debt balloon at maturity — a risk that could weaken credit metrics for middle market CLOs if borrowers can't repay.

**Quotable:**
> "As PIK income has grown, there has been a greater mismatch between accounting earnings and actual cash coming in the door." — *Meghan Neenan, head of non-bank financial institutions for North America, Fitch Ratings*

## Markets & Finance

### Kalshi, Polymarket, Jesus and Ken Paxton 📌
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 8 mins

1. Prediction markets have demonstrated sharp accuracy in recent elections. Two days before Trump-backed Ed Gallrein demolished incumbent Rep. Thomas Massie in Kentucky's 4th Congressional District primary, both Polymarket and Kalshi had flipped to favor Gallrein. That same week, the sites correctly called Ken Paxton's Texas Senate primary win and Democrat Johnny Garcia's TX-35 congressional runoff victory.

2. The academic evidence supports the accuracy claim — but with caveats. Barnard College economics professor Rajiv Sethi and colleagues found that in the 2024 presidential vote, prediction markets outperformed polls, but accuracy degraded in down-ballot races where volume and liquidity are thin. The Iowa Electronic Markets, a predecessor platform running since 1988, has historically matched or beaten opinion polls over decades of electoral contests.

3. Prediction markets extend beyond politics with similarly promising results. A 2015 corporate prediction markets study found that platforms where employees bet real money on business outcomes outperformed internal experts — including projecting weekly vehicle sales at Ford Motor Co. A January 2026 working paper from researchers at the Federal Reserve, Northwestern University, and Johns Hopkins found Kalshi was at least as accurate as Wall Street analysts at forecasting Fed rate moves, inflation, and unemployment data, occasionally surpassing them.

4. Several structural flaws temper the hype. Markets chronically overestimate low-probability events — Polymarket currently prices a 2% chance that Jesus Christ returns before 2027. Low-volume markets are vulnerable to manipulation. There have been insider trading accusations, including a US soldier allegedly using classified intelligence to bet on Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro's removal from power. Sethi notes a feedback loop problem: market movements can create bandwagon effects that alter the very outcomes they're trying to predict.

5. The "wisdom of crowds" framing is largely a myth — prediction market accuracy traces to a tiny elite. A paper released April 2026 found that on Polymarket specifically, roughly 3% of bettors "generate the bulk of price discovery." The remaining 97% are essentially noise traders. The markets work not because masses of people aggregate knowledge, but because a small number of hyper-informed specialists correct prices when casual bettors push them off.

**Quotable:**
> "It's the most accurate thing we have as mankind right now, until someone else creates some sort of a super crystal ball." — *Polymarket CEO Shayne Coplan, to Anderson Cooper on 60 Minutes*

Micron Technology surged 19.3% in a single day to join the now 10-strong club of US companies worth over $1 trillion, followed hours later by South Korea's SK Hynix — with Samsung having crossed the threshold weeks earlier. Two catalysts drove Micron specifically: UBS doubled its price target (implying ~$1.8 trillion valuation within a year), and Huawei's announcement of a "Logic Folding" chip-manufacturing breakthrough — designed to let China produce competitive semiconductors without access to advanced Western equipment — paradoxically triggered a buying rush into US chipmakers, interpreted as proof of insatiable AI-driven demand.

2. Semiconductor stocks are now the dominant driver of the S&P 500's entire 2026 rally, eclipsing the former market darlings. Of the index's 672-point gain year-to-date, seven big SOX chipmakers contributed 289 points vs. 160 for the Magnificent Seven. Remove Nvidia (which sits in both groups) and the ratio is even starker: the remaining Mag Six added only 79 points while the SOX Eight added 369. 3. Micron's valuation looks deceptively cheap — trading at a lower price-to-forward-earnings multiple than the S&P 500 as a whole — because markets still price chips as cyclical commodities. But this "cheapness" rests entirely on explosive forward earnings estimates that have only been revised dramatically upward in 2026, as brokers finally priced in the reality that hyperscaler AI customers are captive, deep-pocketed buyers. The rally is therefore not a traditional valuation bubble, but it is a bet on earnings forecasts that, during periods of explosive growth, become very hard to gauge with precision.

4. AI-driven chip stocks are masking fragility in emerging markets just as geopolitical risks intensify. Strip the three largest AI proxies from the MSCI EM index and the cohort is back to where it stood immediately after Liberation Day. The four largest MSCI EM constituents — China, South Korea, India, and Taiwan — are all highly exposed to a prolonged closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Oxford Economics' post-Iran-conflict survey found EM risk perceptions at their worst since November 2022, with growth risks skewed downward and inflation fears rising; political instability in Indonesia, Turkey, and across Latin America compounds the picture.

## International Affairs

### The World: Cease-fires but no peace 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. A "cease-fire" is a political declaration, not a military reality — and the current landscape proves it. The US and Iran have exchanged fire twice since agreeing to a cease-fire roughly seven weeks ago: first in early May over disputed incidents in the Strait of Hormuz involving three US warships, then this week when American warplanes struck targets in southern Iran and sank two Iranian speedboats allegedly placing mines. Both sides dispute the facts of each incident, yet both sides still claim the cease-fire is holding.

2. Cease-fire violations almost always happen, and whether the cease-fire survives is purely a function of whether both parties still want it. Laurie Nathan, a Notre Dame professor and former UN senior mediation adviser, says violations are nearly universal in every conflict he can recall — sometimes accidental (a low-level commander out of the loop), sometimes deliberate (a message to hardliners or the enemy). Iran and the US keep the fiction alive because full-scale war would be too costly for both; the violations are part of the negotiation, not a prelude to escalation.

3. Lebanon and Gaza illustrate what happens when one party was coerced into a cease-fire it didn't want: the agreement becomes "weak by design." Netanyahu agreed to the Lebanon cease-fire only under pressure from Trump and, according to Govinda Clayton — who has spent over a decade advising on cease-fire negotiations — probably wouldn't regret its collapse. Israel has since launched hundreds of airstrikes and issued sweeping evacuation orders across southern Lebanon. In Gaza, Nathan uses that same phrase — "weak by design" — to explain why hundreds of Palestinians have been killed since the October cease-fire began.

4. Modern cease-fires increasingly substitute for peace agreements rather than leading to them, leaving civilians in a permanent gray zone. Linda Kinstler observed last year that this proliferation of agreements-without-peace reflects a "pessimistic age": parties settle for a bare promise to stop killing rather than tackle the underlying political compromises. NYT Jerusalem bureau chief David Halbfinger captures the civilian experience: "It ain't peace, either." Meanwhile, Iran's state TV released a 14-point draft deal — Iran reopens the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for the US lifting its naval blockade — which the White House immediately dismissed as a "complete fabrication."

**Quotable:**
> "A cease-fire is assumed to be a complete absence of hostilities by lay people. But that's not the reality in just about any conflict I can think of." — *Laurie Nathan, Notre Dame professor and former UN senior mediation adviser*
