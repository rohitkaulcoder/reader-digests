## Today's Top 3

### Money Stuff: Private Markets Charge More ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg)** · rss · 15 mins

1. Financial intermediation has cost roughly 1.5–2% of assets per year for over a century. Thomas Philippon's 2015 paper finds "the unit cost of intermediation is about as high today as it was at the turn of the 20th century," despite computers, AI, and the internet. This is genuinely puzzling and unresolved in academic finance.

2. Vanguard's total-market index fund charges 0.04% annually — roughly 40x cheaper than the aggregate cost of intermediation. Index funds free-ride: active managers do the hard work of evaluating companies and setting prices, and passive funds just copy the result. Someone still has to do the expensive work; index investors just don't pay for it.

3. Private equity now controls 15–20% of the entire U.S. economy (John Coates's estimate), and accessing that slice costs investors roughly 2% per year plus 20% of profits — far above the 0.04% index alternative. The core business logic of alternative asset management is essentially relocating economic ownership into wrappers that charge more. Index funds killed the 1% mutual fund fee, so the industry responded by moving assets somewhere indexing can't follow.

4. Wealth advisers at major banks have extracted over $2 billion in trailing servicing fees since 2017 just from 16 private-market funds (Blackstone, Blue Owl, Apollo, KKR), before counting upfront commissions. Morgan Stanley, UBS, and Bank of America Merrill Lynch were the primary channels. Shang Chou of Dishmi Capital: "The advisers themselves are stuck in this incentive structure where their behaviour is going to be aligned with pushing clients into these products." Retail investors are now trying to exit, but redemption queues in many non-traded REITs and BDCs make that difficult.

5. Tesla went public with single-class stock — Musk's "original sin." He owns only ~11% of Tesla shares with one vote each, leaving him theoretically removable. Last year Tesla granted him a compensation package with a headline value of ~$1 trillion, solely to give him 25% voting control. Stock exchange rules prohibit issuing new super-voting shares to existing public companies, so the only mechanism was giving him more ordinary shares — the trillion-dollar payout was an accidental byproduct of buying him votes, not a reward for performance.

6. SpaceX, still private and planning an IPO, will avoid Tesla's constraint by going public with dual-class stock: Musk's Class B shares carry 10 votes each. SpaceX also awarded Musk 60 million additional shares contingent on growing market cap from $1.1T to $6.6T and building space data centers delivering "100 terawatts of compute per year" — orders of magnitude above total U.S. peak power consumption. A separate earlier award gives him up to 200 million shares if SpaceX establishes a Mars colony with at least 1 million inhabitants. Unlike the Tesla package, these can't be justified as buying voting control (that's already secured), so they're just money.

7. SpaceX's IPO prospectus includes a mandatory arbitration clause preventing shareholders from pursuing certain legal claims in court. No major public company has done this before. If it succeeds at SpaceX — where investors are explicitly buying into Musk's governance-flouting vision — it creates a template that far less visionary companies will adopt.

8. Scammers are impersonating Iranian authorities and demanding Bitcoin or USDT payments from ship operators for safe Strait of Hormuz passage. The scam is plausible because Iran actually is charging transit fees payable in yuan or stablecoins (U.S. sanctions cut it off from dollar clearing). Greek risk firm Marisks says at least one vessel paid and was fired on anyway. The downside of this phishing attack is your ship gets shot.

**Quotable:**
> "At some level, the rise of private equity and private credit and 'alternative asset management' generally is about shifting ownership of the economy to vehicles that charge more fees." — *on why the alternatives boom exists*

### Tim Cook's Impeccable Timing ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 11 mins

1. Tim Cook's 15-year tenure (2011–2026) produced staggering financials: Apple revenue up 303%, profit up 354%, and market cap from $297 billion to $4 trillion — a 1,251% increase. Jobs' death just six weeks after Cook became CEO was the pivotal circumstance that made Cook's success possible: he inherited Apple only a few years after its most important 0-to-1 product (the iPhone), leaving him positioned to scale it rather than invent the next thing.

2. Cook's operational genius predates the iPhone era. When he joined Apple in 1998, the company ran its own factories and warehouses — a cost drag he systematically dismantled, shifting manufacturing to China and building a just-in-time global supply chain. The result: hundreds of millions of iPhones shipped annually without a single significant product recall, across five models and a plethora of colors and carriers worldwide.

3. Cook's new product record is mixed. AirPods and Apple Watch are real successes — the "Wearables, Home, and Accessories" category generated $35.4 billion in revenue last year (Fortune 500 rank: 128) — but both are iPhone derivatives. Apple Vision Pro, Cook's only genuine 0-to-1 attempt, is widely considered a 0.5 at best.

4. Services became Cook's most consequential financial lever, but it was inherited, not invented. The Google search deal originated in 2002 and was extended to the iPhone in 2007; the App Store launched in 2008 with Jobs announcing the 30% cut. By last year, Services was 26% of Apple's revenue and 41% of its profit. Phil Schiller's July 2011 internal email — written before Cook officially became CEO — suggested cutting Apple's take once the App Store hit $1 billion in annual profit; Cook ignored it, optimizing for margin over developer goodwill.

5. China is Cook's deepest contradiction. He built Apple's most important competitive capability — world-class manufacturing at astronomical scale — by making Apple more dependent on China than any other major tech company. As Patrick McGee documented in *Apple in China*, Apple didn't just use China's manufacturing base, it helped create it. That dependency is now a strategic liability as U.S.-China relations deteriorate, and it violates the Cook Doctrine's own tenet: "own and control the primary technologies behind the products we make."

6. The AI question is the unresolved legacy risk Cook leaves his successor, John Ternus. Apple has avoided the hundreds-of-billions AI buildout; instead it white-labeled Google's Gemini for the new Siri. The trap: once Gemini works well enough that users are satisfied, the bar for Apple to replace it with its own model rises continuously while Apple's in-house AI capabilities stagnate without market pressure. Cook may have made a short-term optimization that locked Apple into third-party AI dependency permanently.

**Quotable:**
> "Cook created the conditions for a crash out, by virtue of he himself forgetting The Cook Doctrine and what makes Apple Apple." — *Thompson's closing verdict on Cook's legacy risk*

### The Growing Importance of Order to Cash in the Age of AI ⚡
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 11 mins

1. Traditional SaaS had a built-in cash conversion buffer that AI pricing has destroyed. Under the old license model, you collected payment upfront, AWS billed net 30–45 (adding another ~30 days), and payroll ran two weeks in arrears — giving ~60 days of float before COGS came due. With usage-based (now ~90% of companies) and outcome-based (~10%) models, LLM inference costs hit the moment a user interacts with the product, often before any payment is collected. Free trials with LLM credits are now effectively CAC with hard dollar costs — the negative cash conversion cycle within COGS has evaporated.

2. Order-to-cash is fragmented across most companies, and that fragmentation is a direct threat to growth. The typical stack: sales reps use an order intake tool, a homegrown billing system ("Admin") handles invoicing, and someone on finance checks a spreadsheet to confirm payment arrived. These systems are disconnected, unowned, and not designed together. Kunal Agrawal, CFO of Gorgias, describes arriving to find reps manually copying contracts from a contract tool into a billing tool, then manually chasing payment — every handoff a potential day added to DSO.

3. The fix starts with mapping the ideal state before touching tools or auditing the current mess. Most companies skip straight to auditing their broken process and end up optimizing it rather than replacing it. The right sequence: write down what the ideal deal-to-cash flow looks like (what reps touch, what they don't, what the single system of record is), then map where reality diverges, then assign a day value to each gap. Specific examples: 2 days lost because contracts take too long to send; 3 days lost to customers confused about what they were charged for.

4. A product catalog with too many SKUs compounds billing complexity faster than most people expect. A 40-SKU catalog isn't 4× harder to manage than a 10-SKU one — it's closer to 10× harder because pricing logic, proration rules, upgrade/downgrade paths, and revenue recognition treatment all multiply combinatorially. Companies that fix this reliably land on the same destination: 3–5 core packages, a handful of add-ons, and a deliberate sunset of everything else. Ironically, LLMs are useful here — feed order history into one and ask it to find which SKU combinations customers actually buy together.

5. "AI agent cost per interaction" is becoming a critical KPI, and LLM tokens are only part of the fully loaded number. Gorgias tracks this metric internally, factoring in security and compliance tooling, infrastructure required for the agentic architecture, and everything else that runs around the model in production. Kunal's framework for his engineering team: "If we didn't have an AI agent product, how much of this would we still need?" — the delta is what actually belongs in the AI cost center. Knowing this baseline is also required to price rationally.

6. Margin vs. customer experience is not a binary choice — it's a dial to tune continuously. The temptation is to protect gross margin by cheapening model calls; the right CFO approach is to treat customer quality as the North Star and build the agentic architecture (model mix, guardrails, routing) that allows you to maximize "wow moments" while maintaining cost discipline. Companies that treat this as a one-time decision rather than an ongoing optimization consistently underperform on both dimensions.

**Quotable:**
> "I've really developed conviction that the order to cash process is really a strategic growth asset. It's really a core part of the growth engine that makes it work. Because a unified order to cash makes you go faster, reduces friction, and also leads to a better customer experience." — *Kunal Agrawal, CFO of Gorgias*

### It doesn't matter what Kevin Warsh has to say 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. **Warsh's confirmation hearing is theatrically constrained before it begins.** The real blockage is Senator Thom Tillis, who refuses to let the nomination proceed until the separate case against incumbent Jerome Powell — over Fed building renovation spending — is dropped. Whatever Warsh says on Tuesday cannot resolve that. The market assumption is Tillis's objection will eventually be bought off, and Warsh gets the job regardless.

2. **Confirmation testimony has zero predictive value.** Alan Greenspan was a committed Ayn Rand acolyte who opposed the existence of central banks — then spent his tenure as one of the most activist Fed chairs in history. Warsh has a paper trail as a vocal Fed critic from his time as a governor during the GFC, but the past repeatedly shows that nominees morph once in the chair. Ben Bernanke's 2005 confirmation hearing never once mentioned QE, balance sheet, subprime, CDOs, or Lehman — the exact issues that would define his entire chairmanship.

3. **Warsh's leaked opening statement threads a careful needle on independence.** He frames the post-GFC Fed as having stretched its credibility "to the very edge of, if not beyond" its statutory responsibilities — language pointed enough to signal sympathy with Trump's critique, without endorsing political interference in rate-setting. His formulation: monetary policy is fully independent, but bank regulation, stewardship of public money, and international finance are "fair game for politicians." It is calibrated to reassure markets while keeping Trump onside.

4. **The balance sheet is the live grenade Warsh won't touch.** He is loudly on record as wanting a smaller Fed balance sheet — which would mean less market liquidity and higher bond yields. The balance sheet has recently begun to enlarge again. Powell's first serious market clash came in late 2018 when he said he'd shrink it "on auto-pilot," triggering an immediate selloff and the famous Powell Pivot. Warsh's five-page opening statement contains zero mentions of the balance sheet or QE — a deliberate omission that leaves his most potentially disruptive view unexamined before he takes power.

**Quotable:**
> "Fed independence is at its peak in the operational conduct of monetary policy. That degree of independence does not extend to the full range of its congressionally mandated functions... And the Fed must stay in its lane." — *Warsh's leaked opening statement, drawing a line between rate-setting independence and everything else*

### The Morning: Pain at the pump 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. The Iran war and U.S. blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — which has already turned back 27 ships trying to enter or leave Iranian ports — have spiked oil prices and sent U.S. retail gas prices sharply higher. The newsletter's author pays $106 to fill his full-size pickup truck; the cascade from global conflict to neighborhood pump is now viscerally personal for millions of American drivers.

2. Massachusetts station owner Alex Weatherall illustrates the math that squeezes operators in a price spike: the rack (wholesale) price he pays jumped from $2.398/gallon on Feb. 25 to $3.347/gallon the following week — a 39.6% increase — and his last truckload cost $39,488. He raised his retail price by only 33.8%, deliberately eating some of the increase because he feared losing customers if competitors didn't follow, and fewer pump visits directly cut his convenience-store and restaurant sales.

3. Gas stations make almost nothing on fuel itself; their survival depends on what customers buy inside. Florida station manager Cameron Joudi earns only 10–15 cents per gallon, and with an 8,000-gallon tank lasting a couple of weeks, his fuel profit is just $800–$1,200 per fill cycle. Station owners are caught between needing to raise prices to stay solvent and the social cost of doing so to regulars they personally know — Joudi described raising prices "on people he really cares about" who are "already stretched very thin."

4. The human floor of this pain is not middle-class inconvenience but genuine deprivation. A veteran who served in Afghanistan and spoke to The Daily covers higher gas costs by cutting his grocery budget — visiting food banks, relying on local churches, feeding his three children first, and sometimes skipping meals himself with his wife. "Pain at the pump is relative" is the newsletter's summary verdict.

**Quotable:**
> "I hope they understand that I'm not pricing my gas to make a quick buck. I'm pricing my gas how I need to price it in order to stay afloat." — *Cameron Joudi, gas station manager in suburban Jacksonville, Fla.*

## AI & Product Development

### LLMs and the Techno-Traditionalist Turn 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 8 mins

1. LLMs excel at explaining the *why* behind cooking rules that predate their scientific justifications — salting meat early dissolves long protein fibers (but wrecks delicate fish), patting food dry enables the Maillard reaction, resting meat equalizes temperature gradients. Getting these explanations inline rather than hunting for them separately is the real upgrade over Google.

2. Potatoes are an existence proof that folk wisdom encodes life-or-death knowledge centuries before science catches up. Potato toxins solanine and tomatine survive cooking, so the Inca had to independently discover three detoxification methods (clay-water dipping, freeze-drying, fermentation). The Inca empire reached ~12 million people — more than any European nation except France at the time — only because this culinary wisdom worked and propagated.

3. Folk wisdom has a massive sample size but draws from a different distribution than the present. Relationship advice passed down from grandparents is probably basically right; career advice from parents is almost certainly wrong. The problem is you can't evaluate the half-life of any rule without knowing its context — and that context is exactly what LLMs can reconstruct.

4. LLMs are unusually good at *atemporal* search, filling a gap Google fails at. A search for "gulf of hormuz war iraq iran" returns 8 of 15 first-page results timestamped in the last 24 hours, despite the Tanker War of the 1980s being the more historically relevant reference. LLMs let you escape the recency bias baked into search engines and actually reach the historical distribution behind a piece of wisdom.

5. Reverse-engineering folk wisdom cuts both ways — a risk the piece flags via Primo Levi and the monks-with-a-cat parable. Take any tradition-selected behavior, throw reasoning at it, and you'll get a plausible story either validating it ("timeless wisdom") or invalidating it ("made sense then, doesn't now"). The same analytical tool that recovers Chesterton's fence can also manufacture a convincing blueprint for a fence that shouldn't exist.

**Quotable:**
> "LLMs are great at summarizing text, and conventional wisdom is a summary of specific experiences that aren't necessarily well-represented in text. But if you take that wisdom and ask an LLM to explain what it came from, you basically get a view-source button for every rule of thumb you've ever encountered." — *closing the essay*

### claude code is not making your product better 📌
**Ethan Ding** · rss · 8 mins

1. **Productivity gains from AI coding agents follow a k-shape — seniors gain, juniors stagnate.** Commit-size data from Q1 2015–Q1 2025 shows senior engineers (blue line) saw measurable output growth after the LLM inflection in 2023, while junior output (red line) went flat or declined. The productivity win is real, but narrowly distributed.

2. **The practitioners building the best products are not seeing product velocity accelerate.** Dax (building opencode.ai), Karri Saarinen (CEO of Linear), and David Cramer (who grew Sentry to $10M/month) all observed the same pattern within days of each other. Cramer's diagnosis: LLMs exhibit "poor performance on incremental development in complexity," "inability to truly simplify and create idiomatic interfaces," and "pure slop test generation." His summary: "it's mostly bloat."

3. **Anthropic's own Claude Code is a falsification test — and it fails.** Claude Code is entirely self-coded; if agentic coding compounds product improvement, Anthropic's 7-month head start should have made every competitor irrelevant. Instead, OpenAI's Codex launched months later and is already functionally competitive; Cursor, Cognition, and Factory are still closing serious enterprise contracts. The compound advantage never materialized, which means code production was never the bottleneck.

4. **Lines of code are a liability, not an asset — and LLMs inflate the balance sheet.** TinyChat (comma.ai's software subsidiary) ran an alarm that triggered when its codebase exceeded a certain size and celebrated deleted code. Product surface area compounds fractally: adding a Slack integration forces a Teams integration, then an email fallback; adding MFA requires Duo, Okta, and SAML compatibility. Linear, at 178 people and $100M ARR, sits in the top-right quality quadrant with the smallest codebase bubble on the chart. Jira has 56× more cumulative engineering effort and scores 6 points lower on consumer quality.

5. **At the frontier, the bottleneck is taste, not throughput.** The gap between Jira and Linear isn't better-drawn boxes — it's a specific creative vision executed with restraint over years. Kelly Johnson's Skunk Works team built the SR-71, still the fastest air-breathing manned aircraft 60 years later, not by producing more blueprints but by having a theory of what to leave out. Dax's warning to his team — "we're losing our ability to delay gratification" — names exactly what agent-assisted sprinting erodes: the slow, uncomfortable thinking that frontier product ideas require.

6. **AI coding agents are Camry factories, not Ferrari workshops.** For 0-to-1 products, agents genuinely reduce time to first working version. For companies already at the frontier (e.g., Ramp, whose engineers reportedly doubled their salaries in token spend over the past year), there is no measurable product improvement — no win-rate or P&L data supports the ROI. The net effect is that commodity software gets dramatically cheaper while technical debt accumulates in the rafters, and the artisanal taste that separates #1 from the field becomes more valuable, not less.

**Quotable:**
> "the taste to delete, compress, refuse — that isn't on any frontier model's roadmap. if anything, it's more valuable now that the floor beneath it is rising." — *on why human product taste becomes a stronger competitive moat as AI raises the baseline*

## Global Economics

### The World: War shock in Asia 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. The war in Iran — launched with the first U.S. strikes on Feb. 28 — has hit Asia-Pacific harder than any region outside the Middle East, because the region imports most of its energy from the Middle East and its economies are laced together by cross-border supply chains. Within hours of those first strikes, trucks, ships, and planes halted across a region "defined by near-constant motion," setting off simultaneous shocks to transportation, manufacturing, and household incomes.

2. Air travel has collapsed at a scale not seen in decades: more than 92,000 flights were canceled worldwide in March alone, with Asia-Pacific accounting for the largest share. Jet fuel has nearly doubled in price; Batik Air of Malaysia cut flights 35% this month just to avoid insolvency, and one aviation expert told correspondent Damien Cave that Asia's air traffic could revert to levels last seen in the 1980s.

3. Manufacturing supply chains are snapping at multiple nodes simultaneously. Indonesian nickel processors — which need natural gas and sulfur — have cut output. Bangladesh's garment hubs of Gazipur and Ashulia, which produce for Walmart, Zara, and Uniqlo, face severe production and shipment disruptions because polyester and nylon derive from petroleum. Helium prices have spiked (helium is a gas byproduct essential for semiconductor fabrication), and while Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company says it has enough stock for now, a prolonged shortage could force chip makers to source from Russia or cut production — rippling into electronics and cars.

4. The human toll is accelerating: a new U.N. report estimates 8.8 million people across Asia-Pacific are at risk of falling into poverty, including roughly 5 million in Iran itself. In India, entire industrial clusters have shut down and workers are reversing urbanization — returning to villages to thresh wheat by hand. In Manila, minibus drivers staged a three-day strike over fuel prices. In the northern Philippines, harvested crops are rotting in fields because farmers cannot afford the fuel to transport them to market.

**Quotable:**
> "Even if there is a peace deal soon, the region, which has driven global economic growth for decades, is likely to face months of canceled flights, surging food prices and shelves emptied of everyday products: plastic bags, instant noodles, vaccines, syringes, lipstick, microchips and sportswear." — *Damien Cave on the irreversibility of the supply shock even after a ceasefire*
