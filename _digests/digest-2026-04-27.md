### The Forecast: Climate inflation is here ⚡
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 8 mins

1. Climate shocks are no longer just temporary price spikes — they are becoming structural inflation drivers. The 2022 European heatwave (peak 46°C in Spain) raised average European temperatures 1.25°C above baseline and up to 5.7°C in some regions, scorching Spanish olive groves, cutting UK chicken production 9%, and triggering Northern Italy's worst drought in 70 years. Researchers were able to directly attribute those price effects to climate change — a rare and alarming precedent for what economists now call "climateflation."

2. Standard macroeconomic tools were not built for this. Mark Blyth, who runs Brown University's Rhodes Center for International Economics and Finance, notes that every inflation textbook frames the problem as "too much money chasing too few goods" — none accounts for a supply side being destroyed by heat, drought, and flooding. If climate shocks begin shifting consumer inflation expectations upward (research suggests they might), the price increases stop being one-time events and become self-reinforcing, making central banks' job structurally harder.

3. AI-heavy executives are the most bullish on AI productivity *and* the most bearish on their own headcount. A survey of 6,000 managers across the US, UK, Germany, and Australia found that executives who use AI more frequently predict larger productivity gains *and* larger employment declines at their firms — a correlation that holds after controlling for age and sector. Stanford economist Nicholas Bloom, one of the study's authors, explains the pattern bluntly: heavy AI users are "riding the tech wave" and expect to be protected even as they anticipate displacing others.

4. BCG's four 2050 scenarios hinge almost entirely on whether governments keep pace with corporations. The best-case "AI abundance" scenario features a four-day work week, cheap energy, and regulated AI — but BCG's Alan Iny says it only becomes plausible after a catastrophic cyberattack in the 2030s forces an AI treaty. The worst-case "digital Darwinism" scenario sees AI gains captured entirely by tech elites in compute-rich nations, with gig work proliferating, workplace surveillance normalized, and citizens retreating into "AI-generated virtual worlds tailored to personal preferences and moods."

5. Steve Wozniak was wrong about robots and coffee, just off on the timeline. In 2007 he predicted robots would *never* be able to walk up to an unfamiliar coffee machine and operate it. The LEAP forecasting project — run by the Forecasting Research Institute — now puts the median AI-expert prediction for passing that test at 2034; superforecasters (experts with verified track records on unrelated questions) are slightly more optimistic at 2032. The test requires success across three different unstructured, unfamiliar home environments with zero mistakes.

**Quotable:**
> "If you pick up a textbook and you look up the bit on inflation, inflation is too much money chasing too few goods. There's no textbook that says, 'the climate's cooking and we're running out of stuff.'" — *Mark Blyth, Brown University's Rhodes Center for International Economics and Finance*

## Technology & Product

### Apple's new CEO 📌
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 10 mins

1. John Ternus officially becomes Apple CEO on September 1, 2026 — and Apple deliberately timed the handoff so that less than two weeks later, on roughly September 10, he will host the launch of the first foldable iPhone. The sequencing is not coincidental: Apple wanted its new leader to own the debut of what it considers its biggest product category in a decade, and Ternus personally oversaw the engineering of the device.

2. The foldable iPhone targets four differentiators over rival Samsung and Huawei devices: durability, performance, a less-visible display crease, and a horizontal unfolded screen sized and interfaced like an iPad. It will launch at $2,000 or more, supply chain complexity will constrain early availability (likely heightening demand), and Gurman expects it to meaningfully lift Apple's average selling price and pull over Android users who have been using foldables for more than five years.

3. Ternus is set up for an unusually strong start: his first full quarter as CEO is the holiday period, when analysts forecast Apple will exceed its prior December-quarter record of roughly $150 billion in revenue. For comparison, Tim Cook's first two months in 2011 also benefited from a tailwind product — the original Siri — and a flood of devices in 2012, but Ternus is poised to enter approximately 10 new product categories across the next few years, versus the three Cook launched across 14 years (Apple Watch 2015, AirPods 2016, Vision Pro 2024).

4. The near-term product pipeline spans three domains. AI-powered smart home: a HomePod with a 7-inch screen mountable on a speaker base or wall, with a Siri OS and facial recognition; a tabletop robot with a 9-inch screen on a movable robotic arm for videoconferencing; and a privacy-focused home security sensor/camera to compete with Ring and Google Nest. AI wearables: smart glasses to rival Meta Ray-Ban with oval cameras, music, and Siri; camera-equipped AirPods that feed low-res visual data into AI for contextual reminders; and a circular pendant worn as a necklace or pin that pipes computer-vision data into Apple Intelligence. Hardware expansions: a touch-screen MacBook expected late 2026 or early 2027, and a roughly 20-inch foldable iPad that Gurman notes is a Ternus priority but may never ship due to development snags. AR glasses targeting 2028–2030 represent the longer-term bet on a form factor that could ultimately replace the iPhone.

5. Ternus has committed publicly to continuing Apple's $100 billion services empire — run by Eddy Cue — and expanding it, citing Apple Pay as his favorite example of hardware-software-services integration. On AI, he articulated two tracks in a town hall: AI to transform how Apple builds products internally (using decades of engineering data to surface insights), and AI to power customer experiences that were "previously in the realm of science fiction." His biggest unresolved challenge is Siri, which has been plagued by delays; a new interface for iOS 27 is due at WWDC in June, and whether he can fix the team behind it will determine whether the hardware pipeline delivers on its promise.

**Quotable:**
> "We don't ship technology for technology's sake. And I am incredibly excited about this wave of technology because I think it is really profound, and it's going to allow us to do some things that, honestly, were previously in the realm of science fiction, and now we see a path to being able to do it." — *Ternus, in his first town hall as incoming CEO*

### The AI doctor will see you now 📌
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 8 mins

1. Amazon has the data to build a compelling AI shopping assistant but not necessarily the execution. Dave Lee notes that Rufus, Amazon's AI shopping bot, still requires user confirmation before completing a purchase — it adds items to your cart but won't buy without approval. The institutional friction is the bottleneck, not the underlying data advantage.

2. Meta is installing tracking software on employee computers to capture mouse movements, keystrokes, and random screen screenshots — explicitly to train AI models to replicate what humans do at work, including navigating drop-down menus. The same employees being surveilled are the ones the AI is being trained to replace; they don't receive paychecks, health insurance, or "common courtesy."

3. Japanese nursing homes are deploying plushie robots the size of human babies to keep elderly residents cognitively engaged. Catherine Thorbecke, who visited Tokyo facilities, notes that the same "engineered friendliness" that raised concerns about chatbot manipulation of young users may be genuinely beneficial when directed at isolated seniors — though no one is claiming these bots solve the underlying loneliness problem.

4. Sullivan & Cromwell LLP apologized to the chief judge of Manhattan's US Bankruptcy Court for a filing containing AI-hallucinated legal citations. Gautam Mukunda extends the risk beyond law firms: Boston Consulting Group, Goldman Sachs, and the Big Four accountants share the same professional-services business model, but their AI errors happen in private — not in public court records where they can be caught.

**Quotable:**

### Snapchat CEO: Why distribution has become the most important moat 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 6 mins

1. Only two consumer social apps have broken through in the 15 years since Snapchat — TikTok and Threads — and both won through distribution, not product. TikTok spent billions subsidizing creators and viewers to flood the top of the funnel; Threads piggybacked on Instagram's existing social graph to hit 100M users in days. Organic app discovery is effectively dead, meaning distribution strategy now precedes product strategy for anyone building consumer tech.

2. Pure software is no longer a moat — Snap learned this the hard way as Stories, Lenses, swipe navigation, and Snapchat+ were all copied by larger platforms. What can't be easily cloned are deep ecosystems (millions of developer-built AR lenses, entrenched creator relationships) and hardware, which is why Spiegel has kept investing in products like Spectacles and Specs despite the difficulty. AI accelerates this further: if software is increasingly auto-generated, the only durable advantages are things that can't be forked.

3. Snap's design team is an intentional bottleneck of 9–12 people with no titles, no hierarchy, and nothing shipping without their approval — a structure that directly contradicts how most tech companies treat design as a service function. New designers present work on their first day; hundreds of ideas go in front of Spiegel weekly. Snap also hired 200 employees before bringing in its first PM, deliberately locking in design-led culture before adding coordination layers that would have subordinated it.

**Quotable:**
> "If you want to have a good idea, you have to have lots of ideas." — *Spiegel on Snap's high-volume, non-hierarchical design process*

## Economics & Finance

### Bw Reads: How Monaco fights dirty money 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 5 mins

1. In June 2024, six months before the Mareterra luxury development's inauguration, the Paris-based Financial Action Task Force (FATF) — a G7-created watchdog founded in 1989 — added Monaco to its "grey list" of jurisdictions deemed insufficiently vigilant about dirty money, alongside Syria, Venezuela, and Yemen. The designation sent the principality of 39,000 residents into crisis mode: Prince Albert II shook up the Finance Ministry and Monaco stood up a new regulator, the Autorité Monégasque de Sécurité Financière, to handle financial intelligence and anti-money-laundering enforcement.

2. Real estate is ground zero. Mareterra, 114 waterfront homes built on 15 acres reclaimed from the sea, sold at prices ranging from €16 million to nearly €500 million — making it among the most expensive addresses on the planet. FATF singled out four sectors as money-laundering fronts: real estate, yachting, sporting agents, and private banks. In the past year, Monaco's new regulator penalized six firms for due-diligence failures, including two real estate agencies that insufficiently vetted buyers — one of which handled a Mareterra transaction.

3. The tightened know-your-customer rules, which require businesses to trace the source of clients' wealth and report suspicions, are already having a chilling effect on demand from buyers who can't document clean money. Bloomberg reviewed leaked emails and preliminary deeds from Distributed Denial of Secrets (a nonprofit that preserves hacked and leaked materials) covering early Mareterra sales, finding that the new compliance pressure is visibly diminishing Monaco's appeal to the ultrawealthy whose fortunes resist scrutiny.

**Quotable:**
> "We were maybe not alert enough in the past." — *Pierre-André Chiappori, Monaco Finance Minister (March 2024 – March 2026), on the grey-list designation*

### Free Lunch: Britain acts richer than it is 📎
**Tej Parikh** · email · 5 mins

1. Britain's political class routinely overstates the country's economic weight. Chancellor Rachel Reeves attended the IMF spring meetings in April 2026 touting the UK as a "safe harbour economy" and urging other nations to follow Britain's lead on energy costs — while the IMF simultaneously cut Britain's 2026 growth forecasts by more than any other G7 nation, partly because the country has among the highest industrial electricity prices in the developed world due to exposure to the oil-and-gas shock from the Iran conflict. The same pattern held with Brexit: executing the UK's largest bureaucratic undertaking since WWII overestimated both the state's administrative capacity and Britain's leverage to carve out winning trade deals outside the EU.

2. Public spending expectations are structurally disconnected from fiscal reality. UK citizens broadly expect Scandinavian welfare and public services while resisting US-style income tax rates; Labour cannot rein in benefit spending under backbench pressure, and no party will touch the "triple lock" pension guarantee (which rises by the highest of inflation, earnings growth, or 2%). The result: debt-to-GDP hovers above 95% and the OBR projects it could surge to 275% by the 2070s — the UK already spends nearly twice as much servicing debt as it does on defence. On the private-sector side, OECD data shows taxes on wages for the average British worker rose more than in any other rich country last year, and the country runs one of the developed world's highest minimum wage rates.

3. Chronic political instability makes every structural problem worse. No UK prime minister has completed a full standard electoral term in over a decade, and the current crisis — revelations about Starmer's appointment of Lord Mandelson as US ambassador to the US — is the latest in a long series of Westminster self-inflicted dramas that baffle investors and business leaders amid global economic turbulence. Each leadership churn saps investment, stalls reform, and pushes up government borrowing costs, compounding the fiscal gap that self-flattery prevents the country from confronting.

**Quotable:**
> "A frequent critique is that UK citizens want Scandinavian levels of welfare support and public services with the US's low income tax rates." — *on the structural mismatch between public expectations and fiscal capacity*

### Cash, AI, & Cinema in a Changing India 📎
**The Core** · email · 4 mins

1. India's cash economy isn't shrinking — ₹40 lakh crore is currently in circulation, replenished daily across 70,000+ ATMs by logistics companies like CMS Info Systems. UPI's rise hasn't cut cash volumes; it has simply added a second layer on top. Cash persists because it functions everywhere without infrastructure, anchors healthcare and fuel transactions, and remains the default for large parts of India that are digitally connected but cash-dependent by habit.

2. First American Financial Corporation CTO Jody Mulkey reports that agentic AI is compressing weeks-long title and settlement workflows in real estate to minutes, with enterprises logging 2–5x productivity gains. The bottleneck is no longer the technology but organizational adaptation: teams must learn to work with non-deterministic systems and trust AI-led decisions. A structural advantage is emerging for firms that treat data exhaust — emails, chats, transaction logs — as structured intelligence rather than noise.

3. Filmmaker Nikkhil Advani (Emmay Entertainment, producer of *Rocket Boys* and *Mumbai Diaries*) identifies a split within Indian cinema: OTT platforms have opened space for research-driven, collaborative storytelling, but theatrical releases remain locked to star-led economics that dictate budgets and casting. Despite high output volume, India is a small global player — constrained by limited screen infrastructure, fragmented audience behavior, and immature subscription patterns.

**Quotable:**
> "India isn't transitioning from cash to digital—it's layering one system on top of another." — *Govindraj Ethiraj on India's hybrid financial reality*
