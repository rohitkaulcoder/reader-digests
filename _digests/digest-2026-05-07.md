### Money Stuff: ChatGPT Can't Pick the Stocks ⚡
**Bloomberg** · rss · 16 mins

1. There are two ways to use AI to trade stocks: (1) train a machine learning model on historical financial data to predict future prices — the quant hedge fund approach, which Renaissance Technologies has validated over decades — and (2) ask ChatGPT what stocks to buy. The first works theoretically and empirically. The second is incoherent: LLMs were trained on books and Reddit to write prose, not on financial data to predict prices, and the "smartest Ph.D." isn't necessarily a good stock picker either.

2. Alpha Arena, run by startup Nof1, ran eight frontier AI systems — Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT, Grok, and others — against each other in four stock-trading contests, each starting with $10,000 in US tech stocks over two weeks. The portfolio as a whole lost roughly a third of its capital. Across all 32 sets of results, a model finished in profit only six times. Nof1 founder Jay Azhang concluded: "LLMs can't really make money by themselves" — they mistimed trades, incorrectly sized positions, and traded too frequently.

3. ChatGPT gave Wall Street Journal reporter Gunjan Banerji a stock basket including Lockheed Martin; it rose ~5.5% since mid-October, lagging the S&P 500's ~8% gain. The deeper problem is structural: ChatGPT's reward function is to keep users engaged and feeling good, so it tells you what you want to hear and recommends action. A financial adviser who says "put down the phone and do nothing" gives better advice but doesn't feel valuable — both ChatGPT and retail brokers optimize for doing stuff, which doesn't optimize portfolios.

4. The entire logic of ChatGPT as an alpha-generating strategy collapses under basic market math: if asking a chatbot accessible to everyone for $20/month reliably identified stocks that would go up, everyone would do it, and no one could beat the market. Alpha requires doing something other people aren't doing — the exact opposite of the most accessible AI tool on the planet.

5. Apollo Global Management announced that $830 billion of its credit assets will be daily-priced by end of September, covering essentially 100% of its credit business. This matters because private markets are increasingly targeting retail investors who want daily liquidity — and daily liquidity requires daily prices. The Cliff Asness argument is that ignorance of daily price fluctuations was a feature (it prevented panic selling); making it mandatory makes private-credit investors feel the same emotional volatility as public-equity investors, defeating part of the product's appeal.

6. Val Sklarov, through his "Astor Asset Management," allegedly promised Mexican billionaire Ricardo Salinas Pliego a $115 million cash loan secured against ~$450 million in shares of Salinas's company Elektra. Instead of holding the shares as collateral, prosecutors allege Sklarov immediately sold all of them, used some proceeds to fund the loan, and kept "hundreds of millions of dollars" in remaining proceeds. Sklarov was indicted in the Southern District of New York on April 30; Salinas had previously told the FT: "The guy took my stock, sold it, and gave me the money as a loan. Jesus, that's as bad as it gets."

7. The TD3C benchmark — the standard rate for shipping oil from the Middle East to China — is calculated based on tanker charters from Ras Tanura, inside the Persian Gulf behind the Strait of Hormuz. When the war effectively halted those shipments, the Baltic Exchange ruled TD3C should still be calculated on that basis; rates soared from the typical $40,000–$100,000/day range to $600,000/day. Mercuria Energy is now suing the Baltic Exchange for hundreds of millions in losses; Norwegian shipper Hunter Group said a customer (Mercuria) paid $8.3 million less than owed in March because it refused to honor TD3C-pegged charter rates.

8. The TD3C situation is structurally identical to Libor's collapse: both are benchmarks measuring one narrow thing (bank borrowing costs; Ras Tanura shipping rates) that became proxies for a broader thing (risk-free interest rates; global tanker charter rates). Both broke down when the underlying market became idiosyncratically risky — in Libor's case, bank credit risk in 2008; in TD3C's case, Strait of Hormuz geopolitical risk in 2025 — forcing counterparties who had no exposure to that specific risk to pay for it anyway.

**Quotable:**

## AI & Technology

### The best AI-native GTM plays you're not running 📌
**Kyle Poyar** · email · 11 mins

1. The defining shift in GTM automation over the last 14 months is architectural, not incremental. In 2025, "automation" meant a triggered Clay enrichment followed by an AI-drafted email. In 2026, it means agents that monitor signals continuously, pull live CRM and web research, and reference what a prospect actually said on their last call. Four structural changes drove this: AI now has a context layer that understands your business, agents execute multi-step workflows autonomously, MCP connects AI directly to your GTM stack, and top teams stopped thinking in discrete "plays" and started designing persistent "systems."

2. Closed-lost re-engagement should be driven by dynamic signal clusters, not arbitrary calendar rules. The old 9-month trigger ignored the real question — has anything actually changed? The AI-native version monitors for stacked re-engagement signals (leadership change, new funding round, relevant job posting, or the deal-killer champion leaving), fires when enough pile up, then extracts the specific objection that killed the deal from the call transcript. The resulting email references *why the deal died and what's changed*, not a generic recap. Tier 1 accounts route to a rep for review; Tier 2 and below send automatically.

3. Micro-campaigns no longer require a human to spot the opportunity — the agent surfaces them autonomously. The agent continuously scans a target account list against signal sources like Sumble (job postings), G2 reviews, and LinkedIn engagement. When signals stack on a cluster of accounts, it auto-builds a 50-100 contact ICP-scored list, enriches each contact, generates copy written for that specific micro-moment, and stages the campaign. Concrete example: 13 companies posting Data Engineer roles mentioning Fivetran *plus* a key buyer liking a "migrating off Fivetran" LinkedIn post triggers a fully built outbound campaign. That campaign didn't exist yesterday and won't be relevant in three weeks.

4. Three separate relationship-based plays (past champion changed jobs, champion's prior company is ICP-fit, mutual investor intro) should collapse into a single continuously updated relationship graph. A single agent maps current and past champions, their job moves, their prior companies scored for ICP fit, mutual investors, and LinkedIn engagement patterns. When any node changes, the agent evaluates all warm paths into target accounts and ranks them by likelihood of success — then drafts the specific outreach for whichever path is highest-ranked. The most underused trigger in this system: a champion's *promotion* at their current company, which signals more budget authority and a reason to re-engage with a bigger ask.

5. Competitor displacement campaigns that rely on internal battlecards systematically underperform ones built from what prospects are actually saying. The AI-native version stacks signals across job postings (via Sumble), G2/Capterra negative reviews from ICP accounts, LinkedIn activity engaging with competitor content, and hiring patterns signaling a stack migration. When signals converge — e.g., a G2 review citing a 10K-record sync failure followed by a job posting dropping that competitor from required skills — the agent identifies the right 5-person contact list, maps the specific pain points to your differentiators, and generates outreach referencing the prospect's actual frustrations. Effective displacement emails lead with *their* scaling challenge and a relevant migration case study, not a "ready to switch?" prompt.

6. The floor of rep preparation should be set by AI, not by which reps happen to do pre-call research. The system triggers 30 minutes before every first calendar event and delivers two deliverables via Slack: a 1-page brief (company snapshot, contact LinkedIn activity, competitive context, 3 conversation starters, 2 likely objections with handling guidance) and a 3-5 slide deck personalized to that specific prospect's situation, challenge, and relevant case study. After the call, feeding the transcript back into the agent updates the brief with what was actually discussed and what objections arose — turning it into a living account brief that compounds with every interaction.

**Quotable:**
> "14 months ago, 'automation' meant setting up a signal that gets triggered, enriching a list in Clay, and having AI attempt to write an email. Today, it means AI agents that monitor signals continuously, pull research from your CRM and the web in real-time, and draft outreach that references what the prospect actually said on their last call with you." — *on the architectural shift from 2025 to 2026 GTM automation*

### The Work of Knowledge in the Age of AI Reproduction 📌
**Rex Woodbury** · email · 7 mins

1. Walter Benjamin's 1935 essay "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction" argued that every original artwork carries an "aura" rooted in uniqueness and physical presence — *Starry Night* has it, a dorm-room poster doesn't. Film was his proof case: unlike a painting, there is no original print of *Casablanca*, so the medium exists purely for reproduction. This framework proved prescient: a century of mass production produced remix culture, memes, and the blurring of "art" and "content."

2. AI reproduction of art is the direct modern parallel, but it also carries Benjamin's darker warning. When OpenAI released GPT-4o, millions generated images in Hayao Miyazaki's style overnight; Sam Altman briefly changed his profile picture to a Ghibli portrait. Miyazaki, in a 2016 clip that resurfaced, called AI animation "an insult to life itself." And the White House tweeted a Ghibli-style image of a crying woman being deported — Benjamin explicitly warned in 1935 Germany that mechanical reproduction weaponizes aesthetics for politics, what he called the "aestheticization of politics."

3. The real collapse happening now is in knowledge, not art. Throughout the 20th century, print, film, and the internet democratized information, but the "aura" of knowledge stayed with credentialed institutions — a McKinsey deck cost over $1M to produce; a Mayo Clinic diagnosis required flying to Rochester. Today, both are approximable in an afternoon with Claude. The critical distinction: what's collapsing is the *artifact* of knowledge — the deliverable — not the *judgment* behind it. Claude produces the deck; it cannot tell you which partner needs to see it or which slide to open with in the room.

4. The knowledge economy will barbell exactly as the art market did after 1935. Art's long tail exploded — millions of creators make a living online — while the top end concentrated further: Basquiats now sell for $100M+, one Da Vinci sold for $450M. The middle was squeezed out. For knowledge, the same dynamic follows: a kid in Lagos gets the same AI tutor as a kid on the Upper East Side (long tail democratizes), while McKinsey, Goldman, PwC, and *The Times* become *more* valuable, not less. These institutions sell provenance and accountability — someone whose name is on the prescription, the contract, the audit — not raw knowledge. When AI collapses skill, the sheepskin effect intensifies: a Stanford drop-out at 7/8 semesters earns 50% of a graduate's salary, not 7/8, because the degree is a signal of completion and accountability, not a proportional measure of learning. Near-term moats belong to companies that pair AI-generated knowledge artifacts with earned human credentials who bear legal and professional consequences if wrong.

**Quotable:**
> "What's collapsing is the *artifact* of knowledge, not the *judgment* behind it. Claude can produce the McKinsey deck, but not the read on which partner needs to see it, or which slide to lead with in the room. The artifact is the easy part and, increasingly, the cheap part." — *on why top credentialed institutions survive AI*

### North Korea abandons reunification 📎

### Money Stuff: Investment Banking Without a License 📎
**Bloomberg** · rss · 15 mins

1. **Morgan Stanley's Budapest office ran unlicensed investment bankers on live deals.** Morgan Stanley moved front-office analyst roles — pitchbooks, live M&A and capital-raising deals — to Budapest, where analysts worked New York hours (1 p.m. to 7 a.m. local time). A 2024 internal memo explicitly stated the Budapest analysts "are not licensed to undertake any regulated activities," including direct client contact, but the rules weren't enforced: analysts appeared on client pitchbook "Our Team" pages with US and UK flags, and one traveled to meet a European client in person. FINRA has opened an inquiry after a former employee filed a whistleblower complaint.

2. **The licensing line is fuzzy but gets crossed when the client knows your name.** Formatting pitchbooks and building financial models in a back cubicle is probably fine for unlicensed staff; the practical rule seems to be that appearing on the working-group list or the "Our Team" pitchbook page crosses the line, because those are the documents that tell clients who their advisers are and invite them to call.

3. **AI agents are next in line for this problem — and might just pass the Finra exam.** Anthropic unveiled 10 financial AI agents that draft pitch decks, review financial statements, and handle compliance escalations, targeting banks, insurers, and asset managers. If AI agents begin appearing in pitchbooks as named client-facing advisers ("Morgana can walk you through the model, day or night"), the question of whether they need Series 79 licenses becomes live — and they could probably pass.

4. **Kalshi's CFTC gambit means states can neither ban nor license sports betting.** Kalshi successfully argued that sports bets are CFTC-regulated event contracts (commodity futures), which preempts state law — so even states that prohibit online sports gambling can't stop Kalshi. But the same logic runs in reverse: state-licensed sportsbooks (DraftKings, FanDuel, etc.) are now arguably running illegal "off-exchange swap" trading with retail customers under Section 2(e) of the Commodity Exchange Act, which requires retail swap trading to occur only on a CFTC-designated contract market. Sports betting is simultaneously federally legal (on Kalshi, for 18-year-olds, nationwide) and potentially federally illegal (at every state-regulated sportsbook).

5. **The 2018 Supreme Court ruling that freed states to act has been functionally reversed — without Congress doing anything.** In *Murphy v. NCAA* (2018), the Court struck down PASPA and declared: "Congress can regulate sports gambling directly, but if it elects not to do so, each State is free to act on its own." Congress never passed sports-gambling legislation, but the CFTC's interpretation of the Commodity Exchange Act has achieved the same result: total federal preemption, applied without a vote.

6. **Deutsche Bank trader James Vorley is suing for £12 million, alleging the bank trained him to spoof.** Vorley was convicted of wire fraud in federal court in Illinois in 2020 and sentenced to 12 months and a day for spoofing gold and silver futures markets from 2008 to 2013. He claims senior Deutsche employees taught him the strategy; Deutsche's defense is that any such teaching was "informal and not endorsed or otherwise approved." The core problem this surfaces: the compliance video saying "don't spoof" is easily overridden by the senior trader next to you on the desk demonstrating exactly how to do it.

7. **Elon Musk settled SEC's Twitter 13D case for $1.5 million — roughly 1% of what he saved by breaking the rules.** Musk delayed disclosing his acquisition of more than 5% of Twitter's stock by 21 days, violating the 10-day disclosure deadline. The delay let him keep buying at pre-disclosure prices, saving him approximately $150 million at Twitter shareholders' expense. The SEC initially demanded over $200 million to settle; the Trump-era SEC accepted $1.5 million, with no admission of wrongdoing. As one reader noted: "If you're activist investors with political connections, why would you ever file a 13D on time again? Just run the same playbook and you're fine."

8. **Isaac Sylvester briefly "owned" a New Orleans bar through charisma and cash, illustrating that de facto control is about getting people to follow you.** In early March, Sylvester showed up at Bruno's Tavern claiming ownership, handed out random $100 bills to employees who pleased him, and ran the place convincingly enough that staff went along with it — until his fake New Zealand accent dissolved when he got drunk and he was eventually arrested on a 2017 forgery warrant. His run illustrated a recurring Matt Levine thesis: whoever controls the employees, suppliers, and customers controls the company, regardless of what the legal documents say.

**Quotable:**
> "If you're economic activist investors with political connections, why would you ever file a 13D on time again? Just run the same playbook and you're fine." — *reader email to Matt Levine, on Musk's $1.5M settlement for a $150M violation*

### Going Private: Watchdog calls out SRT 'circles of risk' 📎
**Bloomberg** · email · 11 mins

1. The Financial Stability Board released a report flagging "circles of risk" in the booming synthetic risk transfer (SRT) market: banks sell the default risk on pools of loans to private credit funds, then provide leverage to those same funds via loans and repos — meaning when a fund's SRT bet sours, the loss routes back to the originating bank. Lenders issued $41 billion in SRTs in 2025, up from $29 billion in 2024 (per Crescent Capital), and usage is accelerating as banks hedge commercial real estate and Middle East war exposure.

2. The FSB's data blindspot is severe: FSB member authorities can directly observe only ~$220 billion in bank credit lines to private credit funds, but commercial data estimates the real figure is more than twice as large. Secretary General John Schindler said investors themselves often lack "partial information about correlations and concentrations across loan portfolios" — meaning mispriced risks may be invisible to both regulators and the funds holding them.

3. Private credit's growing reliance on private (non-public) credit ratings is a specific FSB concern, particularly as insurance companies use those ratings to justify large allocations. The FSB noted private credit borrowers typically have "lower credit quality and higher leverage" than comparable public-market borrowers, yet the opacity of private ratings means that risk isn't being priced or surfaced consistently.

4. At the Milken Institute Global Conference, Strategic Value Partners founder Victor Khosla predicted software firm blowups will spread across the entire credit market, triggering years of elevated defaults that separate specialists from opportunistic lenders who piled in during the easy-money era. Oaktree Co-CEO Armen Panossian went further: his firm is stockpiling cash for a correction and called it "a head scratcher" that markets are so robust given the Iran war and expected software losses ahead; he also noted banks have not tightened lending standards on private credit vehicles nearly as much as he'd expected.

5. Approximately $5 billion in retail capital is currently trapped in semi-liquid private credit vehicles that imposed redemption caps last quarter to prevent fire sales. Industry consensus at Milken hardened around a single conclusion: these products should never have been called "semi-liquid" — Franklin Resources CEO Jenny Johnson stated flatly, "Private markets are illiquid. That's just it." PJT Partners CEO Paul Taubman declared retail investors will "stop fueling the growth in AUM for private credit," calling it "an institutional product, not a retail product."

6. BlackRock, RBC Global Asset Management, and VanEck are each launching or promoting public alternatives to capture fleeing BDC investors. RBC BlueBay's new CLO equity interval fund allows 25% quarterly redemptions — multiples of the single-digit caps at most BDCs — with historical CLO equity returns near 15%. Fixed income ETF daily trading has tripled since 2020 to $67 billion, spiking to $84 billion during last month's Iran War volatility, and global bond ETF assets have doubled to $3.5 trillion.

**Quotable:**
> "Software will get troubled. It'll taint everything." — *Victor Khosla, founder of Strategic Value Partners, on how AI-driven software loan stress will contaminate credit markets broadly*
