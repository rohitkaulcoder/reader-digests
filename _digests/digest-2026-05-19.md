## Today's Top 3

### Data Center Discontent, Understanding the Opposition, Fixing the Problem ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 9 mins

1. Data center opposition is now more intense than nuclear opposition ever was. A Gallup poll found 71% of Americans oppose data center construction in their area — 48% strongly — compared to just 53% who oppose nuclear plants, which peaked at only 63% opposition since Gallup began asking in 2001. Hill County, Texas passed a yearlong county-level moratorium on data centers, and in Missouri a small town voted out all four incumbent city council members who approved a data center.

2. Current opposition is inseparable from AI anxiety, not data centers per se. Previous internet-era data centers faced zero grassroots opposition — local governments *competed* to host them for the tax and economic benefits. The shift is that AI is openly pitched as a technology that will cut corporate costs by replacing workers, making data centers a visible, permissible target for people who had no recourse when factories closed during globalization.

3. The opposition is structurally bipartisan in a way that leaves tech politically isolated. Democrats are more intensely opposed (56% strongly) than Republicans (39% strongly), but both sides have reasons to hate Big Tech — the left distrusts its corporate power and job displacement, while the right resents what it saw as political censorship during COVID. Tech has no base in either party.

4. Tech's messaging problem has three interlocking causes it cannot easily escape. Some AI researchers genuinely believe most jobs are going away and won't convincingly lie otherwise; the benefits of AI are inherently future and speculative ("cures not yet discovered"), making them impossible to make concrete; and Silicon Valley is constitutionally bad at relating to mainstream society — a valley that was skeptical of Facebook, a platform for connecting with friends and family, cannot credibly speak to working-class concerns.

5. Misinformation spreads on platforms that have structural reasons not to suppress it. TikTok's algorithm is still controlled by ByteDance, a Chinese company with obvious interest in slowing U.S. AI buildout. Meta has sworn off overt content moderation after the backlash it received. X, now owned by SpaceX, has a direct financial motive to be anti-datacenter since SpaceX's upcoming public offering is partly premised on building data centers in space.

6. Direct cash payments to residents — not vague tax deals routed through local government — are the only solution likely to work. A proposed 1.6 GW data center near DeForest, Wisconsin (pop. ~11,500) could generate ~$3 billion in annual operator revenue; paying every resident $10,000/year would cost just 3.8% of that revenue, easily passed through to enterprise customers. By comparison, QTS's $50 million one-time community commitment looks trivial. This would function as a localized, opt-in UBI funded by AI infrastructure — absurd in design but pragmatic in effect, fitting the American tradition of kludgy policy that works precisely because it doesn't look like policy.

**Quotable:**
> "People who didn't have a say in globalization are suddenly finding they do have a say about AI, and it's not a surprise they are expressing their disapproval by blocking data centers." — *on why data center opposition is structurally different from past tech backlash*

### What Can You Learn from a Deterministically Chaotic Market Simulation? ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 12 mins

1. A working agent-based market simulation requires four simultaneously balanced forces: noise traders (random buys/sells that cancel out), fundamentals-aware traders, companies doing real corporate finance (issuing shares when priced high, buying back when low), and market-makers quoting two-sided prices. The system can't be built incrementally — it only becomes testable as a whole when it reaches a realistic equilibrium where prices execute a rough random walk.

2. The standard of realism for such a simulation is whether it can reproduce genuinely weird events: the 1987 crash (S&P 500 down ~20% in a single day for no clear reason), Volkswagen shares rising 5x during the 2008 financial crisis, or GameStop rising ~100x. Without occasionally producing these "chaotic" outliers, the simulation isn't realistic enough to be useful — which is also what makes it hard to debug.

3. Byrne spent on-and-off years trying to build this since 2019, repeatedly hitting an irreducible debugging wall. 24 hours of intermittent work with OpenAI's Codex finally produced a backend that simulates ~4,000 trades per second across a couple dozen discrete strategies, with a browser-based UI — a jump that previously wasn't achievable.

4. The simulation reproduces the paradox that value and momentum — which are logically opposite strategies — both generate alpha and work even better together. Value (buying stocks cheap relative to book) bets that accountants have better information than the aggregate market. Momentum (buying recent winners) bets that whoever moved the price first had high conviction and hasn't blown up yet. Both are proxies for inferred private knowledge, not valuation per se.

5. The simulation also reproduces the *lifecycle* of a strategy: capital inflows chase successful factors, push prices past fair value, and eventually cause blowups — the Antti Ilmanen "Expected Returns" chart of alpha getting crowded out then going negative. A real-world buyback/dilution factor (long heavy repurchasers, short heavy issuers) shows identical cycle shapes to the stylized chart, validating that the simulation captures genuine market dynamics rather than curve-fitted noise.

6. The simulation even generated a "compounder" spontaneously: one simulated company with above-average steady-state ROE and a preference for retaining capital grew indefinitely, and momentum strategies independently detected and chased it — an emergent property no one programmed in.

7. LLMs are the largest force-multiplier for people who have long-harbored ambitious technical projects but lacked the coding depth to execute them. The precision of prompts scales with how long someone has wanted to build something — domain expertise accumulated over years translates directly into better AI-assisted code. The backlog of over-ambitious projects by "not-technical-enough" people is now suddenly executable.

**Quotable:**
> "The longer you've wanted to attempt some technical project, the more precise your prompts are likely to be, and the bigger a force-multiplier LLMs will be. The cumulative stock of over-ambitious technical projects pursued by not-technical-enough people is yet another category subject to the sonic boom of LLMs." — *closing argument on LLMs as execution multipliers*

### Money Stuff: Sometimes Andrew Left Was Right ⚡
**Matt Levine** · email · 15 mins

1. Andrew Left (Citron Research) is on trial in Los Angeles for criminal market manipulation. The DOJ/SEC theory is a reverse pump-and-dump: Left would short a stock, tweet that he was short and the company was a fraud, his followers would sell pushing the price down, then Left would quickly cover his short — all while implying he was a long-term short. He also allegedly did the same on the long side. The specific lie, per the government, was that Left claimed long-term conviction while actually running short-term trades against his own followers.

2. Left's defense has two prongs: (a) covering a short position is normal risk management, not fraud, and (b) his underlying analysis was correct — several stocks he called frauds actually were frauds, with the SEC later bringing its own enforcement actions against them. His position is that people who followed his advice and shorted those stocks *made money*, because he was right about the companies.

3. The government's opening witnesses backfired badly. Retired Texas firefighter Billy Banks testified he took $110,000 from his 401(k) mutual funds and bought shares in cannabis penny stocks CVSI and Namaste Technologies because he believed in their CBD products. Left publicly called Namaste "a total joke" in September 2018 with a $0.25 price target (shares were at $2.89). Banks lost ~80% on Namaste. The problem for the prosecution: Namaste has since lost 99.95% of its value over eight years, the CEO was fired after Left's investigation, and CVSI has its own SEC fraud case. The defense noted Banks would have lost *less* had he sold the day Left issued his report.

4. The short-seller-as-villain narrative has a fundamental flaw: if you are investing your retirement savings in fraudulent cannabis penny stocks, the person going on TV to call them frauds is doing you a service. The damage came from whoever sold Banks those stocks, not from the analyst who correctly identified them as worthless. Left allegedly did have a cynical private text — "now that I know who owns [a stock], candy from a baby" — but the trial evidence so far supports his core claim that he was often right on the underlying analysis.

5. Private credit is generating its first wave of securities fraud litigation. BDCs (Business Development Companies) are publicly traded vehicles that hold private credit loans; their appeal is that NAV valuations barely move quarter-to-quarter, unlike public bonds. But when a BDC does slash its NAV — BlackRock TCP Capital Corp (TCPC) filed an off-cycle disclosure in January cutting asset values by 19%, sending shares down 13% in a single day (worst drop since March 2020) — the result is class-action lawsuits claiming the BDC made "materially false" statements about loan quality for months prior. The Manhattan US Attorney's office is now separately probing TCPC's valuation practices.

6. The structural problem is that BDC loan valuations are inherently lagging and subjective, which is precisely why private credit is attractive to investors (stable marks) and precisely why it becomes legally exposed when reality hits. Law firm Quinn Emanuel warned in a May 2026 client memo that "fund managers [allegedly] misstated net asset values, delayed recognition of losses, and employed inadequate valuation processes," and that regulatory enforcement actions will likely follow the civil suits.

7. The SEC proposed in early May 2026 to let public companies report financial results every six months instead of quarterly. The SEC's logic: ~20% of reporting companies (mostly small and marginal ones) would switch; those companies face high quarterly reporting costs that deter them from going public; semiannual reporting beats the alternative of staying private and disclosing nothing. The proposal is not aimed at Apple — it's aimed at getting marginal companies to list at all.

8. r/wallstreetbets (18 million members) filed a formal SEC comment letter opposing the proposal, arguing the 10-Q is "the single most important leveling mechanism between retail and institutional investors." Institutions have expert networks, channel checks, satellite imagery of parking lots, credit card transaction data, and one-on-one management access. Retail investors have the 10-Q and a Discord server. Cutting filings to twice a year doesn't eliminate the information — hedge funds with alternative data will still know the numbers — it just ensures the only people who have it are the ones who were going to outperform retail investors anyway.

**Quotable:**
> "Take away the 10-Q and you have not eliminated the information. You have just made sure that the only people who have it are the ones who were going to outperform us anyway." — *r/wallstreetbets SEC comment letter opposing semiannual reporting*

## AI & Tech Impact

### DealBook: Booing A.I. 📌
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 8 mins

1. **Graduating students are openly booing A.I. at commencement speeches — and the fear is grounded in data.** At the University of Central Florida, real estate executive Gloria Caulfield was met with loud boos when she called A.I. "the next industrial revolution." Eric Schmidt, former Google CEO, was booed repeatedly while speaking at the University of Arizona. ZipRecruiter research shows 47% of recent graduates say A.I. has already affected hiring in their field, and 51% of soon-to-be grads believe it will reduce entry-level jobs.

2. **The job displacement threat isn't limited to entry-level or routine work — it's hitting the most credentialed workers.** At the Stanford Leadership Forum, Citadel billionaire Ken Griffin said A.I. agents completed work at his hedge fund in days that previously required teams of Ph.D.s working for months. He described going home one Friday "actually fairly depressed by this." Companies including Coinbase and PayPal have already announced workforce reductions explicitly tied to A.I. adoption.

3. **Beyond job loss, A.I. is degrading the educational experience of the cohort entering the workforce.** Stanford senior Theo Baker, writing in the NYT Opinion section, reported that ChatGPT and other generative tools have made cheating "omnipresent" among the first college class of the A.I. era. Emerging research shows that relying on A.I. for cognitive tasks reduces intellectual capacity and resilience — a particular problem in classroom settings where difficulty and struggle are pedagogically essential.

4. **A proposed NextEra-Dominion Energy merger — the biggest deal of 2026 excluding SpaceX/xAI — is directly powered by A.I. data center demand, but faces serious regulatory risk over consumer electricity prices.** NextEra is offering roughly 1/8 of a share per Dominion share plus a $360 million cash payout, creating a utility serving ~10 million customers across Florida, Virginia, and other states. Data centers are expected to push peak electricity demand up more than 20% in coming summers and winters. The problem: NextEra's Florida customers just absorbed a $7 billion rate increase, and Virginia Gov. Abigail Spanberger has pledged to make data centers pay more for electricity — a tension that will directly test whether federal and state regulators clear the deal.

**Quotable:**
> "These are not mid-tier white collar jobs. These are extraordinarily high-skilled jobs." — *Ken Griffin, Citadel founder, describing A.I.'s displacement of Ph.D.-level researchers at his hedge fund*

## Finance & Markets

### India's Currency Crisis Is Now 📌
**The Core** · email · 7 mins

1. The rupee has fallen from 73 to the dollar during the pandemic, to 84 a year ago (May 2025), to 96 today — a steady decline that accelerated after Washington imposed punitive tariffs on India specifically for its continued purchase of Russian crude. The slide is not a blip: foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) have yanked roughly $50 billion from Indian equities over the past 18 months, driven by structural doubts about corporate growth, stretched valuations, capital gains taxes on foreign investors, and chronic regulatory unpredictability.

2. Dollar drain is coming from multiple directions simultaneously. FDI is being repatriated as VC and PE firms find exits; domestic companies are deploying capital abroad; and Indian consumers are burning forex on gold imports, offshore education, international travel, and large offshore investments. Iran's blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — through which 55% of India's crude ordinarily flows — has pushed global crude up over 50% since the West Asia conflict began, compounding the import bill and triggering the first petrol/diesel price hike in four years (Delhi petrol now Rs 97.77/litre, diesel Rs 90.67 — highs since May 2022).

3. The policy response has been dangerously passive at the federal level. The Finance Ministry has been "remarkably quiet," and private meetings with visiting institutional investors are insufficient when global capital is shifting at scale. By contrast, Andhra Pradesh under CM N. Chandrababu Naidu is making near-weekly investment announcements — data centers, motorcycle plants — totaling tens of billions, replicating his successful 2000s transformation of Hyderabad. Most other state leaders and the central government remain conspicuously absent from global financial capitals.

4. India's leadership needs to do what Trump did when he visited China flanked by Nvidia, Apple, and Tesla executives: embark on high-profile roadshows across major global financial centers. Finance ministry officials and India's business titans must pitch the India story, field hard questions, and — critically — listen to what global capital actually requires to return. Returning the rupee toward 84 requires wooing back portfolio capital; promises of future reform won't move foreign investor sentiment today.

**Quotable:**
> "A roadshow isn't just public relations; it is a market signal of intent." — *on why passive diplomacy is insufficient to reverse the rupee's slide*

### Unhedged: Bonds bad 📎
**Katie Martin** · email · 7 mins

1. Global bond yields surged late last week with no obvious news catalyst — the trigger was renewed alarm over inflation from the war in Iran. Mike Riddell at Fidelity International estimates UK domestic politics contributed only 5–10 basis points of the ~45bp rise in 10-year gilt yields over the past month; the other 35bps is oil.

2. Long-end yields are hitting historic levels: Japan's 30-year crossed 4%, the UK's 30-year is near 6%, and the US 30-year just broke 5%. The US 2-year Treasury is up 20bps in a week and a half, and futures markets now price a better-than-50% chance of at least one 25bp Fed rate hike by year-end — versus just 15% odds a week earlier.

3. The bond selloff isn't just an inflation story — the structural buyer base has collapsed. The Bank of England is doing active gilt sales, incoming Fed chair Kevin Warsh is a committed balance-sheet shrinker, long-end pension demand has faded, and governments are borrowing heavily for defense and green energy while struggling to raise taxes. Gulf and Chinese demand for Treasuries is also in question.

4. Anshul Pradhan at Barclays wrote Friday that breaking 5% on the US 30-year was "not the end of the story" — the market sailed through that level "without much difficulty" — and that 5.5% "does not seem far-fetched" given continued economic resilience and persistent fiscal concerns, with little prospect of Treasury or Fed intervention soon.

5. The US stock market has been narrowing since roughly April 20: before that, the equal-weight S&P 500 outperformed its cap-weighted sibling, small-caps beat large-caps, and European stocks moved in step with the US. Since then, gains have been driven exclusively by info tech — and within that, specifically by hardware and semiconductors tied to AI data centers — while everything else has stalled.

6. The setup echoes 2021, when the market rose while narrowing behind the Magnificent Seven, then collapsed in early 2022 once the Fed accepted that pandemic inflation was not transitory and began hiking rates. If rising yields compress the discounted value of AI companies' future profits, the one remaining pillar of the rally disappears.

**Quotable:**
> "How far can 30-year yields go? We are wary of drawing a line in the sand, but 5.5 per cent does not seem far-fetched." — *Anshul Pradhan, Barclays, on US 30-year Treasury yields after breaking 5%*

## Politics & Geopolitics

### The World: When nationalism beats nationalism 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. Reform UK routed the governing centre-left Labour Party in English local elections in May 2026, but in Wales and Scotland a different kind of nationalism held the line. Plaid Cymru (Party of Wales) will govern Wales for the first time in its history; the Scottish National Party won a fifth consecutive term. Both parties defeated Reform in their respective nations — the only parts of the U.K. where the right-wing surge failed.

2. The key divergence is ethnic vs. civic nationalism. Right-wing movements like Reform, MAGA, and India's BJP define belonging by ancestral claim — who got there first — which readily morphs into ethnic exclusion. Plaid and the SNP practice civic nationalism: the nation is open to anyone who pledges allegiance to a set of values rooted in Welsh and Scottish tradition. Reform wants portraits of the king in every classroom; Plaid and the SNP want more Welsh and Gaelic taught in schools — both use cultural heritage to build identity, but around different stories.

3. The Welsh and Scottish nationalist story casts Westminster — not immigrants — as the threat to the nation. Since Margaret Thatcher shuttered Scotland's and Wales's heavy industries, voters in both nations have consistently elected to the left of England. Immigration is framed as a solution, not a problem: both Rhun ap Iorwerth (Wales's new First Minister) and Scotland's John Swinney argue their nations need immigrants because of falling birthrates and youth emigration. Former SNP leader Humza Yousaf — a descendant of Punjabi and Kenyan immigrants — was designing his own Yousaf tartan to sit alongside the MacDonalds and MacDuffs, embodying the argument.

4. Nationalism has no fixed political content, making it available to the left. Bernard Yack, a political theorist at Brandeis University, calls it "an incomplete ideology" that has historically fused with fascism, communism, and national liberation movements alike. Left-leaning parties elsewhere are experimenting: former Green Party leader Caroline Lucas published *Another England*, reclaiming national identity around Robin Hood and the NHS rather than empire; German Greens campaigned in dirndls while quoting the national anthem. But Richard Child of Manchester University cautions that narrative alone isn't enough — parties must actually deliver improved material conditions, and Reform is already making inroads in Scotland and Wales.

**Quotable:**
> "People who love our nation include those who were born here and those who moved here yesterday." — *Rhun ap Iorwerth, new First Minister of Wales, after Plaid Cymru's election victory*

### The Morning: The fighting never ends 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. Cease-fires in the Trump era have become "a tool of performative diplomacy, stand-alone commodities used to manage media cycles while the machinery of war grinds along," according to Ukraine war correspondent Marc Santora. A cease-fire generates good headlines and briefly reduces deaths, but leaves the underlying conflict entirely untouched — and may even allow leaders to avoid the harder conversations about war's origins and the possibility of justice.

2. The three-day Russia-Ukraine truce expired at midnight Monday with front-line fighting that never actually stopped, followed immediately by Russia resuming bombardments of Ukrainian towns and cities. The prior week, Russia blew up an apartment building in Kyiv; by Friday, rescue workers had recovered the bodies of at least 24 people, including two children.

3. The Israel-Hezbollah cease-fire exists almost entirely on paper. NYT Jerusalem bureau chief David Halbfinger reports Israeli airstrikes are killing Palestinians in Gaza nearly every day, Hezbollah drones are targeting Israeli soldiers, and one Israeli division alone reported killing 60 Hezbollah militants in Lebanon over a single week. There are no Iranian missiles hitting Israel at the moment, but that could change before a newsletter publishes.

4. In Lebanon, hundreds of people have been killed since the truce took effect, Israeli surveillance drones maintain a constant presence over Beirut, and evacuation warnings for entire towns in the south remain a daily occurrence. Correspondent Euan Ward, writing from Beirut, notes Hezbollah continues broadcasting drone-footage propaganda videos of attacks on Israeli forces — "this is not a war that feels over."

5. Cease-fires hold only if accompanied by real political compromises from both sides, per writer Linda Kinstler. Without that, by using cease-fires world leaders have "avoided difficult conversations about the origins of wars and the possibility of justice, and left the entire world less stable" — making the brief reprieve no reprieve at all.

**Quotable:**
> "In Israel and Gaza, it's hard to talk about cease-fires with a straight face, or at least without an ironic tone... it ain't peace, either." — *David Halbfinger, NYT Jerusalem bureau chief*

### Is Modi's sheen coming off? 📎
**Veena Venugopal** · email · 7 mins

1. Modi announced a raft of austerity measures urging citizens to cut foreign travel, reduce gold purchases, and use less fuel — framing India's economic stress as a "Covid-type" crisis. The announcement came only after state elections were safely won, which amplified the sense of political manipulation. Stock markets have been deep in the red and the rupee has been consistently hitting new lows, making it one of the worst-performing major currencies for several quarters running.

2. For the first time in Modi's tenure, discontent is spreading within his core constituency: urban middle-class India. This cohort had previously been shielded from the worst of demonetisation and the pandemic, but the rupee's slide has directly hit the cost of overseas education, foreign business travel, and leisure trips. When media reports speculated the government was considering a surcharge on overseas travel, the backlash on social media was so severe that Modi personally dismissed the story, calling it "totally false" with "not an iota of truth" — an unusually direct rebuttal that signalled how seriously his team took the anger.

3. Chief Justice Surya Kant poured fuel on the fire by describing unemployed young Indians as being "like cockroaches" who, failing to find jobs, turn to social media, journalism, and activism to "attack the system." Within 24 hours, a satirical group — the Cockroach Janta Party — had been formally registered, published a manifesto, adopted an election symbol, and attracted more than 50,000 members. Some observers see it as the seed of a genuine Gen Z political movement of the kind that has already convulsed India's neighbours but has never taken root here.

4. The structural stakes depend heavily on the Middle East conflict. If the conflict ends quickly and economic anxiety eases, the discontent will likely dissolve before the 2029 general elections. But the window for Modi to paper over these fissures is narrowing: the combination of a weakening currency, a squeezed middle class, and a suddenly activated younger demographic is a configuration his political machine has not had to manage before.

**Quotable:**
> "Within 24 hours, a satirical political outfit — the Cockroach Janta Party — had been registered, published a manifesto, adopted an election symbol and reportedly attracted more than 50,000 members." — *on the political fallout from Chief Justice Surya Kant's remark comparing unemployed youth to cockroaches*
