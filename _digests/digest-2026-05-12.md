## Today's Top 3

### The Inference Shift ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 10 mins

1. Cerebras is IPO-ing in May 2026 at a raised price range of $150–$160/share (up from $115–$125), selling 30 million shares, riding AI chip demand. Its WSE-3 chip is a full-wafer single chip with 44GB of on-chip SRAM at 21 PB/s bandwidth — vs. the H100's 80GB HBM at 3.35 TB/s. That's half the memory but 6,000× the bandwidth, making it extremely fast for inference but impractical for training.

2. GPU dominance in AI stems from two structural facts: training is serial across steps (every GPU must sync before the next step), requiring massive pooled HBM and high-speed networking — both Nvidia strengths; and inference reuses the same architecture because decode alternates between reading the KV cache and the model weights, both memory-bandwidth-bound. Anthropic's deal with SpaceX to use Colossus 1's 220,000 Nvidia GPUs (originally a training cluster) for Claude inference illustrates how GPU flexibility has made one architecture serve both workloads.

3. There are two fundamentally different kinds of inference: "answer inference" (an LLM responding to a human waiting for a reply) and "agentic inference" (a model doing work autonomously, potentially tasked by other machines, with no human in the loop). Speed is the critical constraint for answer inference; memory — KV cache, host DRAM, SSDs, databases, embeddings, object stores — is the critical constraint for agentic inference.

4. Agentic inference inverts the chip economics: if no human is waiting, latency stops mattering and slower, cheaper memory (DRAM, SSDs) becomes the right trade-off. Chips don't need to be cutting-edge; "good enough" compute suffices. Nvidia's own Dynamo inference framework and its standalone memory/CPU racks acknowledge this disaggregation, but cost and simplicity favor hyperscalers building non-GPU-bound agentic stacks.

5. Agentic inference will dwarf answer inference and training as a market because its scale is bounded by compute, not by the number of humans interacting with it. China, despite lacking leading-edge GPUs, has everything needed — adequate GPUs, CPUs, DRAM, storage — making it competitive specifically in the agentic inference segment. Space-based data centers also become more viable: simpler, cooler chips on older process nodes handle radiation better, consume less power, and are more reliable without on-site repair.

**Quotable:**
> "The true power of agents will not be that they do work for humans, but rather that they do work without human involvement at all." — *on why agentic inference will eventually dwarf answer inference*

### Why does Resource Extraction Produce Such Concentrated Wealth? ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 12 mins

1. Mining punches above its weight in producing billionaires: 3.1% of *Forbes* billionaires made their money in metals and mining, despite the sector being only ~1.9% of global listed equities market cap. Eight of the hundred richest people in the world's largest economies got there via mining — yet the S&P Metals & Mining ETF has returned just 6.4% annualized since launch vs. 11.4% for SPY. The sector produces extreme individual wealth while delivering mediocre aggregate returns to public shareholders.

2. Cyclicality is the structural trap: mining absorbs the most capital precisely at cycle peaks, when valuations are highest and projects most ambitious. The equity of a high-cost miner can 100x in a good cycle but then be wiped out in the bust — Warrior Met Coal's shareholders have compounded at 33% since its 2017 IPO, but "Warrior Met" was the new holding company formed by Walter Energy's first-lien creditors after shareholders were zeroed in 2016. Bondholders capture the 100x; equity holders get zeroed.

3. "Engineering Ego" drives capital destruction at cycle peaks: engineers attracted to mining want to attempt extreme, record-setting projects, and malleable estimates give them enough wiggle room to justify them. The 1982 Mukluk disaster — a billion-dollar dry hole in Alaska — briefly suppressed this behavior, but ego-driven project inflation reliably returns when commodity prices rise. Unlike prestige industries where ego subsidizes investor returns (workers accept below-market wages), mining ego extracts wealth from investors toward grandiose capex.

4. Luck compounds fractally at every scale — land ownership, timing, and supply shocks. The accident of who holds mineral rights is obvious, but within that, idiosyncratic bets can be validated by unrelated geopolitical events (betting on oil demand and then bombs falling on Tehran). At the operations level, n-of-1 equipment and complex supply chains create fragility: in 2009 a single mine responsible for 10% of global uranium production went offline for seven months — and these failures are most likely when equipment runs at full utilization, i.e. exactly when prices are highest.

5. Political risk is the most extreme wealth concentrator in weak-institution countries. Host governments nominally own subsurface resources and collect royalties, but where rule of law is fragile, resource revenues become a negotiated distribution among elites. The Dutch Disease effect (resource exports appreciate the currency, hurting other exporters, making luxury imports cheaper) efficiently routes wealth to the politically connected — the local "Wabenzi" — while immiserating everyone else. *Forbes* calls it "mining" wealth; it's more accurately bagman wealth. These fortunes stay private precisely because public markets would require financial disclosure.

6. Commodity exchanges themselves are a source of unhedgeable risk. Unlike equity exchanges, commodity exchanges serve constituencies with physical delivery obligations — miners and commodity consumers — giving powerful real-world actors leverage over market rules. In the 1970s this happened with potato futures; in 2022 the London Metals Exchange simply canceled a day's nickel trading when a major producer faced a short squeeze. Traders on the winning side of these interventions accumulate concentrated wealth; everyone else absorbs the uncertainty.

7. Information asymmetry between insiders and public investors is the final lever. Headlines routinely announce "trillions in resources" for Afghanistan, the ocean, asteroids — a figure totally disconnected from "reserves," the technically and economically extractable subset. Many SIC-coded mining companies primarily manufacture press releases and capital raises. Insiders who understand the cycle and commodity trader psychology can time their own exits as the public is most overweight; the result is that founders and executives have a systematically better financial experience from the same company than outside shareholders.

8. The corrective path mirrors what airlines achieved: credible multi-cycle governance signals that earn a lower cost of capital. A mine without a bribery line item is worth more; a company that doesn't drill for optics is worth more. Whoever can credibly commit to those standards will end up with the lowest cost of capital — and over time, resources flow toward the lowest-cost-of-capital owner regardless of who originally held them.

**Quotable:**
> "Forbes might list the relevant source of wealth as 'mining,' but it's more apt to say that these rich people are bagmen." — *on how political capture, not geology, explains the billionaire-per-mine ratio in weak-institution countries*

### Money Stuff: KKR Buys Back Some Private Credit ⚡
**msprvs1=20591rz93YrZI=bounces-280172-1781@bounce.news.bloomberg.com** · rss · 12 mins

1. **KKR injects $300M into its own BDC via a deliberately split structure.** KKR put $150M into FS KKR Capital Corp. (FSK) as preferred equity convertible at $18.83/share — the fund's NAV — and simultaneously tendered for $150M of common shares at $11 (against a market price of $10.84 and that same $18.83 NAV). The preferred leg is optically confidence-signaling; the common tender is economically superior, buying $18.83 of assets for $11.

2. **Fund managers face two logically contradictory obligations simultaneously.** Investing at NAV says "I believe the portfolio is worth what we say it is." Investing at the market discount says "I'm not an idiot — buying good assets cheap is what I'm paid to do." KKR's split trade is a synthesis that avoids fully choosing either horn of the dilemma.

3. **Apollo is exploring selling MidCap Financial Investment Corp. (MFIC), but the only viable buyer is another BDC.** MFIC's stock trades at ~85% of NAV. No cash buyer will pay 100 cents; Apollo can't in good conscience sell at 85. The only plausible mechanism is a stock-for-stock deal where a similarly discounted acquirer exchanges its own 85-cent dollars — the discount cancels out and neither side concedes they're worth less than NAV.

4. **Private credit's lending volume fell 14% in Q1 2026 while bank lending surged 12.7%, the fastest pace since 2022.** The OCC explicitly announced in January it was relaxing post-crisis leveraged-loan rules to let banks compete with private credit on price. Private credit built its dominance on a regulatory arbitrage (banks couldn't underwrite risky loans cheaply); deregulation is unwinding that edge.

5. **The Virginia redistricting referendum (51.7% yes) was declared "null and void" by the Virginia Supreme Court, and Kalshi's prediction markets produced contradictory resolutions.** The binary "did it pass?" contract ($5.7M in bets) resolved Yes in April via accelerated media-declaration rules; the "margin of victory" contract ($10M in bets) remains unresolved because there are no certified final results to trigger its payout. The referendum simultaneously passed and did not pass depending on which contract you examine — a consequence of Kalshi's two-track resolution mechanism rather than any underlying ambiguity in the vote itself.

6. **75 OpenAI employees each walked away with the full $30M cap in the most recent tender offer, tripled from a prior $10M limit.** The cap was raised "in response to demand from investors" desperate to buy OpenAI shares from the only willing sellers: employees. The conventional logic — keep founders hungry by making them illiquid until IPO — has collapsed: AI researcher compensation has reached the level where the gap between "competitive enough to hire" and "comfortable enough to retire at 28" no longer exists. SF rents are already rising in consequence.

**Quotable:**

Also the AI researchers are all like 20 minutes out of PhD programs and haven't had time to learn which Hamptons are déclassé. The gap between 'enough money to be competitive' and 'enough that they never need to work again' has been completely erased." — *on OpenAI's compensation dilemma*

### Chips have an earnings season of their own 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. Semiconductor earnings are blowing past estimates in a way that's genuinely unusual, not just the routine sandbagging seen every quarter. AI demand is surging while supply remains more constrained than expected — partly because the same Strait of Hormuz bottleneck threatening oil also affects chip-manufacturing inputs. Brokers now forecast that 2026 earnings for the SOX semiconductor index will be almost double 2025's, a revision scale that has no recent precedent.

2. The market has repriced who wins from AI: semiconductors have overtaken the Magnificent Seven. Since ChatGPT launched, the conventional wisdom was that the AI platforms (Microsoft, Google, Meta, et al.) would capture most of the value, with chipmakers as mere "picks and shovels." Over the last six months that narrative inverted — the SOX index has now outperformed the Mag-7 in cumulative returns since the AI era began, driven by real revenues and profits rather than multiple expansion alone. Nvidia sits in both camps, but the directional shift is clear.

3. Gains are concentrating relentlessly at the top. The cap-weighted S&P 500 is dramatically outpacing its equal-weighted version (where every stock counts 0.2%), a spread that has widened throughout the AI era. The Magnificent Seven beat the rest of the S&P 500, but the broader index rally itself is essentially a story of a handful of mega-caps lifting the average; the median stock is much less impressive.

4. April non-farm payrolls came in at +115,000 with unchanged unemployment — solid enough to confirm the recession fears of 2024–25 were overblown, but not hot enough to reignite them. The problem is inflation: higher oil prices from the US-Iran conflict are widely expected to push headline CPI to ~4% when Tuesday's print arrives. Oxford Economics' Nancy Vanden Houten pushed her next-Fed-cut forecast from June to December; futures markets go further and price the Fed on hold into the second half of 2026. Macquarie's Viktor Shvets describes an "unstable equilibrium" where AI investment boosts GDP but aggravates inequality, while trade and geopolitical whiplash makes non-AI investment uncertain — making Fed tightening as likely as easing.

5. Waymo's robotaxi fleet now completes 500,000+ trips per week, but Uber alone handles roughly 1 million mobility trips *per hour* — a 100x scale gap that frames how far AVs must travel to matter. Goldman Sachs forecasts the global market at $415 billion by 2035 ($48B US). A vertically integrated company that both builds and operates robotaxis could achieve 30–50% gross margins, with cumulative sector gross profits of $440 billion over the next decade, per Goldman's Mark Delaney; that math depends on total cost-per-mile falling below $1 and human supervisors covering 26 vehicles each (vs. 6 today). Tesla's camera-only Full Self-Driving — which uses probabilistic inference with no redundancy — doesn't yet qualify as true autonomy by critics' standards, and timeline slippage has let Waymo and others leapfrog it for commercial fleet contracts.

**Quotable:**
> "It is a camera-only system running probabilistic inference. The car is making statistical guesses about what it sees thousands of times per second, with no redundancy when those guesses are wrong." — *George Noble of Noble Capital Advisors, on Tesla's Full Self-Driving system*

### 🎙️ How I AI: Quests, token leaderboards, and the elite AI adoption playbook & Notion's spec-driven development 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 7 mins

1. **Sendbird CEO John Kim gamified AI adoption with a platform called "Automators"** — an internal marketplace where anyone posts a "quest" (a request for automation), engineers or AI agents claim it, and completers earn XP redeemable for gift cards, exec tea-time, or a slot to present at Wednesday all-hands. Each quest displays its risk level, weeks saved, and beneficiaries. This turns AI adoption from a top-down mandate into a product people actually want to use.

2. **Token consumption is Kim's primary organizational health metric**, with five tiers from Beginner (<1M tokens/day) through Intermediate, Expert, Architect, Catalyst, up to "AI God" (>100M tokens/day). Managers see every direct report's tier and tailor enablement accordingly. Critically, Kim watches the *shape* of the token-usage curve over time — a smooth curve means AI agents are working 24/7 even when humans are offline; dips on weekends mean the org hasn't yet unlocked autonomous AI work.

3. **Non-technical teams ship to production at Sendbird because pre-vetted, InfoSec-approved app templates remove the fear of doing something insecure.** Marketers, salespeople, and CSMs extract a template with auth, databases, and environment pre-configured, then build on top. Kim's marketing team shipped a live Stripe-integrated swag store — complete with a Konami Code Easter egg unlocking secret conference details — in days, work that previously would have consumed two engineering sprints and likely been deprioritized.

4. **Notion engineer Ryan Nystrom identifies CI speed as the hard ceiling on AI coding velocity.** An hour-long CI pipeline means every coding agent sits idle for an hour per iteration; a 3-minute pipeline enables 20x more iterations in the same window. Notion is actively cutting its CI to 25% of current runtime specifically to unlock parallel agent work. Nystrom's "Boxy" system already demonstrates the ceiling in action: @mention Codex from a Notion task, and 20 minutes later a PR arrives with implementation, UI screenshots, and a preview URL — no IDE, no local environment, no context switch.

5. **Specs as version-controlled Markdown files replace code as the primary source of truth for feature development.** Notion engineers maintain plain-English spec files in the repo with code pointers and verification steps; updating a feature means updating the spec and pointing Codex at it. Nystrom's "yap your spec" workflow — speaking feature requirements into Whisper, passing the transcript to Codex with existing spec examples — produces more thorough technical documents than typing because verbal explanations naturally surface edge cases that written bullet points omit.

**Quotable:**
> "Fast CI is the mathematical limit on your AI coding velocity. If your CI takes an hour to run, your agents sit idle for an hour waiting for test results." — *Ryan Nystrom, engineering manager at Notion*

## Markets & Policy

### DealBook: Private equity's new tax fight 📌
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 8 mins

1. Yale's Budget Lab just upended the long-running carried interest debate with a new revenue estimate of $87.7 billion over a decade — significantly higher than prior Congressional Budget Office projections. The key methodological difference: instead of looking at aggregate capital gains, Yale used Schedule K-1 tax form data from academic research to isolate the specific performance-related distributions flowing to general partners (fund managers) versus limited partners. This revealed that far more ordinary income was being reclassified as capital gains — and taxed at lower rates — than previously understood.

2. The private equity industry's lobbying arm, Private Investment Works, immediately attacked the Yale report as "a splashy claim. It's also wrong." Their core counterargument is behavioral: if carried interest rates jump to the ordinary income rate of 37% or higher, fund managers will simply launch fewer funds or move operations overseas, producing a revenue mirage where total tax receipts actually fall. PIW also cites an economist's study claiming the loophole's closure would *reduce* federal tax receipts — a position that runs counter to CBO analyses and mainstream economic consensus.

3. The legislative history explains why this math fight matters now. In 2022, Democrats dropped a carried interest provision from the Inflation Reduction Act at the last minute to secure Senator Kyrsten Sinema's vote — after heavy industry lobbying of her specifically. That episode cemented private equity's reputation for Washington clout. Last month, Senator Ron Wyden (D-OR) reintroduced a bill to close the loophole, reviving the fight under a very different political landscape.

4. Separately, U.S.-Iran war negotiations have stalled, with Brent crude at ~$104/barrel and U.S. gas averaging $4.52/gallon. Trump rejected Iran's demands including war reparations and sovereignty recognition over the Strait of Hormuz. Saudi Aramco CEO Amin Nasser warned that continued Hormuz disruption beyond a few more weeks could keep oil prices elevated through 2027; Morgan Stanley analysts said prices could spike to $150/barrel if the strait stays closed into late June or July. Energy Secretary Chris Wright — who said in March there was a "good chance" gas would fall below $3/gallon — now says high prices will persist as long as the war continues.

I say that with great affection and sympathy." — *John Cochrane, senior fellow at the Hoover Institution, on Trump's Fed chair nominee Kevin Warsh, who faces war-driven inflation, Trump's pressure for lower rates, and predecessor Jerome Powell who isn't leaving yet*

### Another thorn in Europe's side 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 8 mins

1. Bulgaria's April 19 election produced a new premier — Rumen Radev, 62, a retired general and former fighter pilot with a 2003 master's degree from the US Air War College in Alabama — who shares several of Viktor Orbán's most troubling positions. Radev strongly advocates closer ties with Russia (Bulgaria's main energy supplier until Putin's 2022 invasion of Ukraine), labels backers of military aid to Kyiv as "warmongers," and has praised Orbán's "sober assessment" of geopolitics. He has also repeatedly criticized EU sanctions against the Kremlin, arguing better relations with Russia would unlock economic growth.

2. The Orbán comparison, however, is partly misleading. Radev centered his campaign on dismantling corruption in a country tied with Hungary as the EU's most corrupt member, comparing the business-politician relationship to the Mafia. Crucially, he promised not to block common EU decisions — Orbán's signature tactic — as long as Bulgaria is not required to contribute money or weapons to Ukraine. Dennis Shen, a lecturer at the International School of Management in Berlin, assessed that a Radev government "could slow consensus building within the EU, especially on issues linked to the Russia-Ukraine war, but not derail it."

3. Radev's Progressive Bulgaria party won a parliamentary majority in the country's eighth general election in five years, giving him a stronger mandate than any premier since the 1990s. That electoral dominance matters because Bulgaria's dysfunction is severe: it has the EU's highest income inequality, one of the bloc's least efficient healthcare systems, near-bottom rankings in media freedom, and only about half of Bulgarians believe EU membership has benefited the country — the lowest score in the entire EU. The country adopted the euro only in January 2026.

4. If Radev delivers on anti-corruption reform, the payoff could be substantial: the EU has withheld funding pending reforms, and success could also jumpstart foreign investment and boost Bulgaria's anemic economic growth. His own parliamentary leader, Petar Vitanov, compared him not to Orbán but to Hungary's Péter Magyar — the reformer who just ousted Orbán — saying: "The peoples of both countries were seeking a fundamental change." Whether Radev governs more like the Orbán he admires or the Magyar he is compared to will determine whether Bulgaria becomes Europe's next problem or its next turnaround story.

**Quotable:**

### The World: Shady shipping 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. The U.S. blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — imposed during what the newsletter describes as the war's third month — has trapped more than 1,500 ships in the Persian Gulf and turned the strait into an accidental lens on the lawless side of global shipping. Reporter Jenny Gross and colleagues spend days cross-referencing databases and interviewing analysts, yet still miss ships: the Iran-flagged tanker *Huge*, a vessel over 300 meters long, materialized in Southeast Asia on May 3 having apparently slipped through the strait undetected with its transponder off.

2. Ships evade detection through three main techniques: going "dark" (switching off the AIS transponder that feeds tracking platforms like MarineTraffic), spoofing (falsifying transponder data — recently producing one vessel that appeared to be anchored in downtown Tehran and another in the Saudi desert), and ship-to-ship mid-ocean oil transfers that obscure cargo origins. On a single Thursday when MarineTraffic showed zero strait transits, a satellite analysis by Windward AI found nine heavy commercial vessels had actually passed through that day.

3. The U.S. government itself has blurred the line between licit and illicit evasion: under "Project Freedom," U.S. officials advised some commercial ships trying to exit the strait to go dark for their own protection — the same tactic deployed by Russia, Iran, Venezuela, and drug cartels running "shadow fleets" to circumvent sanctions. This normalizes AIS-off transits among operators with no sanctions exposure, complicating future enforcement.

4. The deeper structural problem is jurisdictional: the International Maritime Organization sets rules for global shipping, but ships cross multiple jurisdictions in international waters, making rule-breaking easy for those motivated to evade. Richard Meade of Lloyd's List summarizes it plainly. The safety risk is also real — Andreas Enger, CEO of Norwegian shipping company Höegh Autoliners, warns that widespread dark sailing creates "a huge risk problem for everybody" by eliminating the collision-avoidance data that maritime traffic relies on.

**Quotable:**
> "The rules upon which global trade is based are not that enforceable." — *Richard Meade, editor of Lloyd's List*

### India's Twin Streams Of Investment 📎
**The Core** · email · 7 mins

1. Net FDI into India has collapsed while multinational physical presence expands — these are not contradictory signals but a redefinition of what investment looks like. In the first 11 months of FY26, net FDI fell to just $6.3 billion, down from a $35.1 billion average between FY16 and FY20, driven by rising foreign investor repatriation and increasing outward investment by Indian firms. Yet multinationals are setting up new Global Capability Centers (GCCs) in India at a rate of more than one per week, and GCCs have accounted for roughly 40% of all office space leasing in India over the last decade.

2. GCCs have moved far beyond back-office outsourcing — they now run core technology and AI operations for their parent companies. There are 2,117 GCCs operating across 3,728 units in India, generating $98.4 billion annually (per Nasscom), which constitutes nearly a third of India's $315 billion software services export market. Over 1,200 of these centers have advanced AI/ML capabilities, with 250+ operating as dedicated AI/ML centers of excellence; Chevron, for instance, runs global refinery monitoring and real-time well-data operations out of Bangalore.

3. India's 250,000-strong GCC AI workforce represents 28% of global GCC AI talent — second only to the United States — making it the dominant offshore destination for high-complexity technology work. Executives at British Telecom, Fiserv, and others confirmed that global AI initiatives, including AI-assisted engineering and complex cloud migrations, are often orchestrated from Bangalore, Hyderabad, or Pune. The GCC boom is also no longer concentrated in traditional tech hubs; it is spreading into Coimbatore, Ahmedabad, Kolkata, Vadodara, and Kochi.

4. GCCs cannot fully substitute for traditional FDI — but they create compounding economic effects of their own. Manufacturing FDI brings supply chains, hardware technology, and direct consumer market investment that GCCs do not inherently provide; the economic multiplier from building an automotive plant is immediate and broad. However, GCC expansion drives real estate absorption at scale, generates high-skill employment that upgrades a generation of Indian professionals, and anchors multinational decision-making in India rather than merely execution.

**Quotable:**
> "You may not be building your next factory in India, but you will almost certainly be building your next algorithm there." — *summarizing multinational boardroom consensus on India's evolving investment role*
