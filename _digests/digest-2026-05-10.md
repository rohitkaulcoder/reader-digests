### SpaceX's Compounding Hardware Advantage ⚡
**Contrary Research** · email · 4 mins

1. ### Reactions to "We Have Learned Nothing" ⚡
**Jerry Neumann** · rss · 11 mins

1. The BLS startup failure rate chart — plotted slightly differently than the bureau presents it — is flat over 30 years. Lean startup, customer development, the business model canvas, design thinking, and effectuation all entered mainstream pedagogy starting in the 2000s, yet the survival curve didn't move. Morten Ansteensen at NTNU replicated this for Norwegian startups (all-company and tech-only subsets) and found the same flatness at nearly identical failure rates as the US, which makes the stability even harder to dismiss as a data artifact.

2. The most common pushback — "your data is too broad / too narrow / my company used these and succeeded" — collapses against a simple Bayesian frame: the null hypothesis is that the methods don't work, and a flat curve is consistent with that. The stronger challenge is that the curve is *weirdly* flat — flat through boom and bust cycles, expanding and contracting founder pools, 30+ years of macroeconomic change. Something is holding the rate constant, and Neumann says he genuinely doesn't know what.

3. Redefining success as "fail faster" or "bigger outcomes" doesn't rescue the methods. If lean works by catching bad ideas before they kill companies, the effect should show up as higher company survival rates — founders pivot, they don't just quit. On "bigger outcomes": the power law distribution of startup returns hasn't changed shape; the higher peaks are explained by more draws from the same fat-tailed distribution. More startups → higher maximum outcome, with no improvement in the underlying hit rate.

4. The "people don't use them correctly" objection is straight No True Scotsman. The "Red Queen" counter — once every competitor knows a technique it no longer confers advantage — is the one valid alternative explanation. If that's true, founders still need to learn the methods to avoid falling behind, but the methods can't raise the overall success rate. This is empirically distinguishable with more data, but that data doesn't yet exist.

5. The rift between practitioners and researchers is structural and cultural. Neumann brought observations from 30 years and 100+ companies to a meeting with two academics who had read his book; one replied, "it's not your job to interpret what you experienced — that's what we do." Academic research is largely paywalled past graduation day, so practitioners never read it. Neumann's proposed fix: something like the theoretical-physics / applied-physics split in universities, with explicit pipelines running in both directions.

6. Frank Knight said 100 years ago that what makes good entrepreneurs good is *judgment* — learnable but not teachable. Neumann spent years trying to find something better. His own data leads straight back to Knight. The one partial exception he's warming to is effectuation (Saras Sarasvathy's work, built on Herbert Simon): it describes what entrepreneurs actually do rather than prescribing steps, and Rob Wuebker at the University of Utah argues it functions as design patterns for generating diverse strategies rather than a framework — which would sidestep the Red Queen problem. Neumann isn't convinced yet, but calls it "the best we have right now."

**Quotable:**
> "One of them said 'it's not your job to interpret what you experienced; that's what we do.' I'm not interested in just being the object of observation. No one thinks harder about the situations I was in than I did." — *Neumann on the practitioner–researcher divide, after bringing 30 years of hands-on observations to a meeting with academics*

### "Authenticity is a story the tourist tells about herself" ⚡
**The Substack Post** · email · 12 mins

1. Young Westerners have stopped caring about travel ethics — not because they can't afford to go, but because they no longer believe travel delivers self-transformation. Grant David Crawford, who teaches a travel ethics course, noticed ~2 years ago that students stopped arguing about David Sze's "The Myth of Authentic Travel" — a piece that used to reliably ignite debate about gap years in Cambodia, ethical itineraries, and whether it's possible to travel "right." Students who can't afford to travel still typically want to travel and debate how it should be done; this generation's silence is different.

2. Material conditions set the stage: new international student enrollment on U.S. campuses fell 17% in fall 2025, the largest non-pandemic decline on record, eliminating the most accessible cosmopolitan experience for Americans who don't leave. Tightening visa regimes, hollowed-out middle-class travel budgets, and the re-politicization of border crossing across the Middle East, North Africa, and Southeast Asia have all narrowed the physical possibility of wandering. But Crawford's argument is that economics explains the inability, not the loss of desire.

3. The real loss is ontological: travel used to serve as the mechanism by which Westerners — from Rousseau through the Romantics, the Beats, and Bourdain — imagined becoming more authentic, freer, more real versions of themselves. When students stopped arguing about whether it was possible to travel "right," they stopped believing in the category of the self that travel was supposed to produce. Sze's thesis — that "authenticity is a story the tourist tells about herself, not a property of the place she visits" — no longer provokes because the underlying aspiration has quietly collapsed.

4. LLMs cannot make great art because great art is structurally defined by surprise — and LLMs optimize for high-probability next tokens. Nabeel S. Qureshi uses the predictive processing model of the brain: when the brain's predictions are violated in a satisfying way, it registers pleasure and novelty; when predictions are confirmed, it registers dullness. Henrik Karlsson's test case: "an interesting and exciting finding" is bad prose because "exciting" is statistically implied by "interesting" — a language model asked to fill in the blank would likely produce it. Larkin's "A hothouse flashed uniquely" has never had a student correctly guess either word in a blanked-out exercise.

5. Surprise in great art operates below conscious perception, not just at the word level. Christopher Alexander's analysis of a Fra Angelico painting shows that covering the black stripe on a priest's robe and door reveals how much of the painting's haunting quality depends on that single unexpected element. Tolstoy's defamiliarization technique — describing someone being whipped as "to strip people who have broken the law, to hurl them to the floor…" without using the word "whipping" — makes a familiar act viscerally felt by treating it as an alien observer would. Both cases show that artistic power comes from controlled violation of expectation, something that high-probability generation systematically undermines.

6. The Met Gala's descent into cringe traces a clear arc: Rihanna's 2015 yellow Guo Pei cape went viral and taught celebrities that the Gala was a stunt platform, after which everyone chased their own Rihanna moment. Tom Ford and André Leon Talley were already calling it a "costume party" by 2019 — Ford's standard was "very chic people wearing very beautiful clothes going to an exhibition about the 18th century." Demna Gvasalia told the New York Times in November 2025 that he neither cares nor knows if people still get excited about it. The backlash has now completed a full cycle: Odessa A'Zion is being praised for having no stylist at all.

**Quotable:**
> "Authenticity is a story the tourist tells about herself, not a property of the place she visits." — *David Sze, "The Myth of Authentic Travel," quoted by Crawford as the thesis his students have stopped bothering to argue with*

## Technology

### 🧠 Community Wisdom: What to do when non-PMs start shipping directly to production, thoughts on Claude Code's pricing A/B test, and more 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 13 mins

1. AI "vibe coding" doesn't eliminate the resource bottleneck — it relocates it. The old constraint was engineering capacity; now it's prioritization capacity. A technical program manager at Cy Sack's org vibe-coded a prototype, demoed it to VPs who said "Let's go!", and left Sack with stakeholder buy-in, no available engineering resources, and engineers confused about how to handle a sudden flood of quasi-PM requests — a new flavor of a classic problem.

2. The canonical product development process (Goal → Problem → Opportunity → Solution → Design → Development → Deploy) hasn't changed; what's changed is who participates at each stage. Joshua Herzig-Marx's framing: "pulling quality leftward" — bringing engineers earlier into discovery — now extends to every stakeholder across the company who can build a clickable prototype.

3. Vibe-coded prototypes should be treated exactly like PRDs: as input to the PM/engineering prioritization process, not as commitments to ship. Ilya Subkhankulov: "I, as an engineer, would treat these prototypes like PRDs. You can write as many as you want, but don't assume product/eng will build/production-ize/maintain them going forward." Engineering teams will push back on inheriting AI slop and application sprawl regardless of VP excitement.

4. The biggest quality risk isn't prototypes shipping — it's collective incoherence. Even with infinite engineering capacity, unconstrained vibe-coded feature shipping produces the car Homer Simpson designed. PM discipline remains the mechanism for making products coherent, not just making them possible. If someone bypasses product to go straight to engineering managers with a prototype, the community consensus is: "there would be A Conversation" — for now.

5. A new "utility engineer" role may emerge to de-slop vibe-coded ideas into production-viable state. Community member "g" described it as a utility infielder who roves among internal "slop factories," refactoring AI-generated code to meet security, data policy, and stack compatibility requirements before it reaches alpha/beta. The prediction is this role may roll into platform engineering teams that set and enforce standards.

6. Longer-term, non-technical departments may own and maintain their own bespoke software the way finance teams maintain complex spreadsheets — while engineering refocuses on harder problems (hardware, firmware, AI infrastructure). Subkhankulov specifically envisions TPM teams maintaining project management software they built themselves.

7. Anthropic's April 2026 A/B test removing Claude Code from the Pro plan pricing page caused outsized Twitter backlash despite being a reported 2% rollout — likely because the targeting was misconfigured, making the change visible to any subscriber who checked the page. Lovable, by contrast, changes pricing "10 times a year" without customer backlash by making changes *for* customers, building in public, testing on new users first, and normalizing frequent iteration. Anthropic's additional complication: it's simultaneously serving two incompatible ICPs (prosumers and enterprises) with limited managerial bandwidth.

8. The average person can no longer detect AI-generated content, and acceptance of AI in creative work now correlates more with platform demographic than with quality. Mobile and web game players largely don't notice or care (one mobile game used AI assets for over a year without player complaints); indie and AAA game players are more sensitive and vocal. The practical implication: the relevant metric for companies isn't whether AI was used, but whether customers can detect and dislike it.

**Quotable:**
> "Anyone can vibe code a prototype, but the question of who will production-ize it and maintain it going forward remains. That's still a huge eng constraint. Eng teams will naturally push back on inheriting AI slop and application sprawl." — *Ilya Subkhankulov, on the limits of the "vibe-PM" model*

### DealBook: Who invited the bot? 📌
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 8 mins

1. AI note takers have become so common in corporate meetings that lawyers are now acting as bouncers. Jeffrey Gifford, a corporate governance and M&A attorney at Dykema in San Antonio, routinely kicks AI bots out before calls begin, naming the participants aloud: "Hey, Mike, Jim, Barbara, I see the A.I. note taker popped up. I'm going to turn it off." The problem is that these tools — now a full product category, including wallet-sized AI recorders for live interactions — capture offhand remarks, quickly corrected mistakes, and jokes that human-drafted meeting minutes would never include, making every word potentially discoverable in litigation or a government investigation.

2. The New York City Bar Association issued a formal opinion in 2025 urging lawyers to evaluate whether recording and transcribing meetings is "tactically well advised" and to warn clients of the downsides. Two concrete risks dominate: accuracy failures (a transcript recording "does matter" as "doesn't matter" could be nearly impossible to refute years later in court) and indiscriminate completeness (a board member's offhand acknowledgment of a risk, or an executive boasting an acquisition would help the company "dominate" a category, becomes a gift to plaintiffs or antitrust regulators). Christoffer Lee of Pillsbury, who focuses on corporate investigations and white-collar defense, says sophisticated litigants will increasingly request these transcripts specifically.

3. The most legally dangerous risk is the potential voiding of attorney-client privilege. Sharing information with an AI note-taking service — whose vendor may have access to transcripts and data — could be treated the same as disclosing to a third party, which strips privilege. Two February 2026 federal rulings reached opposite conclusions: Judge Jed S. Rakoff (SDNY) ruled that Claude AI transcripts were not privileged because the app's privacy policy explicitly disclosed potential sharing with third parties including government regulators; Judge Gershwin Drain (Detroit) ruled that a self-represented plaintiff's ChatGPT transcripts were protected and that using the tool didn't constitute third-party disclosure. Lee calls AI note-taking "the next frontier" for privilege case law.

4. Public companies have largely retreated from AI meeting transcription after legal warnings spread — but the retreat is quiet. Doug Raymond of Faegre Drinker Biddle & Reath says clients initially embraced the tools as a way to skip writing corporate minutes, then reversed course: "If they've gotten the word, they're not doing it — or if they're doing it, they're not fessing up." In a separate interview, journalist Joanna Stern — who spent a year AI-maxxing for her book *I Am Not a Robot* — found that AI tools amplify existing human expertise rather than replace it: without underlying musical knowledge, her Suno prompts for an '80s-style running song produced "truly just slop," and she warns that humanoid robots are still years away from home use because they lack sufficient training data on everyday tasks like folding laundry.

**Quotable:**
> "Before the meeting even starts, when I see that A.I. note taker pop up, I'll just say: 'Hey, Mike, Jim, Barbara, I see the A.I. note taker popped up. I'm going to turn it off and kick it out of the meeting.'" — *Jeffrey Gifford, corporate lawyer at Dykema, on his standard pre-call routine*

## Finance & Investing

### What Do You Have To Believe? 📌
**Kyle Harrison from Investing 101** · email · 9 mins

1. Every investment pass comes down to one question: "how many things have to go right, and can I get comfortable with that?" The right frame isn't binary (easy vs. hard) — every startup is hard. It's a weighted average across all the risk dimensions, a "gradually deteriorating feeling of comfort" as the dial on each variable rises.

2. Marc Andreessen's "onion theory of risk" is the most useful checklist for this: ten discrete risk layers — Founder, Market, Competition, Timing, Financing, Marketing, Distribution, Technology, Product, and Hiring — each a dial, not a checkbox. Founder risk is non-negotiable: get that one wrong and there's no path forward regardless of the other dials.

3. Comfort with risk is a skill built through repetition, not an innate trait. Across thousands of deals, market risk has become far less frightening because nearly every market evaluated has proven larger than expected. Hiring risk is now inseparable from storytelling risk — the ability to recruit top talent is a direct function of how compellingly a founder can narrate the company's future.

4. Sequoia (originally from Larry Summers) runs two complementary thought experiments at investment time: the **pre-parade** (if everything goes right, what does this look like in 5–10 years?) and the **pre-mortem** (what specifically went wrong if it fails?). Roelof Botha frames the pre-parade as a discipline of asking "if I'm right about everything, am I right *enough*?" — a $300M best-case outcome mathematically breaks early-stage venture math before you've even assessed probability.

5. The best founders are "paranoid in private and confident in public" — they've already war-gamed every death scenario before you meet them. David Sacks at PayPal literally asked himself: "If I were running eBay Payments, what would I do to kill PayPal?" Andy Grove's answer from *Only The Paranoid Survive* is the right operating posture: don't try to predict the specific failure mode, build the kind of organization capable of responding to any unanticipated crisis — "the way a fire department plans."

**Quotable:**
> "The pre-parade is the discipline of asking, 'if I'm right about everything, am I right enough?'" — *on why best-case scenario math must be run before probability, not after*

### Is the UK's political duopoly over? 📌
**FT Opinion** · email · 3 mins

1. Reform and the Greens made major gains in the May 2026 UK local elections, prompting a direct debate between two FT columnists. Camilla Cavendish takes the maximalist view: "the old party duopoly has been comprehensively broken. The new insurgent parties are here to stay." She frames this as a structural shift, not a protest cycle.

2. Robert Shrimsley pushes back, arguing the more accurate framing is "the end of the duopoly we are used to" — not the end of duopoly itself. First-past-the-post inherently rewards two broad coalitions, so even if proportional representation advocates win and the voting system changes, new big-tent parties would likely consolidate the field and "the new politics may look rather similar to the old one."

3. Labour's local election drubbing has triggered factional infighting within the party, and internal plotting over Keir Starmer's leadership could spook bond markets — Katie Martin separately offers governments a lesson: combativeness toward investors is not a viable strategy when borrowing costs are in play.

**Quotable:**
> "The new politics may look rather similar to the old one." — *Robert Shrimsley, arguing that structural electoral forces reassert duopoly regardless of which parties fill the two poles*

## Also Notable

### 'They don't get paid unless they win' 📎
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 5 mins

1. Bowling has spent decades branded as a symbol of American decline — a Robert Putnam-style casualty of eroding social capital and vanishing communal life. That narrative has persisted even as tens of millions of Americans still bowl regularly, suggesting the death-of-bowling story says more about elite cultural assumptions than about actual participation.

2. HBO's documentary *Born to Bowl* reframes the sport by centering on its economics: professional bowlers operate on pure contingency — no win, no paycheck. That structure creates a pressure-cooker environment ("they live and die on every single roll of the ball") that more resembles prizefighting than leisure, and that tension is precisely what sustains a passionate subculture even as the broader economics of bowling centers grow more precarious.

**Quotable:**
> "These bowlers live and die on every single roll of the ball. They don't get paid unless they win." — *HBO's Born to Bowl, on professional bowlers*
