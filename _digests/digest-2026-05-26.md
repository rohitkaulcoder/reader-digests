## Today's Top 3

### India's $500 Billion Question Mark ⚡
**The Core** · email · 7 mins

1. India is hemorrhaging capital at precisely the moment it is pledging to send $500 billion abroad. Over the past 18 months, foreign portfolio investors pulled ~$50 billion out of Indian equities. Net FDI, which historically averaged 1.5% of GDP, has completely dried up since 2024 — heavily correlated with US 10-Year Treasury yields, meaning India's investment inflows are driven by global "push" factors rather than domestic "pull." Vietnam, by contrast, consistently attracts FDI above 4% of its GDP regardless of global financial conditions, because it has built structural magnetism India lacks.

2. The $500 billion purchase commitment — announced in the February 6, 2026 India-US Joint Statement — was originally the price India would pay for the US reducing reciprocal tariffs on Indian exports from 25% to 18% under a Bilateral Trade Agreement framework. On February 20, the US Supreme Court struck down the legal basis for those reciprocal tariffs entirely, nullifying the BTA's economic rationale. The Trump administration subsequently applied a flat 10% tariff on all countries under Section 122 — the same rate India now receives with or without offering any concessions whatsoever.

3. India is continuing to honor a commitment whose contractual counterpart no longer exists. The math is unambiguous: India faces an identical 10% tariff whether it purchases $500 billion in US energy, technology, and agricultural goods or purchases nothing. Malaysia recognized the same logic — on March 15 it formally declared its own US trade agreement "null and void" after initially accepting a 19% negotiated tariff rate, then receiving the same 10% as everyone else. India, by contrast, reaffirmed the commitment during Secretary of State Marco Rubio's visit and offered no public renegotiation.

4. The strategic signal India is broadcasting is the wrong one entirely. Buying $500 billion in American goods — including possible Boeing aircraft fleets — at a moment when a pending EU free trade agreement could make Airbus cheaper, when the rupee is approaching 100 to the dollar, and when the country desperately needs inbound technology and capital, is structurally self-defeating. India needs to generate the domestic "pull" that drove FDI during the 2005–2010 corporate capex cycle. Instead it is positioning itself as America's half-trillion-dollar customer while its own currency weakens and its capital account empties.

**Quotable:**
> "If India receives the exact same 10% tariff treatment regardless of whether it offers sweeping concessions on market access, digital trade, and agriculture, what exactly is it buying for $500 billion?" — *on the collapse of the BTA's rationale after the US Supreme Court ruling*

### The World: Are we pandemic ready? ⚡
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. Current outbreaks — hantavirus on a cruise ship and an Ebola epidemic in the Democratic Republic of Congo with hundreds of suspected cases and dozens of deaths — are alarming but are not on track to become pandemics. Hantavirus spreads poorly between people and tends to fizzle out; the WHO expects the DRC Ebola outbreak to stay mostly confined to Congo and its immediate neighbors. The anxiety is real, but the threat level is not Covid.

2. A new report released at the WHO's annual World Health Assembly meeting in Geneva concluded that outbreaks are happening more frequently and causing more damage, and that the world is increasingly struggling to respond and recover. This was the backdrop for urgent, painful discussions among health officials from every country — except one.

3. Vaccine equity has gotten worse since Covid, not better. Mpox vaccines reached low-income countries nearly two years after that outbreak began in 2022 — a slower gap than even the Covid vaccine rollout. Pandemic treaty negotiations that started in 2022 remain deadlocked: low-income countries are willing to rapidly share pathogen samples and genetic sequences, but only if richer countries guarantee them equitable access to the tests, vaccines, and treatments developed from that data. Richer countries have refused.

4. The Trump administration's withdrawal from global health infrastructure has created a concrete, measurable gap in outbreak response. USAID was shut down, most foreign aid ended, the U.S. withdrew from the WHO, and it rejected the global framework that obligates countries to report outbreaks. American officials were absent from the hantavirus cruise ship investigation and didn't learn of the new Ebola outbreak until nine days after the WHO had already alerted all other global health authorities.

5. The U.S. absence translates into weaker disease surveillance, delays in testing, and a shortage of protective gear for frontline health workers. Before the Trump administration's decisions, the U.S. was the undisputed coordinator of outbreak response — funding, expertise, and political pressure to move faster. That leadership role is now vacant, and the World Health Assembly in Geneva closed with no American delegation in the room.

**Quotable:**
> "Viruses don't respect borders, and as the world responds to Ebola and hantavirus, cross-country cooperation remains crucial to global health." — *Apoorva Mandavilli, closing the piece after describing the U.S.'s complete absence from global health diplomacy*

### India's bizarre $500bn commitment ⚡
**Veena Venugopal** · email · 6 mins

1. India is now being held to a $500bn purchase "commitment" it never actually made. When the two countries announced an interim trade deal in February 2026, the White House readout said India "*intends*" to buy more American energy, ICT, coal, aircraft, and other products — softer language that India's commerce ministry buried as the penultimate bullet in its own press release. US Secretary of State Marco Rubio escalated that "intends" into "has committed" in New Delhi on May 25, 2026, and India is not pushing back.

2. The deal's original rationale has evaporated, making the commitment even harder to justify. In February, India accepted the $500bn figure in exchange for the US lowering additional tariffs from 25% to 18%. Then the US Supreme Court ruled Trump's tariff legal basis invalid, and the administration replaced it with a blanket 10% tariff on every trading partner — no deal required. India is now locked into a $500bn supplier preference while receiving no preferential treatment over any other country.

3. The commitment compounds India's most pressing economic vulnerability. India is already running negative net FDI — more capital leaving the country than entering — which is weakening the rupee. Routing $500bn in procurement exclusively through US suppliers forecloses competitive bidding (relevant for big-ticket items like aircraft and aviation engines worth ~$100bn alone) and adds $500bn of outward pressure on a currency already under strain. Commerce minister Piyush Goyal's defence — that the number is "too conservative" given India's growth — avoids the question of why supplier options should be surrendered at all.

**Quotable:**
> "It would be foolish for India to sign an agreement that appears to take far more than it gives." — *on the Modi government's pressure to deliver a trade deal with Washington despite the terms having shifted against India*

### 🎙️ How I AI: How the engineer behind Claude Cowork actually uses Claude Cowork & What launched at Google I/O 2026 📌
**Lenny's Newsletter** · email · 6 mins

**Segment 1: Felix Rieseberg on using Claude (engineering lead, Claude Cowork & Claude Code Desktop at Anthropic)**

1. Your email inbox is already a structured personal database — you just haven't queried it yet. Felix Rieseberg used years of purchase receipts and order confirmations in his Gmail to inventory every piece of furniture he owned while moving houses. Claude parsed the emails, extracted dimensions, and generated a 3D interactive floor plan from a 2D house scan — no manual data entry required. The same technique works for clothing, medical records, and travel history.

2. The "go one abstraction layer up" rule is the core productivity unlock — applied recursively. Felix's floor plan started with him manually typing furniture dimensions; he then asked Claude to figure out what furniture he had; then asked Claude to find it in his email. Each layer eliminates a class of tedious human work. The pattern: whenever you catch yourself doing something repetitive, ask Claude to do it — then ask how Claude could figure out what to do without being told.

3. Live artifacts auto-refresh from connected services (Spotify, Gmail, Calendar, Notion), eliminating the need to ever manually update dashboards, briefings, or pitch decks. Model selection heuristic: use Sonnet for well-scoped, specific tasks; switch to Opus when you're not sure what you're actually asking for and need the model to interpret intent, not just execute instructions. When Claude makes mistakes, treat it as a workflow debugging problem — ask "where did things go differently?" rather than "Claude can't do this."

**Segment 2: Google I/O 2026 recap (tested live by Claire)**

4. Gemini 3.5 Flash runs 4x faster than competing frontier coding models in Google's benchmarks and is positioned as their agentic coding model. Antigravity 2.0 (Google's IDE) now matches Claude Code and Codex on projects, scheduled Cron tasks, and subagents — features Anthropic and OpenAI shipped months prior. Google's speed advantage with Flash could make Antigravity competitive for well-scoped, fast-turnaround coding tasks even if it's playing catch-up on features.

5. Roughly half of Google I/O 2026's announced features didn't work during live testing. Workspace integration in AI Studio (build no-code apps reading Sheets, drafting Gmails) was inaccessible. Avatar creation in Flow failed. The pattern — ambitious keynote announcements, missing integrations, "coming soon" disclaimers — is eroding builder trust in Google's roadmap. Gemini's strongest real differentiator remains multimodal work: document-to-video, image animation, and podcast-to-blog-post generation, where the 3.5 family is genuinely best-in-class.

**Quotable:**
> "Adults have spent 20 years in a 'mind prison' learning what computers can't do. Unlearning that is the unlock." — *Felix Rieseberg on why kids outperform adults as AI users*

### The Briefing: Salesforce, Snowflake Earnings Coming 📌
**Martin Peers** · email · 5 mins

1. **AI revenue at legacy SaaS firms is replacing existing revenue, not adding to it.** Workday reported 13.5% year-on-year revenue growth last quarter — one percentage point below the prior quarter — despite claiming $500 million in annualized revenue from AI agents. CEO Aneel Bhusri declared "With AI, we are essentially a startup again," yet growth actually decelerated and the company guided to a slower subscription revenue growth rate for Q2. The pattern holds across the sector: Zoom also posted tepid, slightly-accelerating growth. Neither result suggests AI is lifting the top line; instead, AI product dollars appear to be cannibalizing revenue from older SKUs.

2. **Enterprise buyers are demanding shorter contracts and AI performance opt-outs, structurally weakening SaaS vendor lock-in.** Corporate software buyers are moving from five-year deals to one-to-three-year contracts and negotiating exit clauses triggered if a vendor's AI capabilities fall short of specific benchmarks. This shift, documented in The Information's prior reporting, reflects customers hedging against picking the wrong AI platform — and it raises renewal risk across the board just as incumbents like Salesforce (reporting Wednesday, consensus: $11.05B revenue, +12.5%) and Snowflake (consensus: $1.32B, +27%) need multi-year commitments to justify their valuations.

3. **The AI-disruption-of-enterprise-software debate will stretch years, not quarters.** No SaaS apocalypse is imminent — Workday and Zoom earnings showed no sudden collapse — but the structural pressures are real and compounding slowly. Which vendors survive the transition depends on whether their AI products are genuinely differentiated or just rebranded features; this week's Salesforce, Snowflake, and Asana prints (Asana consensus: $203M, +8.4%) will add one more data point to a story that won't resolve for several years.

**Quotable:**
> "With AI, we are essentially a startup again." — *Workday CEO Aneel Bhusri on earnings, even as the company's revenue growth rate fell to 13.5% and guidance came in below expectations*

### Why cultivating agency matters more than cultivating skills in the AI era | Max Schoening (Head of Product, Notion) 📎
**Lenny's Newsletter** · email · 3 mins

> Note: The article body is paywalled. This digest is synthesized from the episode description and topic outline visible in the preview.

1. **Agency — not technical skill — is the differentiator in the AI era.** AI has commoditized the execution layer: Notion gets designers and PMs to ship code, prototype in the terminal, and launch AI products without being traditionally "technical." The first 10% of every project is now effectively free — you can spin up a working prototype instantly — so the bottleneck has moved from "can you build it" to "do you have the drive and judgment to decide what to build and push it through."

2. **Vibe coding expanded software supply without raising quality, and that gap is the opportunity.** The amount of software has exploded (Jevons paradox applied to code generation), but quality has not kept pace — more mediocre apps do not displace well-crafted ones. For product builders, this creates a wider moat around software that genuinely solves a job-to-be-done rather than software that merely exists.

3. **Great products are defined by a "tiny core" primitive, not feature breadth.** Max identifies: iPhone multitouch, the GitHub pull request, Notion blocks, and Dropbox's menu bar icon — each a single interaction mechanic that made everything else possible. This is the lens Notion uses when evaluating what to invest in deeply versus what to ship and move on.

4. **The "SaaSpocalypse" — the thesis that AI will kill most SaaS businesses — is overstated.** The concern is that AI agents will replace point solutions, but durable products built around a genuine tiny-core primitive are not easily disintermediated; they become the substrate agents operate within, not the layer being replaced.

**Quotable:**
> "Agency — not skills — is the thing that separates people who thrive from those who fall behind." — *Max Schoening, Head of Product at Notion, on how AI shifts what makes someone valuable*

### Americans need to stop moaning on taxes 📎
**FT Opinion** · email · 3 mins

1. Wealthy Americans have normalized treating taxes as a burden to be avoided at all costs rather than as civic obligation — the price of living in a republic with rule of law, courts that protect property, public schools, roads, and a functioning police force. Rana Foroohar argues this framing is both self-serving and incoherent: the same businesses and rich individuals who pocketed the tax cuts enacted since the 1980s are the loudest voices complaining about the deficit and demanding cuts to Social Security and Medicare, while claiming there is no money for infrastructure, education, or healthcare.

2. Foroohar identifies a structural political problem: the coalition needed to overhaul the U.S. tax system is difficult to assemble precisely because the beneficiaries of the current regime — wealthy individuals and corporations — are the ones with the most political leverage. This connects to a parallel critique from Martin Wolf, who calls Britain's equivalent paralysis "economic infantilism," warning that meaningful tax and spending reforms require policy stability "over many years" — a condition he calls "quite unobtainable" in today's political environment. Mohamed El-Erian adds a market dimension: investors can no longer count on a "policy put" from policymakers whose capacity to absorb shocks is constrained by rising prices and high debt levels.

**Quotable:**
> "The price we pay to live in a republic with rule of law, including courts that protect our wealth and property, public schools that educate our citizenry, roads to drive on and a police force that makes sure we get home alive." — *Rana Foroohar, defining what taxes actually pay for*

### The Yuppie legacy 📎
**FT Edit** · email · 3 mins

1. Dylan Gottlieb's book *Yuppies* argues that 1980s Reaganomics required — and produced — an army of "young urban professionals" who became the dominant cultural and political force in cities like New York. Before the tech bro, before the hipster, the Yuppie defined what striving meant in America: it was a generation that remade urban life and redefined success on its own terms. Now their children are taking over, and the uncomfortable questions being asked about inequality, gentrification, and ambition are, in part, a reckoning with the world those parents built.

2. The newsletter's wider selection maps other pressure points in the current moment: short sellers have reaped more than $2.3bn betting against gambling companies, squeezed by the rise of US prediction markets and steep UK tax hikes; Martin Wolf argues Britain's political dysfunction is self-inflicted and that stability is a prerequisite for solving any of its problems; European industry has grown addicted to subsidies through the compounding shocks of the pandemic, Ukraine, and now Iran — with the question of whether that fends off China or merely fragments the EU single market still unresolved.

**Quotable:**
> "Before Tech Bros, before influencers and hipsters, there were Yuppies. They transformed 1980s New York and, arguably, modern America with it. But now their children are taking over and asking difficult questions about what their parents wrought." — *FT Edit editorial intro*
