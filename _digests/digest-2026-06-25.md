### Anthropic accuses Alibaba of 'illicitly' accessing Claude ⚡
**FirstFT Asia** · email · 6 mins

1. Anthropic told the U.S. Senate Banking Committee in a June 10 letter that Alibaba ran "the largest campaign to illicitly extract Claude's capabilities," using 25,000 fraudulent accounts to generate over 28.8 million exchanges with Claude. Anthropic does not offer Claude to Chinese companies, so Alibaba created fake accounts to circumvent that restriction — a direct violation of Anthropic's terms of service.

2. The targeted capabilities were specifically Claude's highest-value features: agentic reasoning, software engineering, and long-horizon tasks. This practice — known as "distillation" — involves using a more capable model's outputs to train a rival model, effectively transferring advanced AI capabilities without developing them independently.

3. Anthropic used the letter to lobby Congress to "close loopholes allowing PRC AI labs to access advanced US chips, and penalise PRC labs responsible for distillation attacks." This frames the issue not just as a terms-of-service violation but as a national security and export-control matter requiring legislative action.

4. Alibaba denied any connection to China's People's Liberation Army (it is simultaneously petitioning a U.S. court to be removed from the Pentagon's blacklist of PLA-linked companies) and did not respond to Anthropic's accusations. This is a pattern: Anthropic has previously accused DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax of the same distillation behavior, and OpenAI has lodged similar complaints against Chinese groups.

**Quotable:**
> "Alibaba's campaign targeted some of Claude's most valuable capabilities, such as agentic reasoning, software engineering, and long-horizon tasks." — *Anthropic's June 10 letter to the Senate Banking Committee*

### How Meta Is Reinventing Product Management ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 13 mins

1. Jagjit Chawla, VP of Product over Facebook Feed, Reels, and Search, replaced the executive information pipeline with a nightly AI agent. The agent reads every diff checked in, every pending email, chat, and doc overnight, then delivers a red/yellow/green punch list by 7 AM — decisions made, decisions waiting on him, decisions to escalate. Where it's deployed, the agent also writes the product review doc, flags the three questions worth debating, names the five people who should be in the room, and records what was decided; the formal slide-deck review is simply gone.

2. The PRD has collapsed to one paragraph, a prototype, and — for ML features — an eval set. Engineers no longer wait for a detailed spec to surface holes; the PM sits next to the engineer and judges each output in real time ("you agree or disagree whether this is a good outcome or a bad outcome"). A data question that previously required a 24-hour handoff to data science — e.g., what share of Reels views come from followers vs. non-followers — now gets answered in five minutes by an analytics agent grounded in queries Chawla's data-science partner already wrote and validated.

3. None of this was an enterprise purchase — it was built "N of one, which is me" using generally available tools. There's a nightly tuning loop: the system compares comments Chawla actually left on documents against the ones it predicted, and adjusts. Six months in, it's accurate; at the start it wasn't. The operating rule is to never do a task the old-school way, because every failure is a teaching moment in a closed loop — browse Gmail by hand once and you'll keep missing things forever.

4. AI lowered the floor on ideation so far that Chawla now receives five 50-page decks a week, each assembled in roughly 30 minutes of chatting with tools. "Now, I am the bottleneck." The career ground is shifting for mid-career PMs: for a decade the scarce skill was process (prioritization, stakeholder management), and ideas barely mattered because the engineering bottleneck killed most of them anyway. That constraint dissolved — when agents can prototype and test almost anything, "what are your ideas?" becomes a real interview question for the first time.

5. The PMs outperforming on Chawla's team are PhDs in economics and physics, not engineers. An engineer's instinct on hearing an idea is "how are you gonna build this?"; an economist asks first what the idea does to creator, user, and advertiser incentives across Meta's three-sided marketplace. Two forces drive the flip: domain expertise is diluted because AI builds context for anyone in days, and technical expertise is discounted because tools now handle architecture decisions. AI fluency is the floor, not the bar — the bar is whether you can name the stakeholders, map their incentives, and say whether your product moves them.

6. To unlock real adoption, Meta canceled a full week of meetings across the entire org — every review, every one-on-one — so people could spend uninterrupted time learning and building with the tools. The week surfaced a concrete gap: AI design tools were generating prototypes that violated Meta's design system, a problem invisible until the whole org was building at once. That discovery produced AI captains — one per function (PM, design, DS, engineering) — always internal hires, never outside recruits, because the role is blending institutional knowledge with new ways of working and only insiders have the first half.

7. Products got measurably better, not just processes. Facebook's "shake to file a bug" feature generates tens of thousands of reports daily at 2 billion users — far more than any team could read. Now three agents run in series: one triages, one validates by watching attached screen recordings, one drafts the fix and routes it to the right engineer. Chawla calls the result "ten, maybe a hundred times better." The flip side: AI-written code increased site incidents (a 0.1% crash rate means tens of millions of people at Meta's scale), so Meta deployed safeguard agents to validate changes before production — AI created the problem, AI solved it, and the next set of problems is already forming.

**Quotable:**
> "AI tools lower the floor of what you can try, but also raise the ceiling of what you can achieve." — *Jagjit Chawla, VP of Product at Meta, on why judgment — not tools — is now the scarce resource*

### My Vibe Coding Adventure, The App and the Experience, Ten Takeaways 📌
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 11 mins

1. Thompson, who hasn't programmed in 25 years, built a home inventory app using OpenAI's Codex in a matter of days without writing a single line of code. The app lets a personal assistant fully organize his house while Thompson can still find things: rooms → storage structures → locations, each with a QR code, with AI photo-identification for cataloging objects and natural language search to retrieve them.

2. Vibe coding's best fit is software that no developer would ever build commercially — a niche-of-one app tailored to having a human assistant who does the physical organizing. Existing home inventory apps are either too simple, too complicated, or use AI badly (photo-room-identification gets "half of them wrong if lucky"); building exactly what you need and nothing more is the core value proposition.

3. AI coding agents (Codex, Claude Code) are architecturally local-first: all project files, instruction docs, and the database live on one always-on machine (Thompson uses a Mac mini in a rack), while the agent's heavy compute runs on OpenAI/Anthropic servers. Remote work from a phone or laptop is thin-client streaming — essentially no computation on the remote device — which makes a desktop or home server the optimal hardware, reversing a decade of laptop-centric assumptions.

4. Long agentic tasks (20–30 minute execution plans during scaffolding) create genuine "productive downtime" — Thompson cleaned his kitchen, watched baseball, and drove his car while the agent worked. The biggest frustration was Codex starting to code before he'd finished giving feedback, or skipping the plan-approval step.

5. Domain knowledge substitutes for coding ability, but doesn't substitute for software intuition. Thompson credits his speed and lack of dead-ends to understanding data modeling, UI tradeoffs, and how to break a problem into sequential pieces — not to any programming skill. The AI will give you *something* whenever asked; knowing when what it gave you is architecturally wrong is the human's irreplaceable job. His conclusion: you still need a developer, just not a coder.

6. AI is a bad UI designer and a bad UX thinker. Multiple times Codex chose technically correct approaches that were terrible to actually use; Thompson had to override them by reasoning through real use cases. The designer and architect archetypes gain value in a vibe-coding world precisely because AI optimizes for technical correctness, not human usability.

7. The enterprise productivity argument for AI always seemed clean; the consumer entertainment argument seemed weak. Building this app felt genuinely entertaining and immediately generated more ideas. The flip side: it's easy to *feel* productive while vibe coding without *being* productive, which is the exact measurement problem enterprises face when evaluating AI ROI.

8. The experience viscerally confirmed Thompson's intellectual view that hand-coding software will soon be essentially obsolete — "it seems absolutely insane and nigh on unthinkable to me that anyone would even want to code by hand ever again." Six months ago this level of viability didn't exist; feeling "late" after only six months captures the pace of change.

9. Owning your own hardware (Mac mini, Intel NUC running Docker, local NAS, Tailscale for remote access) solves vibe coding's biggest liability — security — by keeping everything off the public internet. This resurrects the tinkerer/hacker advantage: after a decade of tech becoming appliance-like, the people who understand networking and servers again capture the most value from the newest tools.

10. A new generation of micro-entrepreneurs — analogous to high-schoolers building GeoCities websites for local businesses in the late 1990s — can now build custom apps for friends, family, and niche use cases. The barrier isn't coding anymore; it's having the product instinct to know what to build and the software intuition to avoid architectural debt.

**Quotable:**
> "It seems absolutely insane and nigh on unthinkable to me that anyone would even want to code by hand ever again." — *on the visceral shift from intellectually believing AI will replace coding to having actually experienced vibe coding*

## Business & Operations

### What AI-native GTM looks like at public company scale 📌
**Kyle Poyar** · email · 8 mins

1. **monday.com's AI agents produced measurable enterprise-scale results in 2026.** In a single quarter, monday's AI GTM system handled tens of thousands of leads, booked thousands of meetings, and generated millions of dollars in pipeline — at a $1B+ ARR company growing 24% year-on-year with 1,000+ GTM employees. The most striking metric: demo request response time dropped from ~24 hours to under 2 minutes. VP of AI for GTM Oran Akron built this as an internal startup called RevAI, starting in July 2025.

2. **"Amanda," the inbound qualifying agent, works by removing reps from the top of the funnel entirely.** Amanda is a voice agent set up as a dedicated CRM user; a "contact sales" form submission triggers 20+ preparation workflows — timezone resolution, accent matching, local phone number selection — within 60 seconds, then Amanda calls the lead within 2 minutes. Calls average 5 minutes, are fully disclosed as AI ("You've been connected to a very enthusiastic robot"), and are objective-based rather than scripted: qualify intent, identify pain, assess needs, verify buying authority, and book the meeting. A personalized voice memo gets pushed to the assigned rep via Slack. Amanda now covers 100% of English-speaking inbound and has expanded to Brazil, France, Germany, ANZ, and EMEA.

3. **"Jax," the in-trial activation agent, converts at 2.5x the rate of the control group.** Jax appears as an in-app avatar during free trials, acts as a live personal sales engineer, and can actually configure the product frontend for the user. It drives 3,000+ monthly calls, with 50% of users returning for a second call — unusually high engagement for a sales automation tool. monday has a PLG motion with large trial volume, and Jax functions as a scalable replacement for human trial success reps.

4. **The most important architectural decision was stripping AI out, not adding more of it.** monday rebuilt their agent system four times in under a year. The Nov–Dec 2025 pivot was the most consequential: the team adopted "smart tools and naive agents" — replacing AI reasoning with deterministic flows via n8n wherever possible, and building in-house microservices to handle scheduling (achieving 0% scheduling failures and human-readable error messages instead of "Error 153"). They shifted from model-specific rigid prompts to simpler objective-based prompting in early 2026 to avoid model lock-in. As of June 2026, they are moving back toward a single deep agent using new harness capabilities (Claude Code SDK, modular skills) that can now do what previously required engineered multi-agent workarounds.

5. **The change management was as hard as the engineering.** monday's internal rollout used gradual cohort deployments, cultivated internal AI champions, and maintained full transparency on agent performance data. Amanda was programmed to send reps funny voice summaries of booked meetings to build internal goodwill. Their three operating principles: don't get married to your stack (they use n8n, Salesforce, ElevenLabs, Twilio, SendGrid, ChiliPiper, Clay, Claude, and Cursor — several not in the original build); deploy at 60% readiness and iterate continuously; and over-communicate internally at every step.

**Quotable:**
> "Sales reps shouldn't handle leads; they should only receive qualified meetings with the context already prepared." — *Oran Akron, VP of AI for GTM, monday.com, describing the design philosophy behind Amanda*

Yet the industry's implicit bet may be that it can outrun public opinion — either by assuming persuasion won't work, or by genuinely exploring bypasses like orbital data centers. Both stances treat democracy as an obstacle rather than a constituency to win.

### Why 20% of Neoclouds Won't Survive The AI Boom 📎
**Contrary Research** · email · 5 mins

1. **Compute demand is so extreme it's pulling in completely unrelated businesses.** Goldman Sachs projects $7.6 trillion in cumulative global AI infrastructure spending by 2031. Compute brokers describe demand outpacing supply at a 50:1 ratio — so acute that H100 rental rates are rising in 2026 despite the GPU being four years old and meaningfully newer alternatives existing. Allbirds (a shoemaker), a whiskey importer, a recruitment website, and a TV production company have all pivoted to become GPU cloud providers.

2. **Hyperscalers' structural bias toward 100 MW+ campuses creates a gap that small neoclouds can fill — but only if they have an edge in accessing fragmented power.** Large providers cannot justify the overhead of scattered 2–5 MW power pockets at industrial sites; a waste-to-energy plant's 2 MW on-site output is a rounding error to Microsoft but a real deployable asset for a small operator. The most viable newcomers are ex-crypto miners (who already have power contracts, hardware expertise, and retrofittable facilities) and real estate operators with grid interconnects — not random pivots from unrelated industries.

3. **Newcomer neoclouds face a structural Catch-22 in financing that will kill ~20% of them.** Infrastructure is debt-financed via SPVs, but lenders treat GPUs as poor collateral due to depreciation risk and instead underwrite against offtake contracts. Most buyers won't sign offtake without first seeing chips in a facility, but chips can't be purchased without financing, which can't be secured without signed offtake. Only a small number of neoclouds land quality offtake in their first deal. Hyperscalers, by contrast, are spending $5.3 trillion from their own balance sheets by 2030 — no credit committee required — because they view losing the AI race as existential.

**Quotable:**
> "A signed $100 million offtake contract is worthless if the buyer only has $1 million in the bank and cannot pay." — *on why counterparty credit quality matters as much as contract size in neocloud financing*

## Finance & Markets

### Money Stuff: Insider Trading Isn't Romantic 📌
**Bloomberg** · rss · 16 mins

1. **Justin Jennings made $2.7M by secretly mining his girlfriend's work laptop.** On June 23, 2026, the SEC charged Jennings and his LLC Vortex Strategies with insider trading spanning February 2022 to October 2024. His girlfriend worked at Joele Frank, a strategic communications and investor relations firm. He didn't trade on her specific deals — he accessed the firm's internal database of MNPI (draft press releases, M&A talking points, earnings materials) for clients he had no relationship with, then bought call options in 8 public companies ahead of announcements. There is a parallel criminal case.

2. **The mechanics worked because trust eroded operational security completely.** The girlfriend routinely left her work laptop unlocked when stepping away; Jennings had her password because she trusted him. Crucially, she demonstrated how to search and filter the firm's database because he told her he was "interested in coding and database projects." The SEC complaint's most damning line: she believed he was "expressing legitimate interest in her work." The SEC refers to her as his "*then*-romantic partner."

3. **Prediction markets are best understood as gambling, not finance infrastructure — and Meta is leaning into that.** Meta is building a smartphone app codenamed "Arena," modeled on Polymarket and Kalshi, but likely using a video-game points system rather than real money. Zuckerberg frames this as capitalizing on "emerging social behavior online" rather than creating a financial instrument. The product will leverage Meta's 3.56 billion daily active users across Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and Messenger.

4. **The open question is whether points-based prediction markets can generate the same dopamine as real-money gambling.** The expected financial value of prediction markets is obviously negative (the platform takes a cut), placing them in the same category as sports betting for most users. But Zuckerberg has already built a trillion-dollar company by getting billions of people to perform behaviors for Facebook points — likes, shares, check-ins — so the precedent for non-monetary engagement rewards is strong.

5. **A Tesla/SpaceX merger would be a corporate governance novelty because each company has something the other wants.** SpaceX has a dual-class share structure giving Musk permanent voting control even as a minority shareholder; stock exchange rules prohibit Tesla from adding that structure after the fact. Tesla is in the S&P 500 (creating permanent institutional demand for its stock); SpaceX isn't and can't be added for at least a year. The unanswered structural question: can a single merger be structured so the combined entity inherits both SpaceX's governance and Tesla's index membership?

6. **Texas's 3% lawsuit threshold is the practical moat protecting Musk from shareholder challenges.** Delaware allows any shareholder to sue; Texas requires 3% ownership. Only Vanguard, BlackRock, and State Street are likely near that threshold in Tesla. Those firms generally don't sue portfolio companies. In SpaceX, the obstacle is total: the entire IPO float was barely bigger than 3%, Musk controls the company absolutely, and his retail "cheering section" would follow him anywhere — Columbia Law professor Eric Talley notes there is a limit to how much Musk can lowball Tesla shareholders "before he starts to lose the room," but that limit is very high.

7. **Index fund managers are the only meaningful counterweight in a Tesla/SpaceX deal — and only on the Tesla side.** Because Tesla is in the S&P 500 and SpaceX is not, Vanguard, BlackRock, and State Street have fiduciary obligations to Tesla shareholders but little formal stake in SpaceX's valuation. In a merger, the Big Three would want fair exchange value for Tesla stock; most of SpaceX's other investors are ideologically aligned with Musk and indifferent to allocation. BlackRock reportedly asked for $5B of SpaceX IPO stock and holds ~$80B of Tesla.

8. **SPACs are back, and AI is the new EV/crypto as the target flavor.** Agility Robotics — humanoid robots for warehouses backed by Nvidia, SoftBank, and Amazon, with $390M+ already raised — is going public via Michael Klein's Churchill Capital Corp. XI SPAC at a $2.5B valuation, with $420M cash and $200M+ PIPE led by Foxconn. Globally, 44 SPAC mergers have been announced YTD worth $36.9B, up from 33 deals worth $15B at this point last year. As of June 17, 359 SPACs hold $56.8B in undeployed capital waiting for targets.

9. **US public markets are now the only venue with enough capital depth for trillion-dollar AI companies.** SK Hynix — South Korean memory chip maker, stock up 850% in 12 months, market cap above $1 trillion — is seeking $29.4B in a US ADR listing. SpaceX's recent IPO was the largest in history, raising $86B in equity and $25B in investment-grade bonds in two weeks. Private markets could support a $1T valuation, but getting to $2T required the US public market. TSMC's US ADR trades at a persistent premium to its Taiwan-listed shares, validating the strategy for Asian issuers.

**Quotable:**
> "It is a bummer for US public investors that they only get access to the best companies after their first trillion dollars of growth." — *on SK Hynix's US ADR listing, after SpaceX's historic IPO*

### Going Private: SEC sounds alarm on credit fund disclosure bloat 📎
**Bloomberg** · email · 10 mins

1. The SEC's Investor Advisory Committee convened earlier this month following a wave of semi-liquid private credit funds imposing exit caps when redemption requests surged. Panelists concluded that years of well-intentioned disclosure rules have produced "C.Y.A." legal tomes that shield managers from liability far more effectively than they educate retail investors — and SEC Commissioner Mark Uyeda floated a full rulebook revamp.

2. Journalist Jason Zweig (*Your Money and Your Brain*) cited neuroscience research to argue that disclosure bloat actively backfires: the more risk warnings investors receive, the lower the probability they assign to any single risk materializing. A 5,000-word explanation of illiquidity risk produces the intuition that the risk is remote — precisely backwards when those risks are most likely to bite.

3. Some private credit managers are also misrepresenting volatility through Sharpe ratios above 10 in marketing materials. Zweig relayed a private conversation with Nobel laureate William "Bill" F. Sharpe himself, who objected to alternatives funds using the ratio at all: since illiquid assets don't trade, comparing their returns per unit of "risk" against traded assets is a category error.

4. FINRA VP Joseph Schreier confirmed his agency is on alert for unbalanced investor communications — but FINRA's jurisdiction covers only broker-dealer-sold products, estimated at roughly 20% of the private markets retail channel. The remaining ~80%, distributed via financial advisors and other intermediaries, falls entirely outside FINRA's supervision.

5. State Street's fifth annual Private Markets Study (480 senior executives, March–April poll) found 84% of private capital firms plan to target individual investors. Yet optimism is cooling: only 43% now expect individual vehicles to represent at least half of private markets fundraising within three years, down sharply from 56% last year. Nearly 80% cited liquidity management — redemptions processing, cash forecasting, stress-testing — as a top operational challenge.

6. Castlelake has gone hostile on its £4.74 billion ($6.3B) bid for budget airline EasyJet after the carrier rejected three separate offers, including a raised bid of 625 pence (up from 560p). Because US entities cannot hold majority control of a European aviation asset, Castlelake has partnered with Ireland-based Oneiros Aerospace and EasyJet's former COO Peter Bellew to provide the required EU footprint.

7. John Aylward, CIO of Sona Asset Management ($17B AUM, founded 2016 with $300M), prefers public credit over private in the near term. He flagged imminent maturities across highly-leveraged software-sector firms as "a source of substantial problems" for direct lenders, and questioned the risk management discipline of private credit funds that deployed capital throughout the last cycle. Supporting data: only 18% of the 2021 US PE vintage has been monetized four years post-investment, versus 31.9% of the 2017 vintage at the equivalent stage (PitchBook 2026 PE Outlook).

**Quotable:**
> "If you take 5,000 words to explain a risk, my natural inference from that explanation is 'that's not going to happen'. And of course, risks like those might well be the ones that are most likely to happen." — *Jason Zweig, author of "Your Money and Your Brain," at the SEC Investor Advisory Committee panel*

**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 8 mins

1. The US is building a 50-bed quarantine facility in Nanyuki, Kenya — a city of 73,000 in the highlands 120 miles north of Nairobi — to isolate Americans exposed to Ebola before they can board flights home. Kenya has never recorded a single Ebola case; the active outbreak is 600 miles away in the Democratic Republic of Congo, which is dealing with the rare Bundibugyo strain and has logged more than 1,000 confirmed infections and 250 deaths across its 17th epidemic.

2. Two protests in June 2026 turned lethal when thousands marched through Nanyuki and police responded with live ammunition, killing two young men. Grace Wanjiru, whose 27-year-old son was shot dead, speaks for many locals: the facility should be in Congo or Uganda (which has reported at least 20 cases), not in a country with zero outbreak history. Activists are further inflamed by the facility's location on the grounds of a military base, which they say will bar ordinary Kenyans from access.

3. The US has roughly a dozen biocontainment units domestically — infrastructure built after the 2014–15 epidemic that killed 11,000 people — yet Secretary of State Marco Rubio declared at a cabinet meeting: "We cannot and will not allow any cases of Ebola to enter the United States." Washington has pledged over $270 million for overseas containment efforts while simultaneously banning entry to foreigners who have visited Congo, Uganda, or South Sudan in the past three weeks; Americans returning from those countries face intensive screening.

4. The WHO says it is "playing catch-up" on an outbreak that spread undetected for months, partly because USAID and similar agencies cut funding for early-detection infrastructure. Manenji Mangundu, Oxfam's country director for Congo, says: "There is simply not enough funding to mount the scale of response needed." Meanwhile the Kenyan government — invoking a 2015 Obama-era bilateral agreement — is planning 24 quarantine sites and 800 trained medical workers, though two nonprofits filed a court petition on May 28 to block construction, and a Nairobi judge ordered disclosure of details that the government has not yet provided.

**Quotable:**
