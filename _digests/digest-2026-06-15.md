## AI & Technology

### Siri, are you fixed? 📌
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 10 mins

1. The new Siri's "personal context" and "on-screen awareness" features — first promised at WWDC June 2024 and delayed ever since — genuinely work. Gurman tested on iPhone, Mac, and iPad and successfully completed tasks like moving calendar events by referencing the attendee's name, searching across emails and texts for a TV show a family member recommended weeks ago, and creating a calendar appointment by reading an email open in a third-party app. Apple executives downplayed these delays in 2024 as covering "only a subset" of Siri improvements; in practice, they were the only improvements that mattered.

2. The new Siri is functionally competitive with leading AI chatbots as they stood roughly six months ago — not today. It handles everyday queries (web search, recipes, text editing, local theater showtimes) well enough that many casual users won't need ChatGPT at all. But it still can't touch the advanced workloads — in-depth research, programming, data analysis, complex report writing, analyzing large PDFs, generating charts or slide decks — that drive power users to ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. The analogy: Siri AI is iMovie, ChatGPT is Final Cut Pro.

3. Apple withheld a "Siri Extensions" framework from its WWDC announcement that would open Siri to third-party AI models beyond ChatGPT. The feature is live in iOS 27 beta 1 code, Apple has already held detailed discussions with OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google about integration requirements, and an App Store section for compatible apps is built. Gurman's four theories for the no-show: it would undermine Apple's EU regulatory arguments about third-party interoperability; it would overshadow Apple's own Siri relaunch; an ongoing legal dispute with OpenAI made dropping ChatGPT's exclusive status publicly risky; and explaining Google AI model usage alongside external chatbot options would create messaging chaos.

4. iOS 27 and macOS 27 betas contain specific technical evidence for two major hardware products: the foldable iPhone (internal code name V68, expected September launch) and a touch-screen MacBook Pro (K114/K116, expected late 2026 or early 2027). Foldable hints include iOS code references to multiple displays, a device that detects its own fold angle, and iPhone Mirroring on macOS now stretching to wider iPad-like layouts. Touch MacBook hints include pull-to-refresh UI tweaks across macOS, full touch input now supported through Sidecar on an iPad screen, and the new pill-shaped Siri interface on Mac designed to emerge from a Dynamic Island — a feature that doesn't exist on Mac yet.

5. Apple's "privacy-first" positioning for Siri AI is marketing spin covering for capability gaps, not a principled product choice. If Apple could build tools as capable as the competition, it would — and the new Siri is in fact substantially powered by Google AI models. Gurman expects Apple will eventually charge for Siri AI: baseline and on-device personal context features free, conversational and generative AI features first rate-limited (iCloud+ users get a higher limit already) and then paywalled as a separate subscription, mirroring ChatGPT's model.

**Quotable:**
> "Apple has delivered a Siri that, for the first time, actually works the way it's supposed to. There's nothing revolutionary or innovative about it but Apple deserves credit for finally entering the modern AI market." — *Gurman's first-hand assessment after testing the iOS 27 beta on iPhone, Mac, and iPad*

### The hidden pattern behind successful products | Mark Pincus (Founder of Zynga) 📎
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 4 mins

1. **Your instincts are right 95% of the time, but your ideas are wrong 75% of the time.** The failure mode is latching onto a "B+" idea — directionally correct but wrong in execution. In 2003, Pincus had the instinct that you should be able to order a taxi from your phone, but wrapped it in the wrong idea: text a broker who calls the taxi company. Uber didn't start until a year later and became a $144 billion company. The diagnosis: if you're asking whether your product is an A, it isn't — "it's like dating: when you're with the right person, you know it." Pincus recently killed one of his own projects after four years and $25 million, and felt more inspired in the following two weeks than in the prior four years combined.

2. **The "Proven, Better, New" framework explains nearly every breakout product.** Step one: become the world's leading expert on what already works and copy those elements directly — Pincus calls this "moral arbitrage," since users hate change and copying lets you skip validation of basics. Step two: make one change so that 10 out of 10 existing users say "f\*ck yeah, I'll switch to this" — not 7 out of 10, not 9. Step three: add one genuinely novel element never tried with this product and audience. Slack (email mechanics + real-time), iPhone (existing phones + touch UI), and Instagram Stories (existing sharing + ephemerality) all follow this pattern. Being less ambitious is the path to the most ambitious outcomes: Pincus started Zynga at 41 with an embarrassingly small idea — a poker game — after his prior company Tribe failed by trying to be too ambitious. Eight of Zynga's ten major game launches became massive hits reaching over a billion players total.

**Quotable:**
> "People thought I had no dignity." — *Mark Pincus on starting Zynga with a poker game at 41, after previously building social networking company Tribe*

## Finance & Markets

### How Much Did the Bankers Actually Make on the SpaceX IPO? 📌
**CJ Gustafson from Mostly Metrics** · email · 8 mins

1. SpaceX paid $500M in underwriting commissions on a $75B raise — a 0.67% gross spread. Typical IPOs raising $500M–$1B pay 4–5%; even large deals like CoreWeave and Cerebras paid more than 1%. SpaceX's scale let it compress the fee into the ground, but the sheer size still made $500M the largest IPO fee pool ever in raw dollars.

2. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley each captured $100M of that pool. The industry has shifted from a single "lead left" bank to a "joint lead" model for large IPOs, and each of the two co-leads received 20% of the share allocation — an easy calculation from the 424B4 prospectus showing per-bank share counts and the $0.90-per-share commission rate.

3. SpaceX killed the greenshoe commission entirely, a buried clause that cost the banks ~$75M. Normally, underwriters get an over-allotment option to sell 15% more shares (83,333,333 shares here) in the 30 days after pricing, earning the same spread on each. On an oversubscribed book like this one, they almost always exercise it. Instead, the banks distribute an extra $11B of stock for free — no discount, no commission — capping Goldman and Morgan Stanley at $100M rather than the ~$115M they'd normally expect.

4. The effective spread is actually 0.58%, not 0.67%, because the banks are distributing $86B of stock but only getting paid on $75B. For context, 0.58% is less than what most people pay a financial advisor to simply trail the S&P 500 for a year, and the $500M fee pool is roughly what Goldman books in a *single quarter* of equity underwriting revenue ($535M last quarter).

5. The $500M understates total banker compensation — the real long-tail revenue is future trading commissions from every fund that was allocated shares in the deal. Banks control allocation in oversubscribed IPOs, and every fund that got in owes future order flow: a mutual back-scratching arrangement that doesn't show up in the prospectus but compounds over years of relationship capital. This syndicate also just set the pricing benchmark that OpenAI and Anthropic will face when they go public later in 2026.

**Quotable:**
> "SpaceX zeroed it out. Buried in the underwriting section is a line that says the banks collect no discount and no commission on a single greenshoe share. So the syndicate will hand out an extra $11 billion of stock for free, the $500M is capped, and Goldman and Morgan Stanley stay at $100M." — *on SpaceX's greenshoe kill clause*

## Crime & Security

### The Forecast: Scams will get worse 📌
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 7 mins

1. Nigeria's "Yahoo Boys" scam economy traces directly to the 1980s oil bust, when inflation and sudden joblessness left young men seeking income. They operate independently and at smaller scale than Southeast Asia's organized-crime scam compounds, which rely on forced labor — but both feed a booming global fraud industry. Fraud is now among the most commonly reported crimes in many countries, with scammers stealing tens or even hundreds of billions of dollars annually.

2. AI is collapsing the cost and labor floor for running scam operations. According to a Stimson Center report, AI "allows criminal networks to scale up the scope, precision, and efficiency of their operations and is rapidly lowering the barriers to entry." AARP's Bob Sullivan puts the change in concrete terms: scammer call centers have gone from sending out one or two AI-generated scam messages per year to tens of thousands per minute.

3. Enforcement is escalating on both ends: Southeast Asian governments now frame scam networks as national security threats, and the US established a "Scam Center Strike Force" in 2025. But enforcement only raises the cost side of the ledger. The supply of scammers in places like Nigeria will only shrink if legitimate, well-paying jobs become available — economic conditions that created the Yahoo Boys still largely persist.

4. AI poses a second, indirect threat to scam rates beyond just making fraud cheaper: if it displaces employment opportunities for young people globally, more of them may calculate that online fraud is worth attempting. The same technology being deployed to detect scams is simultaneously expanding the pool of people with little to lose by running them.

**Quotable:**
> "A couple of years ago, you might have encountered one or two AI-generated scams a year. Now scammer call centers are sending out tens of thousands of scam messages per minute." — *Bob Sullivan, AARP's The Perfect Scam podcast*

## Science & Healthcare

### Well Informed: Have scientists unlocked a master switch for cancer? 📎
**The Economist** · email · 3 mins

1. At a cancer conference in Chicago in late May 2026, oncologists gave a spontaneous standing ovation for trial results on daraxonrasib, a new drug for pancreatic cancer — one of the deadliest and hardest-to-treat cancers. The results showed dramatic extensions in patient survival times, a genuine rarity in a disease historically resistant to most therapies.

2. Daraxonrasib's most far-reaching property may not be its direct tumour-killing effect but its apparent ability to strip away pancreatic cancer's defenses against the immune system. Pancreatic cancer had been notoriously resistant to immunotherapy — treatments that enlist the body's own immune cells to attack tumours — so cracking that resistance could compound the survival benefit substantially.

3. The real prize is the drug's target: RAS proteins, a family of signaling proteins discovered in the 1980s that, when mutated or mis-regulated, are estimated to drive roughly 20% of all cancers diagnosed annually. RAS proteins had been considered "undruggable" for four decades; daraxonrasib is the first proof that they can be targeted, potentially opening an entirely new treatment class across many cancer types beyond pancreatic.

4. The journey from RAS protein discovery to daraxonrasib took 40 years, illustrating the slow compounding nature of cancer research progress. Clinical trials still need to validate broader applications, and even confirmed benefits will take years to reach patients at scale — but the proof-of-concept that RAS is targetable is the kind of foundational unlock that can accelerate an entire research field.

**Quotable:**
> "Since their discovery in the 1980s RAS proteins were thought to be untargetable with drugs. Daraxonrasib proves that they are not, potentially opening the door for an entirely new avenue of treatment." — *Tim Cross, Senior science writer, The Economist*
