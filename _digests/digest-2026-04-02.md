## China AI

### 100 Hours Inside Kimi ⚡ {#100-hours-inside-kimi}
**Liu Mo (transl. Rui Ma)** · *Renwu* · 26 min

1. Moonshot AI is three years old, has ~300 employees averaging under 30, and has crossed RMB 120B (~$16B) in valuation. Each employee effectively carries RMB 400M of enterprise value, and the company measures itself accordingly.

2. DeepSeek's emergence over Lunar New Year 2025 was simultaneously Kimi's most demoralizing and clarifying moment. The growth team had a devastating holiday; the algorithm team was excited. DeepSeek proved a lower-cost Chinese startup with strong models could earn global respect. Kimi pivoted hard to model-focus and has not looked back.

3. Founder Yang Zhilin's hiring filter is "taste" — not credentials. At least 50 employees have prior startup experience. A 17-year-old high school intern was brought in, co-authored a research paper, and had his name placed first by his mentor Bob; Elon Musk later praised the work. The company also points to Cursor's alleged heavy reliance on Kimi's model as commercial validation.

4. The organizational structure is radically flat: no formal departments, no titles, no OKRs, no KPIs, no clock-in attendance. Yang Zhilin's personal status message is four words: "Communicate directly." Each co-founder interfaces directly with 40–50 employees. The most recent all-hands group event was office massages.

5. "Generalization ability" is the explicit hiring bar. Kimi wants people who function like base models — capable of being algorithm architect, distributed-systems debugger, and data curator simultaneously. Senior hires from big tech often fail because they are "overfit" to one KPI system, one reporting language, one political game. At least three mid-level big-tech hires have left or been outpaced since joining.

6. The company is genuinely AI-native in workflow. Leo (product) describes commanding "an army" of agents: a strategy agent filters 3,000 feedback items for high-priority patterns, a translation agent handles Japanese dialect and Korean honorifics in real time, a competitor agent monitors Cursor and ChatGPT and produces technical comparisons. By 11:30 a.m. the PRD is done; a coding agent has generated ~70% of base implementation, leaving creative design work for the afternoon.

7. The cultural texture is predominantly introverted (~80% "I people" in MBTI terms). More than ten employees said they increasingly prefer dealing with AI over humans. Florence, approached by a competitor offering double her salary, declined immediately: "There's no 'officialdom smell' here" — the stale, hierarchical, self-important atmosphere associated with bureaucracy.

8. The core organizational thesis is what the author calls the "two-dimensional foil" (from Liu Cixin's *Three-Body Problem*): Moonshot is deliberately collapsing its own hierarchy from three dimensions to two — no vertical depth, no horizontal walls, only model and intelligence facing each other. This is irreversible. The company cannot re-add hierarchy without tearing itself apart.

9. Resilience is the quality that matters most, not raw intelligence. Bob (day-one employee): "The smarter you are, the more clearly you see the risks, and the easier it becomes to walk away. Foolish persistence will not succeed either. Only those who see the truth, calculate the odds of failure, and still continue deserve to be called resilient."

10. The long-context breakthrough (MoBA) required three retreats over 2+ years. Freddie's team started in May 2023 with a goal to read 128K context in a single pass (vs. the 4K industry standard). Version 0.5 was too expensive to run mid-training. Version 1 worked on small models but failed on large ones with loss spikes. Version 2 passed the "needle in a haystack" test but then failed on long-summary tasks due to sparse training signals; the fix required changing the attention mechanism in the final layers.

11. The flat structure has a documented fragility: historical experiments with extreme flatness (holacracy, Haier's internal cells) hit decision bottlenecks around 500 people. Without hierarchy, some employees report walking in some mornings not knowing what they should be doing — the OKR system, for all its dysfunction, provides a baseline of existential certainty.

12. The strategic bet is binary: "Either they become godlike through ascent. Or they are sealed away in collapse. There is no third path." If model intelligence crosses the critical threshold before organizational strain becomes fatal, the two-dimensional structure wins. If not, there is no recovery path.

13. The company has shifted strategy multiple times: heavy ad spending → model focus; closed source → open source; chatbot → Kimi Agent/Code/Claw; consumer → enterprise → consumer. Employees attribute every pivot to one cause: "make the company align better with objective reality." The co-founders are easy to persuade as long as the facts are clear enough.

**Quotable:**
> "In a way, DeepSeek saved us." — *Algorithm engineer Alex, on how DeepSeek's breakout in January 2025 clarified Kimi's strategic priorities rather than threatening them*

## Capital Markets

### Money Stuff: OpenAI Is Almost Public ⚡ {#money-stuff-openai-is-almost-public}
**Matt Levine** · *Bloomberg* · 16 min

1. OpenAI raised $122B at an $852B valuation — more money in a single private round than the proposed $75B SpaceX IPO (which itself would have been the largest IPO ever, dwarfing Saudi Aramco's $29B record). CFO Sarah Friar: the deal "blows out of the water even the largest IPO that's ever been done."

2. The anchor investors: SoftBank $30B, Nvidia $30B, Amazon up to $50B (first $15B now; $35B conditional on IPO or achieving AGI). Additionally, $3B+ raised from individual investors via bank private-wealth channels and inclusion in Cathie Wood's ARK Invest ETFs — the first time OpenAI has offered retail-adjacent access.

3. Private markets are the new public markets. For an AI company seeking capital to build data centers, the private market can raise more, distribute more broadly, and with less friction than a public offering. The hypothetical of "everyone will finally be able to buy OpenAI stock when it IPOs" is increasingly moot when everyone already can, at whatever markup they're willing to pay.

4. The retail-access paradox: private assets historically outperform because they are exclusive. Once you democratize access — ARK ETFs, bank private-wealth channels, secondary markets — that exclusivity premium erodes. Going to parties to brag about being an "early investor in OpenAI" loses its cachet when everyone at the party got the same offer.

5. OpenAI secondary shares have become nearly impossible to unload. Ken Smythe (Next Round Capital): "We literally couldn't find anyone in our pool of hundreds of institutional investors to take these shares." About $600M of OpenAI stock hit the market from half a dozen institutional holders (hedge funds, VCs) — last year it would have sold in days.

6. Meanwhile, $2B of institutional capital is queued to buy Anthropic stock. Anthropic's $380B valuation vs. OpenAI's $852B has investors reasoning: "better risk-reward right now" and betting Anthropic's valuation will catch up. The ratio of buyer/seller pressure has completely inverted between the two companies.

7. The traditional private-company advantage — stock only moves on fundraising rounds, which you do only on good news — erodes at $852B. At that scale, the stock already effectively trades publicly on secondary markets, and Levine documents that it fell even as the company raised at a higher headline valuation.

8. SpaceX has filed confidentially for its IPO, targeting a June listing that would raise ~$75B. This means 2026 could see a trio of mega-IPOs: SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic — but OpenAI's $122B private round already eclipses what any of them will raise publicly.

**Quotable:**
> "We literally couldn't find anyone in our pool of hundreds of institutional investors to take these shares." — *Ken Smythe, Next Round Capital, on the $600M of OpenAI stock that hit the secondary market and found zero buyers*

## AI & Security

### Axios Supply Chain Attack, Claude Code Code Leaked, AI and Security ⚡ {#axios-supply-chain-attack-claude-code-code-leaked-ai-and-security}
**Ben Thompson** · *Stratechery* · 8 min

1. On March 30, 2026, an attacker hijacked the npm account of Axios lead maintainer @jasonsaayman via social engineering (targeting a long-lived classic token that hadn't been revoked), published two poisoned versions across both the 1.x and 0.x branches within 39 minutes, and injected a phantom dependency ("plain-crypto-js@4.2.1") that dropped a cross-platform RAT. The malware self-destructed after execution. Axios has over 100 million weekly npm downloads.

2. This is the third major npm supply chain attack in a year: the Shai-Hulud worm (September 2025, 500+ packages compromised, CISA advisory issued); the PackageGate zero-days (January 2026, six zero-days across npm/pnpm/vlt/Bun); and now Axios. GitHub overhauled npm authentication after Shai-Hulud, deprecated classic tokens, mandated FIDO 2FA, and capped granular tokens at 7 days — but the maintainer credential remained the single point of failure.

3. Anthropic accidentally leaked 59.8MB of Claude Code source code (a JavaScript .map file) in npm package version 2.1.88. Over 500,000 lines and 1,000+ files were exposed. Anthropic confirmed "human error, not a security breach." The most valuable recipients are companies like Cursor and Microsoft building their own Claude harnesses — not OpenAI, which is building competing models.

4. The real moat is harness-model integration, not the harness alone. Thompson: "the most important differentiator is not just the harness but rather the integration of harness and model." The fact that Claude Code generates $2.5B ARR while OpenAI Codex's source code has been public for a year supports this.

5. AI is both a near-term threat multiplier and a long-term security solution. Near-term: vibe-coding will produce insecure code; AI will help bad actors find vulnerabilities faster. Long-term: AI can examine every line of code ever released (not just new code), trace full dependency trees no human would follow, navigate inconvenient but secure workflows, and stress-test authentication flows repeatedly — tasks that are impossible for humans at scale.

**Quotable:**
> "AI is going to be bad for security in the short-term, but much better than humans in the long-term." — *Thompson's framing for why both incidents today are feature, not bug, of the current AI transition*

## AI Strategy & Investing

### Anish Aya @ Andreessen 📌
**Anish Aya (a16z)** · *20VC Podcast Transcript* · 1 hr 10 min

1. The "SaaS apocalypse" is the wrong frame. SaaS and IT spend represent only 8–12% of total enterprise budgets. Even if companies vibe-coded their entire ERP, payroll, and CRM stack, they'd save 8–12%. The innovation bazooka should be pointed at the other 90% — extending core competitive advantage or optimizing workflows that never had software before.

2. Seventy-five percent of public SaaS companies have raised prices since ChatGPT launched, with the mean increase at 8–12% and a large cohort raising 25%+. Companies with genuine competitive pressure cut prices; rising prices signal that incumbents are winning with AI, not being disrupted by it.

3. Coding agents lower SaaS switching costs in a way that converts "hostages" to genuine customers. Moving from SAP to Oracle used to be a multi-year, high-risk project that got CIOs fired. That complexity collapses with coding agents. The consequence: incumbents get competitive pressure but the overall ecosystem benefits from more genuine customer relationships.

4. Incumbents improve existing categories; startups win native new categories. History consistently shows this. Microsoft Word will become the best word processor ever made. But "AI-generated film" or "background coding agent" are categories that had no incumbents, and native companies will own them.

5. Foundation models are ~80% substitutable, with meaningful specialization in the remaining 20%. This creates an aggregation-layer opportunity for application companies. Cursor's value is letting a developer use Gemini for frontend and Codex for backend in one IDE, without switching CLIs. Multi-model access is a moat a single-lab provider can never match.

6. Cursor faces a near-term existential test. Anish estimates it could lose 50% of its revenue this year due to Claude Code cannibalization. "When I hear that someone's still on Cursor, I'm like, wow." The category will grow, but product-specific revenue is not safe.

7. Models will copy primitives but cannot own full feature surfaces. OpenAI built meeting transcription into ChatGPT — but if Granola's real ambition is a full productivity suite (docs, spreadsheets, etc.), OpenAI lacks the prioritization, domain focus, and opinionated UX to compete across that surface. Feature primitives can be replicated; category depth cannot.

8. "Weird wins" over "boring wins." AI models can express human qualities — persuasion, disagreement, intimacy — that big corporations explicitly design out of their products through committees. Startups can build for companionship, contextual play, and spiritual nourishment in ways Google and Apple will never approve.

9. Contextual companions are the most defensible form of AI companionship precisely because of indirection. An elderly person won't accept an AI "companion call," but will accept an AI that calls to check on their medication, asks about their day, and lightly flirts. The functional outcome (social connection) is delivered via a useful pretext.

10. Voice is the future for enterprise UI; browse-based stays for consumer; chat is overstated for mass market. Most people don't want to save time — they want to spend it. Chat boxes are optimized for the highest-agency users (Altman, Musk). The median consumer wants a browse interface where they discover what they want.

11. Live proprietary data is emerging as a real moat. A commodity model with access to live health data outperforms a frontier model without it. Systems of record with deep human workflows built around them (core banking, high-transaction-volume databases) remain highly defensible; lightweight SaaS with no engagement layer is vulnerable.

12. AI power users are paying 10x the pre-AI consumer price ceiling. Grok Heavy: $300/month. ChatGPT: $200/month. Gemini Ultra: $250/month. Versus Spotify's premium ceiling of ~$25/month. Free trials are "zero-margin CAC with healthy calories" — they convert into high-paying users, unlike 2021 when startup subsidies went to Google and Facebook as empty ad spend.

**Quotable:**
> "You have this innovation bazooka with these models. Why would you point it at rebuilding payroll or ERP or CRM?" — *Anish Aya, on why the SaaS disruption thesis misidentifies where AI's leverage is highest*

## Emerging Markets

### From Copycat To Creator 📌
**Dev Chandrasekhar** · *The Core* · 8 min

1. On March 20, 2026, Novo Nordisk's Indian patent on semaglutide expired. Within 48 hours, 16+ generic versions were on Indian pharmacy shelves. Prices crashed 70–90%. Zydus launched three brands and anchored supply for Lupin and Torrent. Dr. Reddy's launched "Obeda" with plans for 87 countries. Torrent was the first Indian company to offer a generic tablet alongside an injectable.

2. India's generics machine is unmatched in scale: 40% of US generic medicines, two-thirds of global vaccines, Rs 4.72 lakh crore total industry turnover in FY25. But a generics company earns nothing from the originator's premium — the royalties, licensing income, and durable pricing power flow entirely to the drug inventor.

3. Drug discovery odds are brutal: 10–15 years, $1–2B per drug, 9-in-10 failure rate. Kallam Anji Reddy built India's first discovery program in 1993 alongside his generics business. The generics side succeeded spectacularly (Rs 366 crore from 180-day exclusivity on generic fluoxetine 40mg in FY2002 alone). The novel-drug side produced nothing commercially.

4. The current generation — led by Zydus, which is furthest along on an obesity candidate — is the most serious Indian drug discovery effort since Dr. Reddy's was licensing molecules to Novo Nordisk in the 1990s. The logic that drove 30 years of copying ("faster, cheaper, almost guaranteed to succeed") is now eroding as patent cliffs fill with 16 identical generics in 48 hours and margins collapse within weeks.

**Quotable:**
> "India had, in two days, demonstrated something it does better than any country on earth: copy any drug." — *On the semaglutide rush, framing the world-class capability that also defines India's strategic ceiling*

### Going Private: Buyout firms push to 'divide & conquer' creditors 📎
**Sinead Cruise** · *Bloomberg Going Private* · 10 min

1. American Securities (owner of Vibrantz Technologies, a paint-additives maker) required all lenders to sign NDAs before sharing its restructuring plan, then struck side deals with larger creditors — GoldenTree Asset Management, Monarch Alternative Capital, and Oak Hill Advisors — awarding them "first-out" status via a fresh $350M loan. Smaller lenders were left with "very limited visibility" on what peers were receiving.

A Harvard Law paper by Mark Roe and Vasile Rotaru: "a majority of coercively restructured firms end up filing for bankruptcy anyway." LMEs mostly defer rather than solve.

3. The SEC's Division of Investment Management lost nearly 25% of its staff; the SEC overall lost 18% (871 employees out of ~5,000). The GAO warns of "lost expertise on rulemaking." This coincides with calls for stronger oversight of the $1.8T private credit sector and semi-liquid retail funds facing unprecedented exit requests.

4. Hong Kong raised $14B in IPO listings in Q1 2026 (best quarter since 2021), capping $76B in total 2025 equity activity. The Iran war — via long lockup risk and geopolitical uncertainty — is making institutional investors increasingly selective, while Beijing's demands that offshore red-chip structures be overhauled before listing are adding timeline complexity.

5. The DOL finalized guidelines for 401(k) investments in private markets, arriving simultaneously with record redemption requests from retail-facing credit funds and the SEC's depleted regulatory capacity.

**Quotable:**

### Viktor Shvets on castles without moats and lots of sharks 📎
**Viktor Shvets (Macquarie Capital)** · *Bloomberg Odd Lots* · 8 min

1. Warren Buffett's "castle with a deep moat" framework is now antiquated. The "Fujiwara Effect" — the merger of disruptive technology waves and deep financialization, named for the reinforcing hurricane phenomenon — is destroying competitive moats in months rather than years. Google's TurboQuant (memory optimization) and Anthropic's Claude Mythos announcement each caused immediate multi-sector selloffs, demonstrating how fast intangible-asset disruption now travels.

2. Central banks have no choice but to remain backward-looking ("data-dependent") indefinitely. Technology creates a persistent disinflationary backdrop; geopolitical and societal polarization creates unpredictable inflationary spikes from outside conventional cycles. This is unlike the 1970s (pure inflation) or 2000–2020 (pure disinflation). Central banks can no longer transfer risk back to private sectors; "sound money" cannot be recreated.

3. Intangibles are blurring the boundary between "molecules" and "AI." As marginal costs approach zero, even tangible scarcity erodes — land, energy, commodities, and possibly food production will all be optimized. Labor will "also gravitate toward zero," with only highly human-centric (non-technical) skills maintaining or increasing value.

4. The anti-Buffett investment framework: abandon conventional economic cycles and mean-reversion; ignore tech/non-tech distinctions (technology permeates everything); when zero-marginal-cost waves arrive in a sector, model negative perpetuities in DCF frameworks even if near-term revenue looks strong. "Get used to weird."

**Quotable:**
> "As soon as zero marginal waves arrive (such as in some areas of software), investors should consider placing negative perpetuities in their discounted cash flow (DCF) models, even if there's still a strong short-term outlook." — *Viktor Shvets, on the correct analytical response to disruptive intangible-asset waves*

### Unhedged: Buckling up for the stagflation trade 📎
**Katie Martin** · *FT Unhedged* · 5 min

1. Economist Kenneth Rogoff: the Iran war is "shaping up as the biggest stagflationary shock the world has seen in five decades." Stocks and bonds both had a bad March 2026 — reminiscent of 2022 — as fuel costs surge and some airlines enter "emergency mode." The FTSE 100 is up 4% YTD while the US is down 4%.

2. Historical stagflation data (past 100 years, Schroders/Duncan Lamont): median real stock return in stagflation years is ~0%; in about half of stagflation years stocks were positive, averaging +16% real; when negative, -14%. Only 8 stagflation years produced positive returns — not a large sample. The implication is wide uncertainty, not doom.

3. Stagflation-resilient sectors: utilities, consumer staples, energy, materials. The UK market — 16% consumer staples, 10% energy, minimal IT/communications exposure — is a structural beneficiary of what Katie Martin calls "making boring great again." In Europe, over 90% of sectors have fallen, a dislocation that historically preceded a rebound 2/3 of the time (median +2.5% for MSCI Europe).

**Quotable:**
> "The Iran war is shaping up as the biggest stagflationary shock the world has seen in five decades." — *Economist Kenneth Rogoff, quoted in Schroders' note on historical stagflation scenarios*
