## Today's Top 3

### WAC* ⚡
**Benn Stancil** · email · 9 mins

1. **AI benchmarks are following the same failed playbook as corporate brain-teasers.** The standard AI evaluation method — administer a standardized test, count correct answers — mirrors what companies like Google once did with logic puzzles and math tests in job interviews. Google's own Laszlo Bock eventually called those brainteasers "a complete waste of time" that "don't predict anything" and "serve primarily to make the interviewer feel smart."

2. **Jobs can't be contained in tests, and neither can AI agents.** Companies like Linear have abandoned conventional interviews entirely in favor of 2–5 day paid work trials, because real job performance depends on "good taste and judgment" exercised with access to a company's actual internal tools, culture, and coworkers — none of which a test captures. AI agents now have the same properties: persistent memory, tool access, and context-dependent behavior that makes standardized benchmarks structurally unable to measure what matters.

3. **Baseball solved this problem with WAR — a relative, not absolute, metric.** Traditional stats (ERA, batting average, RBI) were too crude and context-blind. "Wins Above Replacement" (WAR) answered a better question: how much better is this player than the generic minor-leaguer a team could easily call up for almost nothing? It's a measure of how far a player exceeds an easy, accessible baseline — not an arbitrary fixed standard.

4. **The real baseline for every AI product is now C* — Claude/Codex/Copilot/ChatGPT plugged into your stack.** Anyone building a coding agent competes against Claude Code integrated with Linear. An analytical agent competes against Codex connected to Slack, a dbt MCP, and Databricks. A personal assistant competes against Claude running on a Mac Mini with full computer access. The question isn't how a product scores on a benchmark; it's how much better the product is than this always-improving, freely-available default.

5. **Context is the critical variable that standardized benchmarks structurally cannot measure.** When Benn Stancil's team built a new analytical benchmark, nobody cared about model scores. Users cared about how their specific hacked-together system with their own data and tools performed against the new product. "Wins Above C*" — the margin by which a product outperforms an LLM plugged into relevant internal tools — may be the only metric that actually predicts value, but it's also the one that's impossible to standardize.

6. **Google's search box update is a real-world case study in UI as the "harness."** The conventional view of an AI harness is a complex orchestration layer with recursive self-authoring prompts. But Google just expanded its search box for the first time since 2001 — a pure CSS change — and that interface shift is what allows users to ask longer, more context-rich queries and upload photos and video. The meaningful capability improvement came from removing friction in how users express intent, not from a new model or a new orchestration architecture.

**Quotable:**
> "The question that every product—and maybe every employee—will have to answer is, how much are you worth above C*?" — *on the new competitive baseline replacing traditional benchmarks*

### Pod Shops Got Many Shots to Get AI Right ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 8 mins

1. Ken Griffin's view on AI flipped completely within roughly a year. In 2025 he called it "a productivity enhancement tool — nice, but not revolutionary." By 2026 he said: "work that we would usually do with people with master's and PhDs in finance over the course of weeks or months is being done by AI agents over the course of hours or days." The speed of the reversal is itself the data point — models crossed a threshold Griffin could actually feel in his business.

2. Citadel is structurally the perfect AI canary because every part of the business independently stress-tests a different AI capability. Code must survive contact with live markets (a giant collective bug bounty that exploits sloppy algorithms), so they're acutely sensitive to the quality/speed tradeoff. Citadel Securities as a market-maker reveals exactly where a slightly dumber but faster model beats a smarter slower one. And back-office workflows that were designed for the telex, barely changed for fax, and barely changed again for email — those are suddenly eliminable end-to-end once a model can extract PDF fields reliably.

3. P&L is an automatic reinforcement gym: if an AI model improves alpha, the scoreboard says so immediately; if it doesn't beat the efficient frontier, that's visible too. This creates a compounding discovery loop — 2022 was GPT-2 for back-office categorization, 2023 was ChatGPT porting Perl to Python, 2024 was channel-checks confirming GPU shortages, and by 2025 a quant reportedly finished all of first-half 2026's deliverables during their holiday break. The best use-cases get discovered continuously across the whole firm, not in a single top-down initiative.

4. Finance is the leading sector for AI deployment not just because of capital, but because funds can both bet on AI and *be* AI — simultaneously. PE firms are buying companies expressly to AI-ify operations; discretionary investors use LLM summaries to ingest more data; systematic funds taxonomize noisy signals; HFTs harvest the "Peace Dividend of the Inference War" (the massive investment in cheap floating-point operations). Unlike solar — where you could only ride the stock price, not meaningfully power your datacenter with panels — AI lets funds make a directional portfolio bet and then lever it up operationally in a way the risk-management system can't even see.

**Quotable:**
> "To be blunt, work that we would usually do with people with master's and PhDs in finance over the course of weeks or months is being done by AI agents over the course of hours or days." — *Ken Griffin on Citadel's AI adoption, 2026*

### Clouded Judgement 5.22.26 - The Neocloud Boom ⚡
**Clouded Judgement by Jamin Ball** · email · 9 mins

1. **The AI infrastructure buildout is on track to be one of the largest capital deployment events in history.** Anthropic and OpenAI alone are projected to reach ~3–3.5GW of capacity by end of 2025, with OpenAI targeting 30GW by 2030 and Anthropic likely similar. Assuming those two represent 30–40% of total new builds, the industry is on track to bring ~150GW online in 4.5 years — costing roughly $7.5 trillion ($50B per GW) and representing ~5% of annual US GDP, comparable to the railroad boom of the 1880s (~6% of GDP) and dwarfing the telecom buildout of the early 2000s (~1.2%).

2. **Neoclouds — the GPU-cloud providers serving AI labs and enterprises — stand to capture trillions in enterprise value from this buildout.** Current public neoclouds (CoreWeave, Nebius, IREN) are trading at roughly $90B of enterprise value per live gigawatt deployed. If that ratio holds and neoclouds capture just 20% of the 150GW coming online, the implied enterprise value creation exceeds $2.5 trillion over 4.5 years. CoreWeave trades at ~6x NTM revenue / ~10x NTM EBITDA; Nebius and IREN at ~10x NTM revenue / ~20x NTM EBITDA.

3. **SpaceX's acquisition of xAI instantly created a neocloud business nearly equal in revenue to its 23-year-old rocket business.** xAI built Colossus 1 (~300MW) and Colossus 2 (~2GW total, ~500MW operational). After SpaceX bought xAI, Anthropic signed a deal to rent capacity — reportedly $15B per year according to the SpaceX S-1 — for an estimated ~500MW. SpaceX's full-year 2025 revenue was $18.7B; the Anthropic contract alone adds ~$15B in a single stroke.

4. **The $15B/year Anthropic-SpaceX contract implies a pricing rate (~$30M/MW) roughly 3x above neocloud market rates, which raises questions but has a plausible explanation.** Typical neocloud pricing is $10–12M/MW; CoreWeave guides to ~$18.5B annualized run rate on 1.7GW by end of 2026, implying ~$11M/MW. At $11M/MW, the Anthropic contract would imply ~1.4GW from SpaceX — more than the reported available capacity. The premium likely reflects Anthropic's acute compute scarcity and the contract's 90-day cancellation clause, which is high-risk for the supplier and could justify a price premium.

5. **The neocloud market will likely fragment into a few large players plus a long tail of single-site operators, mirroring the independent power producer (IPP) market structure.** The total addressable opportunity is too large for only a handful of companies. Private equity will be a major force financing the build-out. Neoclouds also function as an AGI-hedge within a venture portfolio: if AI captures most economic value, whoever builds and operates the compute infrastructure wins regardless of which software layer prevails.

**Quotable:**
> "SpaceX was founded in 2002. It took them 23 years to get to an annual revenue figure of ~$19b. And with their new 'neocloud' they generated almost that same amount. The opportunity for Neoclouds is massive!" — *on the Anthropic-SpaceX compute deal disclosed in the SpaceX S-1*

### DealBook: An A.I. dividend? 📌
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 9 mins

1. California Gov. Gavin Newsom issued a first-in-the-nation directive ordering state officials to study Universal Basic Capital (U.B.C.) — equity stakes in AI wealth funds distributed to all citizens — as a response to AI-driven job displacement. The concept differs from Universal Basic Income (U.B.I.) in that instead of recurring cash, workers would receive proportional profit-sharing through holdings in a fund financed by tech companies. OpenAI's April policy framework proposed exactly this: a "public wealth fund" giving all citizens, including non-investors, "a stake in A.I.-driven economic growth."

2. Competition, she added, doesn't disappear because of AI.

3. SpaceX's IPO — potentially the largest public market debut ever at a $1.25 trillion self-valuation — is creating stark winners and losers. Goldman Sachs beat Morgan Stanley for the coveted "lead left" underwriter position and stands to collect the biggest cut of more than $1 billion in fees; AI coding firm Cursor is being acquired by SpaceX at a $60 billion valuation in stock. Meanwhile, Tesla shares have slumped as investors worry Musk's attention has fully shifted — "It feels like SpaceX is his new baby at the expense of Tesla," said Joe Gilbert of Integrity Asset Management.

4. The war with Iran has pushed Brent crude to ~$106 a barrel heading into Memorial Day weekend, but Mastercard's chief economist Michelle Meyer doesn't expect Americans to cancel travel — only to reprice it. TSA is projecting 18.3 million passengers this weekend (up slightly year-over-year) and AAA forecasts 45 million people traveling 50+ miles. A separate Mastercard data point: customers with AI subscriptions budget more for travel and disproportionately choose obscure destinations — Leipzig, Carcassonne, Orvieto — over traditional hotspots.

5. An Iranian financier reportedly funneled $850 million through a secret payment network on Binance to fund Tehran's military, per the Wall Street Journal — on top of $1.7 billion in Iran-linked transfers the Times reported in March. Binance and founder Changpeng Zhao already admitted in 2023 to violating anti-money laundering laws and Iran sanctions (Trump later pardoned Zhao). Iran's crypto reliance is now a live threat to the Clarity Act, the major crypto regulatory bill the White House is backing.

**Quotable:**

### Going Private: Funds close in on blue-chip takeovers as public listings lose luster 📌
**Bloomberg** · email · 11 mins

1. **Two major blue-chip privatizations are in motion simultaneously.** Japan's Nikkon Holdings, one of the country's biggest logistics firms with a market cap of ~¥764 billion, is preparing a first round of bids in June with Bain Capital, Warburg Pincus, and Blackstone expected to participate; deal value is expected to reach a ¥1 trillion (~$6.3 billion) ballpark, with a second bid round timetabled for August. Separately, London-listed Intertek Group's board said it was minded to recommend EQT's fourth takeover offer of £60-per-share — valuing the product testing company at £9.2 billion ($12.5 billion) — after consulting shareholders.

2. **Public markets have structurally lost their grip on growing companies.** US public equity markets now host roughly 4,000 companies, less than half the 1996 peak. Meanwhile, private companies valued over $1 billion grew 465% — from 280 in 2017 to 1,582 in 2025 — as abundant private capital lets founders skip the regulatory scrutiny and valuation volatility of public listings. Appetite for new IPOs is now effectively confined to multi-trillion-dollar candidates like SpaceX and Anthropic.

3. **"Bad" PIK is climbing in European private credit, masking a shadow default rate.** Lincoln International data shows the share of European private credit borrowers using Payment-in-Kind that was neither elected nor offered at origination — so-called "bad PIK," the stress indicator — rose to 7.8% in Q1 2026 from 7.3% in Q4 2025 and 6.6% a year earlier. At firms using bad PIK, loan-to-values have risen by an average of 28.5%. Lincoln's Nick Baldwin calls this the "shadow default rate," with covenant defaults falling from 1.8% to 1.5% only because prior breaches were restructured, not because borrowers got healthier.

4. **Dry powder at $4.36 trillion is aging dangerously, compressing future IRRs.** PitchBook's Q1 2026 Global Private Market Fundraising Report finds a growing share of that capital sitting in funds two-to-five years old — creating pressure to deploy before investment periods expire. More critically, 39.7% of private capital net asset value now sits in funds seven-plus years old, up from 26% in 2019–2020, as managers delay exits hoping for better prices. PE fundraising fell for an eighth consecutive quarter, with just 133 funds raising $90.9 billion in Q1 — half attributable to the 10 largest funds alone.

5. **Secondaries markets are surging as pension funds and PE firms seek exits.** Canada Pension Plan Investment Board sold a C$4 billion (~$3 billion) portfolio of 33 private-fund stakes — built over two decades — to Blackstone's Strategic Partners unit and Ardian in one of 2026's largest secondaries transactions. The broader shift is stark: 43% of PE firms are now using secondaries to unlock logjammed positions, nearly double the 24% recorded a year ago. Pricing remains the bottleneck — the share of investors willing to accept forced exits at below-valuation prices has collapsed from 29% in 2025 to just 7%.

6. **Private credit is moving into long-duration infrastructure financing, illustrated by the US Tennis Association deal.** The USTA is in talks to borrow at least $400 million in private credit to refurbish Arthur Ashe Stadium, with an average debt maturity of 17 years and a prospective single A- rating — the kind of long-tenor, investment-grade private placement that life insurers favor. The full refurbishment is projected to cost $1.1 billion (per Fitch), with $850 million funded by USTA's National Tennis Center Inc., including a $200 million revolving credit facility. Chick-fil-A and Live Nation have recently used the same US private placement market for similar reasons: better tailoring and longer maturities than public debt, at a modest premium.

**Quotable:**
> "Time is the enemy of IRRs: doubling your money with a 2x multiple is not nearly as impressive if it takes 10 years to do so." — *Hilary Wiek, PitchBook principal analyst, on PE managers holding aging fund positions*

### On Books: Matt Levine's favorite finance reads 📌
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 9 mins

1. Matt Levine's canonical tier-one picks are *Liar's Poker* and *Barbarians at the Gate* — he treats those as table stakes and skips past them. His actual recommendations skew toward books that expose the hidden mechanics of crisis, power, and market microstructure rather than straightforward Wall Street narratives.

2. *Diary of a Very Bad Year* (2010) — anonymous interviews with a multi-strategy hedge fund manager during the financial crisis — yields a key insight Levine highlights: every private investment is a call option written against your own time. In normal times you overbook your time on the assumption that not all positions will need attention simultaneously; in a crisis, correlations go to 100% and all fires burn at once, turning time management into the core failure mode.

3. *The Bond King* by Mary Childs (2022) tells how Bill Gross built PIMCO and was then ousted from it. Levine's distillation: Gross called a meeting to confront rivals but seated them in the audience rather than at the front table alongside leadership. The symbolic demotion was so offensive that the demoted executives went straight back to their offices and fired him. Levine's takeaway — "everything is seating charts" — frames corporate power struggles as fundamentally ceremonial rather than rational.

4. *Expected Returns* by Antti Ilmanen (2011) is Levine's answer to "how should I think about investing?" — the book attempts a comprehensive answer to why any given investment should be expected to pay. Its counterintuitive empirical finding: carry basically works. Financial theory says a 10%-yield asset must carry proportionally higher expected loss, making risk-adjusted returns identical to an 8%-yield alternative — but in practice, higher-yielding instruments tend to outperform, suggesting the theory's risk-adjustment systematically overcorrects.

5. *Trading at the Speed of Light* by Donald MacKenzie (2021) documents how ultrafast algorithms operate via microwave links between Chicago and New York — and how rain, by degrading those links, temporarily reduced predatory HFT speed. The result: market makers, no longer getting picked off by faster traders, could safely quote tighter spreads. Liquidity improved during bad weather, which is about as clean an illustration of how market structure rather than fundamentals drives pricing as exists in financial literature.

**Quotable:**
> "Every time you do a private investment, you're writing a call option on your own time... In a crisis, correlations go to 100%, so you just run out of time because you're dealing with all of these fires on all of your investments." — *Matt Levine paraphrasing the anonymous hedge fund manager in* Diary of a Very Bad Year

## Infrastructure

### What's Your Power Bill Hiding? 📎
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. India doubled its power generation capacity — from ~250 GW in March 2014 to 515 GW by late 2025 — and per capita consumption rose from 960 to 1,460 units per year over the same period. Yet despite this investment, the fundamental question of who bears the cost when things go wrong was never honestly answered. The answer is almost always the consumer.

2. The mechanism is the "pass-through" clause embedded in 20–25 year supply contracts between generators and distribution companies (discoms). When coal prices rise, generators pass the extra cost to discoms; discoms pass it to consumers through tariff hikes. By 2020, roughly 78.5% of India's capacity (291 GW) was locked into such contracts, and nearly 9 in 10 units of power bought by discoms today flow through them.

3. Even as renewable-plus-storage contracts have cleared at Rs 4.37 per unit, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh and West Bengal awarded 4.5 GW of new coal projects in 2024–25 at tariffs above Rs 5 per unit. These procurement decisions will ride on consumers' bills until 2050. Maharashtra and Tamil Nadu have also contracted over 40% above their peak demand, meaning consumers pay twice: once for electricity used, once for electricity never delivered.

4. Distribution companies have no incentive to fix their own inefficiencies because regulators allow them to recover costs regardless of operating performance. A discom that loses 15% of the electricity it carries simply bills consumers for those losses. The counterexample exists: Tata Power turned around Odisha's discoms via better metering, billing, and network maintenance, and the Akanksha Power franchise in NESCO's Khaira sub-division cut losses from leakage, theft, and unbilled use by 66% between 2010 and 2021 across 45,000 consumers — but this model has mostly disappointed at scale nationally.

**Quotable:**
> "There is a particular genius to the way India's power sector manages risk. It does not eliminate it. Nor does it absorb the risk. It simply moves it systematically, backed by decades of policy and contract." — *Dev Chandrasekhar, on the structural design of cost pass-through*
