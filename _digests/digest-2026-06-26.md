## Today's Top 3

### Money Stuff: There's a SpaceX Treasury Company ⚡
**Matt Levine** · email · 14 mins

1. The "digital asset treasury company" (DAT) trade — where a company buys crypto, the market pays $2 for every $1 of crypto on its balance sheet, and everyone profits — was hot in 2024–2025 but is now "stone dead." Most DATs now trade below net asset value, and investors have lost heavily. The concept expanded to gold, GameStop stock, and now SpaceX equity, but the window for easy premiums has closed.

2. Triller Group (Nasdaq: ILLR) — a social media company that lost $174.5M on $21.6M in revenue last year — announced it is acquiring exposure to 3,917,185 SpaceX shares at $105/share ($411M total) through a Bahamas company called Capital Truth Holdings Ltd. Triller is effectively SPV-cubed: it buys SAC1, which owns Fortune Offshore Fund – Gigafund, which owns the SpaceX shares. Buying Triller stock means owning a piece of a company that owns a company that owns a company that owns SpaceX.

3. The economics of the Triller deal are surreal. Triller has roughly $2M in cash but is paying $411M for the position — meaning it is borrowing approximately the entire purchase price, secured by the underlying SpaceX shares (currently worth ~$600M). The press release says Triller "retains a portion of the position as treasury," which implies the lender is taking much of the SpaceX upside in exchange for providing 100% of the capital. Triller may be less "buying" SpaceX stock than renting it as a marketing prop. It worked anyway: shares shot from $0.77 to $5.30 (a ~$104M market cap) before settling around $3.18.

4. SpaceX's IPO has eliminated the main reason to pay a premium for indirect SpaceX exposure. A year ago, ordinary investors couldn't buy SpaceX shares and were paying 50–100% premiums through closed-end funds, SPVs, forward contracts, and tokenization schemes. Now SpaceX is publicly traded on the same exchange as Triller — any investor can just buy the stock directly at market price with no layers of intermediaries.

5. AI is doing to boutique hedge funds what the $99 LBO software package did to leveraged-buyout firms in the 1980s — erasing the scale advantage of large analyst teams. Five-person Palinuro Capital (founded by Alfonso Peccatiello) is using LLMs to parse central bank speeches from Hungary to South Korea, work that previously required regional specialists; it's competing with 20–50-person desks. Michael Milken once credited VisiCalc — which KKR adopted in 1980 after an employee spotted it in a Manhattan electronics store — with enabling the junk-bond boom; the parallel to AI's role in the coming decade of finance is overt.

6. In the potential SpaceX/Tesla merger, the asymmetry in shareholder rights creates dramatically different litigation risks on each side. SpaceX shareholders can only bring a derivative lawsuit, and must own 3% of the company to do so — worth over $52 billion at current prices — making a suit essentially impossible. Tesla shareholders, by contrast, can sue directly (no ownership threshold) if Tesla is acquired at too low a price, and Tesla has not yet barred class actions the way SpaceX has. Law professor Ann Lipton's analysis: the deal faces far more legal exposure from the Tesla side even though proving wrongdoing requires showing fraud or intentional misconduct.

7. JPMorgan won SEC approval for monthly redemptions on a new private/public credit interval fund, arguing the counterintuitive logic that more frequent liquidity windows reduce actual redemption pressure. Quarterly redemption terms create a "use it or lose it" incentive — investors redeem preemptively because missing a window means a 3-month wait. Monthly windows let investors think "I can always get out next month," reducing urgency. This comes as private credit funds have faced elevated redemption requests that have caused forced-sale spirals and PR damage.

**Quotable:**
> "If you buy shares of Triller, you own shares of a company that owns shares of a company that owns shares of a company that owns shares of SpaceX." — *on the absurdity of the SPV-cubed SpaceX treasury structure*

### Japan's $2.3tn investment roadmap ⚡
**FirstFT Asia** · email · 5 mins

1. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has unveiled a ¥370tn ($2.3tn), 14-year investment roadmap — Japan's largest and longest-term such plan ever. It spans 17 strategic sectors and combines public and private capital, with more than $600bn earmarked specifically for AI and semiconductors. Target sectors include shipbuilding, solar cells, quantum computers, and next-generation nuclear reactors.

2. Takaichi's core diagnosis is that Japan's stagnation stems from a deficit of domestic investment, not from any failure of its workforce, innovation, or efficiency. She made this argument explicitly in a speech this week, framing the state's role as fully backing companies with world-leading technologies — a supply-side thesis that differs from demand-stimulus approaches. Economists noted the plan is short on hard detail about how spending splits between government outlays and private sector commitments.

3. Bond markets pushed back sharply: Japanese government bond yields surged to multi-decade highs in late May on expectations that the plan's fiscal ambitions require heavy government borrowing. Yields have since retreated slightly, but the spike raised concrete questions about Japan's debt capacity and whether the state can efficiently allocate capital at this scale — concerns that remain unresolved.

**Quotable:**
> "What is lacking is domestic investment." — *PM Takaichi, in a speech this week explaining her diagnosis of Japan's economic problems*

### Saplings: Outsiders ⚡
**'The Generalist' via PubsforSubs** · email · 14 mins

1. **Outsiderhood is the single most common trait across legendary founders' early lives.** Henry Luce — founder of *Time*, *Fortune*, *Life*, and *Sports Illustrated*, who turned $86,000 into $503M in annual holdings — spent his childhood as an American in China, then a "Chinese" adolescent in Connecticut, then a stammering scholarship boy who waited tables for his paying classmates at Hotchkiss. That accumulated rejection bred a ferocious competitiveness: his letters home at age 10 describe a "hanging torture," while his letters to peers describe his obsessive drive to "lick Hayes" and climb 8 class places in a year.

2. **Racial and ethnic marginalization generates a specific and potent drive to overperform.** Masayoshi Son (SoftBank) was a Korean immigrant's child in Japan, raised under the Japanese alias Yasumoto Masayoshi to hide his origins. His grandmother eating kimchi at school pickup exposed him — he was struck with a stone and called "Hey, you Korean!" The shame was so internalized he found his grandmother "repulsive" because "kimchi equaled Korea." His response: he became junior high president, once skipping an entire family vacation to finish first in his class. Ralph Lauren (born Ralph Lifschitz) changed his name under mockery. Estee Lauder, daughter of Eastern European immigrants, "desperately wanted to be 100 percent American."

3. **Class humiliation at a specific childhood moment can crystallize a founder's entire commercial purpose.** Amancio Ortega (Inditex/Zara), at age 12, overheard a grocer tell his mother he could no longer extend her credit. He quit school immediately, took a job as a shirt-store boy, and vowed: "This will never happen to my mother again." Howard Schultz (Starbucks), raised in the Canarsie Projects, answered debt collectors' calls, discovered that his "summer camp" was actually for underprivileged children, and retreated into the building stairwell — "the first place my imagination took flight." Brunello Cucinelli, one of 22 people in a stone house without plumbing, arrived at secondary school labeled a country boy by his dialect and uncle-trimmed haircut.

4. **Political exile can convert enforced isolation into self-directed intellectual expansion.** Ren Zhengfei (Huawei) survived the Great Leap Forward on extra corn cakes smuggled by his mother, then watched the Cultural Revolution destroy his father: Moxun, a teacher who had worked for the Nationalists and sold banned books, was hauled into a school cafeteria, his face smeared with ink, and beaten with wooden rods. Cut off from classmates and formal education, Ren taught himself mathematics, philosophy, and three foreign languages. The lesson: isolation that forecloses conventional paths forces investment in the self.

5. **Familial rejection — being an interloper inside one's own home — installs the drive to justify one's existence at the deepest level.** Australian mining magnate Andrew Forrest was told by his mother she had tried to abort him, riding a horse bareback for miles then jumping off the roof of their homestead three times a day for a week. Robert Kuok (Malaysian conglomerate billionaire) reports his first memory at age 18 months: crawling under furniture weeping as his mother fled the country with one of his brothers but not him. His father never offered affection: "Growing up, I felt ignored by most of the people around me." Walt Disney's father wanted him at a jelly factory; Disney refused. Both Jobs and Bezos were adopted — Jobs' adoptive mother reportedly couldn't bond with him for six months.

6. **Physical disability forced compensatory cerebration that became a competitive asset.** Thomas Edison began losing hearing to scarlet fever at age 7, was fully deaf in one ear and severely impaired in the other by 12. Andy Grove (Intel) lost 50% hearing in both ears at age 4 from scarlet fever, with perforated eardrums; his friend Jani theorized he had developed unusual cognitive capacity precisely because "he often understood only parts of sentences" and had to exercise his mind constantly to fill gaps. J.P. Morgan, sent to the Azores at 15 to convalesce from rheumatic fever, wrote home with meticulous detail about harbor layouts, exchange rates, customs duties, and vessel-by-vessel storm damage — his mind sharpening in isolation while his body recovered.

7. **At a critical juncture, imposed individuation flips: the founders metabolize shame into a felt sense of destiny.** The pattern repeats across cases — a moment when the outsider stops trying to be normal and starts interpreting their differentness as purpose. Ralph Lauren: "I sort of felt like I was special, but I did not know why. I mean, I had my own mind. I did my own thing." Jobs: "Knowing I was adopted may have made me feel more independent... I've always felt special." Luce, rather than bonding with fellow scholarship boys, looked down on them and aimed at the blue bloods. The exile becomes the founding myth — and the drive to prove it justified becomes the engine.

**Quotable:**
> "The way Father treated me became rocket fuel inside me that drove me on. I was a truculent young man." — *Robert Kuok, Malaysian billionaire, on his cold and abusive father*

3. **Anthropic accused Alibaba and its Qwen AI lab of running the largest known distillation attack against it**: 28.8 million Claude exchanges generated through nearly 25,000 fraudulent accounts between April 22 and June 5. In a June 10 letter to U.S. senators, Anthropic called for Congress to expand information sharing between AI labs, close chip-export loopholes enabling Chinese access to advanced U.S. hardware, and impose penalties on Chinese labs caught distilling. This follows Anthropic's February accusation against DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax for the same practice.

### Gamblers and Data Gatherers 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 8 mins

1. Meta is building a play-money prediction market — not to compete with Kalshi or Polymarket (which together are worth only a few percent of Meta's market cap), but as a training data acquisition mechanism. Real-money gambling carries reputational risk exceeding that upside; play-money markets sidestep this while remaining surprisingly accurate, and user interactions create a feedback loop: prediction market engagement generates labeled data on how well AI generates and resolves contracts, which improves the AI, which improves the markets.

2. The real strategic prize for Meta's prediction market is a better world model for ad targeting. DeepSeek — incubated by a hedge fund — raised at over $50bn, while Millennium Management, one of the best hedge funds ever built, was valued at $14bn last year. The math says: if you can predict the world well, don't use it to trade; use it to show ads. A probability-weighted list of everything a given consumer might buy is worth far more than any edge on prediction markets.

3. Anthropic accused Alibaba of running a systematic distillation campaign using 25,000 accounts and nearly 30 million queries — essentially pirating a model by querying it at scale. This is more expensive and harder to operationalize than scraping a competitor's prices, and the outputs can't be cleanly incorporated into a new model the way a price cut can be matched. But for a Chinese company blocked from buying US-level compute inputs, copying the outputs is the next-best option.

4. Individual AI researchers can now swing publicly-traded companies by tens of billions of dollars — a historically anomalous situation. Google's hedge: it owns ~14% of Anthropic, which trades at ~20x revenue vs. Alphabet's 9.4x, so Google effectively recaptures roughly a third of the value when a researcher defects to Anthropic (plus recaptures TPU revenue). The defections are embarrassing but Google positioned itself to absorb the loss.

**Quotable:**
> "The most lucrative thing to predict is not what stock will be bought next by some miscellaneous trader, but to produce a profit- and probability-weighted list of all the things a given consumer might buy, and use that to show them ads." — *on why Meta's world-model ambitions are really about advertising, not gambling*

### The Evening: Court expands Trump's immigration powers 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 6 mins

1. The Supreme Court issued two 6-3 rulings on June 25 — both split along ideological lines — dramatically expanding Trump's immigration authority. The first allows the administration to terminate Temporary Protected Status (TPS) for 350,000 Haitians and 6,100 Syrians who have lived and worked legally in the U.S. for over a decade; the majority held that federal law bars courts from second-guessing the administration's judgment on which countries are too unsafe for deportation. The three liberal justices dissented, arguing Haiti and Syria were removed from the protected list for political or racial reasons, citing Trump's derogatory public comments about Haitian immigrants.

2. The second ruling permits the administration to physically block asylum seekers from crossing the U.S.-Mexico border — effectively foreclosing the legal right to present at the border and request protection. Together the two decisions signal the court's strong deference to presidential authority on immigration, and the rulings hit South Florida — home to the largest Haitian population in the country — "like a bomb," with community centers inundated by calls asking: "Will I lose my job? When does it go into effect? How much time do we have?"

3. Iranian forces struck a container ship transiting the Strait of Hormuz, prompting a U.N. agency to suspend its evacuation of hundreds of vessels stranded in the Persian Gulf during the Iran war — this came hours after oil prices had returned to near pre-war levels on expectations of a U.S.-brokered deal to reopen the waterway permanently. Separately, back-to-back earthquakes struck Venezuela overnight, killing at least 188 and injuring more than 1,500, with the coastal city of La Guaira among the hardest hit and residents digging through rubble themselves due to a lack of rescue workers.

### The World: Less Islamism, more nationalism 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Political Islam is waning across the Middle East, and citizens are leading the retreat. The Muslim Brotherhood's slogan "Islam is the solution" — which won them Egyptian elections — has lost its hold. In Tunisia, Islamists won elections but then lost the trust of secular voters, who elected a populist strongman instead. As Oxford historian Faisal Devji puts it, terrorism "tarnished the brand," and popular demand for religion as a governing answer has collapsed.

2. Iran — the most elaborate modern experiment in political Islam — is pivoting to Persian nationalism under military pressure. Since Israel's 12-day war last summer, the Iranian government has been playing patriotic ballads and hanging posters of pre-Islamic Persian folk heroes rather than figures from the Islamic Revolution. State television now features women's military parades with pink guns and jeeps, and prominently shows women who support the regime but are *not* wearing hijabs — a direct inversion of the republic's founding ideology.

3. Saudi Arabia's Mohammed bin Salman has dismantled the kingdom's Islamist architecture to meet young Saudis' expectations. Women can now drive, movie theaters have opened, and music festivals have come to Saudi Arabia — all forbidden under strict Shariah enforcement that had defined the kingdom for decades. MBS is recasting Saudi identity as proudly Saudi first, Muslim second.

4. Syria's Ahmed al-Sharaa illustrates how thoroughly the Islamist political brand has been abandoned even by former jihadists. Al-Sharaa once led an Al Qaeda branch and vowed on television to build an Islamic state; since overthrowing Assad and becoming president, he has not attempted to implement Shariah, has met Western leaders, and appears publicly in suits with his wife — behavior his former Al Qaeda comrades would never have permitted. The calculation: overt Islamism costs more externally and domestically than it earns.

5. The war has not revived Islamism — it has accelerated Iran's drift into a military dictatorship with an Islamic veneer. Iran is now run by the Islamic Revolutionary Guards Corps more than by clerics, per Iranian scholar Mohsen Kadivar (now at Duke University). What remains fortified is one specific plank of political Islam — anti-Zionism and resistance to American influence in the Middle East — which the war has handed to Iranian hard-liners as a durable rallying point even as broader religious governance erodes.

**Quotable:**
> "Islam is the solution has been losing its hold." — *Ben Hubbard, describing the collapse of the Muslim Brotherhood's defining slogan across the region*

## Business & Economics

### Can Amazon Catch Blinkit? 📎
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. Amazon's quick commerce entry is real but tiny relative to incumbents. Amazon Now launched as a 4-dark-store pilot in Whitefield, Bengaluru in early 2025 and has expanded to Delhi and Mumbai, targeting 300 dark stores by end of 2025 with Rs 2,000 crore in infrastructure investment. Meanwhile, Blinkit already operates ~2,100 dark stores with ~48% market share, Zepto has 1,100+ stores across 66 cities, and Swiggy Instamart holds 1,100–1,200 stores and 23–25% share — together controlling over 90% of the market.

2. Amazon skips the cold-start problem that hurt every other entrant. Quick commerce's hardest early task is convincing FMCG and grocery brands to list inventory — Amazon already has those supplier relationships, warehousing, and last-mile logistics from its marketplace business. Its Prime customer base is also a natural fit: high-income urban shoppers who overlap heavily with existing quick commerce users.

3. The defensive motive is as important as the offensive one. Quick commerce has been actively pulling customers away from Amazon's regular marketplace, particularly in long-tail product categories. Amazon's move into 10–15-minute delivery is partly about recapturing shoppers it has already lost, not just capturing new ones.

4. Neighbourhood-level density is the moat Amazon cannot buy quickly. Analyst Sandeep Abhange estimates Amazon needs $1.5–2 billion and 3–4 years just to build a network comparable to what Blinkit and Zepto already have — because a dark store only becomes profitable once order volume in the immediate catchment area is high enough, and incumbents have spent 4–5 years calibrating that at the block level. The window is also narrowing: Blinkit has already reached EBITDA breakeven and Zepto is approaching profitability ahead of its IPO, so Amazon is entering a market that is no longer bleeding cash and will not be competed out on subsidies alone. A top-four position looks achievable; cracking the top three will take materially longer.

**Quotable:**
> "Amazon has the capital. Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart still have the execution advantage." — *analyst Sandeep Abhange*

## Global Finance

### Free Lunch: An EU financial geopolitics plan 📎
**Martin Sandbu** · email · 8 mins

1. The EU needs a proper monetary geopolitics strategy — not just a digital-payments upgrade. Making the euro genuinely attractive internationally requires bringing in the digital euro faster than current timelines, with higher (ideally unlimited) holding limits and more generous access for non-euro-area residents. The EU should also actively encourage euro-denominated stablecoins, building offshore euro-denominated financial markets analogous to the "eurodollar" markets that cemented the dollar's global dominance in the postwar era.

2. The ECB should become a provider of international monetary public goods by extending swap lines and refinancing facilities to non-euro countries. The ECB did beef up its repo facilities for foreign central banks earlier in 2026 — a welcome step — but those lines should be standing (not time-limited) and attractive in peacetime rather than triggered only by crisis. Foreign central banks that can rely on ECB liquidity have a structural incentive to hold more euro-denominated securities in their reserves.

3. Research presented at the Economic Policy conference shows that deeper domestic markets and closer trade and capital links with other economies increase a currency's international attractiveness — while protectionist tariff walls (Trump's approach) make a currency less useful as a hedge. The EU should exploit its natural trade-liberalisation DNA by linking trade negotiations directly to the monetary agenda: offer ECB swap lines as part of deal packages and limit preferential trade concessions to transactions invoiced in euros.

4. The EU's single most critical gap is the shortage of common government-backed assets. A large supply of EU-backed debt would give international investors a safe, scalable entry point to the Eurozone, lower European funding costs, and recapture the hundreds of billions in net savings Europeans currently export to fund other economies. Germany leads the political resistance, but the statecraft required is recognising that being a great power means offering the world a safe place to park savings — not hoarding fiscal conservatism while the savings leak out.

5. The EU already holds underused financial leverage. Its European Stability Mechanism carries hundreds of billions in idle borrowing capacity. On the regulatory side, a Kiel Institute report highlights that US Treasuries receive privileged treatment in EU rules for European financial institutions; simply treating them on par with comparable securities would pressure US funding costs while redirecting capital to lower-cost European financing — defensible on pure domestic terms, not just as a geopolitical weapon.

6. The ~€200bn in immobilised Russian central bank reserves, mostly held as deposits in Euroclear Bank in Belgium, should be unlocked. European policymakers worry that seizing sovereign assets would undermine the euro's credibility — but this gets the logic backwards. The EU's hesitation to touch them is what reveals it as not yet an autonomous financial power. Transferring the frozen claims to a new isolated banking entity (distinct from Euroclear's broader financial system) designed to satisfy Russia's clear obligations to pay war compensation would enhance both the EU's geopolitical standing and its financial credibility.

**Quotable:**
> "A global power cannot behave as if its own financial security is hostage to what is, at the scale of its economy, a small amount of money." — *on the EU's reluctance to touch the €200bn in frozen Russian central bank reserves at Euroclear*

### How Warsh sank the debasement trade 📎
**John Authers** · email · 7 mins

1. **Kevin Warsh's Fed nomination in January was the single pivotal catalyst.** Gold tumbled 13% from an all-time high on the day Warsh was announced as Powell's successor — its steepest single-day decline in more than four decades. Warsh was chosen over candidates seen as beholden to Trump's dovish rate-cut agenda, and his reputation as an inflation hawk immediately rattled debasement-trade positions in gold, Bitcoin, and other dollar-alternative assets.

2. **The debasement trade was built on fiscal fear, not just monetary permissiveness.** Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution argues the core logic was always about policymakers inflating away unsustainable debt burdens, with monetary policy as the "accessory" rather than the root cause. The S&P 500 denominated in gold — the cleanest measure of whether growth reflects real strength or currency erosion — reversed sharply upward three months ago, confirming the market's reassessment.

3. **The Strait of Hormuz peace deal compounded Warsh's hawkish signal, but inflation isn't dead.** Reopening the waterway collapsed energy prices and pulled inflation expectations down sharply, giving Warsh political room to cut rates if he chooses. But Vincent Deluard of StoneX warns that "inflation will not return to the 2% target and will remain stuck at a 3.5%-4% plateau until the next inflationary shock" — and Apollo's Torsten Slok adds a counterintuitive wrinkle: lower oil prices act like a tax cut, pumping more demand into an already-hot economy, which Bloomberg's rate-probability data confirms is already pushing rate-hike expectations higher even as crude falls.

4. **The dollar and Bitcoin are the clearest scoreboard of the un-debasement trade.** The DXY broad dollar index closed Wednesday at a 14-month high; Bitcoin fell below $60,000, hovering near its lowest since 2024. Standard Chartered's Steven Englander attributes dollar strength to real and nominal rate differentials since early May, with the Fed on hold and the ECB expected to cut only once in H1 2027. Gold is now down roughly 8% year-to-date after having been up significantly. Absent a fresh inflationary shock, the debasement trade is on its last legs.

**Quotable:**
> "So it starts with fiscal and then monetary provides the assist. When you inflate away debt, you turn on the printing presses. So monetary policy is an accessory. But the root cause is bad fiscal policy. That's why you have to turn on the printing presses." — *Robin Brooks, Brookings Institution, on why the debasement trade was always a fiscal story*
