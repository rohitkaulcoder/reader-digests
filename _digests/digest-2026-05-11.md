## Today's Top 3

### Investor Relations Goes Retail ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. Retail shareholders are structural cost centers under current IR rules. Someone who owns 10 shares at $50 each still triggers Broadridge proxy mailings, printed annual report requests, and other per-shareholder liabilities — all costs written into regulation when commissions were high and information was expensive to find. Companies rationally defaulted to institutional-only IR because the economics of small shareholders were negative before they even picked up the phone.

2. Institutional investor meetings are a two-way intelligence exchange that management values beyond just capital. Investors bring alternative datasets companies can't generate internally — cell tower pings triangulated with credit card transactions to estimate foot traffic and purchase conversion by location, anonymized panel data for retention benchmarking against competitors. Tiger Cubs exploited this by showing companies they were outperforming peers on some metric, earning reciprocity (i.e., a check) through flattery backed by data.

3. GameStop and AMC were the first retail IR stress-tests, and they adapted at different speeds. GameStop added Ryan Cohen to the board in January 2021 but its 10-K filed months later still treated the meme rally as a "Risk Factor," noting a $100 investment became $1,607 in a year in an apologetic footnote. AMC's CEO was explicitly following "apes" by May 2021 and offering free summer popcorn to shareholders the next month — a Japan-style shareholder perk weaponized as a capital-raising mechanism.

4. Retail shareholder populism at AMC degraded into visibly bad capital allocation. The company bought a stake in a gold mine, and when shareholders rejected a share-issuance vote, management issued preferred shares with equivalent economic rights conditioned on shareholder approval of more issuance — effectively a workaround for the failed vote. Those preferred shares traded under the ticker APE.

5. SpaceX is building the most deliberate retail IR operation yet: two of the five stated responsibilities in their new IR hire posting reference retail investors directly. SpaceX will be the largest company in the Elon complex and its primary public fundraising vehicle, giving Musk strong incentive to cultivate retail enthusiasm. The upside is that retail investors who accept a lower risk premium in exchange for the entertainment value of owning a piece of space exploration are subsidizing SpaceX's cost of capital — a deal both sides can rationally accept even if the "apes" never reach orbit.

**Quotable:**
> "Anyone who's done consumer-facing direct marketing knows how powerful it is to say: 'Act now! For a limited time, you'll also get a FREE...'" — *on AMC offering free popcorn to shareholders as a capital-raising tactic*

### Apple's new macOS ⚡
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 8 mins

1. **macOS 26 Tahoe's Liquid Glass redesign is already being patched for macOS 27.** The transparency effects and shadows introduced with Liquid Glass make text-heavy areas harder to read — most visibly in Control Center, Finder, and apps with sidebars and dense lists. The root problem is that Liquid Glass was designed for OLED displays (iPhone, iPad Pro, Apple Watch), but most Macs still run LCD panels that don't render the glass effects as intended. Apple's MacBook Air design dates to 2022, MacBook Pro and iMac to 2021 — the hardware lags the aesthetic. macOS 27 will address the shadow and transparency quirks, and the eventual OLED touchscreen MacBooks will make the interface look the way the design team originally intended.

2. **The fix is a deliberate Snow Leopard moment — polish over features — across all 2026 OS releases.** iOS 27, iPadOS 27, and macOS 27 are planned as "no-frills" refinement updates: bug fixes, battery-life improvements, performance gains, and AI enhancements. The historical parallel Gurman draws is iOS 7 (2013) getting refined with iOS 8, which similarly dialed back aggressive transparency. WWDC 2026 is scheduled for June 8. The main software additions are: a standalone Siri app with a chatbot-like interface, automatic AI-driven tab grouping in Safari, deeper Apple Intelligence integration, and an expanded choice of rival AI models — OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic — usable across Writing Tools, Image Playground, and Siri directly.

3. **Apple has effectively wound down Vision Pro as an active product line.** The Vision Products Group (VPG) has been disbanded: hardware staff shifted to AI wearables (camera-equipped AirPods and an AI pendant), and software lead Mike Rockwell now spends nearly all his time on Siri. Apple canceled the N100 Vision Air project last year, and new CEO John Ternus — who was not a proponent of Vision Pro during its development — has no near-term enclosed headset in the pipeline. visionOS 27 will be light on features, mainly achieving parity with the other 27 OS updates. The $3,499 device is widely considered a dead end by Apple's own senior leadership given its price and weight; AR glasses remain the actual priority.

4. **Apple is quietly moving to diversify chip supply away from TSMC.** Apple is in talks with both Intel and Samsung as potential chipmaking partners, motivated by three converging pressures: TSMC's Arizona factories opening slower than planned, silicon becoming harder to secure, and geopolitical risk from concentrating supply in Taiwan. The approach mirrors Apple's existing multi-supplier strategy for displays, storage, and memory. Nothing is imminent — Gurman puts the earliest realistic timeline at within the next year — but the intent is to build chips in the US with backup suppliers in place before any Taiwan disruption forces the issue.

**Quotable:**
> "In some ways, it's a rare instance of Apple's software feeling more futuristic than its hardware." — *on Liquid Glass being designed for OLED Macs that don't yet exist*

### The World: A one-party India ⚡
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. Congress, the founding party of independent India and its only true national opposition, has been reduced to a rump: fewer than 100 seats in the 543-member Parliament and control of just 4 of India's 28 states. With Congress gutted, regional parties and their leaders became the last meaningful counterweight to Modi's BJP — so the BJP spent the past two years systematically targeting them state by state.

2. The BJP's winning streak since the 2024 national-election scare is striking: Haryana (October 2024, where Congress was heavily favored), Maharashtra home to Mumbai (November 2024), Delhi for the first time in 27 years (February 2026), Bihar, and now West Bengal — ending 15 years of rule by Mamata Banerjee, one of Modi's loudest critics and a state the BJP had never come close to winning before. The turnaround followed a deliberate pivot away from flashy culture-war projects (currency invalidation, revoking Kashmir's statehood, the Ram temple) toward door-to-door retail politics and bread-and-butter economic messaging.

3. Opponents allege the victories weren't all clean. In Delhi, the former chief minister was repeatedly raided and arrested by federal police on charges that never resulted in conviction. In Bihar and West Bengal, voter rolls were "housekept" by an Election Commission whose leader is chosen by Modi — with 9 million names struck from West Bengal's rolls, disproportionately belonging to Muslims. Even so, margins in both states were wide enough that these maneuvers alone don't fully explain the results.

4. Financially, the BJP's dominance is lopsided: estimates put its fundraising at ten times more than all opponents combined last year. No clear successor to Modi exists, the next parliamentary election isn't until 2029 (when he will be 78), and India's regional parties are dwindling rather than coalescing. Jawaharlal Nehru's founding vision — political pluralism to match India's diversity of religion, language, and culture — is being replaced by a BJP project of building an orthodox Hindu nation defined by cultural majority.

**Quotable:**
> "When Narendra Modi first campaigned to lead India more than a decade ago, he vowed not to just defeat the main opposing national party, but to eliminate it entirely. He pretty much succeeded." — *Alex Travelli, NYT India correspondent*

## Technology

### The Briefing: Cerebras To Go Public 📌
**Martin Peers** · email · 5 mins

1. Cerebras Systems, the largest of the new AI chip designer cohort, is expected to IPO on Thursday at a $35 billion valuation — and Wall Street is primed to overlook its cash burn. CoreWeave, an AI cloud firm with a comparable profile (burning billions while ramping data centers), went public a year ago at $40/share and now trades at $114. Bloomberg reported strong IPO demand for Cerebras, and the same AI-exposure premium that lifted CoreWeave is expected to lift Cerebras — despite an objectively crowded chip market.

2. The structural problem for Cerebras is that AI chip buyers already have many alternatives. Nvidia dominates, AMD, Google, and Amazon all offer competing chips, Meta and Microsoft are building their own, and Chinese buyers have Huawei and others. The market is so difficult that other AI chip startups have effectively capitulated: Groq licensed its tech to Nvidia (which hired its top people), and Meta absorbed a key engineering group from Graphcore. Cerebras has secured OpenAI as both customer and shareholder, plus a deal to supply chips to AWS as a supplement to Amazon's Trainium — meaningful validation, but not immunity from the crowding.

3. Elsewhere in the week's outlook, Figma's Thursday earnings are a bellwether for whether enterprise AI spending is sustainable. Figma CFO Praveer Melwani noted in February that 75% of paying customers of any size were consuming AI credits every week, and that new AI features were drawing "an ever-increasing number of credits over time." But software contract negotiation firm UpperEdge's Adam Mansfield warned that businesses are growing anxious about unpredictable AI usage bills — if customers get surprised by costs, they'll pull back, threatening the consumption-based AI revenue model that Figma and every other software firm is betting on.

**Quotable:**
> "We are at a moment of peak AI infrastructure optimism, with shortages of computing capacity apparent constantly." — *Peers, contextualizing why Cerebras' IPO timing may outrun its competitive fundamentals*

## Geopolitics

### The Forecast: Trump and Xi, friends forever? 📌
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 8 mins

1. Trump has called Xi a friend repeatedly and publicly for nearly a decade — most famously declaring in April 2018, as he launched his first-term tariff assault, "President Xi and I will always be friends." Xi, whose public utterances are rarer and more restrained, has called Trump a friend only once on record (2019). At their last meeting in South Korea in October, Xi offered only that he felt "very warm seeing you again" — a significant asymmetry for two leaders about to negotiate the world's highest-stakes bilateral relationship.

2. Trump showed visible frustration with this imbalance in September, posting that Xi should "give my warmest regards to Vladimir Putin, and Kim Jong Un, as you conspire against The United States of America" — after Xi hosted a Beijing military parade for the visiting Russian and North Korean leaders. For a leader who consistently prioritizes personal loyalty over policy consistency, this kind of perceived slight matters more than it would for a conventional statesman.

3. Xi enters the Beijing summit with structural leverage that has grown over the past decade. China demonstrated its economic stranglehold through rare earths last year, and Xi faces no midterm elections or lame-duck constraints. Despite demographic and economic pressures at home, his negotiating position is stronger than it was when he first made the pilgrimage to Mar-a-Lago. The asymmetry in urgency may mirror the asymmetry in affection.

4. The formal agenda for the summit includes major overlapping crises: whether China can help broker a reopening of the Strait of Hormuz amid the US-led war in Iran, whether the US-China trade truce can hold, whether the two powers can find common AI governance ground, and whether Chinese electric vehicles might one day be built on US soil. But the outcome that will shape all subsequent diplomacy is harder to quantify — whether Trump leaves Beijing still believing Xi is his friend.

**Quotable:**
> "President Xi and I will always be friends." — *Trump in April 2018, as he launched his first-term tariff assault on China; a sentiment he has repeated many times since*

## Business, Finance & Markets

### How to build a company that withstands any era | Eric Ries, Lean Startup author 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 4 mins

1. **"Financial gravity" corrupts companies structurally, not morally.** The mechanism isn't founder greed or age — it's governance: boards legally obligated under standard corporate charters to maximize shareholder value, which means accepting the highest offer regardless of harm. Philip Morris acquired Vectura (a respiratory health company) and destroyed it within three years. Standard U.S. charters say companies exist to pursue "any lawful activity," and shareholder primacy has only dominated for ~40 years — it's a recent default, not an eternal law.

2. **80% of venture-backed founders are ousted within three years of IPO — and the window to prevent it closes at each funding stage.** At every step, advisors say "not yet": lawyers want PMF first, Series A VCs say later, growth VCs warn it'll hurt fundraising, bankers at IPO say bundle it post-listing, and by S-1 the CFO says it's too late. One founder ignored the advice, went public successfully, and was fired five months later when the stock dipped and investors reversed course. The structural fix — becoming a Public Benefit Corporation (PBC) — is a two-page Delaware filing that legally defines your actual purpose and blocks forced mission betrayal. Anthropic used this from day one, enabling it to refuse risky model releases and lucrative deals no standard company could turn down. Cloudflare gave away SSL certificates for free even though SSL was its top conversion driver; top-of-funnel grew 10x and the company reached $70B. Principled decisions always lose on short-run ROI spreadsheets, which is exactly why structural protection matters more than any values statement.

**Quotable:**
> "At every stage, they say 'not yet' — which means you have to do it early." — *Eric Ries on why founders are systematically talked out of governance protection until it's too late*

### Wealth taxes are rich in appeal, poor in practice 📎
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 6 mins

1. **The top 1% pay nearly half of New York City's tax revenue — and they can leave.** Guest columnist Nicolas S. Rohatyn notes that the tax base is "increasingly mobile," with high earners having far more flexibility than in the past to shift residency across states or countries. This makes heavy wealth taxation not merely a fairness question but a risk management problem: the more concentrated the revenue base, the more catastrophic the loss when it migrates.

2. **IRS migration data confirms the exodus is already happening.** Between 2019 and 2023, Florida absorbed a net $137 billion in income from other states; California lost $91 billion and New York lost $76 billion. Conor Sen notes that this influx of wealthy migrants is itself a problem — it drove home prices and inflation higher in Florida than in the US overall, redistributing cost-of-living pain rather than eliminating it.

3. **Wealth taxes on assets are nearly impossible to collect and often raise less revenue than they cost.** Allison Schrager argues the core problem is valuation and enforcement: illiquid assets like private businesses or art have no market price until sold, forcing arbitrary assessments and constant legal disputes. The taxes are "self-defeating" precisely because the administrative friction and avoidance incentives erode the base faster than the rate generates receipts. Her sharper observation: the real argument for wealth taxes isn't economic efficiency — it's redistribution of *power*, a goal the tax code is poorly suited to achieve.

4. **California's proposed 5% billionaire tax could legally demand more than 100% of some founders' net worth.** Justin Fox ran the numbers on the California ballot measure's quirk: wealth is calculated using *voting* shares, not economic shares — and tech founders typically hold supervoting stock to retain control while issuing lower-vote Class A shares to the public. DoorDash CEO Tony Xu's effective liability under this formula could reach 122% of his Forbes-estimated $1.6 billion net worth. The dual-class structures causing this absurdity were banned for US public companies from the 1930s until the 1980s, after being blamed for contributing to the 1929 crash.

**Quotable:**
> "Wealth taxes — that is, taxes on assets as opposed to income — are bad economics because they are nearly impossible to collect, to the point where they are self-defeating and can often result in less tax revenue." — *Allison Schrager*

## Law & Litigation

### Bw Reads: The rise of the Texas Two-Step 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 6 mins

1. **Asbestos litigation created a multi-decade, multi-billion-dollar liability crisis for US industry.** Despite British doctors detecting mesothelioma links in the early 1900s, American companies downplayed the causal connection for decades. By the mid-2010s, nearly 3,000 people were diagnosed with mesothelioma each year, personal-injury lawyers were spending tens of millions of dollars annually on advertising to find claimants, and the cumulative damages had reached tens of billions of dollars — ultimately rendering asbestos products uninsurable and effectively forcing them off the market where regulators had failed.

2. **The Texas Two-Step is a legal maneuver, invented roughly a decade ago by a Dallas attorney, designed to cap corporate asbestos liability via a staged bankruptcy.** The steps: reincorporate in Texas (which permits "divisional mergers"), spin off a new subsidiary that absorbs all asbestos liabilities, immediately move that subsidiary to North Carolina, and file for bankruptcy there — because North Carolina courts have shown willingness to let such cases proceed. The parent company (e.g., Johnson & Johnson or Georgia-Pacific) remains legally on the hook, but since the responsible entity is now in bankruptcy, it can seek court-supervised finality over the total damages it will ever pay.

3. **Three simultaneous bankruptcy cases in a Charlotte courthouse will determine whether the Texas Two-Step survives — with enormous stakes for both victims and corporate liability law broadly.** If the tactic is validated, it could fundamentally reshape mass-tort civil litigation and erode consumer protection law built up over decades. Meanwhile, thousands of ill and dying claimants are frozen out of compensation for years by the proceedings. Lori Knapp, whose father died in 2020 after decades of exposure to Georgia-Pacific's asbestos-containing products, says the companies "spend billions of dollars on attorney fees to keep this from being settled."

**Quotable:**
> "They spend billions of dollars on attorney fees to keep this from being settled. They don't care that they have harmed anybody." — *Lori Knapp, whose father died from mesothelioma in 2020 after using Georgia-Pacific products for decades*
