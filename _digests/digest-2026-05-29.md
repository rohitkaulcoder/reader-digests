## Today's Top 3

### An Interview with Eric Seufert About Models and Ads, and AI's Upside for Humanity ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 51 mins

1. Ad platforms have become black boxes where advertisers retain only two levers: creative and objective function. Everything else — targeting, bidding, delivery — sits behind what Seufert calls "an event horizon past which light cannot escape." The only rational response is to stop fighting the ocean and build around that opacity.

2. Seufert's Harvard thesis introduced DeCANT (Deep Creative Attention-based Network for pre-Testing), which treats the ad platform itself as a "teacher model." By observing historical ROAS outcomes conditioned on context (country, language, deployment date), a deep neural network can predict creative performance before spending real dollars — outperforming tree-based baselines like XGBoost in empirical testing.

3. The financial case for pre-testing is large: industry standard allocates roughly 10% of ad budget to creative testing with zero ROAS accountability — pure experimentation absorbed as cost of goods. DeCANT could reclaim a significant fraction of that. Ad platforms may be quietly hostile to this methodology since unaccountable testing budget is effectively free margin for them.

4. Two distinct advertiser audiences benefit differently. Brand marketers use DeCANT to retain creative control and brand consistency across Meta, Google, Reddit, Pinterest, and TikTok — rejecting the "give us your credit card" model. DTC performance marketers use it to "shoot the fire hose": generate unlimited AI-produced variants at near-zero cost and test all of them without blowing the testing budget.

5. Zuckerberg's failure to publicly champion advertising has materially hurt Meta. Sheryl Sandberg appeared on every earnings call naming 10 million advertisers with concrete ROI anecdotes; current COO Javier Olivan is unknown even to industry insiders. Zuckerberg's "just give us your credit card" framing resonates with DTC operators but alienates brand marketers — and no one at Meta is making the affirmative case for advertising as a societal good.

6. ChatGPT's Instant Checkout failed for structural reasons, not interface reasons. Amazon refused to participate and is actively expanding ChatGPT's exclusions in its robots.txt. Walmart reported 3x worse conversion via ChatGPT checkout versus direct traffic. Average order value was lower (single-item purchases vs. cart expansion). Without Amazon's inventory, the product was never viable — and Amazon had every incentive to kill it.

7. In-platform agentic commerce is succeeding precisely where third-party checkout failed. Amazon's Rufus and Walmart's Sparky are working because they're embedded natively: cart consistency is maintained, recommendation expansion still drives AOV, and there's no cross-origin conversion drop from handoff latency. The failure was always about conflicting incentives with the platforms, not about the conversational interface.

8. Auction-based advertising is structurally superior to affiliate fees. Advertisers bid their true value in an auction; the clearing mechanism selects for high-value products adjusted by conversion rate. A flat affiliate percentage systematically selects for low-cost, high-conversion items and captures less total economic value. This single mechanism difference explains why OpenAI's Instant Checkout economics were unworkable.

9. Google executed a successful "Ship of Theseus" transformation of Search over 18 months: AI Overviews displaced link-based results with summaries, then AI Mode introduced conversational interface, and now the full transformation is complete. The constraint was that flipping the switch overnight would have driven users to ChatGPT ("this is disorienting, might as well go elsewhere"). Paced incrementally — building on habits consumers had already formed with chatbots — it worked. Search revenue grew 19% last quarter, queries are up, monetization is at parity.

10. Google transformed Search from a "distribution mechanic" (users click away as fast as possible) into an "engagement sink" (users stay and converse). This unlocks entirely new advertising real estate — the 80% of search queries that were historically non-monetizable can now be surrounded with display-style ads anchored to conversation context rather than keyword intent.

11. OpenAI's initial ads launch was a deliberate embarrassment function. The product was primitive and drew harsh industry criticism. But $100M in annualized ad revenue materialized within roughly six weeks of the US beta, and OpenAI is targeting $1 billion for the year. By being bad publicly, they generated internal organizational support for engineering resources that a polished internal demo never could have.

12. OpenAI has since shipped CAPI, pixel, and self-serve — the technical infrastructure of a real ad platform. The CAPI is currently measurement-only (no conversion-event optimization yet), but Google built this over years; OpenAI has replicated the skeleton in months. The next step, bidding optimized against CAPI-captured events rather than just CPC, is described as "not too far off."

13. By waiting on ads, OpenAI let Google define the chatbot-advertising category on Google's terms. Google's Universal Commerce Protocol won the merchant integration standard (Stripe switched, Shopify aligned), while OpenAI's Instant Checkout approach lost. Now any advertiser comparing chatbot ad placements gets Google's option for free bundled with their entire existing account — compared to a standalone OpenAI integration with no cross-surface amplification.

14. Recommendation systems are undergoing a quiet architectural revolution. The DLRM "two towers" model introduced by YouTube in 2016 (user tower + content tower, dot-product similarity over billion-item embedding tables) is being replaced by generative recommendation — Google's 2023 TIGER paper showed you can compress a billion-item catalog into ~1,024 tokens using RQ-VAE hierarchical codebooks (4 codebooks × 256 classes), mid-train an LLM on that vocabulary, and predict "next item" as a token in a sequence.

15. This generative RecSys shift is live and being discussed at earnings calls. Meta, Kuaishou, and Google are all rebuilding their ad-ranking architectures around LLM-powered next-item prediction. The payoff is "world knowledge" — an LLM trained on internet text brings semantic understanding of products, categories, and context that a dot-product over embedding tables never could. Meta says in earnings calls that LLM-for-ad-ranking is a live component.

16. Meta's GEM (Generative Ads Model) is a foundation model, not a product feature. It's too large for direct inference; Meta's SUM (Scaling User Modeling) paper describes approximately 40 distilled point-solution models deployed across different Facebook and Instagram ranking surfaces. The structure mirrors how any LLM lab operates: one large foundation model distilled into many deployed task-specific variants.

17. Owning the LLM used for RecSys mid-training is existential for Meta. If Meta ran its ad-ranking LLM on Google's Gemini, Google could degrade or pull access. Meta Spark (their frontier model) is competitive but not SOTA — Seufert argues that's fine, because what matters is proprietary control. A 5-6% quarterly improvement in time-spent or conversion rate from LLM-powered RecSys is too valuable to share with competitors who could then distill and replicate it.

18. Model upgrade friction is an underappreciated lock-in mechanism. Changing the LLM powering a workflow isn't just swapping a variable name — it changes the output distribution, requiring new prompt engineering and full re-testing. Flash 3.5 costs more per token and uses more tokens than Flash 3; if Flash 3 produces stable, validated results, there's no clear economic incentive to upgrade. This makes early-adopter model choices stickier than commonly assumed.

19. Apple practices "ad-washing": it publicly positions as privacy-first while earning massive, near-100%-margin ad revenue through its Google search partnership and App Store ad products. Apple structurally cannot build frontier models because doing so would require collecting user data at scale, directly contradicting its privacy brand. The strategy is to continue paying Google (or eventually a model garden of providers) for frontier inference, capturing the distribution value without the data-collection exposure.

20. Amazon has built the most valuable advertising data asset ever assembled: purchase-intent and transaction history covering approximately 90% of US consumers, all signed in with real identity. This is retail data, not engagement proxy signals. Amazon's DSP already deploys this across the open web; the growth strategy is "not more surface area, it's serving more surface area" — filling ad inventory wherever high-intent users appear.

21. The Amazon-OpenAI advertising deal likely gives Amazon the ability to serve product ads in ChatGPT, structured similarly to Amazon's sign-in partnerships with Snap and Meta. OpenAI's existing deals with Criteo and others are bidding arrangements, not traditional RTB; Amazon's identity spine and purchase data would outperform anything OpenAI could build independently for product-intent queries.

22. AppLovin launched Gist, a Pinterest/RedNote hybrid social app available by referral code only. The logic: AppLovin sold its gaming catalog to Tripledot (90-180 day LTV per user), while a sticky social network generates viral acquisition and multi-year retention. The more credible near-term expansion vector is CTV via their Wurl acquisition (a CTV supply-side platform) — AppLovin's algorithmic precision in matching users to advertisers without login data maps well to connected TV's fragmented identity problem.

23. Seufert's "Prosperous Society" thesis: when AI causes an explosion of supply (goods, content, services), the binding constraint shifts from production to distribution. Digital advertising's recommendation systems are the most efficient distribution mechanism ever built. Result: more granular products meet more specific individual needs, matching efficiency reaches maximum, and previously unaddressable SMBs get onboarded to the ad economy via end-to-end automation. All three axes — advertisers, consumers, platforms — capture more value simultaneously.

24. Galbraith's 1958 Affluent Society argued advertising manufactures demand to absorb oversupply — a claim that treats consumers as passive recipients of manipulation. Seufert inverts this: algorithmic ad platforms are indifferent to what you want and find the best match to your actual desires. Human desires are not manufactured; they are infinite, combinatorial, and deeply individual. 83% of retail still happens offline — doubling the online share at maximum matching efficiency would represent economic growth of a different order of magnitude than marginal optimization.

25. The AI pessimism that dominates public discourse (degrowth ideology, the lump-of-labor fallacy, assumptions that AI will create gray uniformity) is the same failure of imagination that produces hostility toward advertising. OpenAI's multi-year resistance to ads, Silicon Valley's contempt for ad-supported models, and Zuckerberg's reluctance to champion Meta's ad platform are all symptoms of a paternalistic worldview that underestimates the diversity and infinitude of human desire. Pro-advertising and pro-AI-optimism are, Seufert argues, correlated precisely because both require believing in the infinite productive capacity of human wants.

**Quotable:**
> "The demand-matching system is totally indifferent to what you want — whatever you want, it'll find you the thing that you want most and that you're going to enjoy the most. There's more things to discover, so they're discovering more granular things, and the matching is happening at the maximum possible efficiency." — *Seufert rebutting Galbraith's claim that advertising manufactures rather than matches demand*

### Money Stuff: The Bitcoin Lost-and-Found ⚡
**Matt Levine** · email · 15 mins

1. An estimated 20% of all Bitcoin is permanently inaccessible because early holders lost private keys — often people who bought drugs with Bitcoin in 2012–2013, received change, forgot about it, and woke up a decade later to discover they'd forgotten the password to wallets worth millions. Technologies like AI-assisted password cracking (a guy recently used Claude to recover a password lost for 11 years) and, theoretically, quantum computing offer partial solutions, but the more basic option is law: a court order declaring legal ownership of inaccessible Bitcoins doesn't give you the cryptographic keys to spend them, but it does give you something you can tell people about.

2. James Howells, the archetypal Bitcoin tragicomedy figure, has 8,000 BTC (currently worth hundreds of millions) on a hard drive buried in a Welsh landfill. He cannot dig it up; authorities won't allow it. His form of "ownership" is entirely reputational — a decade of consistent, public, credible insistence that the coins are his. He was planning to tokenize this reputational ownership into something called Ceiniog Coin, which trades at near-zero, but the concept illustrates that legal/social proof of ownership has some non-zero value even without cryptographic proof.

3. A New York man identified only as "Noah Doe" has filed *ABC Co. v. John Does 1–39,069* in New York Supreme Court, claiming legal ownership of 39,069 abandoned Bitcoin wallets under Article 7-B of New York Personal Property Law — the state's "lost and found" statute. Between December 2024 and April 2025, he ran a self-developed algorithm to identify wallets dormant for 5+ years, self-custodied, and unresponsive through multiple price-appreciation events. He physically carried USB drives containing the wallet addresses to the NYPD's 17th Precinct, received official property receipts, waited out the statutory notice period, and filed suit after 424 wallets demonstrated on-chain activity (and were removed from the claim) while the remaining 39,069 did nothing.

4. The legal theory is genuinely novel and its viability is uncertain. A Bitcoin wallet's "public key" is just a string of numbers anyone could copy; handing a USB drive of those strings to the NYPD is arguably delivering a trivially reproducible *reference* to property rather than the property itself. Even if the court grants the declaration, Noah Doe still won't possess a single private key — he'd have legal ownership of wallets he cannot spend. What he could do: truthfully claim to own many millions of dollars in Bitcoin, potentially borrow against it (though the "double-spending" problem looms — someone else might still hold the private keys and move the coins).

5. Paramount is financing its $50 billion acquisition of Warner Bros. with roughly $50 billion in debt, an alarming leverage load in a turbulent media environment. CEO David Ellison privately promised S&P and other ratings agencies that the controlling Ellison family would backstop deleveraging efforts. S&P insisted on public disclosure, and Paramount published a commitment in an SEC filing: net debt/EBITDA below 3.75x by fiscal 2028, below 3.0x by 2029. Without that family backstop pledge, S&P would have cut the post-merger rating by two notches; with it, only one notch, landing at BB.

6. An oral promise to a ratings agency in a confidential meeting is nearly unenforceable — the agency can't easily sue for fraud, and the lenders can't cite a conversation they weren't present for. Moving that commitment into a public SEC filing transforms it into a quasi-covenant: not a formal default trigger, but potential securities fraud liability if Paramount grossly misses the target without a good explanation. "Everything is securities fraud" is a useful commitment device — making a deleverage promise in a filing credibly binds management because they know investors will sue if the numbers are fabricated.

7. Google software engineer Michele Spagnuolo (a/k/a "AlphaRaccoon") was charged in federal court in New York with insider trading on Polymarket. He had access to internal Google data tracking search volume and used it to bet that the singer D4vd would be 2025's most-searched person — a bet Polymarket priced at near-zero probability against Pope Leo XIV and Kendrick Lamar. When Google publicly confirmed D4vd topped the list in December, Spagnuolo allegedly collected approximately $1.2 million. The case arrives less than nine months after Bill Ackman publicly told Eric Adams "there is no insider trading on Polymarket" — meaning, accurately, no rules against it, but incorrectly implying no legal risk.

8. We are currently in what might be called the golden age of prediction-market insider trading detection: many people genuinely believed Polymarket was a regulatory-free zone, traded carelessly on material non-public information, and are now discovering that U.S. securities fraud law applies regardless of the venue. Going forward, sophisticated actors will either avoid it, cover their tracks, or trade from non-extradition jurisdictions — but for now, the cases are easy because the defendants made no effort to conceal.

9. Bank of New York Mellon CEO Robin Vince tripled the bank's intern and analyst class sizes since 2022, explicitly to harness younger workers' native fluency with AI. This bets on the contrarian thesis: rather than AI enabling banks to shrink junior headcount, AI-fluent 22-year-olds should be hired *more* to automate the workflows of senior employees who are reluctant to change. The counterargument is twofold — senior partners still control hiring decisions and have little incentive to import their own replacements, and entry-level tasks (spreadsheets, presentations) are exactly what AI handles best, leaving senior judgment work intact.

**Quotable:**
> "Being constantly in the news for a decade for owning that stash of 8,000 Bitcoins is also a *kind* of proof of ownership. Like, I could go around saying 'actually those 8,000 Bitcoins are mine,' and Howells has no more cryptographic proof of ownership than I do, but you wouldn't believe me, would you?" — *on James Howells, whose hard drive with Bitcoin private keys is buried in a Welsh landfill*

### All about Infrastructure as Code and Terraform ⚡
**Technically** · email · 12 mins

1. Cloud infrastructure is infinitely configurable — AWS alone exposes databases, servers, load balancers, file storage, search indexes, and more through menus with dozens of knobs each. A real Series C company cited in the piece makes ~5 infrastructure changes per week across 8 engineering teams, with one dedicated infra engineer per team — the scale that makes manual, click-driven management untenable.

2. Click-driven infrastructure management produces four specific failure modes: collisions (two engineers change shared config without knowing it), quality gaps (no review process before changes hit production), cost drift (unused resources accumulate — e.g., a forgotten Google Cloud API server billed at $0.53/month), and unrecoverable disasters (no version history means no rollback path when things go wrong).

3. Infrastructure as Code reframes physical servers and databases as text files that can be tracked in Git. The key insight: infrastructure governance needs were identical to software governance needs, and engineers had already solved those with version control and pull requests — they just hadn't applied the same solution to the layer below.

4. Terraform, created by HashiCorp in 2014, dominates modern IaC because it is cloud-agnostic — one declarative language (HCL) works across AWS, GCP, and Azure via provider plugins. You declare end state (`engine = "postgres"`, `engine_version = "15.4"`), run `terraform apply`, and Terraform diffs current reality against your spec and reconciles the two. Bumping a version is a one-line file change.

5. Terraform's architecture has three distinct layers: the core framework (reads `.tf` files, computes a diff plan, orchestrates changes), provider plugins (AWS/Google/Azure-maintained packages that translate Terraform's plan into actual API calls), and the `.tf` code itself (authored by engineers, reviewed via pull requests, applied via CI/CD GitHub Actions). This mirrors the full software development lifecycle.

6. The IaC approach is expanding beyond servers to govern anything API-driven: data warehouse table definitions (previously created ad hoc in SQL with no audit trail) and permissions (notoriously ungoverned — engineers accumulate access, rarely surrender it, and admins have no clean picture of who has access to what). Any service that exposes an API can ship a Terraform provider.

7. The two real downsides of Terraform: centralization kills local velocity (a data engineer who used to write `CREATE TABLE` in SQL now waits for an infra PR), and everyone must learn HCL. Pulumi (a Series C startup) is attacking the learning curve by letting engineers write infra specs in Python, Go, TypeScript, etc., but hasn't yet matched Terraform's ecosystem scale.

8. AI coding tools are inverting the historic bottleneck. A search feature that once took weeks for application code and weeks for infra to run in parallel now completes app code in days — making platform/infra the new constraint. Three plausible responses: hire more platform engineers (default), let AI write Terraform with guardrails, or collapse the role boundary so application engineers own their own `.tf` files while platform teams shift to a strategic/enabling function.

**Quotable:**
> "DevOps is a sharp blade." — *hiring manager's warning about infrastructure work, where success is invisible and errors are catastrophic and public*

## Economics & Labor

### The Coasian Margin Frontier 📌
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. The most strategically interesting quadrant of AI's economic impact is high-growth/shrinking-margin: companies that deliberately compress their own margins by spending on inference at every opportunity with a positive ROI. The other three quadrants are less novel — mature firms using AI to cut costs (low-growth/high-margin), businesses disrupted outright (worse on both axes), and the rare n-of-1 company that achieves both high growth and high margins by doing something nobody else can copy.

2. The old growth playbook reliably expanded margins because expansion reused existing infrastructure. Early Google indexed everything and was multilingual by default, so international expansion cost little more than registering google.fr and translating a few buttons ("J'ai de la chance!"). Nike double-dipped on globalization by shifting manufacturing from Japan to progressively cheaper markets while simultaneously selling to the emerging middle classes in those same countries. Donald Keough, Coca-Cola's CEO in the early 90s, summarized the logic about Indonesia — 180 million people, median age 18, alcohol-banned — as "I feel I know what heaven looks like."

3. AI inverts this margin dynamic: instead of spreading fixed costs over more revenue, companies will now burn variable compute on every micro-opportunity that clears the hurdle rate. Subtitling a movie into any language costs under $1; dubbing it with GPT-Realtime-2 costs ~$12. If offering *2 Fast 2 Furious* in Esperanto retains even one Netflix subscriber for one extra month, it's worth doing — but offering it is strictly worse for margins than serving the original English. Every personalized ad, every e-commerce chatbot, every targeted product listing is cheaper in its static form; inference-powered versions improve absolute profit while compressing the margin percentage.

4. The Coase connection explains why this is structurally new. Ronald Coase argued firms exist because acquiring new information carries transaction costs — it's cheaper to employ people full-time than to source each micro-task externally. AI drops the marginal cost of acquiring and acting on new information close to zero, so the same economic logic that historically built large, integrated firms now drives companies to constantly seek opportunities to spend a few tokens for a fractionally positive return. This saturates every activity that is "potentially worth doing but not worth the overhead of arranging for someone to do it" — and the more fractally complex the economy, the more such spaces exist.

5. Several "Elsewhere" items extend the thesis. FTSE Russell joined other indices pre-committing to fast inclusion of SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic post-IPO — a rational move since indices that implicitly go underweight a company they'll eventually have to own are running an accidental short. Meta is deploying engineers and PMs to outside companies (FDEs) to drive adoption of its AI tools, compressing its own margins on headcount-linear work in order to improve net dollar retention on usage-based model revenue — AI optimists everywhere are racing to reduce margins faster than token depreciation can organically restore them. Meanwhile, a Google employee was charged with insider trading on prediction markets; the system is self-policing because first-time insiders are less sophisticated than the professional market-makers they trade against, but the equilibrium is temporary as insiders grow more paranoid.

**Quotable:**
> "The same general tendency that creates big companies in an information-scarce world tends, in an information-rich one, to create companies that are constantly seeking opportunities to burn more tokens for an even slightly positive ROI." — *on the Coasian inversion at the heart of AI economics*

### Free Lunch: A tale of two EU-US comparisons 📎
**Martin Sandbu** · email · 8 mins

1. The EU's productivity lag relative to the US is conventional wisdom — foregrounded in Mario Draghi's landmark 2024 report, which concluded "EU economic growth has been persistently slower than in the US over the past two decades." But Seth Ackerman (editor-at-large, Jacobin) showed in February 2026 that this conclusion depends entirely on your measurement choice: in constant 2021 prices, western Europe has gradually declined relative to the US; but in "current" purchasing-power-parity terms — comparing GDPs using the PPP exchange rate of each respective year — the two blocs have kept roughly level for decades. The same split appears for EU GDP per capita: constant-price measures show a stuck gap, while current-PPP shows the EU catching up from 60% of the US level at the turn of the century to 74% today.

2. Nobel laureate Paul Krugman sided with Ackerman: when an economy like the US rapidly expands production of tech and software, those goods become cheaper, so the rest of the world — including Europe — can buy more of them at lower cost, boosting European real incomes and PPP-measured GDP. Luis Garicano (LSE professor, former MEP) pushed back: the constant-price measures accurately capture that the US has simply produced far more software and tech than before, regardless of price declines, and that the current-PPP approach masks this genuine expansion in American productive capacity. Both are making valid but incommensurable claims — Krugman is measuring relative prosperity (how much Europeans can afford), Garicano is measuring relative productive capacity (how much the economy can make).

3. Sandbu's own synthesis: even if Krugman is right that Europeans are as prosperous relative to Americans as ever, Garicano is right that the US's faster expansion of leading-sector volumes matters — it affects the durability of Europe's prosperity and raises the question of why the continent hasn't matched American tech output. Conversely, when Garicano asserts "the gap in material wealth has shifted dramatically in America's favor," he ignores what PPP correctly measures: how much Europeans can actually afford relative to Americans, which has barely changed. The contradiction arises from the fundamental incommensurability of what goes into GDP — computer chips, Nordic films, Parma ham, large language models, luxury bags — which forces a choice of price indices that yields multiple valid answers.

4. The deeper methodological trap is illustrated by the Cambridge capital controversy: British Cambridge economists (Robinson et al.) argued it was incoherent to model the economy with a homogeneous mass of "capital" because capital takes incommensurable forms; American Cambridge (MIT) did it anyway using dollar values and built tractable models. "The British were right but the Americans won." The same applies here — there is no single correct way to compare GDP growth across countries and time simultaneously. The consistency test exposes this: if you accept the constant-price story that Europe has been falling behind, you must also accept that 30 years ago the EU's aggregate GDP was almost 30% larger than the US's, and that EU GDP per capita (including the post-communist east, then in economic collapse) stood at the same ratio to the US as today. If that claim sounds implausible, the same price index that generates it also generates the "falling behind" story.

**Quotable:**
> "His report was, as someone in the know expressed to me, commissioned to find a 'burning platform' that would scare political leaders into action. The scare succeeded. The action, not so much yet." — *on the Draghi 2024 competitiveness report*

### A $42 billion AI bonus for workers 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 6 mins

1. Samsung Electronics' chip unit unions approved a 10-year deal on May 20, 2026, giving 78,000 workers 12% of the company's annual operating profit — worth $26 billion this year, paid mostly in shares. Based on Bloomberg calculations, the 2026 bonus averages ~$340,000 per employee, nearly three times the average 2025 salary. Rival SK Hynix struck a similar deal yielding ~$476,000 per worker this year. The combined total across both companies approaches $42 billion going to rank-and-file manufacturing staff.

2. The union's leverage came from AI's physical dependency: the technology runs on advanced memory chips that only a handful of factories on earth can produce, and South Korea's semiconductor workers — engineers and technicians in punishing high-precision environments — are indispensable to that supply chain. Fibonacci Asset Management CEO Jung In Yun explains the dynamic plainly: Samsung wanted operational stability during a semiconductor upcycle, and avoiding a strike disruption was worth more to management than winning the wage fight.

3. Samsung and SK Hynix's market caps both crossed $1 trillion this month, driven by insatiable demand for high-bandwidth memory powering Nvidia's AI accelerators — joining Nvidia, Alphabet, and Apple in that club. This chip supercycle created the profit pool that made the bonus possible: 12% of Samsung's operating profit this year alone exceeds what most workers earn in a lifetime.

4. The gains are structurally unequal within South Korea. Korea's biggest chaebol channel more of their profits to direct employees than Silicon Valley's winner-take-all model does, but workers at Samsung and Hynix suppliers and subcontractors get lower pay and less job security. KB Securities managing director Peter Kim notes that the Samsung deal "is not representative of Korea's working class" — the windfall is concentrated at the top tier of the industrial pyramid.

5. The bonanza faces two hard limits. First, the semiconductor industry is cyclical — if AI spending cools or new chip fabrication capacity comes online (the US, Saudi Arabia, and others are pouring billions into exactly that), the profit pool shrinks fast. Second, Samsung and its rivals are continuously automating, and as Yun puts it: "The long-term direction is still toward automation and leaner operations." Workers captured an extraordinary moment of leverage, but there is no guarantee it recurs.

**Quotable:**
> "The union succeeded largely because timing worked in its favor. Samsung wanted operational stability during an important semiconductor upcycle, and avoiding disruption was likely worth more to management than resisting wage demands aggressively." — *Jung In Yun, CEO of Fibonacci Asset Management Global*

### India Brief Q&A: Winning quick commerce 📌
**Veena Venugopal** · email · 11 mins

1. Blinkit holds ~50% of India's quick commerce market with roughly Rs180bn in quarterly net order value, and is profitable at that scale — while every other player combined burns $2bn to generate only $5-6bn in NOV. Zomato acquired Blinkit in 2022 for $560mn, and Goyal says he has no interest in matching competitors' discounts or zero-delivery-fee tactics. Being profitable while rivals burn is the structural moat.

2. Rather than expanding geographically, Blinkit is doubling down on wallet share in existing cities by massively expanding assortment. In Delhi NCR, the product catalogue grew from 35,000-40,000 items a year ago to 200,000 items now — a 5x increase — which Goyal believes creates a "moat" against Amazon, whose logistics are still catching up on service quality even if its selection is broad.

3. Amazon's quick commerce entry (Amazon Now) is a structural problem for Amazon, not just a competition problem for Blinkit. Amazon's original model works by starting with massive assortment and compressing delivery time as volumes rise — quick commerce inverts this entirely, starting with 10-minute delivery and adding SKUs over time. Goyal notes Amazon tried food delivery in India and shut it down, and expects the same difficulty in quick commerce because it requires an operationally alien playbook.

4. Zomato's food delivery business is recovering — currently at 20% annual growth — after a post-Covid base hangover four to five quarters ago. Goyal expects growth to re-accelerate to 30%, citing the macro: Indians in the addressable top ~100 million order only 4.5-5 meals per month from platforms, compared to significantly higher frequencies in China, Singapore, the US, and Europe. The gap is a growth runway, not a ceiling.

5. India has produced strong consumer internet companies but almost no deep-tech companies, and Goyal attributes the gap to a missing talent spark. Zomato, Flipkart and peers had to build India's software engineering talent pool from scratch — the engineers trained at those companies diffused across the industry. Deep tech has had no equivalent foundational success story, and without one big win to train and scatter deep-tech engineers, the ecosystem can't compound.

6. Goyal has launched two post-Zomato ventures: Temple, a wearable health tracker placed on the temple (which he calls a physiological "gold mine" that no wrist or finger device can match), and Lat Aerospace, a full-stack aviation play targeting 50-meter landing strips and 300km trips in 40 minutes using pilotless planes on infrastructure entirely owned by the company. He describes Lat as "99.9% impossible" and says they've completed roughly 3 of hundreds of required milestones — he left public-company constraints precisely to pursue bets this size.

**Quotable:**
> "If it comes to outspending Amazon, we can. But let's leave that aside, nobody wants to go down that path." — *Goyal on Amazon's quick commerce push, signalling confidence without a price-war posture*
