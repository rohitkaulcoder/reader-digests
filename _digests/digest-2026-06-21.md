## Today's Top 3

### "I have never felt very comfortable with the stance that writing, as an undertaking, is both very difficult and em…" ⚡
**The Substack Post** · email · 11 mins

This is Substack's weekly "Weekender" digest — a curated roundup of five distinct excerpts from different writers on the platform. Each piece stands alone.

1. **Writing is not torture — Monica Heisey, "WORK: it's supposed to be fun."** The widespread writer's-life-as-suffering pose is false and mildly harmful: Heisey loved writing from the start, yet early in her career was made to feel she was "doing it wrong" by peers who performed anguish on panels and Twitter. Ali Smith — prolific, objectively celebrated — publicly called herself "immensely lazy" at the Hay Festival, beer in hand, saying she does "basically nothing until she has to" and treats staring into space as part of the creative process. That's the model worth emulating.

2. **Heisey's practical tip: outline intensely, then write the fun parts first.** TV-room discipline taught her to front-load the hard cognitive work (figuring out *what* to say) in an outline, so that the actual writing session is purely the pleasurable *how*. Once outlined, she writes whatever scene seems most fun given that day's mood, weather, and available time — the duller connective tissue gets written last, after she's accumulated enough scenes she's proud of to feel motivated.

3. **A vocal polyp, speech therapy, and the surprising peace of forced quiet — Brendon Holder, "Small Talk."** Holder lost his voice after Lady Gaga's "Mayhem Ball" at Madison Square Garden (where he sang along loudly), then four consecutive long-haul flights — 36 hours to LA, then Tokyo to film a documentary — in altitude-dehydrated air. An ENT camera confirmed a right-vocal-cord polyp; the prescription was severe vocal rest, weekly Manhattan speech therapy (practicing "vvvv," "mmm," blowing bubbles through straws), no voice notes, no fried food, no soda. Forced to speak softly and never compete with background noise, he found something "refined, even sensual" about low-volume, one-on-one conversation in a city as loud as New York.

4. **The Animorphs covers became internet memes by accident — David Mattingly, "I Made the Animorphs Covers."** Mattingly has painted more than 2,000 covers in 50+ years, for Baen, Bantam, Scholastic, Tor, and seven years as a Disney matte artist. Around 2012, a Tumblr user photoshopped one of his Animorphs covers to show rapper Pitbull morphing into a pit bull; it was reblogged tens of thousands of times in a week, spawning Adele-into-a-Dell, and has never stopped. The format survived because it maps perfectly onto the cover template (person → transformation → animal) and the pun structure is so replicable that anyone can recreate it — which Mattingly concedes is "a form of success" he never planned for.

5. **Olivia Rodrigo is succeeding where earlier teen idols were broken — Eliza McLamb, "The teen idol survives."** The teen-idol industrial complex historically demanded a child maintain a commercially viable "personal brand" while going through puberty publicly; the industry and fanbase treat the famous child as something to be consumed and destroyed. Rodrigo stands apart by having clear artistic references and citing them openly, prioritizing live song debuts over algorithmic single-waterfall releases, collaborating across mediums, and sustaining a coherent visual language rather than chasing one-off viral moments — treating pop stardom as a craft, not a content strategy.

6. **Changing your mind is a form of intellectual growth, not inconsistency — Ayan Artan, "things i've changed my mind about."** A short list including: debut albums deserve no harshness ("how odd to expect anything remotely complete from an artist just beginning to give themselves permission to create"); art doesn't save lives — "people save themselves, all we do is reflect our audience back at themselves"; and revisiting old work is valuable because "you are not the same person you were a year ago" and every film, book, or album contains thousands of possible versions.

**Quotable:**
> "when people say things like 'writing is torture,' i often think, if you really feel this way, why not do something else?" — *Monica Heisey, on the performative suffering pose common among writers*

### Every Moat Becomes Moot ⚡
**Kyle Harrison from Investing 101** · email · 8 mins

1. **Moats have a shelf-life, not a fixed wall — and the data confirms it.** Amazon's e-book market share collapsed from 90% in 2010 to under 60% by 2012, just two years after dominating the category. Permanent Equity research backs this up structurally: averaged across industries, a business in its 25th year has roughly the same probability of dying as it did in its 10th year. A "moat" that offers no improvement in survival odds after 15 additional years of compounding is better described as a temporary condition than a durable defense.

2. **Thin margins are the counterintuitive moat — they repel competitors who can't afford to be seen running them.** Bezos argued explicitly that "high margins justified rivals' investments in R&D and attracted more competition, while low margins attracted customers and were more defensible." IBM, Microsoft, and Google could have followed Amazon into low-margin retail or cloud; they structurally wouldn't, because doing so would crater the blended margins their own shareholders were paying for. When Scott Devitt at Stifel Nicolaus upgraded Amazon to a buy in 2007 despite deeply negative margins, he was "laughed out of portfolio managers' offices" — people called it "some kind of nonprofit scam." That reaction is exactly what a moat looks like from the outside while it's being dug.

3. **A moat built as an engine must be actively maintained — eBay's collapse proves what happens when it isn't.** In the 1990s, Amazon threw significant talent and capital at online auctions and lost; eBay's network effects were a genuine moat at that moment. But eBay left its engine running without maintenance: consumer preferences shifted from 7-day auction formats to immediate purchases, and while "Amazon had battled and mastered chaos, eBay was engulfed by it." The moat didn't get breached — it rotted from within because the company stopped digging.

4. **Amazon turned its cost structure into a product line, and that forced a relentlessly expanding competitive surface.** Since 2005, Amazon has productized every major cost line — fulfillment became FBA, infrastructure became AWS, advertising became a standalone business — and is now competing against Walmart in e-commerce, Google and Microsoft in cloud, Netflix and Disney in streaming, UPS and FedEx in logistics, and Costco in grocery. OpenAI made the opposite mistake: expanding simultaneously across language, image, and video models while pursuing Jony Ive consumer hardware and a trillion-dollar compute raise, none of it funded by an existing engine. Anthropic, by contrast, stayed within its core competency around the intelligence value chain and expanded logically into adjacent verticals like cybersecurity and legal — engine-first, then scope.

5. **The only durable moat is the capacity to build the next moat faster than the current one decays.** Every moat — patents, network effects, switching costs, brand — is an asset that competitors can erode, replicate, or simply wait out. What can't be easily copied is the organizational muscle to repeatedly generate new defensibility: Bezos spent two decades building "an organization that can operate lean enough to not need margin," and Anthropic's compute efficiency advantage came from forced constraint — being more compute-constrained than OpenAI from the start forced them to get better at converting kWh into FLOP into output tokens. That engine is the real moat; the margin structure or market share position is just evidence it was built.

**Quotable:**
> "Amazon had battled and mastered chaos; eBay was engulfed by it." — *Brad Stone's The Everything Store, cited as the definitive illustration of what happens when a moat-as-engine stops being maintained*

### Longreads + Open Thread ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. Ted Chiang wrote a piece arguing against AI consciousness, and philosopher Henry Shevlin rebutted it. Fiction lets authors sidestep hard problems by simply not writing about them — but as models scale, dismissing machine consciousness on substrate grounds ("silicon can't do what carbon does") becomes harder to defend, since all silicon-based intelligence so far is the product of carbon-based intelligence. We're on a path to either finding the hard limits of AI or discovering there aren't any.

2. MIT economist Morris Adelman's *Genie out of the Bottle* (covering world oil 1970–1995) made a lonely contrarian claim: oil is a normal commodity, not a geopolitical special case. The conventional story blamed American oil monopolies for artificially low prices that made consumers vulnerable to OPEC; Adelman said it was backwards — pre-OPEC oil was *overpriced* because U.S. domestic producers had high costs but also lots of votes and lobbying power, forcing prices above Aramco's much cheaper equilibrium cost.

3. Adelman's analysis of OPEC cuts is equally heterodox: some of the apparent price spike was buyers stockpiling oil as supply insurance and paying temporarily elevated prices to preserve supplier relationships — not evidence that oil had been chronically underpriced. A cartel that withholds supply to raise prices is simultaneously inviting non-members to fill the gap and risking internal defection. Short-run prices are manipulable; decade-scale prices converge to supply cost.

4. Adelman died in June 2014, the same month oil production was 60% higher than a decade earlier, driven by fracking — a vindication of his thesis that supply responds to price over any meaningful time horizon. The lesson generalizes: there are many ways to spike oil prices tomorrow, and almost no way to durably alter what oil will be worth in ten years.

5. The medieval church's slow acceptance of interest (usury) tracks underlying economic conditions more than theology. Pope Leo X — who was a member of the Medici banking family — issued a papal bull allowing interest in some cases. Broader permissiveness tracked the post-bubonic-plague labor scarcity (which raised the return on capital), technological progress, and more stable property rights; banning usury made sense when illiterate peasants couldn't evaluate loan terms, and faded when markets could clear non-pathologically.

6. Wealth inequality statistics systematically overstate the persistence of the rich because they measure the *mean* net worth of current rich people (which advances) rather than the *median* net worth of people who *were* rich a decade ago (which reveals surprising rates of failure). The missing variable is volatility drag: concentrated portfolios have high arithmetic-average returns but poor geometric (compounded) returns, and tend to zero over time.

7. Catastrophe bonds are a larger and older market than prediction markets, but serve a structurally different function: insurers hedge tail natural-disaster risk to bond buyers who want uncorrelated returns (natural disasters have weak macro correlations). Cat bonds are hedging instruments; prediction markets are closer to gambling/price-discovery. As prediction markets grow, arbitrage between Kalshi hurricane contracts and cat bond positions becomes conceivable, but for now neither side of the cat bond market needs what prediction markets offer.

**Quotable:**
> "There are a lot of ways that one country or one bad actor can make oil prices higher tomorrow, and very few ways that anyone can do anything that affects what oil will be worth in a decade." — *on Morris Adelman's vindicated thesis about oil as a normal commodity*

## Business & Startups

### 🧠 Community Wisdom: Fractional CPO compensation, free e-signature tools, why some users pay but never use your pr… 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 15 mins

1. **Bootstrapped pre-seed startups offering equity-only to fractional CPOs is a bad deal for the fractional.** Experienced operators like Tamara Ceman (18 years in-house, 2 years fractional) and Julie Kopp refuse equity-only arrangements because the odds against a bootstrapped startup raising capital make the expected value negative. The only viable fractional models are: stack multiple cash-paying clients to offset equity-only bets, or be independently wealthy from prior exits — neither of which fits someone transitioning from an enterprise career.

2. **Pre-seed startups don't actually need a fractional CPO — they need an advisor.** Julie Kopp's framing: at the pre-seed stage, the founder *is* the CPO. What they need is guidance on focus, prioritization, and reaching PMF, which is fundamentally an advisory role that can be compensated with a small fee and bounded time commitment, not a senior exec title with equity-only pay. Conflating the two roles lets founders extract executive-level work at advisor-level cost.

3. **Serious bootstrapped founders signal commitment by finding some way to pay cash.** Tamara Ceman's pattern: truly serious founders either have alternative financing or take a loan so they can pay even a modest advisory fee, while first-time founders asking for equity-only are often following a YC-style template and can't clearly articulate what they need. The equity-only ask is thus a useful filter — it often predicts both underfunding and unclear scope.

4. **For freelance e-signatures, free tools already cover most use cases — DocuSign's paid tier is rarely necessary.** Community recommendations converged on PandaDoc (free tier, documents + signatures), Agree.com (free, includes payment collection), and Adobe's built-in signature tool (sufficient for simple cases). Google Workspace also includes e-signature for paid subscribers. Matthew Stublefield's working setup: PandaDoc for contracts he sends, Novo for invoicing — keeping tools separate reduces cost without adding friction, since half his clients prefer their own contracts anyway.

5. **Paying but never using a product is primarily a habit formation failure, not a billing trick.** Bal Sieber's diagnosis: "Paying is a one-time bet on who you hope to become; using is a behavior that has to survive a normal week." Users churn silently when the product doesn't deliver a first real win fast enough, or when nothing in their existing weekly routine pulls them back. B2C-for-work products are hardest because no manager enforces usage — the product must earn the habit entirely on its own.

6. **Subscriptions also persist through inertia — cancellation is its own friction.** Aaron Nichols adds a second mechanism: customers forget they subscribed, and products that don't send pre-billing reminders exploit this by design. Some states are beginning to legislate against silent auto-renewal, implying this passive revenue source is material enough to regulate. The diagnostic question for PMs: look at the gap between payment date and first real value delivery, then whether any recurring trigger brings users back.

7. **Referral programs need legal terms from day one — fraud scales with virality.** Akshay Sharma's rule: every referral program faces fraud, and the more viral it goes, the higher the incidence. Foreseeable exploits — same device/IP multiple sign-ups, self-referrals, household referrals, temp emails, duplicate accounts — should be explicitly addressed in terms before launch, not patched reactively. With ~500k MAU, Josh Herzig-Marx offers a contrarian threshold: if viral upside isn't a real risk yet, the cost of full legal review may exceed the fraud exposure.

8. **Sharing Claude Code context across a team requires governance infrastructure that simple file sync can't provide.** Lindsay Hunt's org-wide solution: a shared GitHub repo for skills, templates, and context files, with a PR-review process before anything gets merged — preventing "file silos" and unreviewed changes from polluting shared context. Aaron Nichols identifies the core problem with shared folder approaches: you can't know who changed what, why, whether context is still trustworthy, or lock critical files that took significant effort to calibrate. For non-technical teams (execs, program managers), GitHub is a barrier — Google Drive sync or custom context systems like FAVA Trails (fava-trails.org) offer lighter governance with memory lifecycle hooks.

**Quotable:**
> "Paying is a one-time bet on who you hope to become; using is a behavior that has to survive a normal week." — *Bal Sieber, on why subscribers never open your product*

## Culture & Society

### The Morning: Game change 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 7 mins

1. The New York Knicks won the NBA championship for the first time in 53 years, and the more surprising side effect is that it cracked open New York City's signature social armor. Strangers — people who would normally perform what sociologist Erving Goffman called "civil inattention" (a respectful glance that never engages) — are now stopping each other in delis and elevators, bonding over a shared blue-and-orange hat.

2. This temporary collapse of social distance has a name: anthropologist Victor Turner called it "communitas," an ecstatic sense of kinship that emerges when society's usual structure is suspended. Turner warned against trying to institutionalize it — he believed resuming normal life with its customs and boundaries is essential, and that people "return to structure revitalized by their experience of communitas."

3. The Knicks fever functions as a laboratory for what ordinary human connection could look like — not a permanent state, but a proof of concept. NYT columnist Matt Flegenheimer called it "good, gleeful, uncomplicated." The argument: if people can spend 10 minutes dissecting plays with a stranger in an OG Anunoby jersey at a street-corner watch party, the memory of that openness can carry forward even after the city retreats into its default isolation.

**Quotable:**
> "You could stand in silence while waiting for the elevator, or you could ask the person next to you if he saw the game." — *Melissa Kirsch, on the small choices that the Knicks championship made newly possible*

**FT Weekend** · email · 4 mins

Edinburgh, Oxford, York, and London's King's Cross (home to the Platform 9¾ trolley photo spot) are all now overrun with what Jo Ellison calls "Pottourists" — visitors whose sole purpose is to consume a children's fiction franchise layered over real places of cultural significance. ## Science & Health

### The hidden link: Your gut microbiome, depression, and processed food 📌
**The Big Think Interview** · email · 5 mins

1. The gut microbiome functions as a hidden "virtual organ" containing trillions of bacteria, fungi, viruses, and parasites — collectively carrying 200 times more genes than all human cells combined. That genetic depth makes microbes far more chemically versatile than our own cells: they convert ingested food into hundreds of signaling chemicals that regulate mood, immune response, and brain development. Without a healthy microbial colony, brains don't develop normally — a finding that links disrupted microbiomes to depression, dementia, and chronic inflammation.

2. Diversity is the single most predictive measure of gut health, and colonization is largely non-genetic. Two random people share only about 20% of their gut microbes; even identical twins share little more than that. The mechanism matters: a highly diverse colony leaves no nutritional waste, starving out harmful bacteria; a depleted colony leaves leftovers that pathogenic species exploit. One bacterium in Spector's own research eats almost exclusively coffee — sitting dormant for years until that first cup — illustrating how specific and layered the ecological niches are.

3. The Western diet has spent 50 years systematically degrading microbiome diversity, and the corrective isn't supplements or fad diets — both consistently fail long-term. Spector's evidence-based prescriptions: eat 30 different plant foods per week (variety matters more than quantity of any single one), avoid ultra-processed foods, eat a color-diverse diet, and fast overnight. Oral health is also a proxy for systemic inflammation — gum disease signals the same underlying dysbiosis linked to dementia risk.

**Quotable:**
> "There is one bug that only really eats or drinks coffee. It is so fussy, it hangs around maybe for years just waiting for that first cup of coffee before it will replicate." — *Tim Spector, on the extreme dietary specificity of individual gut microbes*

## Technology & Innovation

### Midjourney's Pivot to Medical Scanners 📌
**Contrary Research** · email · 5 mins

1. Midjourney launched a new division called Midjourney Medical, unveiling a whole-body ultrasound scanner it claims will be "in many ways superior to even MRI machines." The device lowers patients into a ring of underwater transducers that reconstruct a full 3D body map in as little as 60 seconds — roughly 100x faster than current MRI — with no radiation or magnetic fields. The company plans a San Francisco spa with 10 scanners by end of 2027 and targets 50,000 scanners worldwide delivering 1 billion scans per month by 2031.

2. The medical case against the scanner is substantial: ultrasound physically cannot penetrate bone or air, which rules out imaging the brain, lungs, and bowels — precisely the organs MRI and CT are most needed for. Psychiatrist Scott Alexander consulted radiologists who were "uniformly critical," with one noting the entire premise rests on an unproven assumption that detecting incidental abnormalities in asymptomatic people improves outcomes. Whole-body screening MRIs already exist, cost ~$2K, and are explicitly not recommended by the American College of Radiology for the same reason: high false-positive rates with no proven mortality benefit.

3. Two other major stories in this issue: Anthropic's Managing Director of International, Chris Ciauri, stated at a Seoul press conference that Fable and Mythos will be available again "in coming days," after a White House directive blocked both models for foreign nationals (triggering a worldwide shutdown) over a Korean telecom company's suspected ties to China. Separately, Valar Atomics took its Ward 250 reactor critical — the second advanced reactor under the DOE's Reactor Pilot Program and the first DOE-authorized reactor built outside the national laboratory system — moving toward a July 4 power operations deadline set by a Trump executive order requiring at least three advanced reactors to reach criticality.

**Quotable:**
> "The entire premise rests on the unproven assumption that earlier detection of incidental abnormalities improves patient outcomes." — *radiologist consulted by Scott Alexander, on Midjourney Medical's whole-body scanner*

## Geopolitics & Economics

### 'Iran is like the punch in the face' 📎
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 5 mins

1. The Iran war is the latest in a sequence of compounding shocks — Brexit, Covid, Ukraine, now Iran — that have left food producers across the supply chain with no recovery buffer between crises. Bloomberg traced a chocolate croissant from dairy farms and flour mills through bakeries and cafés to show how each upstream cost increase (energy, fuel, palm oil, wheat) gets locked in before it reaches the consumer shelf. One farmer who survived all four shocks coined the title metaphor, capturing how cumulative damage changes the nature of each new blow.

2. A peace deal in Iran won't produce quick food-price relief because the supply-chain transmission lag is structural, not political. Contracts for ingredients are set months in advance, energy surcharges are baked into freight and bakery costs before war news hits supermarkets, and retailers absorb margin pressure before passing it on. Reporters Eleanor Thornber, Maddie Parker, and Nayla Razzouk find that even once the geopolitical trigger resolves, the price signal continues moving downstream for months.

3. The war's human geography is often invisible in coverage focused on shipping lanes and oil prices. On Qeshm Island — the Strait of Hormuz's largest island, which had built a small tourism economy as a refuge from Tehran's restrictions — hotels emptied and livelihoods collapsed the moment hostilities began. The same strait that Bloomberg markets watch for tanker flows is home to communities that Golnar Motevalli reports are now left without visitors, income, or clarity about what comes next.

**Quotable:**
> "Iran is like the punch in the face, isn't it?" — *a farmer who had already weathered Brexit, Covid, and the Ukraine war, on the cumulative toll of successive global shocks*

### Editor's Choice: Burnham's path to power 📎
**Patrick Jenkins** · email · 4 mins

1. Andy Burnham won the Makerfield by-election on June 19, 2026, with 55% of the vote — a larger majority than Labour achieved there in the 2024 general election. After nine years as Greater Manchester mayor, he now has a Westminster seat from which to formally challenge Keir Starmer for the Labour leadership, and he already has enough MP support to trigger a contest. Meanwhile, Kemi Badenoch's Conservatives won Aberdeen South — their first Westminster by-election win in Scotland in nearly 60 years. FT columnist Stephen Bush reads both results as evidence that anti-Reform tactical voting remains the decisive structural obstacle to Farage reaching Downing Street.
