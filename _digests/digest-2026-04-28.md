### Money Stuff: Everything Is Commodities Fraud ⚡
**Matt Levine / Bloomberg** · rss · 17 mins

1. **Army Master Sergeant Gannon Ken Van Dyke made $400,000 betting on Nicolás Maduro's capture via Polymarket.** Van Dyke, 38, a special forces soldier at Fort Bragg involved in planning Operation Absolute Resolve, opened a Polymarket account on Dec. 26 and placed 13 "YES" bets from Dec. 27 through Jan. 2, 2026 that Maduro would be removed. He routed his trades through a VPN to fake a foreign geolocation, because Polymarket's terms formally bar US users.

2. **Prediction markets now function as a probabilistic leak of classified military plans — and as a financial incentive to manufacture volatility.** If a soldier can bet at 1% odds and then cause the event to happen, the payoff is 99x. The more unlikely the operation, the more profitable it is to execute it. Targeted foreign leaders should treat a sudden Polymarket probability spike as a tactical early warning; it may mean someone is already rappelling toward their compound.

3. **Van Dyke was charged with commodities fraud because the CFTC claims jurisdiction over all "event contracts" as a category of swap.** The Commodity Exchange Act defines a "swap" as any agreement "dependent on the occurrence, nonoccurrence, or the extent of the occurrence of an event or contingency associated with a potential financial, economic, or commercial consequence." Maduro's removal qualified because it has "potential impacts on the global prices of crude oil, Venezuelan government bonds, and the Venezuelan bolívar."

4. **The logical extension of the CFTC's theory is that every bet anywhere — sportsbook, casino, March Madness pool — is a federally regulated commodity swap.** If Kalshi's event contracts are swaps (CFTC's established position), and if Polymarket's identical unregistered contracts are also swaps (the Van Dyke prosecution's premise), then the form of the platform is irrelevant. Any promise of money contingent on a future event is a swap, meaning insider trading law applies to any use of nonpublic information to place any bet.

5. **Rezolve AI launched a hostile all-stock takeover of Commerce.com at a 47% discount to market price, arguing its stock is "really" worth 4x its trading price.** On April 8, Rezolve offered 0.5 of its shares (trading at $2.88) per Commerce.com share (trading at $2.73) — an implied price of $1.44. The justification: six sell-side analysts have a consensus $10.75 price target on Rezolve, so the offer represents $5.50 in "implied value." Commerce.com's board rejected it and Rezolve's stock has not closed above $2.82 since the announcement.

6. **The Rezolve gambit is a novel inversion of the take-under logic: if a target's inflated stock can justify a below-market deal, then an acquirer's *undervalued* stock should justify paying above-market in stock.** The historical precedent cited is Zuckerberg's argument to Instagram's Kevin Systrom in 2012 — that $1B of Facebook stock was "really" worth $2B because Facebook should eventually be worth $200B+. That argument was correct in hindsight, which is presumably why Rezolve is trying it now.

7. **Bobby Jain's pod-shop hedge fund, launched with great fanfare, is now folding its external investor business entirely into Millennium after returning only 3.7% to clients in 2025 — despite generating $750M in trading profits.** In pod shops, portfolio managers are paid in cash upfront (not deferred equity), so startup costs are crushing: investors only received ~25% of gross profits after fees and expenses. Studies show established multistrategy funds already give clients only ~40% of profits; for a new fund with partially deployed capital, the math is even worse.

8. **Apoorva Mehta (Instacart co-founder) launched Abundance, a hedge fund where thousands of AI agents autonomously scour the internet for trade ideas, size bets, and execute trades.** This represents the second, stranger model of AI investing: rather than training a neural net on financial data to predict prices, Abundance applies general-purpose language-model intelligence to stock picking — solving the much harder general problem first and redirecting it at markets. The harder milestone, unachieved by anyone, would be using AI agents to raise money from investors without human involvement.

**Quotable:**
> "If you bet on an event at a 1% probability, and then cause it to happen, you will make 99 times your money. My overarching theory of current US policy is that everyone involved in the Trump administration basically loves creating volatility." — *on the Van Dyke Polymarket case and its implications for US foreign policy*

### Are the AI Labs Ready for the AI Backlash? ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 12 mins

1. AI lab founders genuinely believed they were building potentially world-ending technology but built it anyway on the logic that it was inevitable — better for safety-conscious people to be at the frontier than to cede it to less careful actors. This has no real historical parallel; the closest analogy would be Rockefeller founding Standard Oil specifically because monopolistic pricing would *reduce* oil consumption and buy time to figure out alternatives to fossil fuels.

2. The most technically credible warnings about AI come not from critics but from the people running OpenAI, Anthropic, and DeepMind — they are the ones saying AI will let hackers find vulnerabilities in all the world's software, enable DIY bioweapons, or wipe out all white-collar work. The same people also tout the efficiency gains from eliminating legal, accounting, and administrative costs, while acknowledging that what's an expense to the buyer is revenue to the seller.

3. Apocalyptic framing became competitively entrenched — once one lab talked up existential risk, others who downplayed it looked either less serious or less bullish on capabilities. This competitive dynamic means that labs have years of on-the-record statements that function as pre-written opposition research; a politician can just pull up a recent podcast to hear an AI CEO say AI could wipe out half of entry-level white-collar jobs, or all jobs, or that it's as dangerous as climate change.

4. AI is politically homeless despite having slight net popularity in polls. The left dislikes big tech companies; the right dislikes what Hobart calls "polyamorous vegan nerds." GDP growth has no real political constituency — everyone prefers it but it doesn't differentiate voters, so political entrepreneurs have every incentive to construct left- and right-coded frameworks for opposing AI. Even the polling data is misleading: voters 65+ self-report as negative on AI while enthusiastically sharing AI-generated images on Facebook.

5. The Internet contrast is damning: load-bearing industry-shaping laws (ECPA 1986, CFAA 1986) were passed in the mid-80s, and anti-piracy legislation came in the late 90s — a full decade before broadband penetration hit 50% and before displaced travel agents, print journalists, video rental clerks, and bank tellers became a visible political constituency. AI arrived with a political vacuum instead of an early framework, so the hard questions are now being debated in the context of visible job losses rather than in a calmer pre-disruption moment.

6. Effective organizations solve the problems they're good at until only the hard problems remain. "Automating away jobs" was a perfect VC pitch and a powerful researcher recruitment story. But if it's worth paying someone $5m/year to automate away jobs, there are by definition far more votes in appealing to the soon-to-be-automated than to the automators — the political math always tilts against the disruptors once disruption scales, regardless of whether the automation eventually makes society better off overall.

**Quotable:**
> "Automating away jobs was a great pitch to VCs, and building those capabilities was a great one for researchers. But if it's worth it to pay someone $5m/year to automate away jobs, it necessarily means that there are more votes in appealing to the soon-to-be-automated than the automators, even if that automation eventually makes us better-off overall." — *closing argument*

### AI Hardware, Meta Display, Redefining VR and AR ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 11 mins

1. Apple's multi-device hardware strategy has a hidden dependency on AI. New CEO John Ternus is pushing smart glasses, home displays, a tabletop robot, and AirPods with vision cameras — all devices that existing iPhone users would buy on top of their phones. But the more devices there are, the less the phone is the center of computing; what becomes the center is AI. Apple's hardware growth story only works if Siri actually delivers, and it hasn't yet — the home devices and smart glasses are both delayed due to AI model shortcomings.

2. The VR/AR distinction isn't about occlusion — it's about immersion. The relevant divide isn't whether your eyes are covered (headset vs. glasses) but whether the device pulls you out of the real world mentally. Apple Vision Pro has pass-through video but still demands focus: you look at UI elements with eye-tracking and lose awareness of surroundings. Orion, Meta's unreleased full-display glasses, works the same way — eye-tracking as primary input means the real world gets pushed to your peripheral vision even though you can technically see it.

3. The Meta Ray-Ban Display (with its small right-lens display and Neural Band wrist controller) is genuine AR precisely because of its input method. With the Neural Band, you can keep your eyes and attention on the real world and control the display with your hand in your pocket or behind your back — the Display stays in peripheral vision rather than demanding focus. After using both Orion and Display back-to-back, Thompson finds Display more compelling than Orion despite Orion being a far more advanced technical achievement.

4. Orion is a dead end. Making VR-equivalent immersion work inside glasses-form-factor hardware comes with massive display quality trade-offs — brightness and resolution are far below a Vision Pro or Quest. Since Orion still pulls you mentally out of the real world, you'd be accepting worse display quality for no AR benefit. The right split is: headsets (Vision Pro, Quest) for intentional, immersive VR experiences where the app paradigm makes sense; glasses (Display) for ambient AR where serendipitous, anticipatory interaction is the goal.

5. AI — not apps — is the correct interface layer for AR glasses, and Meta is better positioned than Apple here. The best Display features are "just-in-time UI": notifications, real-time captions, live translation, teleprompter. The worst moments are when replying to a notification exposes too much of an app's chrome (e.g., WhatsApp's full interface launching). Everything should be mediated by AI that anticipates what you need and surfaces it without manual navigation. Apple's playbook works for VR; AR will diverge further from VR the way PCs and tablets diverged — Windows 8 proved you can't bridge them — and Meta's AI investment puts it ahead.

6. The Neural Band is the input method for the augmented world beyond glasses. Meta demoed at CES 2026 a partnership with Garmin to use the Neural Band to control car infotainment systems — navigating apps and gestures on a touchscreen without touching it. The deeper idea is that any device in your physical environment becomes controllable via subtle wrist gestures. Smart glasses are the first application, but the Neural Band's real potential is as a universal ambient controller for the augmented world.

**Quotable:**
> "I can focus on the real world, while Display was in my peripheral vision... I can't envision ever preferring Orion to Display; that's how much more compelling Display is in terms of a glasses experience." — *after using both Meta Ray-Ban Display and Orion back-to-back*

### GPU Spot Prices Surge 114% in Six Weeks 📎
**Tomasz Tunguz** · email · 2 mins

1. NVIDIA B200 (Blackwell) spot prices on the Ornn Compute Price Index hit $4.95/hr this week, up from $2.31 in early March — a 114% jump in six weeks. Every major model release since September 2025 has preceded or coincided with a B200 price spike; GPT-5.5's expanded context window specifically requires the memory headroom only Blackwell provides, making frontier model launches a reliable demand-shock trigger.

2. The B200-over-H200 price premium collapsed to just $0.28 by November 2025 as supply flooded the market, briefly reaching near-parity with the prior-generation H200. Since GPT-5.3-Codex launched in February 2026, the spread has re-widened to $1.80 — back near launch levels — and functions as a depreciation signal: older chips lose value when new frontier models demand new architectures.

3. Provider pricing has fragmented sharply: in September 2025, B200 rates clustered tightly across vendors; today the spread between cheapest and most expensive has more than doubled. The dispersion reflects an opaque market where buyers can't see hyperscaler delivery schedules or which AI startups are dumping overbought capacity at a discount. The practical consequence: spot market pricing leads contract pricing by ~90 days, meaning B200 contracts will likely settle above $5.00 for summer 2026 — and cloud providers' six months of margin compression are ending while AI startup inference costs keep climbing.

**Quotable:**
> "Inflationary demand outpaces deflationary algorithmic & chip improvements, but the fog of the GPU market continues." — *closing summary*

### The World: The art of the nuclear deal 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Trump scrapped the 2015 JCPOA in 2018 calling it a "guaranteed Road to a Nuclear Weapon," and is now promising something "FAR BETTER." The original agreement — 160 pages with five technical annexes — required Iran to ship out 97% of its nuclear material, cap enrichment at 3.67% (reactor-grade, well below weapons-grade), and accept intrusive IAEA inspections, in exchange for sanctions relief. It was designed to keep Iran at least one year from a bomb.

2. When the U.S. exited the JCPOA, Iran had too little nuclear fuel to build a single bomb — it had shipped nearly all of it to Russia as required. Today, per international inspectors, Iran holds 11 total tons of uranium at various enrichment levels, including half a ton of near-bomb-grade uranium buried under the rubble of last year's U.S. strikes. With further purification, that stockpile is enough to build up to 100 nuclear weapons. Virtually all of it was enriched after Trump abandoned the deal.

3. Obama's negotiating team brought the CIA's top Iran expert, the energy secretary (a nuclear weapons design specialist), and scores of technical experts to every session. Trump's current team — JD Vance, Jared Kushner, and special envoy Steve Witkoff — learned deal-making in New York real estate. On the Iranian side, Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi served as deputy negotiator during the original JCPOA talks. NYT national security correspondent David Sanger: "He knew every inch of it. Witkoff and Kushner are smart people and quick learners, but they're relatively new to this. The Iranian team has a depth of expertise."

4. Iran has already rejected the two conditions that would have constituted a genuine improvement on the JCPOA: handing over its nuclear stockpile and suspending enrichment indefinitely. Iran now holds additional leverage through its control of the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint it did not dominate in the same way during the 2015 negotiations. Iran's counter-proposal focuses on opening the Strait, lifting the U.S. blockade, and deferring nuclear talks entirely, which is the inverse of what the U.S. wants. Talks have stalled after two rounds of aborted negotiations, and Trump faces a domestic deadline: the war's economic damage (rising oil and gas prices) creates pressure to reach some agreement before midterm elections in the fall.

**Quotable:**
> "He knew every inch of it. Witkoff and Kushner are smart people and quick learners, but they're relatively new to this. The Iranian team has a depth of expertise." — *David Sanger, NYT White House and national security correspondent, on the expertise gap between Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and Trump's negotiating team*
