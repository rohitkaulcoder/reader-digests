## Today's Top 3

### Editor's Pick: Anthropic's Pricing Shift as AI Use Surges ⚡
**The Information** · email · 2 mins

1. Anthropic has switched enterprise pricing from flat subscriptions to usage-based billing, a change some customers began noticing in early April 2026. For heavy users, the shift could double or triple costs — a direct consequence of agentic products like Claude Code consuming far more compute per session than a simple chat interaction.

2. The pricing change is a response to a compute crunch driven by surging adoption: Anthropic's annualized revenue has reached $30 billion, with agentic and coding products leading that growth. Flat-rate subscriptions made sense when AI usage was predictable; agents that autonomously loop, write code, and call tools make per-seat pricing economically untenable for the vendor.

**Quotable:**
> "Subscription models were built for predictable usage. AI agents break that model entirely." — *Jessica Lessin, Founder & Editor-in-Chief, The Information*

### The Forecast: Gen Z sours on AI ⚡
**Bloomberg Weekend** · email · 7 mins

1. A Gallup survey of 1,500 Americans ages 14–29 (conducted late February/early March 2026) found Gen Z is significantly less excited about AI than a year ago — and more angry about it. This matters disproportionately because younger people have historically been both the fastest adopters of new technology and its greatest beneficiaries; their skepticism is a leading indicator, not a lagging one.

2. The standard explanation for AI pessimism — that non-users fear what they don't know — only partially holds here. A 2023 study titled "What I didn't grow up with is dangerous" (John Protzko, Central Connecticut State University) confirmed that people who didn't experience a technology young are more likely to see it as corrupting. Non-users of generative AI are indeed the most anxious. But the Gallup data shows even *heavy* Gen Z users are growing more pessimistic, which breaks the expected pattern.

3. Protzko's explanation for heavy-user disillusionment: daily use exposes AI's flaws directly. "Once you use it consistently, you easily see its shortcomings," he told Bloomberg. The deeper problem is structural: companies are explicitly over-adopting AI with the stated goal of avoiding early-career hires — the exact demographic being polled. Gen Z's sourness isn't irrational anxiety; it's a rational response to watching employers use AI as justification for closing the entry-level pipeline.

4. The job market squeeze is showing up elsewhere: undergraduate entrepreneurship classes at US universities are now "packed," driven partly by AI's promise of small-team leverage and partly by the collapse of entry-level hiring. AI is simultaneously the tool that makes solo founders viable and the stated reason companies aren't hiring the people who would otherwise have become employees.

**Quotable:**
> "We are dealing with a flawed technology that is fun to use for some purposes, but companies are over-adopting with the promise to not have to hire early-career workers, the people polled here. Who wouldn't sour on it under those conditions?" — *John Protzko, psychology professor at Central Connecticut State University, commenting on the Gallup data*

### Why half of product managers are in trouble | Nikhyl Singhal (Meta, Google) ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 5 mins

1. **The PM job is bifurcating, and roughly half are on the wrong side.** There are two types of PMs: those who got into product because they love building, and those who gravitated to it for the salary and communication-centric workflows. The second group — estimated at about half the industry — faces an existential crisis as AI collapses the value of information-moving and stakeholder-wrangling skills that justified their roles.

2. **A mass displacement is already underway, not a future forecast.** Nikhyl Singhal predicts large companies will shed ~30,000 roles and rehire ~8,000 within 12–24 months — and every one of those 8,000 hires will be AI-native builders. The 30,000 lost positions combine roles that didn't generate proportional value with work now automatable by AI agents; Singhal says this cycle has begun.

3. **Pedigree has inverted: six years at a big-name company can now hurt you.** A decade of career advice said "build your brand — work at Meta, Google, prove you've seen the movie." Now, interviewers ask "What tools do you use? Walk me through your judgment in this scenario," not "tell me about that 2019 launch." Candidates who spent years in legacy workflows at prestigious firms emerge behind peers who stayed current at less-known shops.

**Quotable:**
> "Don't say, 'I was an X-level leader, I'll only consider roles at that level.' Brands don't matter. Everything's changing. Be willing to take something smaller to make sure you're on the boat leaving for the new world." — *Nikhyl Singhal on overcoming ego as the hidden barrier to reinvention*

## AI & Business

### Vibe Check: Opus 4.7 Stopped Reading Between the Lines 📌
**Katie Parrott / Vibe Check** · rss · 2 mins

1. Opus 4.7 launched as a surprise drop, tested live on X and YouTube by five Every.to testers in front of ~10,000 viewers, with Anthropic researcher Alex Albert joining the stream to explain the changes. Results split sharply by task type: Kieran Klaassen called it the best model he's ever tested for coding, the first to complete a full e-commerce build including a custom product designer and a stable shopping cart. Mike Taylor got consulting copy he rated better than his own writing and the best slide deck design he'd seen.

2. For tasks requiring inference and gap-filling, 4.7 underperformed its predecessor. Katie Parrott ran a head-to-head on a personal essay and chose 4.6 — 4.7's draft was competent but rhythmically flat. Brandon Gell's monthly P&L analysis showed 4.7 missed a data error that 4.6 had caught unprompted last month. Dan Shipper watched it produce a senior-engineer-quality codebase diagnosis, then refuse to execute the solution.

3. The unifying explanation: Anthropic deliberately dials Claude's "eagerness" between releases, and 4.7 is a hard pullback from 4.6's gap-filling intuition. The model rewards tight, explicit prompts and frustrates users who relied on 4.6 reading between the lines — meaning old Opus prompts will likely need to be rewritten to get equivalent results from 4.7.

**Quotable:**
> "This model rewards people who write tight prompts and frustrates everyone who doesn't." — *Katie Parrott summarizing two hours of live testing and an afternoon in Slack*

### 20VC Newsletter - 19th April 2026 📎
**20VC** · email · 4 mins

1. **We are not in an AI bubble — we are in a GPU wastage bubble.** Compute is not yet fungible: you cannot move a workload from an H100 cluster to a Blackwell cluster without significant friction, so billions of dollars in deployed compute sits stranded and unutilized. Anjney Midha (Founder, AMP PBC) compares this to the 1885 pre-standardization era of the electricity grid — the underlying capability is real, but the crisis is one of interoperability and standardization, not hype.

2. **Incumbents building "60% as good" AI features face a structural doom loop.** Rory O'Driscoll (GP, Scale) and Jason Lemkin (Founder, SaaStr) argue that internal teams may be proud of AI add-ons that are merely 60% as effective as specialized competitors, but the market won't pay for mediocre "check the box" features — a 60% solution must ultimately be free. To survive, incumbents must build agents good enough to charge for independently, or accept becoming stable but irrelevant "value plays" like IBM.

**Quotable:**
> "The crisis isn't a lack of AI capability; it's a standardization crisis. Because compute is not yet fungible, billions of dollars in compute are currently sitting unutilized and 'stranded'. We are essentially in the '1885 pre-standardization era' of the electricity grid." — *Anjney Midha, Founder @ AMP PBC, on the GPU wastage bubble*

## Technology

### Apple's Siri reveal 📌
**Mark Gurman at Bloomberg** · email · 7 mins

1. Apple's WWDC 2026 teaser — a glowing "26" on a black background — directly previews the new Siri interface coming in iOS 27: the Dynamic Island expands with a "Search or Ask" prompt and a glowing cursor using Siri's signature animation colors. The same glow appears around the pill's edges and inside the standalone Siri app. Apple marketing chief Greg Joswiak posted the video teaser on X, and the effect is most visible in dark mode, which is why the WWDC graphic has a black background this year.

2. The Siri overhaul in iOS 27 is the most sweeping since the assistant launched: it includes a chatbot-style interface, a standalone Siri app, multi-command handling within a single query, support for third-party AI agents, and — finally — the ability to draw on personal data and on-screen content to answer questions. These personal-data features had been delayed from iOS 26.4 to 26.5, and are now pushed fully to iOS 27 at WWDC in June.

3. Industrywide memory shortages are delaying Apple's next major Mac hardware. New Mac Studio models (successors to the M4 Max and M3 Ultra machines from last year) had been targeted for mid-2026 but are now expected internally around October. The touch-screen MacBook Pro — which will include a Dynamic Island camera cutout and move to M6 chips — was already slated for late 2026 to early 2027; shortages push it toward the later end of that window. Software will be ready by fall; the delay is purely supply-side.

4. Three significant Apple executives departed around the April 15 stock-vesting date. The most consequential exit is Stan Ng, who oversaw marketing for Apple Watch, AirPods, and Apple's home and health efforts after 31 years at the company — part of three major departures tied to the watch/health division in recent months. AI chief John Giannandrea and former car-project veteran Libo Meyers also left. Apple's second unionized store, in Oklahoma City's Penn Square mall, is not expected to close; unlike the shuttered Towson, Maryland location, it sits in a thriving mall with no competing Apple stores nearby.

**Quotable:**
> "The new visuals are most noticeable in iOS 27's dark mode, which is why Apple's WWDC graphic this year has a black background." — *Gurman explaining how the WWDC teaser image is itself a coded preview of the Siri UI*

## Markets & Finance

### The World: Messy war, happy stock markets 📌
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. **The S&P 500 just hit a series of record highs despite the Iran war threatening a global energy crisis.** Deutsche Bank analyst Jim Reid called the rally's "velocity...nothing short of astonishing." Major indexes in Japan, South Korea, and Europe are also near their own peaks — a counterintuitive outcome given the IMF's warnings of slowed growth, higher inflation, and rising recession odds.

2. **Stock markets are not measuring the economy — they're measuring future corporate profits, and those remain strong.** S&P 500 companies are expected to post double-digit earnings growth for the sixth consecutive quarter. Consumer spending has held up; inflation has risen only modestly outside energy-related goods. The war's impact on corporate earnings has, so far, been limited.

3. **Big tech is insulating the broad indexes from the war's real casualties.** Alphabet, Microsoft, and Meta — still riding AI enthusiasm — dominate the S&P 500 and are largely disconnected from Middle East turmoil. Meanwhile, consumer-facing businesses serving lower-income customers are hurting badly: Dollar General and Dollar Tree are both down by double-digit percentages since the war began on Feb. 28.

4. **Retail investors have internalized a "buy the dip" geopolitical playbook, making markets structurally less responsive to conflict.** The S&P 500 slid for the first month of the Iran war, then fully recovered after Trump announced a cease-fire — not because investors believe Trump, but because they believe the worst has passed. Deutsche Bank noted this "geopolitical playbook" paid off again, reinforcing the pattern: investors now treat global shocks as buying opportunities rather than harbingers of long-term decline.

**Meanwhile, the cease-fire remains fragile.** A U.S. Navy destroyer attacked an Iranian cargo ship defying the American blockade, threatening the truce set to expire Wednesday. Trump announced a second negotiating round in Pakistan — led by VP JD Vance, Steve Witkoff, and Jared Kushner — but Iranian state media said Tehran had not agreed to any such meeting.

**Quotable:**
> "The velocity of this ascent has been nothing short of astonishing." — *Jim Reid, Deutsche Bank analyst, on the S&P 500's wartime rally*

## Global Economics

### When Stability Becomes the Exception 📎
**The Core** · email · 6 mins

1. India's biofuel potential — currently 1–2% of its energy mix — could realistically reach 20% of demand, but the binding constraint is logistics, not technology. Suhas Baxi of BiofuelCircle explains that India generates hundreds of millions of tonnes of agricultural residue annually, most of it burned or wasted. The real challenge is orchestrating collection, compression, transport, and conversion across tens of thousands of villages within narrow harvest windows — an execution problem at national scale, not an innovation gap.

2. JPMorgan's Jahangir Aziz diagnoses India's stubbornly high household savings rate as a response to economic insecurity, not culture: rising costs in healthcare, education, and housing, combined with income uncertainty, are keeping consumers cautious. This suppresses domestic consumption precisely when India needs it to compensate for a "valuation reset" — global investors are pulling back due to slowing earnings momentum and a widening gap between market expectations and macro reality.

3. The rupee's sustained weakness reflects structural pressures — rising energy import dependence and persistent capital outflows — not just short-term shocks like the US–Iran ceasefire. The RBI has responded with heavy intervention, but this raises a credibility question: at what point does "managing volatility" become covert exchange-rate targeting, and what does that do to hedging costs, financial-sector behavior, and long-run capital flow confidence?

**Quotable:**
> "Volatility is no longer episodic — it's structural. And resilience is fast becoming the new growth strategy." — *framing the edition's central thesis*
