### Amazon Buys Globalstar, Delta to Add Leo, The Apple Angle ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 10 mins

1. Amazon's $11.6B Globalstar acquisition is conventionally framed as Amazon vs. Starlink, but Globalstar's assets are weak: 24 aging bent-pipe satellites nearing end of 15-year lifespan, only ~25 MHz of combined L/S-band spectrum (vs. SpaceX's 65 MHz), and 24 ground gateways. Starlink already has 10,000 satellites serving 10 million people — there's no plausible path where this acquisition closes that gap.

2. Amazon does have genuine reasons to build LEO independent of the Globalstar deal: its global logistics network needs connectivity, drone delivery works better with satellites than cell towers (drones fly, avoiding line-of-sight limits), and there are niche AWS use cases for low-latency inter-region links. Amazon's core competency is massive long-term capital investment for durable cost/capability advantages — a satellite constellation fits that pattern, and self-sufficiency from SpaceX fits the "avoid supplier dependency" pattern.

3. Delta signed with Amazon Leo for 500 aircraft starting 2028 (3–5x current speeds), mirroring the iPhone/carrier dynamic: Starlink reportedly demands free Wi-Fi for passengers *and* fleet-wide installation, leaving airlines completely captive. United, chasing Delta in the premium segment, accepted those terms. Delta — the most profitable U.S. airline and market leader — refused, preferring supplier leverage. American is also in talks with Amazon, primarily on cost grounds.

4. The real key to the Globalstar deal is Apple. Apple held a 20% equity stake and rights to 85% of Globalstar's network capacity, giving it a de facto veto over any acquisition. Apple's statement in the press release — delivered by SVP Greg Joswiak — pointedly emphasizes *continuity* ("ensure our users will continue to have access") rather than new capabilities, and Amazon leads its announcement with the Apple partnership before anything about cellular ambitions.

5. The most coherent explanation: Apple engineered this deal. Globalstar was failing (Apple had already injected emergency cash to maintain an experience already worse than Starlink's), and Apple refuses to partner with SpaceX over control dynamics. Rather than get stranded, Apple arranged a handoff — Amazon acquires Globalstar, maintains the existing satellite features (Emergency SOS, Messages, Find My, Roadside Assistance), and eventually folds Globalstar's spectrum/licenses into Leo. Apple already pays an estimated $1–1.5B/year to AWS, giving it ample financial leverage to make the $11.6B acquisition economically rational for Amazon even if the satellite business itself is marginal.

**Quotable:**
> "Apple clearly agrees, because not only are they declining to work with SpaceX, I actually think they were the driving force in this GlobalStar deal." — *Thompson's conclusion after noting Apple had veto power and Globalstar was financially dependent on Apple's business*

### Money Stuff: AIbirds ⚡
**Bloomberg** · rss · 16 mins

1. Allbirds — the wool sneaker company once valued at $4 billion that was days away from liquidating — announced a pivot to AI compute infrastructure and its stock surged 655%, from $2.49 to $18.82, pushing its market cap from ~$22 million to ~$164 million. The $22 million had represented roughly the expected value of a liquidating dividend from the $39 million sneaker-business sale; the $164 million now purportedly represents the value of "NewBird AI," a future GPU-as-a-Service cloud provider with no data centers and no AI experience.

2. The financing structure behind the pivot is a $50 million convertible note with highly investor-favorable terms: 12% interest rate, 5% original issue discount, and — critically — only $5 million committed upfront with the remaining $45 million at the investor's option. This means the anonymous "institutional investor" put up $5 million to trigger a stock moon, then can decide whether to fund the rest only after seeing whether the meme trade worked.

3. The convertible has a dual conversion mechanism. The fixed conversion is at ~$2.99/share (a great deal at $18.82). The more dangerous mechanism is a "death spiral" clause allowing conversion at 93% of the lowest stock price over the prior 10 trading days — meaning the investor profits from downward volatility too, buying low during dips and selling into any recovery. A $50 million note converting at $2.99 would produce ~16.7 million shares worth ~$315 million at today's price, far exceeding the company's market cap.

4. This deal illustrates a replicable playbook for meme-era finance: find ~10 near-defunct public companies with nostalgic brand recognition, offer each a $50 million convertible (10% upfront), and let AI enthusiasm do the rest. With only $50 million deployed across 10 deals, if even one stock goes up 10x before you convert and sell, you more than triple your total investment — and you only need to fund the tranches on deals that actually work.

5. Avis Budget Group has 35.3 million shares outstanding, but two hedge funds — SRS Investment Management (run by Avis board member Karthik Sarma) and Pentwater Capital Management — together own 108.41% of the company's stock when shares, cash-settled total return swaps, and options are combined. SRS holds 49.34% in stock + 8.10% via swap; Pentwater holds 19.95% in stock + 28.81% via swap + 2.20% in options. Adding BlackRock (4.6%), Vanguard (4.32%), and State Street (1.78%) brings the total long exposure to ~119% of shares outstanding.

6. The 119%+ long position is only possible because short sellers created synthetic shares by borrowing stock and reselling it. But those short sellers — who have shorted ~43% of Avis's tradeable float (down from 49% in mid-March) — are now trapped: the supply of borrowable stock is controlled almost entirely by SRS and Pentwater, who can refuse to lend or call in their borrow. Avis stock rose 162% between March 20 and last Wednesday ($261.35), then another 57% to $411.56 in the following four days — quadruple its price from a month ago.

7. The mechanics echo Phil Falcone's 2008 MAAX bonds corner, where Harbinger Capital bought ~190 million bonds — more than 100% of the issue size — then called in all borrow, leaving short-seller banks no one to buy from but Falcone himself. Falcone told the stunned bankers: "sometimes you are just on the wrong side of a trade." The Avis situation isn't quite a full corner, but SRS and Pentwater's combined 108% synthetic ownership means any short-covering demand flows almost entirely back to them.

8. A brief aside on the "meta hustle" of online courses: the global digital education market is projected to reach $134 billion by 2030 (Grand View Research), fueled by platforms like Kajabi, Squarespace, and Beehiiv. The sector has an obvious self-referential problem — courses that teach you to sell courses are the one category where the seller is genuinely proving out the model — but the broader market is broadly unregulated, with "expertise" often simply meaning "confidence."

**Quotable:**
> "This is like going to a Nascar race. The crashes sure are really interesting." — *S3 Partners Managing Director Ihor Dusaniwsky on the Avis short squeeze*

### Iran's Chinese spy satellite ⚡
**FirstFT Asia** · email · 5 mins

1. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Aerospace Force secretly acquired a Chinese-built spy satellite — designated TEE-01B — in late 2024, after it was launched from China. Leaked IRGC military documents, time-stamped coordinate lists, satellite imagery, and orbital analysis confirm that Iranian commanders then directed the satellite to surveil key US military bases across the Middle East, with images captured in March both before and after Iranian drone and missile strikes on those locations.

2. TEE-01B was built and launched by Earth Eye Co, a Chinese firm offering "in-orbit delivery" — a commercial model in which spacecraft are launched in China and legally transferred to foreign customers once in orbit, allowing Beijing-based companies to supply foreign militaries while maintaining nominal distance from the transaction. This model made it possible for the IRGC to acquire a military-grade surveillance asset without a direct state-to-state arms transfer.

3. The revelation is geopolitically explosive because China is simultaneously the largest trading partner of Gulf Arab states and their biggest oil buyer. Beijing's commercial interests in the very countries Iran has been targeting with missiles and drones — guided, in part, by a Chinese-made satellite — puts China in a deeply awkward position with its most important regional economic partners.

**Quotable:**
> "The use of a Chinese-built satellite by the IRGC during a war where Tehran has repeatedly targeted its neighbours with missiles and drones is likely to be highly sensitive across the region." — *FT investigation on TEE-01B*

### Money Stuff: Banks Aren't Worried About Private Credit 📌
**Bloomberg** · rss · 14 mins

1. Banks aren't competing with private credit — they're funding it. During Q1 2026 bank earnings, Wall Street disclosed at least $100 billion in exposure to private credit firms: Wells Fargo $36.2B, JPMorgan $50B, Citigroup $22B (with zero losses over the life of that portfolio). The actual structure is "re-tranching": private credit funds borrow roughly 60% of their capital from banks, then lend 100% to companies. Banks sit behind a ~40% equity cushion — private credit investors absorb the first 40% of losses before banks are touched.

2. Jamie Dimon is "not particularly concerned." His reasoning: losses on private credit loans would have to exceed ~40% of the entire portfolio before banks take a hit, which requires systemic, correlated defaults across many borrowers simultaneously — an unlikely base case in a functional economy. BlackRock simultaneously sees the current private credit turbulence as a "market share opportunity," even while its own retail BDC investors are asking to redeem this quarter (BlackRock said no).

3. Two structural risks remain, even under Dimon's sanguine view. First, *sloppiness*: the re-tranching model enables more total lending than banks would do alone (you lend $60 to a fund that lends $100), which can only happen by making marginally worse loans — riskier borrowers, higher leverage ratios, or outright fraud. A senior claim on bad loans can be worse than full exposure to good ones. Second, *correlation*: Wells Fargo disclosed that software companies make up 17% of its private credit collateral, and roughly half is in business services, software, and healthcare. If AI renders enterprise SaaS valueless, all those loans default together, and the 40% cushion evaporates across the whole portfolio simultaneously.

4. Non-traded BDCs (business development companies) are experiencing a structural market failure. Retail investors bought into non-traded BDCs — permanent vehicles that make no-exit private loans — and the only liquidity mechanism is a quarterly tender offer capped at 5% of shares at net asset value (NAV). This quarter, holders of more than 5% of most major BDCs (Goldman Sachs's was the one exception) have requested redemptions. The probable cause: investors don't trust the stated NAVs. Publicly traded BDCs — which mark to market — consistently trade at ~20% discounts to NAV, signaling what investors actually think the loans are worth.

5. The BDC market failure is self-reinforcing and has no clean exit. If an investor believes a BDC is worth 80 cents on the dollar but the fund redeems at 100, rational investors cash out — but the fund can only accommodate 5% per quarter. Meanwhile, bullish investors who want to buy in at 80 cents *can't*: non-traded BDCs only accept subscriptions at 100 cents. Boaz Weinstein is attempting to bridge this gap by tendering for Blue Owl BDC shares at roughly 65 cents. An instructive parallel: when French property fund Praemia REIM launched a true secondary market for its locked-up fund Priompierre, the first transaction cleared at a **60% discount** to subscription price; the only bid so far for Perial Asset Management's Perial O2 fund is at a **70% discount**.

6. Microsoft has paused what was the world's largest carbon removal program, driven by financial pressure from AI data center buildout. BloombergNEF estimated Microsoft's carbon removal purchases in 2025 accounted for **96% of the entire global market** — a staggering concentration. Its stated goal is to be carbon-negative by 2030, but its own emissions have risen sharply due to AI infrastructure investment, making full carbon offsets prohibitively expensive. Two factors reinforce the decision: the cost scales with emissions (more data centers = more carbon to offset), and the PR value of "carbon neutral" branding has turned negative in the current US political environment — claims of climate virtue are now a liability for major corporations, not an asset.

7. Prediction markets are more correlated than they appear, undermining the "uncorrelated events" argument for their uniqueness. Trader Foster McCoy made ~$10,000 at the Trump State of the Union by spotting Eric Trump's Instagram story showing Barron Trump in a suit — one signal that cascaded into correlated bets on whether Trump would mention his children, which words he'd use, etc. A 2004 paper by Steven Levitt explains why prediction market "bookmakers" differ from financial market makers: bookmakers take directional positions and systematically set prices that deviate from market-clearing prices to exploit bettor biases, rather than neutrally matching buyers and sellers. As prediction markets scale, this structure matters more.

**Quotable:**
> "You have to have very large losses in private credit before at least it looks like banks are going to get hit. It doesn't mean you won't feel some stress and strain and you might have to do something about it. But I'm not particularly worried about it." — *Jamie Dimon, JPMorgan CEO, on the bank's $50B private credit exposure*

### Going Private: Asia's billionaire dynasties hit record wealth 📎
**Bloomberg** · email · 10 mins

1. Asia's 20 richest family dynasties grew their combined wealth 16% to $647 billion in the past year — the highest total and largest annual gain since Bloomberg's Billionaires Index began tracking the list in 2019. The China-based Zhang family posted the biggest single-year jump after shares of their aluminum company China Hongqiao Group returned nearly 200% in 2025, fueled by demand for aluminum in AI server hardware, electric vehicles, and renewable energy equipment. Hong Kong property clans — the Kwoks, Chengs, Lees, and Pao/Woo family — are riding a long-awaited recovery in the domestic real estate market, adding breadth to the surge beyond tech-adjacent plays.

2. These dynasties are thriving partly because governments are becoming more nationalistic about strategic industries. IMD Business School professor Marleen Dieleman notes that families already embedded in data centers, chip supply chains, and infrastructure are uniquely positioned as states push for domestic production capability. This structural alignment between family-controlled empires and national industrial policy creates durable moats that differ from ordinary corporate concentration.

3. Private credit in Asia is growing rapidly and proving more resilient than its Western counterpart. Allocations are projected to reach $92 billion by 2027, up from $59 billion in 2024, with closed-ended Asia-Pacific vehicles drawing investors seeking non-US alternatives as BlackRock, Blackstone, and Blue Owl face significant redemption pressure. The more conservative lending practices and closed-ended fund structures common in Asia mean these vehicles are broadly viewed as better insulated from the liquidity crises hitting US private credit managers who bet heavily on software firms now being disrupted by AI.

4. PE sponsors are the second-fastest-growing client segment for major banks (after hedge funds), generating a 7% compound annual revenue growth rate since 2022 per BCG Expand, with total bank revenue pools from sponsor business expected to reach $791 billion in 2026. JPMorgan has restricted some lending to private credit funds after marking down loan values, but BCG Expand's Chirag Shah describes this as temporary positioning: "Banks that position themselves as balance-sheet partners and build relationships with disciplined risk managers — those emerging strongest from the current cycle — have a competitive edge." KKR raised $23 billion for its latest Americas buyout fund (beating a $20 billion target), and Blackstone closed its flagship opportunistic credit fund above $10 billion at its hard cap, showing the bifurcation between top-tier and weaker managers is accelerating.

5. Private capital poured a record $136 billion into catastrophe bonds and insurance-linked securities in 2024, an 18% increase per broker Aon, as reinsurers step back from natural disaster risk. Major reinsurers covered just over 10% of total insured catastrophe losses in 2024 — well below the 20% historical average per S&P Global — transferring that risk to capital markets investors who may be underequipped to price it. The sidecar market alone nearly tripled since 2023 to $18 billion, and Fitch's Brian Schneider warns that sidecar investors face potentially larger losses than cat bond holders because sidecars carry exposure to secondary perils (hailstorms, wildfires, floods) that trigger more frequently. The Bank of England and EIOPA have both flagged systemic risk: PE firms' short investment horizons and exit strategies may be "misaligned with the undertaking's long-term commitment to policyholders," and could amplify fire-sale dynamics in a crisis.

6. China's major institutional investors are liquidating US software-focused private equity stakes into a hot secondary market. Ping An Insurance — which manages 6.5 trillion yuan ($952 billion) — hired Campbell Lutyens to sell roughly $1 billion in Vista Equity Partners and KKR fund positions, constrained by China's offshore investing quotas. China's sovereign wealth fund CIC separately completed a $1 billion sale to Goldman Sachs (at a single-digit discount) and Ardian (at a double-digit discount). Total secondary market volumes hit a record $226 billion in 2024, up 41% per Evercore, with LP-driven sales rising 35% to $120 billion as investors pursue liquidity outside of GP-controlled exits.

**Quotable:**
> "There is a heightened risk that the opacity of these markets could trigger a wider loss of confidence, even when the precise causes of problems are idiosyncratic to particular borrowers." — *Andrew Bailey, Governor of the Bank of England and Chairman of the Financial Stability Board, on private markets risk*

### For markets, the war might as well be over 📎
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. US equity indexes have rallied more than 10% since last week's Iran ceasefire and now sit higher than when the war began — nearly back to all-time highs. The rally is powered partly by relief on oil: fund managers in the latest Bank of America global survey had been braced for Brent crude at $84/barrel by year-end, and the price has since pulled back toward $80, enough to unlock risk appetite even though oil remains far above its pre-war February levels.

2. The US blockade of the Strait of Hormuz is being read as a strategic de-escalation, not an escalation. Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution argues the blockade "signals a shift in US strategy away from warfare to economic countermeasures" and "the clearest sign yet that we've entered a negotiating phase." Signum Global Advisors' Andrew Bishop adds a caution: Pakistan and Iran have incentives to oversell good news, and US military assets are still en route — the ceasefire optimism may be partly manufactured to keep markets calm.

3. The dollar has surrendered nearly all of its war-driven safe-haven gains; the DXY index has fallen back below its 200-day moving average. Deutsche Bank's George Saravelos says it's "time to sell the US dollar again," pointing to two structural drags: speculation about a dovish Federal Reserve under new management has compressed the yield premium US bonds pay, and the oil shock hit US consumers harder than most Asian economies because Asia imposed price caps while US pump prices "barely moved" — making the US income shock comparatively larger, not smaller.

4. The BofA survey shows the smallest share of fund managers calling US equities overvalued in more than seven years — a dramatic reversal. But by Shiller CAPE, the S&P 500 remains among the most expensive markets in the world, topping Citi's global ranking. The valuation relief appears to be a war artifact: when Trump was perceived to back down (as with last year's Liberation Day tariffs), the US forward-earnings premium over the rest of the world snapped back sharply. With risk appetite restored, US stocks are again undeniably expensive, which continues to push capital out of the dollar.

5. Buy Now, Pay Later is buckling under stressed US consumers. Klarna, the sector's flagship, has fallen more than 60% from its September IPO price, wiping out over $11 billion in market value — the company peaked at $45 billion in 2021 and now sits at roughly $5 billion after two consecutive quarters of losses. A LendingTree study found nearly half of BNPL users paid late on at least one installment in the past year, while 54% say they couldn't make ends meet without the loans — a sign the product is increasingly financing essentials, not discretionary purchases.

6. The next political risk markets are pricing in: Democrats may capture the Senate in November's midterms. The House was already expected to flip. The BofA survey puts the odds of a full Democratic sweep at 36% — up sharply after a month of war — while Polymarket now puts it above 50%. A divided government would impose gridlock on Trump, but the transition itself — from near-unchecked executive power to intense legislative conflict — represents genuine market uncertainty in the months ahead. The key tax cuts have already passed and wouldn't be reversed, but judicial and cabinet confirmations would freeze.

**Quotable:**
> "For all the talk of demand destruction from higher energy prices, prices at the pump for the most exposed region have barely moved thanks to the instigation of powerful price caps. Remarkably, the US consumer is facing a much bigger income hit than all major Asian economies." — *Deutsche Bank's George Saravelos, on why the oil shock is structurally bearish for the dollar*

### An Interview with Bill Gurley About Runnin' Down a Dream 📌
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 40 mins

1. Gurley's book *Runnin' Down a Dream* took 6 years to develop from a video presentation at the University of Florida into a published book. James Clear — author of *Atomic Habits*, which Gurley calls "maybe the best-selling personal development book of all time" — saw the early video and gave Gurley confidence to proceed. The book profiles nine people in careers parents typically discourage (no VCs, doctors, or lawyers) and lays out a methodology for building a career you love.

2. The US academic system has created a "resume arms race" that produces burnout, not curiosity. Elite colleges haven't increased enrollment while applicant pools have grown, so they've become harder to enter — forcing kids to declare majors before visiting campus, and scheduling their days like peak-career adults (lacrosse, chess, Spanish lessons back to back). A Gallup survey shows 53% of US workers say they're not engaged at work.

3. The book's central argument: people most susceptible to AI-driven employment dislocation are those who never developed a fascinated, curiosity-driven approach to their career. Those who love what they do read voraciously on their own and treat learning as pleasurable — that intrinsic drive is both the competitive moat and the thing AI cannot replicate in the same way.

4. Curiosity can legitimately shift over time — Gurley started with computers (Commodore VIC-20, debugging code typed from magazines), then discovered stocks while at Compaq (reading Peter Lynch's *One Up on Wall Street*), then moved to Wall Street, then Silicon Valley. At each of his first two jobs he asked, "Do I see myself doing this in 30 years?" — the answer was no both times; he never asked that question during 25 years at Benchmark.

5. Sam Hinkie was a Bain consultant who read *Moneyball* and decided to pursue sports analytics — a career that "didn't really exist." Harvard gave him what he called "the hand" when he described his goal; Stanford enthusiastically introduced him to five relevant people. Hinkie's choice illustrates the book's thesis: institutions that encourage bespoke paths outperform those pushing students into predetermined tracks.

6. Jen Atkin moved to LA with $200 and enrolled in a free city cosmetology class. She became the Kardashians' primary stylist, then launched a haircare products company. The pattern is consistent across the book's profiles: deep domain obsession leads to expansion — mastery of one field gives people the confidence and learning habits to pursue adjacent ambitions.

7. Writing is not just a craft but a network-building weapon. Gurley launched a fax newsletter ("Above the Crowd") at the start of his Wall Street career, copying competitor Dave Korus at Kidder Peabody who had pioneered the format — and timing it two weeks after Korus quit, leaving a vacuum. Writing forced intellectual consistency ("an inner desire to not be intellectually inconsistent"), but its real payoff was fly paper: it made founders seek Gurley out, rather than requiring him to hunt for deals.

8. "Embrace Your Peers" is Gurley's most underrated principle: find 4–8 like-minded people *outside* your own company (more diversity, less competitive friction) and share ideas and networks continuously. His example — a group of college equipment managers who met at a conference and eventually became Division 1 Athletic Directors at Texas, Alabama, and Kentucky — illustrates what he calls "peer possibility": watching people you know succeed makes you believe you can succeed too.

9. "Go to the Epicenter" was not in Gurley's original video and gets the most reader feedback of any chapter. Physically relocating to the center of your field (Nashville for songwriting, New York for advertising) multiplies luck by 10x–100x — the chance encounters that every successful person credits as pivotal happen constantly in those places, and the density of like-minded obsessives puts all other principles (mentors, peers, learning) on steroids.

10. The 2017 Uber crisis involved simultaneous fires on seven fronts: an Eric Holder cultural investigation, a standoff with London regulators, five state Attorneys General, a Google IP-theft lawsuit, and accelerating market share losses to Lyft. The brand damage was so severe that some riders refused Uber for eight years out of principle — Gurley believes this materially impaired Uber's profitability for nearly a decade.

11. Gurley's unsolicited Uber/autonomous vehicles take: he pushed the company for over 10 years to open-source its self-driving assets rather than selling them off. The strategic logic mirrors Google's Android and Kubernetes plays — use open source to commoditize the layer below you and protect the layer you own. This is why Nvidia releasing open-source models is "not shocking": it's the same playbook.

12. Venture capital has industrialized: mega-funds now rival Apollo and private equity firms in AUM and are proactively forcing money into every successful startup. The resulting burn rates — $2 billion/year was "crazy" in the Uber era but no longer the largest — force companies to scale before mastering unit economics. Gurley compares it to *Game of Thrones*: "the 14-year-old is told they're king" in nearly every successful venture-backed company now.

13. Benchmark's small-fund model (held steady through Gurley's tenure) faces genuine structural pressure. Once you're writing $500M–$1B checks and managing Apollo-scale AUM, your best partners cannot maintain fidelity to a $5M seed — the incentives and attention simply diverge. Gurley believes early-stage-focused funds can still compete but must reckon with this reality rather than pretend it doesn't exist.

14. Both OpenAI and Anthropic concluded that being a pure "mid-layer model company" was existentially risky once open-source models matured. Both have moved to connect directly with end customers — Anthropic more visibly so in the past 12 months — even when that means competing with their own top-paying customers. Cursor, a major Anthropic customer, is actively evaluating other models precisely because of this dynamic.

15. The historical AI stack analogy: the early Internet ran on Sun servers and Oracle databases; five years later it was Linux and MySQL. Optimization cycles follow breakthrough cycles predictably. Benchmark has already invested in Fireworks and Cerebras — early infrastructure bets on the commoditization of frontier model inference that Gurley expects will follow.

16. SaaS multiples compress not because the businesses are broken but because discounted cash flow models are dominated by terminal value — and if the market prices in even modest probability that SaaS incumbents don't exist in 20–30 years, the multiple compression is severe. Gurley's contrarian take: if you believe SaaS survives, you should be buying at current prices rather than complaining on Twitter, and distressed SaaS could be one of the biggest PE opportunities in years.

17. Software is unlikely to die because the auditor problem alone prevents full bespoke replacement: financial ledgers need systematic equivalence across companies for auditors to function, so rebuilding them from scratch in AI is "really stupid." But SaaS companies that were "cookie-cutter artisan-free" in the growth era may permanently lose the multiples they once commanded, regardless of survival.

18. Gurley is planning to launch a policy institute called P3 after the book's promotional cycle. He sees video and podcasting as more efficient than long-form writing for synthesizing ideas, which explains why Above the Crowd's last non-book post was in 2022 — the medium shifted even if the underlying impulse to broadcast ideas didn't.

19. The book's success metric is not copies sold but career pivots triggered. A former schoolteacher who watched Gurley's original video now has a football analytics podcast, an app for organizing offensive plays, and is "well known in the football diaspora" — and called Gurley the week of publication to thank him. That transformation, multiplied across readers, is how Gurley measures the project.

20. Trust in peer networks is built through bidirectional vulnerability: people who never let their guard down are hard to get close to, and group chats only work with people who are willing to share something that shows vulnerability — without anyone taking advantage of it. Gurley's oldest group chat is "Gator Boys," his University of Florida drinking buddies, several of whom went on to notable careers; he credits proximity to two teammates who made the NBA as formative for his own ambition.

**Quotable:**
> "It reminds me of Game of Thrones where the 14-year-old has told they're king — that's what's happening in almost every one of these fields. It's not just Uber/Lyft now, it's almost every successful venture company." — *on mega-funds forcing capital into startups before they've mastered unit economics*

### An Interview with YouTube CEO Neal Mohan About Building a Stage for Creators 📌
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 38 mins

1. YouTube exists as a parallel ecosystem almost invisible to traditional media — the Made on YouTube annual product event, now in its fourth year, barely surfaces in Google search results for a tech journalist, yet the creators in the room have tens of millions of subscribers each. The gap isn't YouTube being small; it's YouTube being so creator-native that it operates outside the cultural lens of legacy media.

2. Mohan's path to YouTube CEO runs through DoubleClick: he joined NetGravity (one of the first internet ad-tech firms), merged into DoubleClick, left for his Stanford MBA, then returned to DoubleClick as product lead before Google acquired it in 2007 for $3.1 billion. His consistent throughline is building business infrastructure for content creators — whether they're web publishers in 2001 or YouTubers in 2025.

3. The DoubleClick strategy Mohan built (captured in a reportedly ~400-slide deck) was to kill the bespoke Madison Avenue model — where ad sales depended on personal lunches and handshake deals — and replace it with open, scalable infrastructure connecting publishers and advertisers at scale. That same democratization logic now underlies how YouTube thinks about creator monetization.

4. YouTube's Partner Program pays creators a 55/45 rev share — creators keep 55%. This structural alignment means YouTube's revenue growth and creator revenue growth are tightly coupled, which Mohan describes as intentional and foundational to the entire flywheel.

5. AI is a production tool, not a creator replacement. YouTube announced Veo 3 integrations for Shorts (video backgrounds, converting raw footage, speech-to-song) at Made on YouTube 2025, but Mohan frames these alongside the drum machine analogy: synthesizers didn't replace musicians, they became instruments. Dua Lipa performed at the event as the embodiment of the argument — audiences come for her, not the tools she used.

6. YouTube is structurally insulated from AI slop because 500 hours of content are uploaded every minute and their recommendation systems already filter for quality regardless of production method. The systems built to suppress spam and repetitive content apply equally to AI-generated filler; the algorithmic infrastructure predates the threat.

7. Brand deals and direct sponsorships represent a majority of revenue for many top creators — money that currently bypasses YouTube's rev share entirely. YouTube's new brand partnership tools (dynamic brand mentions, AI-powered auto-tagging) are framed as creator-empowerment, but also pull that off-platform revenue into YouTube's ecosystem where it can inform ad placement decisions and improve viewer experience.

8. Auto-tagging of products in videos solves a three-to-four-year failure of manual product tagging: creators found it too labor-intensive to implement at scale. AI now enables high-precision automated product identification across video at scale, turning YouTube into a discovery-commerce hybrid — Mohan compares the user behavior to going to the mall on a Saturday without a shopping list, where entertainment and shopping blur together.

9. YouTube's self-conception is "the stage, not the performer." Mohan explicitly refuses the creator role — he has a YouTube channel with minimal content and acknowledges he has "not even 1% of the talent" of the creators on the platform. YouTube's job is the technical infrastructure: the lighting, camera angles, algorithms, 4K rendering, TV OEM integrations — so creators can focus entirely on creative output.

10. YouTube is the #1 streaming service by watch time, including in the living room, a position built over nearly a decade of pre-COVID investment. The challenges were formidable: TV is a highly fragmented ecosystem across heterogeneous OEMs and device manufacturers. YouTube's solution involved investing in cross-device quality, bringing mobile interactivity to the big screen, and optimizing for the big canvas (4K, full-bleed channel previews, episodic content browsing).

11. YouTube operates three products on a TV continuum: YouTube TV (traditional linear with live sports and news), core YouTube (everything from 15-second Shorts to 15-hour livestreams), and Primetime Channels (a la carte streaming service subscriptions). Sunday Ticket is the leading Primetime Channels subscription — purchasable for the first time without a satellite dish or cable bundle, accessible with two taps on a phone.

12. The Sunday Ticket acquisition (widely criticized as an overpay) was justified on three grounds: super-serving YouTube's tens of millions of daily sports fans; adding YouTube-specific product innovations including Multiview, key plays, and fantasy integrations; and integrating the creator ecosystem into live sports via IShowSpeed and MrBeast appearances in actual NFL broadcasts, which reaches a measurably younger demographic for the NFL.

13. Multiview — watching multiple games simultaneously — was the first meaningful UX innovation in live sports TV since HD arrived, exposing how stagnant the bundled monopoly model had made the category. Bill Simmons spent 30 minutes on his podcast discussing it after launch. The feature is now being copied across competing streaming services.

14. The NFL Brazil game was broadcast free to 2 billion YouTube users worldwide in 1080p — the first time an NFL regular season game was freely accessible at global scale with no subscription required. Mohan describes this as a proof point for YouTube's vision of live sports as a free-with-ads product.

15. Creators retain ownership of their content on YouTube — no licensing of rights, no YouTube telling creators what to do with their videos. YouTube must "earn its stripes every single day by delivering audience and delivering revenue." This is the structural difference from traditional media deals and the reason YouTube argues creators should treat it as their primary home.

16. The "hamster wheel" pressure many creators feel — the sense they must post constantly or lose algorithmic relevance — is, per Mohan, a misconception. Major creators with tens of millions of subscribers post once a month or less and retain large audiences by setting expectations. The algorithm reflects audience expectations, not a mandate for frequency.

17. Creator culture on YouTube is non-zero-sum in part because the audience pool is so vast. MrBeast and other top creators actively push Mohan to ensure new tools reach up-and-coming creators, not just incumbents. Brandon (16 million subscribers), whose story was highlighted at Made on YouTube, started with pirated software and grew on a platform that imposed no gatekeepers on who could go on the stage.

18. YouTube is now the world's largest podcasting platform, having absorbed athletes, TV personalities, and traditional media figures alongside native YouTubers. In Mohan's view, the consumer distinction between "traditional media" and "YouTube creator" is already dissolving — Hollywood studios, sports leagues, and music labels are all among YouTube's largest partners and treat it as a primary distribution home.

19. Mohan's connection to YouTube predates the Google acquisition: as a DoubleClick executive, he visited Chad Hurley and Steve Chen at their office above a pizza parlor in San Mateo, California, and they showed him "crazy growth charts" demanding DoubleClick's ad servers keep pace. The core observation then — humans sharing stories with other humans who want to watch them — remains unchanged across 20 years of platform evolution.

**Quotable:**
> "What I know how to do, what my teams know how to do is build the world's best stage, like the best seats in the house, the best lighting, the best camera angles, that is our focus. The people who are on the stage are those creative geniuses and that's what they're really good at." — *Mohan explaining YouTube's self-conception as infrastructure, not content*

## Technology & Business Models

### The slow decay of growth (and how to avoid it) 📌
**Kyle Poyar** · email · 7 mins

1. **SaaS growth endurance is collapsing, and the numbers are worse than most founders realize.** In a dataset of 700+ private software companies (all with ≥$10k MRR, data back to 2023, and at least 20% YoY growth in 2024), the median growth endurance was 43% — meaning a company that grew 65% in 2024 only grew 28% in 2025. Extrapolated forward: 12% in 2026, 5% in 2027. A $10M ARR company ends up at $15M by end of 2027; a top-quartile growth endurance company (80%+) reaches $29M. Only 18% of startups maintained or accelerated their growth rate, and fewer than 1-in-25 companies growing 25–49% in 2024 managed to hit 100%+ growth in 2025. Palantir is the only well-known public company with consistent 100%+ growth endurance; most great public companies aim for 80%+.

2. **Compressing sales velocity — not just speeding up individual steps — is the fastest lever to pull.** ChartMogul eliminated planning-heavy collaboration meetings, imposed a one-hour SLA on lead follow-up, and built a Claude-powered internal tool that reviews call transcripts twice daily to flag missed opportunities rather than waiting for a weekly forecast review. Benji Pays (AR automation) inflected subscriber growth in December by removing third-party onboarding dependencies and splitting its sales function into two focused roles. Aesthetix CRM (vertical software for plastic surgeons and med spas) sped up onboarding, used internal AI tooling to support 500+ locations without adding headcount, and shifted upmarket toward multi-location enterprise deals, driving a visible increase in average revenue per account. influencers.club moved from quarterly to weekly sprints to keep pace with AI-driven market shifts.

3. **Repeatable campaigns outperform one-off tactics; the average software company is wasting effort on 5.5 GTM channel experiments that rarely compound.** Every (AI media and software company) saw growth inflect in February after shifting from treating launches as one-and-done announcements to "bundling" releases around a broader theme — their Agent Native campaign included livestreams, guides, and public GitHub repos that kept conversation alive for weeks. AskElephant (AI workflow automation) built a lightweight co-sell referral program through accounting and RevOps partners; those partner-sourced deals close faster and retain better than cold outbound. The highest-ROI channels by stage: LinkedIn + warm outbound + founder brand under $1M ARR; warm outbound + LinkedIn + intent-based outbound at $1–10M; large conferences + SEO + paid ads above $10M.

4. **Small price increases (10–20%) give a one-time boost; structural pricing changes can reshape growth rates for multiple years.** Liveblocks (real-time infrastructure for multiplayer apps) relaunched with a revamped pay-as-you-go offering and a more transparent pricing page, producing a clear spike in self-serve customers. Predis (AI ad generator) switched from a free trial to a credit card-required trial on September 10, 2025 — MRR spiked immediately, stabilized at a higher baseline, and is now 3× year-over-year. Fyxer saw a similar pattern, with free-to-paid conversion jumping from 5% to 35% after adding a credit card gate. The credit card requirement filters for serious users, which allows the product team to offer more trial value, improving both conversion and downstream retention.

**Quotable:**
> "Instead of treating launches as one-and-done announcements, we bundle related releases and content around a broader theme. That gives us multiple touchpoints over several weeks, creates more ways for people to engage, and keeps the conversation alive beyond day one." — *Brandon Gell, COO at Every, on their Agent Native campaign*

### Paying Up for the Right to be Sued 📎
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. Software companies become full-stack operators when their product creates more value than customers will pay for in SaaS form. If a tool can add 5 points of EBITDA margin but customers won't spend more than 1% of revenue on software, the natural move is to just *be* the customer — as Metropolis (parking software) did by acquiring SP+ (garage operator), or Thoma Bravo did by buying a logistics company to combine with its Auctane software rollup.

2. New technology categories face opposing forces on vertical integration: novel problems push companies toward building everything themselves (enterprise software categories like source control and CI/CD all started as internal hacks before anyone named them), while broadly useful technology pushes toward staying horizontal — early AT&T didn't start an NYSE specialist firm to profit from phones; it just sold phones to everyone and collected an effective royalty on the entire economy's expansion.

3. AI agents break the asset-light business model's liability logic. A small AI startup can cause losses that dwarf its own net worth — through hallucinations, prompt injections, or data leakage — leaving enterprise customers with no one solvent to sue. When a lawyer uses a legal tech tool that makes a mistake, you hold the lawyer responsible; when you use an LLM directly (as with an LLM-drafted contract), the loss is entirely yours. Harvey's positioning captures the dodge: "Delegate the work. Own the judgement" — meaning use their agents, but don't blame Harvey when something goes wrong.

4. The insurance market is responding but remains nascent. AIUC runs 5,000+ adversarial simulations against agent systems (covering hallucinations, prompt injections, data leakage) to build risk profiles for underwriting. Policies currently cover up to $50M in business losses; ElevenLabs was the first major agent vendor to sign on, meaning Fortune 500 companies deploying ElevenLabs voice agents now have a path to insuring hallucination-caused losses. This addresses the liability gap but doesn't resolve the underlying misalignment of incentives for tiny startups.

5. The structural solution is for AI labs to acquire operating businesses in their target verticals — paying for the right to be sued. An operating business gives an AI lab three things: a captive customer for proving out the product, a proof of concept with real-world outcomes, and assets that can be seized and cash flows that can be garnished if something goes wrong. Owning enough assets to be worth suing is itself a commitment device — it means the lab's incentive is now to avoid dumb, avoidable mistakes rather than just collect subscription fees and disclaim liability.

**Quotable:**
> "So it's a way for AI labs to grant themselves the immensely valuable right to be sued; enough at stake that their incentive is to avoid dumb, avoidable mistakes." — *on why AI labs acquiring operating businesses is a feature, not a bug*
