### Money Stuff: AI Builds Some Pyramids ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg Money Stuff)** · rss · 17 mins

1. **AI founders face a structural contradiction: they want control but must raise enormous capital.** Elon Musk's SpaceX IPO pushed the standard solution — dual-class stock — to its logical extreme: he gave himself super-voting stock, the unilateral right to appoint a majority of the board, and near-total immunity from shareholder lawsuits. Investors still put in $75 billion at a giant valuation.

2. **DeepSeek revived the 1920s "pyramiding" technique to achieve even more complete founder control.** Chinese AI lab DeepSeek raised over 50 billion yuan ($7.4 billion) by routing investors into a limited partnership managed by CEO Liang Wenfeng — not into DeepSeek itself. External investors get zero voting rights, a five-year lockup, and no path to influence the company. The structure echoes 1920s-era pyramid holding companies (Company A → Company B owns 70% of A → Company C owns 70% of B), where a founder retains voting control despite owning only ~34% of the economics.

3. **The shared logic: the goals of an AI founder are to raise as much money as possible and give investors as little control as possible — and so far, this proves surprisingly easy.** DeepSeek's $50 billion+ valuation alongside zero investor voting rights is the most extreme version yet. Musk's SpaceX at $1.77 trillion IPO with supermajority board appointment rights is the public-market analogue.

4. **A Polymarket trader lost nearly $1 million betting on what looked like a near-certainty: Spain beating Cabo Verde at the World Cup.** The trader, "betoor619," wagered ~$1.1 million when Spain's odds stood at 92%; a win would have returned only ~$85,000. Cabo Verde drew Spain with a 40-year-old goalkeeper. Structurally, rolling $1M bets on extreme favorites has bond-like expected returns with uncorrelated tail risk — the downside is zero when the 8% scenario happens.

5. **This points to a plausible (if absurd) financial product: a prediction-markets index ETF.** Collect the 100 biggest event contracts on Kalshi and Polymarket, weight by open interest, average prices — and TV anchors could say "prediction markets were up today." The SEC delayed a wave of prediction-market ETFs in May 2026; a pure-index version may follow within two years.

6. **Polymarket demonstrated that prediction markets optimize for fast settlement, not truth, in the Strategy/Bitcoin case.** Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy) sold 32 Bitcoins in the week ended May 31. Polymarket had a contract "MicroStrategy sells any Bitcoin by May 31?" and resolved it "No" — without waiting one day for Strategy's routine Monday announcement on June 1. Hunter Guo, a 20-year-old Kings College London student, had bought thousands of "Yes" contracts after the sale was announced, anticipating a Porsche with his winnings. His position was wiped within seconds. The episode illustrates: prediction markets are consumer gambling platforms designed for finality, not forums for establishing historical facts.

7. **Cayman Islands appraisal arbitrage is losing its asymmetric upside, mirroring what happened in Delaware in ~2018.** The old trade: buy a merger-target's stock near deal price, sue for appraisal, and either collect the deal price plus interest (downside) or more (upside). In the 51job Inc. case — a Chinese recruiting firm taken private in 2022 at $61/share — Cayman Islands Justice David Doyle ruled the company was worth only $31.11/share. Hedge funds including Oasis Management, Pentwater, Millennium, and Man Group may have to collectively repay ~$525 million in merger premium already received. Appeal is set for July.

8. **SpaceX's acquisition of Cursor for $60 billion illustrates how "private markets are the new public markets" cuts against sellers.** SpaceX struck an option to buy Cursor in April when SpaceX was private; the option was exercised post-IPO. SpaceX went public at a $1.77 trillion valuation and rose to ~$2.75 trillion. Cursor receives $60 billion of SpaceX stock at the post-IPO public market price — not the private valuation. If the stock had been issued in April, it would be worth ~$90 billion today. Cursor sold itself for a fixed-dollar claim on a private company and missed the entire IPO pop.

9. **KPMG published a report titled "Redefining excellence in the age of agentic AI" that contained AI-hallucinated case studies about UBS, the NHS, Swiss Federal Railways, and Transport for London.** GPTZero CEO Edward Tian warned that false publications by the Big Four "poison the well of information" because they are then cited by other outlets as credible sources — creating "second-hand hallucinations." The recursive vulnerability: AI models train on trusted human output, and trusted human institutions are now laundering AI confabulations as authoritative research.

**Quotable:**
> "The naive view is 'this contract should resolve according to what actually happened,' whereas the sophisticated view is 'we are running a market here and what matters is quick final resolution, not absolute truth.'" — *on Polymarket resolving the Strategy/Bitcoin contract "No" one day before Strategy's announcement confirmed the sale*

### Money Stuff: The Stock Market Will Get More Stock ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg Money Stuff)** · rss · 16 mins

1. **The US stock market is flipping from capital-return machine to capital-raising machine.** For nearly two decades, S&P 500 buybacks erased almost $12 trillion in shares — a sustained "de-equitization" that removed supply and acted as a persistent floor under prices. JPMorgan now projects that IPOs, secondary offerings, and other share sales will add roughly $1.5 trillion net to US equity markets over the next two years, the strongest net issuance since the late 1990s.

2. **AI is the mechanism forcing this reversal.** Hyperscalers initially funded data centers, chips, and power infrastructure from cash flow, then debt. StoneX Financial's Vincent Deluard captures the progression: "We started with profits and free cash flow, then they started raising debt. Now it's everything — cash flow, debt, and equity." Net share repurchases by hyperscalers fell last year (Citigroup data), meaning the biggest source of buyback support has already partially dried up.

3. **The removal of buybacks as a market prop is the hidden risk.** Robert Buckland, the Citigroup strategist who popularized the term "de-equitisation," warns: "When a correction happens — which it will — it is harder for these guys to prop their stock up than it was before, as when Meta bought lots of Meta shares back after its stock crashed [in 2021-2022]." Companies now shifting capital to capex rather than buybacks lose a key defense mechanism for their own share prices.

4. **SpaceX's IPO was the biggest ever and hit the textbook "Goldilocks" target almost exactly.** Pricing at $135/share ($1.77T valuation — 100x revenue, negative earnings), SpaceX closed its first trading day at $160.95, a 19% gain, ending as the sixth-largest public company in the world at $2.2T. The widely cited "perfect IPO pop" target is 20%, balancing investor reward against money left on the table — SpaceX landed at 19%.

5. **Pre-IPO perpetual futures on crypto platforms beat the official price discovery process.** SpaceX PERP contracts on Hyperliquid traded in the mid-$160s to low-$170s the day before the IPO — a range that bracketed where the stock actually opened Friday. Major institutional investors were actively monitoring Hyperliquid in real time during the days leading up to the IPO. TD Securities analyst Reid Noch argues this signals that future IPO candidates (including OpenAI and Anthropic) may use these markets as additional signals to calibrate offering prices.

6. **Crypto perpetuals now rival traditional markets in scale, blurring the line between speculation and price discovery.** Annual volume in crypto perpetual futures exceeds $90 trillion, per Bank of America. The same convergence that lets retail traders place sports bets in brokerage accounts is enabling institutional investors to track decentralized gambling markets to price the largest IPO in history — what BofA strategists call the "gamification" of markets.

7. **Ken Griffin figured out at age 18 that market-making beats stock-picking on "quality of earnings."** While at Harvard, Griffin made $5,000 on Home Shopping Network options — but was bothered that the market maker who took the other side, though earning less per trade, had a far more reliable business. He taught himself derivatives theory in the HBS library, settled on convertible arbitrage (long convertible bond, short the underlying stock) by 1989, and built Citadel into a firm where the goal is not to be right but to be right *most* of the time: top portfolio managers win on only ~54% of trades.

8. **Citadel operates as a human-consuming factory, not a fund.** As early partner Alec Litowitz put it: "It's not about a portfolio, it's about a business that can re-create great portfolios again and again." The organizational reality is brutal — one former employee kept a "Book of Souls" listing 50 colleagues who left a single small trading unit over six years; another had five bosses in five years. Citadel's official characterization: "We have deliberately built a high-performance culture."

9. **Nubank accidentally sent customers a push notification saying the bank had been liquidated.** A Nubank employee triggered a liquidation-notification protocol while submitting an internal software change request. Co-founder Cristina Junqueira confirmed on Instagram it reached "a very small portion of customers," but the near-miss illustrates a core banking danger: a message telling customers the money is gone is nearly equivalent in effect to the money actually being gone.

**Quotable:**
> "We started with profits and free cash flow, then they started raising debt. Now it's everything. It's the cash flow, it's the debt and it's the equity." — *StoneX Financial's Vincent Deluard on how Big Tech is financing the AI buildout*

### N > 1 ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. Satya Nadella published an essay arguing AI won't concentrate the economy into a handful of big labs — the "N > 1" thesis. He's probably right on the merits: many businesses hold proprietary data and relationship-based value that AI labs can't easily internalize. But Nadella also watched Microsoft's stake in OpenAI erode even as OpenAI became one of the most valuable companies on earth, giving him both a genuine insight and a financial motive for that view to be true.

2. Many sectors face structural forces that cap their scale, keeping them naturally fragmented. A pool installation contractor has solid economics in one city, worse in two, and only recovers scalability past half a dozen geographies. Personal injury law has a few regional firms and exactly one national player — Morgan & Morgan. These businesses can't consolidate into a winner-take-all shape, no matter how much AI improves their productivity.

3. Fragmented industries still pay for centralized information layers sitting above them, and AI accelerates this dynamic on the supply side too. A local contractor buys leads from a national platform; a personal injury lawyer can now use EvenUp to generate more effective demand letters. The pattern is: execution stays local, the intelligence layer centralizes — and whoever owns that layer captures margin from thousands of fragmented operators.

4. Token-rich professions — lawyers, doctors, accountants, therapists — generate enormous amounts of training data and have enough political concentration to lobby their way to a specific AI outcome: AI as a productivity tool for them, not a competitor against them. This is enforceable: a profession that can demand licensing and liability rules can also shape which AI products are allowed to touch its work. The natural result is consolidation into 2-3 approved AI tools per profession, not 20-30.

5. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon all benefit from maintaining a fragmented downstream economy, so they actively push the market structure Nadella claims to merely observe. Microsoft introduces SMBs to AI via managed service providers. Google wants more companies bidding for search placement and AI summaries. Amazon wants more merchants competing on price while bidding up ad rates. Each platform's own value scales with the number of players it intermediates, creating a structural interest in keeping customers numerous and competitive.

6. **Elsewhere — OpenAI financials:** OpenAI hit $13bn in 2025 revenue, beating its own $12bn projection by ~$1bn. Inference margin expanded from 28% in 2024 to 43% in 2025. Operating losses reached $21bn as it reinvests aggressively, but cash burn was ~$8bn — the gap is non-cash. OpenAI's real edge may not be technical but financial: its unmatched fundraising ability makes the rational strategy "be present at every stack layer, then retreat gracefully from whatever becomes a commodity."

7. **Elsewhere — AI ads & self-help:** Adobe's quarterly ad data shows AI-originated traffic converts better for retail and stays on sites longer; WPP raised its global ad spending forecast from +7.1% to +8.9%. Ad growth is the cleanest proxy for AI's economic impact because prices are sticky but customer acquisition budgets aren't. Meanwhile, self-help book sales are collapsing — chatbots are a near-perfect substitute for the private use case (reading in embarrassment about a personal problem), and apparently that was most of the demand.

**Quotable:**
> "OpenAI seems to want to own a piece of everything... one of its biggest *relative* advantages is its ability to raise capital, which means that the best move is to have some presence at every layer of the stack and then gracefully retreat from whichever layer turns out to be either a commodity or too competitive." — *on OpenAI's full-stack expansion strategy*

### We Built Our Own Agent-native Tool. It Overhauled How We Build Software. 📌
**Stella Garber** · rss · 6 mins

1. Hoop, a 5-person pre-PMF startup, was drowning in fragmented customer discovery data — call transcripts in Granola, notes in Slack, interpretations in Claude Code, no shared format. Cofounder Justin (CS background, not actively coding) built a V1 fix in under 10 hours: upload a Zoom transcript, run it through 4–5 prompts, get a structured analysis scored against the PULL framework (a Harvard Business School PMF tool), plus a per-prospect summary aggregating every conversation. Stack was Next.js + ShadCN, Supabase, and the Claude API.

2. V1 had two fatal flaws: friction and dumb search. Users had to manually download transcripts, upload them, fill in metadata, wait minutes, then share a link — every step a dropout opportunity. Worse, search was keyword-only: querying "prospects burned by AI customer support" returned zero results even though one head of CX had spent five minutes describing the embarrassment of their AI sending off-brand replies. Semantic meaning was invisible to the tool.

3. Switching to agent-native architecture fixed both problems. Instead of hard-coding a fixed prompt sequence, Justin gave Claude a small set of tools (upload transcript, edit partner profile) and let it reason about how to use them. The interface moved to Slack — where the team already worked — so users just dropped a transcript into a channel. The agent confirmed details, ran analysis, built a summary page, and posted results to the feedback channel automatically. Justin then used Claude Code's planning mode to audit every new feature against the agent-native principles, deliberately excluding token cost tracking from the agent's toolset since it required no reasoning.

**Quotable:**
> "Instead of building a destination app that requires people to come to you, you bring the tool to where people already work." — *on why agent-native + Slack beat a standalone web app*

### Repo, On-chain 📎
**Lombard Notes** · email · 9 mins

1. DeFi protocols are building leveraged structures on top of tokenized real-world assets (RWAs) — depositing a tokenized T-Bill on Morpho or Euler, borrowing a stablecoin against it, buying more T-Bills, and redepositing in a loop. RWA TVL grew from $15 billion to over $33 billion in less than 18 months, and the plumbing now handles institutional scale. The underlying logic is identical to traditional repo; what's new is that it runs on open, composable infrastructure with no intermediary controlling the loop.

2. A second structural layer has emerged: asset managers wrap looping strategies into on-chain vaults, and depositors receive receipt tokens that themselves become collateral in the same lending markets — a claim on a claim. Protocols like 3F.xyz implement this directly, using tokenized private credit as the loop asset and making the vault receipt token redeployable as collateral. This creates a new actor called the "curator" — an on-chain entity that sets LTV parameters, selects collateral types, and chooses oracles, effectively structuring a credit product without a balance sheet or a banking license.

3. On-chain repo democratizes access that traditional repo structurally withholds. The legal minimum to open a prime brokerage account is $500,000 in equity, and tier-1 prime brokers only work with funds at meaningful AUM scale; GMRA documentation, credit assessment, and relationship management add further friction. On Morpho or Euler, any permissioned wallet runs the same trade with no minimum ticket and no relationship requirement — opening the yield differential between private credit returns and stablecoin borrow rates to smaller hedge funds, family offices, and sophisticated individuals.

4. The transparency properties are reversed between the two systems: the $4.6 trillion non-centrally-cleared bilateral repo segment is so opaque that the Fed only launched a dedicated data collection initiative in December 2024, and aggregate leverage on any single collateral is not reconstructable from any source, even by regulators. On-chain, every position, parameter, and leverage stack is readable by anyone in real time. That cuts both ways — systemic concentration becomes measurable, but publicly visible concentration can also accelerate coordinated exits in stress scenarios faster than TradFi's opacity-dampened contagion.

5. Settlement speed is the sharpest operational difference, and it is also where on-chain repo's greatest risk lives. Building a 5x leveraged position on a T+1 TradFi asset takes roughly 20 days to enter and 20 to unwind; on-chain it can be constructed and unwound in minutes. But automated liquidation bots execute within a single block simultaneously across all affected positions — with no margin call, no dispute window, and no human renegotiation. For tokenized RWAs with quarterly redemption windows or thin secondary markets, the liquidation mechanism assumes a speed of exit the underlying asset structurally cannot support, creating a critical mismatch that becomes acute under stress.

6. Three scenarios govern the outcome. First, regulatory: jurisdictions decide on-chain transparency doesn't compensate for reduced supervisory control and institutional capital exits. Second, endogenous collapse: a liquidation cascade on illiquid RWA collateral produces losses that discredit the model before it reaches scale — a plausible outcome given the speed-redemption mismatch. Third — and most likely — convergence: the system neither fails nor stays distinct, but curators acquire legal and compliance infrastructure, vaults get wrapped into regulated fund structures, and oracle providers become pricing services. The on-chain rails survive but everything built on top acquires a direct institutional equivalent.

**Quotable:**
> "The system does not fail but converges toward what it was meant to differ from: curators acquire the legal and compliance infrastructure of asset managers; vaults get wrapped into regulated fund structures and oracle providers take on the role of pricing services." — *on the most probable long-term trajectory of on-chain repo*

### Fox Buys Roku, The Problem With Fox's Smart Strategy, Streaming That Works 📎
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 8 mins

1. Fox is acquiring Roku for ~$25 billion ($160/share: $96 cash + 0.9693 Fox Class A shares), funded with $12 billion in new debt plus cash on hand, with $400 million in planned annual cost cuts. The market punished the deal immediately — Fox stock fell 17% (a record single-day drop), while Roku's effective deal value dropped to ~$149/share based on Fox's new price, just 5.8% above Roku's closing price.

2. Fox's strategic track record since 2017 supports giving the deal the benefit of the doubt. When Fox sold 21st Century Fox to Disney, the logic was to shed content "filler" and keep only the differentiated, live-event assets (news and sports) that retain pricing power with distributors. That bet has paid off: Disney stock is essentially flat since the March 2019 deal close ($106.47 open → $100.04), while Fox's Class B shares are up ~32.86% from their $35.89 open.

3. Fox's lean-into-live strategy has a structural flaw it hasn't solved: sports rights holders — the Big Ten, NFL, etc. — have wholesale transfer pricing power over Fox, making the network essentially a conduit that moves money from fans to leagues. Fox doesn't own the sports content it airs; it rents it at ever-rising prices, and as the traditional cable bundle shrinks, Fox's bargaining leverage within that ecosystem has only weakened.

4. FAST (Free Ad-Supported Streaming Television) is the escape hatch. Tubi — acquired by Fox for $400 million in 2020 — grew revenue 27% to $1.1 billion in 2025 on 16% viewership growth. Roku's ad revenue hit $2.3 billion in 2025 (up 13%), only disclosed publicly in April 2026 — almost certainly to attract a buyer. The combined FAST entity gives Fox dominant scale in a segment where being big directly increases advertiser value, unlike subscription streaming where scale is harder to monetize.

5. The deal rebalances Fox's leverage position. In the current sports rights model, Fox bleeds leverage upward to rights holders. In the FAST model, scale accrues to the aggregator: Fox/Roku as the combined platform gains bargaining power over struggling content owners like Paramount (which is taking on massive debt to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery and will need Pluto TV revenue), can offer advertisers a single scaled buy across Tubi, Roku Channel, and Fox One, and can cross-promote sports on its FAST channels to grow subscriptions. The core risk is the $12B debt load and execution complexity — exactly the trap that drowned Warner Bros. Discovery.

**Quotable:**
> "Fox is getting exactly that, pointing towards a future where the current rights-renting business that bleeds leverage to rights holders funds the build-out of a new rights-renting business — FAST — that builds leverage for the renter in a way that only advertising can." — *on why the Roku acquisition is strategically coherent despite the market's hostility*
