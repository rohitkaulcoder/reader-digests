### The new inner game: Your unfair advantage in the age of AI ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 18 mins

1. The traditional skills workers have optimized for — effort and knowledge — are now the two things AI does best, making them commodities in the same way machines commoditized physical labor a century ago. Fear is a poor planner in response: it creates binary thinking and primes you for scenarios that never arrive, the same way shower-rehearsed difficult conversations almost never unfold as scripted.

2. AI is triggering the "NBA-ification" of teams: individual amplification means organizations flatten, headcount shrinks, and far more capital rides on each person. Anthropic, Amazon, Shopify, Coinbase, and Block are already creating "player-coach" roles and eliminating management layers — so every broken relationship or unresolved tension is no longer diluted across an org, it's a structural crack in a tight roster.

3. The four skills that differentiate people inside AI labs — what Joe Hudson calls the "wisdom stack" — are discernment, productive conflict ("in conflict we trust"), willingness to fail, and positive self-talk. None of these were traditionally thought of as trainable skills, and none can be outsourced to a model.

4. Discernment degrades not from lack of data but from emotional avoidance. Neuroscience shows feelings are "context-setters" for the rational brain, so unconsciously dodging an emotion narrows your solution space. A CEO Hudson coached couldn't kill any initiative because he feared disappointing people — after working through that fear, his company cut 40% of active projects within six months and revenue per employee jumped.

5. The "Golden Algorithm" reveals how avoidance creates the exact outcome feared: avoiding failure → playing it safe → never winning big → feeling like a failure; avoiding conflict → people-pleasing → constant inner conflict → deeper conflict. Identifying and sitting with the avoided emotion — rather than managing around it — is the corrective practice.

6. AI can draft a difficult message, rehearse a conversation ten times, and produce a perfectly worded apology — but it cannot make you say the hard thing or put down the position you've been defending. Johannes, CEO of Ona (formerly Gitpod), took his team through a two-day conflict-surfacing summit and reported: "Our team did six months of work in two days. Every interpersonal conflict that was standing between us just went away."

7. The "creative genius" who generates breakthroughs is simply someone who took more shots faster: James Dyson built 5,126 failing vacuums before one that worked; Michael Jordan missed over 9,000 shots. The obstacle isn't philosophy — it's the habenula, a brain structure that cuts motivation the moment it registers failure, making deep experimentation genuinely rare even in cultures that claim to embrace it.

8. Anthropic's product team operationalizes rapid iteration through "side quests": any employee can spend an afternoon prototyping an idea outside the official roadmap with no approval, PRD, or alignment meeting. If teammates keep using it the next day, it gets polished and shipped; if nobody touches it, it dies. The metric optimized is not quality of any single idea but the rate at which new ideas hit reality and get tested.

9. Self-criticism shuts down exactly the capabilities that differentiate humans in the AI era. Perseverative cognition research shows the body responds to a thought about a threat identically to an actual threat — spiking cortisol, adrenaline, heart rate, and blood pressure. You cannot out-grind a server farm; the internal voice that once drove harder work now suppresses discernment, conflict tolerance, and experimentation. Hudson's program has measured a full standard deviation improvement in negative self-talk across participants over seven years.

10. A team's culture is its collective relationship to fear, and it's measurable with three questions: How fast does a hard conversation get surfaced? How fast does it reach aligned resolution? How empowered do people feel? Two SaaS companies Hudson coached — one "nice," one "mean" — both organized themselves around avoiding hard conversations and were both losing market share to competitors, demonstrating that pleasant culture and hostile culture can be equally fear-driven.

**Quotable:**
> "You can buy more compute. It's a lot harder to buy your way out of the conversations your team is avoiding." — *on why productive conflict is the scarcest resource in the AI era*

### Money Stuff: Short Squeeze as a Service ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg Money Stuff)** · rss · 17 mins

1. Pentwater Capital accumulated a position representing over 50% of Avis Budget Group's outstanding shares this April — combined with SRS Investment Management (run by an Avis board member) also holding 50%+, more than 100% of the float was effectively locked up by two insiders. This was possible because ~49% of the float was simultaneously sold short, so Pentwater and SRS held all the real shares plus some of the borrowed-and-shorted ones.

2. The structural short squeeze followed mechanically: with virtually no free-float available, short sellers faced the prospect of lenders demanding shares back with nowhere to buy. Avis stock rocketed from ~$100 in mid-March to a peak of $713.97 on April 21, 2025 — a 600%+ move in roughly one month.

3. Pentwater sold ~4.3 million shares during the squeeze for approximately $1.75 billion (average ~$400/share), pocketing over $1 billion in profit. The problem: Section 16 short-swing profit rules require any "insider" (10%+ holder) who buys and sells within six months to disgorge profits to the company — Pentwater couldn't legally keep those gains.

4. Pentwater argued that most trades were not "matchable" under Section 16 because it bought through some funds and sold through different funds — claiming only ~94,000 shares were technically matchable (worth tens of millions), leaving ~$1 billion of profits outside Section 16's reach. Avis disagreed; that dispute became the negotiation.

5. Pentwater's settlement pitch was structurally elegant: "We extracted ~$1 billion from short sellers — your enemies — at zero cost to you. No dilution, no money from loyal shareholders. Keep most of it; we want a fee." The $650 million settlement (pending court approval, representing roughly 10% of Avis's total market cap) is the outcome — Avis gets a nine-figure windfall it did nothing to earn.

6. Avis CEO Brian Choi publicly framed Pentwater as the villain who drove the stock *down* from $700, ignoring that Avis stock is now trading at ~$186, sustainably higher than the ~$100 pre-Pentwater level. The "short squeeze as a service" model — a non-dilutive, adversarially-financed capital raise — may now get pitched to other heavily-shorted companies.

7. MSCI gave SpaceX the lowest possible ESG rating (CCC — equal to Russia's post-2022 sovereign rating) one day before its $75 billion IPO. The governance score is objectively zero: public investors get no meaningful voting rights, no enforceable fiduciary duties, and no constraints on Elon Musk's behavior. The "E" and "S" components are subjective, but on "G" there is no serious counterargument.

8. Private equity hold periods have extended to 7 years on average (up from 5-6 historically), creating multi-year gaps between mark-to-market gains and actual cash distributions. UBS, Citi, and Deutsche Bank now offer non-recourse loans secured solely against unrealized carried interest — if the carry never materializes, lenders cannot pursue the executive's personal assets.

9. Bain & Company is using "vibecoding" (AI-generated code mockups) during PE due diligence to rapidly reconstruct pieces of target companies' software products. The implicit investment rule this creates: if your consultants can recreate the target's core product from scratch in a few hours, the software's moat is defensibility theater and the deal should be passed.

10. An AI law firm (Garfield AI, the first UK-regulated AI law firm) won a civil case at Wandsworth County Court using AI for all legal analysis and document preparation, while still instructing a human barrister for oral advocacy. Meanwhile, investment banking clients are using AI to generate sharper technical questions and compare pitches across banks, which may compress the fees banks can charge.

**Quotable:**
> "It would be difficult for a serious ESG data provider, or for a fund applying its own ESG screening, not to identify major governance concerns at SpaceX." — *Frédéric Ducoulombier, programme director at Edhec's Climate Institute, on SpaceX's CCC ESG rating*

### Should You Trust the Hacker News Peanut Gallery? ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. **HN S-1 discussions have five distinct commenter types — only one is useful.** When a tech company's S-1 drops, Hacker News threads mix genuine enterprise buyers, confident-but-inexperienced armchair experts, ex-employees/competitors, GAAP-loss worriers, and macro doomsayers. That heterogeneity makes the aggregate signal noisy, but it also means the signal can be improved dramatically by filtering to the right subset.

2. **A $44 OpenAI API run produced a measurable trading signal.** Using Algolia's HN search and the HN API, every S-1 comment thread was tagged with sentiment and category via an LLM pipeline (using a multi-armed bandit to route between gpt-5.4-nano at 1/150th the cost and a full reasoning model where needed). The result: a long/short strategy buying IPOs HN liked and shorting ones it didn't, hedged against the S&P, achieved a Sharpe ratio of ~0.48 vs. the baseline ~0.40 — a modest but real edge, concentrated in the first quarter post-IPO before the consensus gets priced in.

3. **Product sentiment is the only category that produces positive Sharpe; everything else destroys value.** Breaking the comments down by topic, product-focused sentiment achieves a Sharpe of 0.55. Financial analysis, founder assessments, governance concerns, and macro commentary all produce negative returns when traded. HN macro commentary is the worst of all: following it as a market-timing signal over the past decade would have returned 13%, versus 320% from just holding the S&P.

4. **The signal is asymmetric: buy the love, ignore the hate.** Going long IPOs that HN commenters liked produced better risk-adjusted returns than shorting the ones they disliked. The "haters" on HN are not a reliable contrarian indicator for shorts — dismissing negative sentiment rather than fading it is the right move.

5. **The signal is real but not deployable in isolation — data quality and structural ambiguity limit it.** The finding would only fit into a "factor zoo" framework that screens millions of strategies for spuriousness before deploying any single one. Edge cases like WeWork — where HN hated the 2019 S-1 but the investable vehicle was a 2021 SPAC — illustrate the judgment calls that complicate systematic backtesting. The conclusion largely confirms what a savvy human reader would have guessed: products matter, GAAP metrics lag, macro is hard, haters are noise.

**Quotable:**
> "We've used a fair bit of machine intelligence to more or less validate the first-pass result that biological intelligence would reveal if it had some of the relevant facts in its context window. But it's nice to be a little more sure." — *closing the piece, on the meta-lesson of the entire exercise*

### Greenspan's legacy is as convoluted as his words 📎
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. **Greenspan's Fed fulfilled every part of its triple mandate — and then some.** Inflation, unemployment, and long-term interest rates all improved across his 18-year chairmanship (1987–2006). Aside from a brief, shallow recession triggered by Saddam Hussein's 1990 Kuwait invasion, the US never posted a single quarter of falling year-on-year GDP, not even after the dot-com crash — a record helped by globalization, the internet, and an influx of Mexican migrants that the Fed wisely did not obstruct.

2. **A single pivotal sequence in 1998 turned the Maestro into a moral hazard machine.** Greenspan had already warned of "irrational exuberance" in December 1996 and nudged rates up. But when Russia's debt default triggered the Long-Term Capital Management meltdown in fall 1998, he organized a Fed-brokered LTCM bailout (to Paul Volcker's public disgust) and cut rates. A stock market that had looked like it peaked instead melted upward. Then Y2K fears prompted another liquidity injection before he finally pulled the plug — creating a boom-bust sequence that Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent is now citing selectively to argue for rate cuts today.

3. **The "Greenspan Put" institutionalized recklessness.** After LTCM, markets concluded that 401(k)s and asset prices had become so systemically important that the Fed would always ease into any stock decline. Circumstantial evidence in the Fed funds rate vs. equity charts supported this reading. The resulting moral hazard — risk-taking backed by an implicit government guarantee — persisted well beyond Greenspan's tenure and is the darkest part of his legacy.

4. **A structural "conundrum" kept long rates low even as the Fed hiked, blowing a housing bubble.** From 2004 onward, the Fed raised short-term rates repeatedly, yet the 10-year Treasury yield barely moved — what Greenspan himself labeled a conundrum. China's accession to the WTO in 2001 had set off a massive accumulation of US Treasury reserves that suppressed long rates globally. Cheap mortgage money poured into housing; prices peaked on the eve of Greenspan's 2006 departure. Money is fungible: the liquidity meant to cushion the dot-com bust funded the subprime crisis. Current Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, who wants to return to Greenspan-style opaque non-committal communication, now inherits the debate over whether that era's legacy vindicates or indicts the approach.

**Quotable:**
> "The Fed needs to have merely an open mind. The open-mind maestro, former Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan, resisted premature rate hikes during the technology boom of the 1990s — and history proved him right." — *Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, invoking Greenspan to argue for rate cuts in 2026; Authers follows immediately with: "Has it?"*

### Why SEBI Raised Red Flags On Rajesh Exports 📎
**The Core** · email · 8 mins

1. **SEBI's 109-page interim order flags Rs 15.15 lakh crore of unverifiable revenue.** Between FY21 and FY26, Bengaluru-based Rajesh Exports reported annual consolidated revenue ranging from Rs 2.4 lakh crore to nearly Rs 7.8 lakh crore — at peak, larger than many of India's most recognised industrial groups. SEBI's interim order found that Rs 15.15 lakh crore of that revenue, reported through overseas subsidiaries over five financial years, could not be reconciled with any audited financial statements.

2. **The 2015 Valcambi acquisition created a structural cover for inflated revenue.** Rajesh Exports bought Swiss refiner Valcambi in 2015 — one of the world's largest precious-metals refiners — giving it a plausible global narrative for extraordinary revenue scale. By FY25, over 98% of consolidated revenue was attributed to overseas subsidiaries, while the Indian parent generated only a fraction of the group's reported turnover.

3. **The smoking gun: Valcambi's audited Swiss books show only refining fees, not trading revenue.** Valcambi's actual Swiss financial statements recorded only processing income — the fees charged for refining gold on behalf of customers — which was tiny compared to the massive trading revenue appearing in Rajesh Exports' consolidated accounts. SEBI alleged the company deliberately bypassed Valcambi's audited figures, instead using unaudited numbers recorded by a separate entity, Global Gold Refineries, for consolidation purposes.

4. **Customer and vendor data was withheld despite repeated regulatory requests.** To verify the revenue, SEBI sought customer-wise and vendor-wise breakdowns that would show who was actually buying and supplying at that scale. Those details were never provided. Rajesh Exports claimed Swiss confidentiality obligations and data-protection laws blocked disclosure; SEBI examined and rejected that argument, concluding Swiss law does not prevent a company from sharing its own corporate financial records with a home-country regulator.

**Quotable:**
> "The discrepancy is so large that it transformed what might have been an accounting dispute into a governance crisis." — *on the gap between Valcambi's audited books and Rajesh Exports' consolidated statements*

## Technology & Semiconductors

### Memory Chips and China, Microsoft and Chinese Models 📌
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 9 mins

1. Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron — the three companies that now dominate global DRAM after decades of boom-bust consolidation — are prioritizing high-bandwidth memory (HBM) for AI accelerators over commodity DRAM. HBM is stacked DRAM with customized base die and interface layers tuned for Nvidia's chips, and Nvidia pays far more per chip than any other buyer, so the big three serve Nvidia first and everyone else with what's left.

2. The resulting DRAM shortage has reached Apple, which raised device prices as a result. Micron's gross margins have hit 80% — record highs — yet the oligopoly remains deliberately cautious about adding capacity, having learned from prior cycles that overbuilding destroys pricing. With only three producers exercising that discipline, there is no competitive pressure to expand supply faster.

3. China's CXMT and Yangtze Memory Technologies (YMTC) are the only entities ready and willing to add major new capacity. CXMT reported 700%+ year-over-year revenue growth in Q1 2026 and is pursuing a $4 billion Shanghai IPO; YMTC is building three new factories that would more than double its NAND capacity by end of 2027.

4. U.S. consumer-tech companies — including Apple (Tim Cook: "everything needs to be on the table") and HP — are already in talks to use CXMT chips in products sold in Asia, bypassing export-control concerns by geographic market segmentation. DRAM is fungible enough that this dual-sourcing is operationally viable, and the volume will make Chinese suppliers progressively more competitive.

5. The big three's oligopolistic capacity restraint has likely sealed their long-run fate: by not expanding fast enough to meet AI-era demand, they created the exact conditions for Chinese competitors to gain market share. Once Chinese suppliers accumulate scale and state-backed subsidies, they will be positioned as the low-cost DRAM provider globally.

6. Microsoft is exploring a fine-tuned DeepSeek V4 (or similar open-source model) as a lower-cost backbone for Copilot Cowork, its usage-priced enterprise AI collaboration product. The business logic: Microsoft's new E7 plan at $99/employee/month bundles both Anthropic (Claude) and OpenAI (GPT) access, but paying per-token margins to both would erode Microsoft's own margins at scale — a cheap open-source model for high-volume/lower-stakes queries is the escape valve.

7. Usage-based AI pricing breaks Microsoft's historical bundling strategy. Teams beat Slack not by being better but by being "free" inside the M365 bundle — enterprises didn't scrutinize marginal value because there was no marginal cost. Once enterprises pay per token, they will compare Copilot directly against standalone Anthropic and OpenAI offerings, forcing Microsoft to actually compete on quality rather than distribution.

8. DeepSeek's combination of frontier-class performance and low serving cost makes it the natural candidate for Microsoft's cost-reduction play. This repeats a structural pattern: the primary beneficiaries of Chinese AI companies offering cheaper alternatives are American companies looking to reduce input costs, not Chinese companies displacing American products.

**Quotable:**
> "The biggest proponents and beneficiaries of Chinese companies offering a cheaper alternative will be American companies. This is a pattern that isn't going away." — *on both the DRAM and AI model dynamics*
