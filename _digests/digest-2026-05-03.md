### 🧠 Community Wisdom: Claude Code tips for ADHD users, resources for managing up, going from corporate to startup, … ⚡
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 22 mins

1. **"Global SMBs in AdTech and public utilities" is not an ICP — it's a continent.** When founder Dan Hollinger described his target customer that way, Joshua Herzig-Marx pushed back with a concrete alternative: something like "Series A B2B SaaS companies in Nordic countries with 20-50 employees that just hired their first sales team and are struggling to close enterprise deals in adtech." The mechanism for finding design partners isn't better outreach — it's a narrower target that makes warm introductions plausible.

2. **Stop trying to sell to your first 3-5 design partners; ask for 15-20 minutes to hear about their problems.** The sequence: ask what problems they face in your space → how they solve them today → who pays and how it's budgeted → thank them → ask for referrals. Only weeks later do you ask if you can show them what you built with those insights. This converts discovery calls into warm relationships without triggering purchase resistance.

3. **The fastest path to design partners is a single advisor whose only job is making "you need to talk to this person" introductions.** Latent Health's founding team had a Stanford professor who made introductions to 100 hospital executives for discovery calls; the team found traction in pharmacy operations and went from $0 to $20M ARR in 12 months. Most founders spend months on cold outreach that could be compressed into weeks with the right intro node.

4. **Claude Code's linear chat UI is structurally hostile to ADHD — the fix lives outside the tool, not inside it.** Multiple community members with ADHD independently converged on the same solution: context needs to live in external `.md` files in a structured folder (Obsidian, GitHub repo, or a local folder Claude Code reads from) rather than in chat history. One member, Vincent DiPaola, built a Markdown-based personal OS on GitHub with scripts that auto-populate a dashboard and send daily email briefs.

5. **A single line in CLAUDE.md — "ADHD, prefers reduced cognitive load" — changes how Claude structures its responses.** Brandon Parker reports it produces: one recommendation instead of a menu, no preamble or filler phrases, visible structure you can skim and re-enter mid-task, and explicit flagging when a conversation is drifting rather than following the drift. Parker frames it as a design constraint, not a preference — "load-bearing words only."

6. **Add a prompt in Claude's "personal preferences" settings instructing it to flag rabbit holes.** Noah Larsen includes a line telling Claude to "yell at me when I start going down a rabbit hole or am overthinking." The AI will still let you pursue the tangent, but names the drift first — giving you a decision point rather than passive complicity. The inverse complaint came from another member who had to explicitly prohibit Claude from being paternalistic about scope.

7. **Managing up in a 5,000-person company is fundamentally a situational awareness problem, not a communication style problem.** Adam Thackeray frames it as learning your manager's communication preferences, incentives, gaps, and leadership style — so you can complement rather than collide. Claire Hughes Johnson's approach from *Scaling People*: write a "how I operate" document for yourself and share it upward, so expectations are set explicitly rather than discovered through friction. Wes Kao's newsletter was the most-cited practical resource; *Radical Candor* (Kim Scott) and *Aligned* (Melissa Appel) were the book recommendations.

8. **Corporate candidates fail startup interviews not because of their background but because they describe process when they should describe ownership.** Hiring manager Eric Metelka reviewed Tanisha Jha's interview answer — a 650-person, seven-department office relocation with layered trackers and weekly meeting cadences — and called it evidence of high-quality operations ability but wrong for a startup. The problem: scale and structure signal the opposite of what startups want. The correct framing is: ambiguous problem → I took ownership → gathered intel fast → made a decision → executed.

9. **Reframe corporate experience as leverage, not liability, when interviewing for startups.** The pitch: "I've learned to operate at scale within mature processes — I've seen which ones are necessary and which are bureaucratic overhead. Now I want to apply that in a hands-on environment, do things scrappily when needed, and introduce the right level of structure as the company grows." This converts a perceived weakness (structured thinking) into a future asset (knowing what to scale into) rather than asking the interviewer to overlook it.

10. **Company-wide AI adoption fails when framed as training; it works when people find a personal spark first.** Kourtney Rose ran a 5-week cohort (1 hour/week) for ~100 people by leading with her own personal projects — a desktop focus buddy, a Y2K throwback site — rather than work use cases. Participants started with personal projects in weeks 1-2, then applied the same skills to work tools. Every person left the cohort with at least one tool they use daily. The mechanism: intrinsic motivation from something you built yourself transfers to professional use; top-down mandates do not.

11. **Most AI adoption failures happen because people never connect the tool to a real decision they own.** Pedro Brandão's observation: before someone uses AI on something that actually matters to their work — a decision, a deliverable, an outcome they're accountable for — it stays experimental. Once they use it on something real, the learning curve collapses. The practical implication: AI evangelists should route adoption through high-stakes work tasks their target audience already cares about, not generic demos.

**Quotable:**
> "That's not a niche, that's a continent. You want something like 'Series A B2B SaaS companies located in Nordic countries with 20-50 employees that just hired their first sales team and are struggling to close enterprise deals in the adtech space.' That's a niche." — *Joshua Herzig-Marx, on ICP definition for early design partner sourcing*

## Defense & Autonomy

### The Anduril Thesis 📌
**Kyle Harrison from Investing 101** · email · 13 mins

1. **The U.S. defense industry was world-class until Robert McNamara systematically broke it.** During WWII, the U.S. held 40% of global manufacturing capacity and the DoD represented 36% of global R&D — funding the space program, the internet, and Silicon Valley itself (Frederick Terman's Stanford Industrial Park was a direct WWII spinoff). McNamara arrived as Secretary of Defense in the 1960s and installed the Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution (PPBE) system — what Ben Rich of Lockheed's Skunk Works called "a plague of auditors and bean counters." Spending went up anyway (Korea, Vietnam, Cold War), but the bureaucratic friction meant the U.S. got drastically less capability per dollar.

2. **Post-Cold War consolidation turned 107 major contractors into 5, hollowing out innovation.** William Perry's 1993 "Last Supper" meeting told defense primes to consolidate or die. Boeing is the poster child: the acquisition of cost-cutting rival McDonnell Douglas replaced an engineering culture with a profit-maximizing one, producing high-profile failures and alleged whistleblower suppression. The structural result is a procurement machine that only knows how to build large, slow, expensive things — the F-35 will cost $1.5 trillion to field and will be technologically obsolete by delivery.

3. **The dominant mode of modern warfare is high-volume, low-cost autonomous systems — the opposite of what the U.S. builds.** Ukraine's Operation Spiderweb sent $1M worth of small drones across the Russian border and autonomously destroyed $7B of assets, wiping out one-third of Russia's long-range bomber fleet. Meanwhile the U.S. responded to a $50K Iranian drone with a $4.5M missile — what Rep. Ted Lieu called "throwing Ferraris at frisbees." China has developed "carrier killer" missiles costing ~$20M that can destroy a multi-billion-dollar U.S. carrier, making the Navy's core asset class nearly unusable in a Pacific conflict.

4. **The China threat is structural, not hypothetical, and the U.S. is already losing the war games.** China's military budget grew 900% from 1999 to 2017. Its military-civil fusion policy means the PLA gets the best of commercial innovation. In basically every war game pitting the U.S. against China since 2015, the U.S. has lost. A conflict over Taiwan alone could represent a $10 trillion hit to the global economy. Warnings have been ignored for decades — from Andrew Marshall's 1992 Office of Net Assessment report to Robert Work's 2017 warning about "rapidly eroding" U.S. technological advantage.

5. **Anduril's core bet is software-defined, hardware-enabled "Mosaic Warfare" — many networked cheap assets replacing a few expensive ones.** The Lattice OS is the connective tissue: a single operator with an Eagle Eye AR helmet can fuse sensor feeds from Sentry ground towers, Ghost drones overhead, and Sentry Seabed underwater sensors, then direct a Ghost Shark submarine, Roadrunner interceptor, and Fury fighter jet simultaneously — a task that would require dozens of people in separate rooms today. Critically, Anduril designs its products (including Fury) to be manufacturable in any U.S. machine shop, not just a specialized facility like Arsenal-1.

6. **Anduril deliberately rejects every structural dysfunction of the legacy defense industry.** Legacy primes run cost-plus contracts (the government prefers a $1B program at 10% margin over a $100M program at 50% margin for the same outcome); Anduril insists on fixed-cost, "we only want to get paid if we do well." Legacy primes are built-to-spec with the DoD dictating the solution; Anduril is built-to-mission, trusting customers to know their problems. Legacy primes are software-allergic (selling software to the government means potentially surrendering source code IP); Anduril treats software as the primary product. In the early days, Anduril had more lobbyists than engineers, treating policy as a product to be shipped.

7. **Autonomous weapons are more ethical than dumb ones, and Palmer Luckey is willing to say so publicly.** Autonomous weapons have existed for decades — Aegis ship defense systems already fire without human authorization. Palmer's framing: "What's the moral virtue in a dumb landmine that can't tell a school bus full of kids from a Russian tank?" The analogy to autonomous vehicles is direct — AVs could save 40,000+ lives annually; refusing to deploy them out of fear of rare accidents is itself a moral failure. Trae Stephens calls this an "Arc of Precision" — warfare has moved from maximum destruction toward targeted, proportionate force that reinforces rather than undermines just war theory.

**Quotable:**
> "I do not love the bright sword for its sharpness, nor the arrow for its swiftness, nor the warrior for his glory. I love only that which they defend." — *Tolkien's Two Towers, cited as Anduril's animating principle: the company's mandate is defending liberal democracy and human rights, not any specific nation*

## Energy & Geopolitics

### Editor's Choice: The twilight of Opec 📌
**Roula Khalaf** · email · 4 mins

1. The UAE announced its departure from Opec on Tuesday (April 29, 2026) after nearly 60 years of membership — a landmark exit because the Gulf state is Opec's third-largest producer and one of its most influential members. Abu Dhabi had long resented the cartel's output quotas and Saudi Arabia's de facto grip on decision-making, but the break was timed to coincide with turmoil: the Strait of Hormuz remains closed amid the ongoing Iran conflict, meaning the full market impact of the UAE's exit won't be apparent until the strait reopens. Brent crude surged to its highest level since 2022 mid-week before reversing, illustrating how the Iran crisis — not Opec discipline — is now the dominant force moving oil prices.

2. The UAE's departure, the FT's editorial board concluded, "gives the impression of an organisation now in its twilight." Opec's market share has been eroding since its peak power in the 1970s, and the Iran conflict has further stripped the group of its ability to coordinate supply and influence prices. The structural shift is underscored by a new FT visual story showing that many energy-importing countries now look to the US — not Opec members — as the primary solution to their supply needs, reflecting American shale's dominance and the fracturing of Gulf cohesion.

**Quotable:**
> "gives the impression of an organisation now in its twilight" — *FT editorial board on the UAE's exit from Opec after nearly 60 years*

## Finance & Policy

### DealBook: Trump and the cannabis industry 📌
**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 9 mins

1. The Trump administration used treaty powers — bypassing the standard rule-making process — to move medical marijuana from Schedule I (alongside heroin) to a lower-risk category comparable to prescription Tylenol, overseen by the DEA. This shortcut limited the initial reclassification to medical cannabis only; a separate hearing to cover recreational marijuana is scheduled for June 29, 2026, where opposition groups like Smart Approaches to Marijuana (which killed Biden's earlier attempt) are expected to fight it.

2. The tax impact is enormous: cannabis companies are currently taxed on gross income rather than profits because of their federal Schedule I status, producing effective tax rates around 70% — more than double other industries. Reclassification would allow licensed sellers to deduct standard business expenses like rent and payroll. Legal cannabis companies collectively owed the IRS $2.24 billion in 2025; Trulieve and Curaleaf alone carried more than $1.6 billion in federal tax liabilities. Treasury is considering making the relief retroactive, and cannabis lawyers are advising clients to hold off filing or prepare amended returns.

3. Trulieve CEO Kim Rivers was directly instrumental in triggering the policy shift: she helped persuade Trump to issue an executive order in December 2025 directing the DOJ to quickly reclassify marijuana. Trulieve, Florida's largest medical cannabis company, and Curaleaf, a global operator based in New York, are the industry's biggest beneficiaries — both plan to redirect tax savings into FDA-approval research for cannabis-based treatments for cancer, nerve pain, and seizures.

4. Recreational-only businesses are caught in a disadvantageous middle ground. Beak & Skiff, a 115-year-old New York apple orchard that sells cannabis and hemp under the Ayrloom brand, faces two simultaneous threats: exclusion from the medical reclassification and an impending national ban on hemp products containing more than 0.4mg THC per container — which would cut its retail footprint from 13 states to just one (New York). Companies selling both medical and recreational cannabis face a different uncertainty: regulators have not yet clarified whether they'll be required to fully separate those revenue streams to claim the tax break.

5. Federal reclassification does not make cannabis federally legal, which still requires an act of Congress, FDA approval, or removal from the controlled substances list entirely. Banks, institutional investors, and insurance companies remain on the sidelines due to ongoing legal risk — leaving cannabis companies with limited financial services and higher borrowing costs. At the dispensary level, consumers still pay cash or PIN debit because Visa and Mastercard prohibit cannabis transactions; even the DEA itself requires application fees from newly registering medical cannabis businesses to be submitted via PayPal or bank transfer.

**Quotable:**
> "This is not some plants in a closet or on a dirt floor. This is real, regulated, highly nuanced business. Millions of Americans are finding relief and want to have assurance that these products are backed by real research in the United States." — *Kim Rivers, CEO of Trulieve, on why federal reclassification was long overdue*

### Longreads + Open Thread 📎
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 9 mins

1. A piece of malware documented by SentinelOne predates Stuxnet by at least five years and worked by very slightly corrupting outputs from simulation software — not crashing systems, just nudging them toward inaccuracy. Statistical sabotage is harder to detect than outright disruption because sabotage isn't the first hypothesis anyone forms when results drift marginally wrong.

2. The cost of equipping one effective soldier shapes land distribution across history. England post-Norman conquest had concentrated ownership because a warhorse required a large land endowment and depreciated fast. American westward expansion produced dispersed land ownership because a musket cost far less in labor-hours than a warhorse, lowering the minimum viable land stake per fighter.

3. Craigslist generates roughly $6M per employee — an extraordinary figure for what is technically a replicable product. Craig Newmark won not by doing something no one else could, but by going first in a winner-take-all market, which frames the Tyler Cowen interview as a meditation on what to do with unexpected wealth and responsibility — a question increasingly relevant to the AI industry.

4. A study of Polymarket traders (Cram, Guo, Jensen, and Kung) finds that only ~3% have genuine predictive skill and just 0.1% are market-makers; everyone else loses money. Prediction markets produce accurate prices not through crowd wisdom averaging many opinions, but because a small minority of skilled traders consistently move prices in the right direction — the majority of overconfident noise traders provide the liquidity that makes this profitable.

5. *Investment Banking in America* (published 1970) argued the industry had peaked in the 1920s and was in long decline — a thesis falsified by 1990. Read with hindsight, US investment banking follows a long cycle: life insurer overextension in illiquid loans caused the 1907 selloff (mirroring today's private credit boom); leveraged investment in electrical utilities in the 1920s maps to the neocloud buildout now. The US's mass equity culture itself was a government byproduct — WWI war bond sales were the first brokerage accounts for millions of consumers, bootstrapping postwar market participation.

6. Algorithmic feeds are optimized but not optimal — they converge users toward the average of their cluster. Used bookstores inject entropy: the selection pool is adversarially curated (survivors of decluttering, moving, and death), meaning recent books skew toward posers and unloved volumes. The signal to find is the book that made someone text their sibling in surprise — a 1970 investment banking history qualifies; a finance bestseller from 2022 almost certainly does not.

> "Craigslist has always been an odd sort of institution, in the sense that anyone could have put together a stripped-down classified ads product (so he's nothing special) but he did it first (so he's the all-time winner in an incredibly competitive field—$6M per employee is not too bad)." — *on Craig Newmark and first-mover dynamics*

## Culture & Society

### The Morning: Talking to strangers 📎
**The New York Times** · email · 8 mins

1. Technology has made avoiding strangers the effortless default. Noise-canceling headphones, self-checkout lines, internet shopping, and the reflexive reach for a phone at any moment of social uncertainty have collectively insulated people from "humanity's intrusion." When non-interaction becomes the default, social muscles atrophy — a cost that's invisible until you notice you'd never have started a conversation on your own.

2. A man named Stefan at a small concert venue demonstrated the alternative: he walked up to two people standing alone and said, in effect, "We're all here solo, so we should talk to one another." The three chatted for half an hour over a shared interest in the musician. His operating assumption — "people simply want fun and interesting things to happen to them" — reframes stranger-initiation not as an imposition but as a service people are quietly hoping someone will provide.

3. Journalist David Sax wrote in a NYT Opinion guest essay that strangers are "one of the richest and most important resources we have," connecting people to community, building empathy, and generating civility. The hesitation to engage isn't shyness so much as self-focus: most people are calculating their own potential embarrassment rather than thinking about what the person across the room might want from the evening.

4. Treating stranger-engagement as a social contract obligation shifts the cost-benefit math. If everyone accepts a duty to generate fun when opportunity presents itself, the aggregate probability of any given evening being interesting rises substantially. And if the other person doesn't reciprocate? Stefan's rationalization: the failed attempt "gives you a good story to tell your friends."

5. The newsletter's news roundup covers: Spirit Airlines shutting down after failing to secure a $500 million Trump administration bailout and canceling all flights; Trump announcing 25% tariffs on European cars and trucks; the Pentagon signing broad AI-use deals with Amazon, Microsoft, and Nvidia (allowing "any lawful use" — a standard Anthropic has declined); Maryland becoming the first state to ban AI-based dynamic pricing at grocery stores; a federal appeals court blocking abortion providers from mailing abortion pills; and Republican congressional impatience with the Iran war after Trump missed a statutory deadline for lawmakers' approval.

**Quotable:**
> "Far from random human inconveniences, strangers are actually one of the richest and most important resources we have. They connect us to the community, teach us empathy, build civility and are full of surprise and potentially wonder." — *David Sax, NYT Opinion guest essay, quoted by Melissa Kirsch*
