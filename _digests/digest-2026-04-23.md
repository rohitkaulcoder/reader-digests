## Today's Top 3

### Money Stuff: There's No Time to Buy Cursor ⚡
**Matt Levine** · email · 17 mins

1. The SpaceX IPO is explicitly vibes-based — and that's fine by design. David Erickson, former co-head of global equity capital markets at Barclays and now adjunct professor at Columbia Business School, said the pitch "centers on Elon Musk's Ability to 'Sell the Dream'" and that "the math doesn't work" from a fundamentals standpoint. SpaceX is targeting a $2 trillion valuation for a company that is simultaneously a rocket company, satellite internet provider, space data center operator, Mars colonization venture, frontier AI lab, and social media company — categories that cannot be coherently reduced to audited financial statements.

2. Musk's restlessness is a feature for dream-selling but a catastrophic bug for the traditional IPO process. In March 2026 — three months before the planned June IPO — Musk tweeted that "xAI was not built right first time around, so is being rebuilt from the foundations up." A prospectus requires audited historical financials, SEC review, and a locked-down business description; a CEO who blows up his AI strategy mid-process makes that nearly impossible. The SpaceX prospectus will capture a single frozen moment in a company that changes radically every few months.

3. SpaceX structured a $60 billion option to acquire Cursor rather than a binding acquisition, specifically to protect the IPO timeline. SpaceX gets the right — not the obligation — to buy Cursor AI coding company "later this year," post-IPO. Bloomberg reported the deal is structured this way because "a major transaction would require SpaceX to update its filings and financial details, potentially delaying the IPO." SpaceX can mention Cursor on the roadshow as a "cool future thing" without having to include Cursor's financials in the prospectus.

4. The $10 billion payment Cursor receives is option premium, not a breakup fee in the Twitter sense. Unlike the Twitter acquisition where Musk's $1 billion "termination fee" was actually a binding obligation to close (a distinction Musk discovered in court), the Cursor $10B is real option premium — SpaceX can walk away after paying it. Levine notes Cursor's management evidently learned from the Twitter saga: get paid up front in cash rather than trust a Musk pinky swear.

5. Cursor is in a competitive trap that makes the SpaceX deal attractive from its side too. Cursor reached peak market position about a year ago as one of the only AI applications besides ChatGPT earning meaningful revenue. But it's being squeezed: it pays frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) for API access to models, while those same labs offer competing subsidized coding products on subscription. Ben Thompson of Stratechery summarized it: "SpaceXAI has a ton of compute, and no one to use it... Cursor has a product, data, and distribution, and is bleeding money." The combination is obvious — compute meets product.

6. There are four reasons SpaceX couldn't simply wait until after the IPO to announce the deal. First, AI moves fast and someone else could buy Cursor in the interim. Second, Cursor employees going to work for a famously demanding boss need to be cashed out — $60B now beats working on spec for months. Third, given Musk's M&A track record (the Twitter near-escape), Cursor demanded serious option premium, not a handshake. Fourth, the IPO roadshow can mention Cursor as a dream-selling talking point even without full financial disclosure.

7. UK defined-benefit pension funds have backed themselves into a liquidity trap as a direct consequence of the 2022 LDI crisis. The Pensions Regulator wrote to 58 funds in November warning that over 50% of each portfolio was in assets taking longer than a week to sell. The crisis dates to Liz Truss's September 2022 "mini" Budget, which caused gilt yields to spike and triggered margin calls on LDI (liability-driven investing) strategies — pensions that had borrowed short-term to buy gilts as a hedge. The post-crisis regulatory fix (hold more liquid assets) left funds overexposed to illiquid private markets, and now they can't rebalance without "huge penalties."

8. Pension funds' structural long-termism is inexorably destroyed by modern finance, in two different ways simultaneously. US defined-benefit pensions avoided run risk by having predictable long-term liabilities — until defined-contribution 401(k)s replaced them, creating retail investors who panic-sell. UK defined-benefit pensions avoided run risk for the same structural reason — until LDI turned them into leveraged gilt vehicles subject to margin calls. Neither country's solution preserved the original feature. As Levine puts it: modern finance has an emergent property of making even run-proof pools of money vulnerable to runs, without anyone planning it.

9. Bad Bunny resolved the basis risk problem in parametric insurance by installing a temporary weather station inside his stadium in Medellín, Colombia. Standard parametric insurance for events pays based on rainfall at the nearest official weather station — in this case, a mile away from the open-air venue, too far for tropical microclimates to be reliable. The insurer (Descartes Underwriting) and weather monitor (Vaisala Oyj) installed a dedicated sensor inside the venue days before three sold-out shows, creating a policy triggered by rain at the exact location of risk. This eliminates basis risk entirely and points toward a future of bespoke parametric insurance for any insurable event.

**Quotable:**
> "I know this is bad but I find something aesthetically beautiful about it. If you have a pot of money that is immune to bank runs, over time, modern finance will find a way to make it vulnerable to bank runs. That is an emergent property of modern finance. No one sits down and says 'let's make pension funds vulnerable to bank runs!' Finance, as an abstract entity, just sort of does that on its own." — *on UK pension funds' LDI crisis*

### How GTM teams are using Claude Code (and Cowork) 📎

Voje's shorthand: think in Chat, build in Code, operate in Cowork — Code is the terminal-based autonomous coding agent that connects to APIs and databases; Cowork is the desktop app for non-developers that shares the same underlying context but is usable out-of-the-box without a terminal.

2. Beyond time savings (92% of users reported those), 67% said Claude enabled something that was previously impossible, and 55% had already replaced a tool or vendor — most commonly ChatGPT, an agency, or a contractor. The compounding advantage cited is that with the right setup, Claude learns your business intimately: your ICP, positioning, writing preferences, and connected systems, so each task builds on accumulated context rather than starting from scratch.

3. Effective setup requires a CLAUDE.md file with company context, custom skill files, and MCP-based tool connections; more advanced users add agents for multi-step reasoning tasks and hooks for automated, deterministic checks (e.g., auto-format on save, block Claude from modifying certain files). Pro tips from heavy users: keep CLAUDE.md scannable in two minutes, save strong conversations as reusable skills via `/skill-creator`, and have Claude interview you to build your personal context file rather than writing it manually.

4. Credits and throttling are the dominant pain point, cited by roughly half of respondents in open-ended responses — including Max-plan subscribers hitting limits 2-3x per day. Practical mitigations: open new chats frequently (reduces accumulated context cost), switch from MCP to CLI/API integrations (a ScaleKit study found MCP is 10-32x more expensive than CLI), and mix in Sonnet instead of Opus for simpler tasks (Sonnet costs ~20% of what Opus does). Sharing context across teams is an unresolved structural problem: skills must be rebuilt separately per person, and there's no native sync when a shared skill file is updated.

5. 27% of Code/Cowork users have already replaced a GTM tool; another 30% expect to soon. What's being displaced first: simple enrichment/automation tools, spreadsheets, analytics tools like Tableau, and contractor/agency spend. Meanwhile, the tools users value most *alongside* Claude are ChatGPT (26%), Clay (21%), and HubSpot (19%) — Claude connects to these rather than replacing them, making the existing stack more productive. The top five GTM workflows in adoption order: social media posts, websites/landing pages/microsites, SDR tooling, segmented messaging, and structured content pipelines.

## Product & Design

### The Interior Design of Software 📌
**Rex Woodbury** · email · 8 mins

1. When software is scarce, engineering is the bottleneck; when software is abundant, editorial judgment becomes the bottleneck. Marc Andreessen's "Mexican standoff" framing reveals this asymmetry in a single word choice: coders *believe* they can design with AI, PMs *think* they can code, but designers *know* they can do both — confidence born from a skill that AI cannot fully replicate. As AI collapses the cost of building software, the premium shifts to knowing what to build and what it should *feel* like.

2. Every major wave of software proliferation has driven design toward flat, legible minimalism — and AI is triggering another such wave. The 80s had tiny monochrome screens (512×342, 128 KB RAM); the 90s brought maximalism once bandwidth allowed it; Mac OS X Aqua (2001) introduced glossy "lick-able" icons; iPhone brought skeuomorphism (leather-bound Notes app, wooden iBooks shelves). Then iOS 7 (2013) stripped it all away into flat colors and whitespace, and every major OS followed within a year. The same pattern holds now: an explosion of AI-generated apps is pushing default output toward clean, inoffensive, unmemorable design.

3. "Everyone is a designer" became Silicon Valley's dominant trend since 2013 — but the numbers show it's real, not just a slogan. Two-thirds of Figma's paying users are non-designers (developers, marketers, PMs), a demographic its early competitors missed entirely when sizing the market. Canva is the cleaner proof point: 265M monthly active users, 31M paying, ~$4B ARR (vs. Figma's $1B), and eight consecutive profitable years — built almost entirely on non-professional designers. AI tools like Claude Design (launched last week on Opus 4.7, which dropped Figma's stock 7.7% on launch day) push this democratization further, generating 12 logo variants from a single image upload in seconds.

4. The problem with democratized AI design is that it produces anywhere — inoffensive, generic, and flat. The products with cultural staying power feel like *somewhere* specific: Discord is a dive bar (dark, noisy, off-putting to Slack users); Notion is a functional corporate office; Craigslist is an uncle's garage frozen in 2004. These are deeply opinionated products, and their design repels the wrong users as effectively as it attracts the right ones. Claude exemplifies this in AI: it uses a custom serif typeface called Copernicus (unusual in modern UI, reading as editorial and considered), paired with an off-white cream-and-burnt-orange palette — a deliberate bookshop warmth. ChatGPT uses a geometric Söhne variant (tech's default typography) on clinical white — an inoffensive hotel lobby meant for everyone and nowhere in particular.

5. When production costs collapse, attention becomes the scarce input, and opinionated design is the mechanism for capturing it. Flat design was rational when the constraint was screen real estate and the goal was legibility across every device from a watch to a TV. But that constraint is lifting — and with AI generating infinite functionally adequate software, standing out requires a point of view. The next design era belongs to products that know exactly what room they're building and who's invited in, not products optimized for universal appeal.

**Quotable:**
> "The best products are the products that feel like *somewhere*, while most AI-generated stuff feels more like anywhere." — *on why opinionated design wins as software production becomes infinite*

## Markets & Finance

### The TACO that ate market strategy 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. **TACO — "Trump Always Chickens Out" — has hardened from trade to market doctrine.** When JD Vance cancelled his peace-talk trip to Pakistan on April 22, Brent crude spiked above $100 for the first time in seven trading days. Within 30 minutes, Trump declared the Iran ceasefire would continue indefinitely, instantly rewarding the TACO bet. Peter Atwater (Financial Insyghts) describes the trajectory: "What started as an event a year ago became a pattern, became a trend, and is now a certain trend that can be extrapolated" — to the point that ChatGPT now coaches traders on rotating into "fear trades" (energy, defense) and "relief trades" (tech, consumer) in anticipation of Trump-driven swings rather than just reacting to them.

2. **Historical retest rates have collapsed to zero, suggesting TACO has broken a fundamental market dynamic.** Longview Economics found that since 1978, 13 of 15 S&P 500 bull-market corrections of 10%+ were retested — an 87% rate. Since the pandemic, five selloff-and-rebounds (pandemic, SVB crisis, carry-trade unwind in 2024, Liberation Day, and the Iran war) have each produced zero retests. Chris Watling (Longview) attributes this to tighter hedge-fund risk controls and a mass retail "buy the dip" reflex: "You used to have retests and you used to have a sense of capitulation at the low. Not any more." TACO traders have effectively eliminated the mechanism that used to shake out weak hands.

3. **Agricultural commodities are a slow-burn threat that equity markets appear to be ignoring.** Gulf fertilizer — critical to global grain production — has barely moved since the Strait of Hormuz closed, forcing farmers to use costly alternatives or cut application, with crop-yield effects due within months. Wheat and rice have already jumped since the war started, though the surge is muted relative to 2022 post-Ukraine levels. Bank of America strategist Daryna Kovalska warns that if the war extends into H2 2026, the biggest upside risk is corn: mounting nitrogen-fertilizer pressure could trigger a bull cycle "resembling the rallies following the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war and the 2012 US drought," which would hit Trump's affordability narrative ahead of the November midterms.

4. **Industrial metals signal a potential new commodity supercycle, with copper and aluminum leading.** Bloomberg's industrial metals sub-index is up more than 10% since mid-March, with copper and aluminum each up over 11%. Watling frames copper as "an everything story — it's AI, and it's electrification," with aluminum as the substitute play for Western grid rebuilding. The copper-to-gold ratio has recently hit four-decade lows — the same kind of signal that preceded the China-driven supercycle after the WTO admission in 2001. Oxford Economics' Bernard Yaros notes that geopolitical uncertainty delays corporate capital spending, but structural demand for both metals remains intact, giving them room to run further if supply disruptions persist.

**Quotable:**
> "What started as an event a year ago became a pattern, became a trend, and is now a certain trend that can be extrapolated." — *Peter Atwater (Financial Insyghts) on TACO becoming an entrenched market strategy*
