## Today's Top 3

### Money Stuff: GameStop Doesn't Have Enough Stock ⚡
**Matt Levine (Bloomberg)** · rss · 19 mins

1. **GameStop has bid $56 billion for eBay — roughly four times GameStop's own market cap.** Ryan Cohen's offer is $125/share (a ~20% premium to eBay's Friday close), half cash and half GameStop stock. The deal would make GameStop the acquirer of a company with a ~$46B market cap while GameStop itself was worth only ~$12B as of the offer date.

2. **The deal math just barely adds up on paper, but all three legs are fragile.** The $56B breaks down as ~$8B of GameStop's balance-sheet cash + $20B of debt (a "highly confident letter" from TD Bank) + $28B in new GameStop stock. TD's confidence is secured against eBay's cash flows, not GameStop's standalone EBITDA (~$469M estimated for FY2027 on $4B of existing debt); this is essentially a leveraged buyout dressed up as a strategic acquisition.

3. **GameStop literally cannot issue the stock required — it doesn't have enough authorized shares.** Paying $28B of the purchase price in stock at GameStop's $26.53 closing price requires issuing ~1.05 billion new shares. GameStop's certificate of incorporation authorizes a maximum of 1 billion shares total, and ~448 million are already outstanding. The company would need a shareholder vote to authorize new shares before it could complete the deal — a fact not mentioned anywhere in the proposal.

4. **Even if the shares were authorized, issuing them would more than triple the share count and gut existing holders.** Legacy eBay shareholders would end up owning roughly twice as much of the combined company as legacy GameStop holders. Under NYSE rules, GameStop shareholders get a vote because they are, in effect, the ones being diluted into a minority stake — this is GameStop getting acquired in economic substance even if Cohen runs the resulting entity.

5. **Ryan Cohen's "moonshot" pay package is a powerful engine driving the deal.** In January, GameStop granted Cohen options worth up to ~$35 billion contingent on hitting $10B of cumulative EBITDA and a $100B market cap within 10 years. Buying eBay (~$46B market cap, ~$3B EBITDA) in one transaction is a shortcut: a company growing at 20%/year from $12B would reach only ~$62B after a decade, but a company starting at $60B and growing at 10%/year reaches ~$156B — enough to clear the adjusted targets.

6. **Moonshot pay packages structurally reward acquisitions over organic growth, even with adjustment clauses.** Cohen's package specifies that milestones will be adjusted "higher to account for acquisition activity for which stock is provided as consideration," and Musk's 2025 Tesla package adds acquisition value directly to the market-cap threshold. But the math still favors bigness-by-merger: it's arithmetically easier to compound from a large base, so even a "fair" additive adjustment leaves the CEO better off having bought size than having created it organically.

7. **The same acquisition incentive applies to Musk's Tesla/SpaceX empire, and a hypothetical merger illustrates the distortion.** If Tesla ($1.5T) needs to reach $8.5T and SpaceX ($2T) needs to reach $6.6T, the combined milestone is $15.1T. Merging the two companies and applying additive adjustments would reduce the effective combined target to ~$10.5T — $4.6T less to achieve for full payout, with no new value created.

8. **A 41.6-million-trade study of Kalshi found that single-name prediction markets (specific companies or individuals) have measurably higher informed trading than broad macro contracts.** In those single-name markets, events with a 46% implied YES probability actually settle YES only 21% of the time — a 25-percentage-point miscalibration — because retail bettors systematically over-bet YES. Market makers exploit this behavioral surplus to offset losses from the insiders who are trading on the other side.

9. **Running only the sell-NO side of prediction markets doubles gross profit per contract but triples variance — which is why sophisticated market makers maintain two-sided books.** An offer-only maker earns roughly 2× the gross profit on single-name markets compared to a full-book maker, but her 20 worst markets are 100% events that actually happened (CEO fired, TikTok banned). The full-book maker's YES bids accumulate cheap long positions from wrong-side NO takers, cutting variance 70% at the cost of half the gross: "the offer side is the revenue engine, and the bid side is the hedge."

10. **Social casino apps — where real money buys fake, non-redeemable coins — generated over $11 billion in revenue in 2025, by making the worthless purchase feel routine through design.** High 5 Casino, Slotomania, and Jackpot Party are free to download, dispense small starting hoards, then trigger pop-ups when the house inevitably wins back those coins. Individual "whales" have spent over $1 million on non-redeemable tokens; High 5's VP of integrated marketing responded to news of a heavy user with "I hope she sits down with a box of wine tonight and plays C. Roma all night."

It's unclear whether Anthropic's inference workloads are counted inside Sundar Pichai's claim that "enterprise AI solutions" revenue grew "nearly 800% year-over-year" — they likely are, at least partially.

With OpenAI and Anthropic competing hard for enterprise, the consumer AI lane is relatively open, and Meta's advertising engine is purpose-built to monetize consumer engagement at scale.

### Dead Airlines ⚡
**Byrne @ The Diff** · email · 10 mins

1. Airline stocks price in near-certain eventual failure: the good ones trade at high single-digit P/E ratios mid-cycle, implying the market expects roughly one more cycle before collapse. This isn't irrational pessimism — planes have multi-year order lead times so capacity commitments peak at the top, unions (especially pilots) negotiate to be senior creditors because they know the industry is cyclical, and shareholders end up the most junior claimants on extremely volatile cash flows.

2. New low-cost carriers have a genuine structural cost advantage — for a while. Union pay scales are seniority-based, so the faster an airline grows, the lower its average employee tenure and the lower its labor cost per hour. A growing fleet is also younger and more fuel-efficient, making maintenance cheaper. Spirit (Florida/Caribbean), Allegiant (Las Vegas), and Ryanair (near-airport Europe) each built real businesses by connecting underserved city pairs and bypassing the legacy hub model (CLT, ATL, DFW).

3. The same cost stripping that creates early advantages becomes a fatal liability in fuel spikes. Because Spirit cut everything else so lean, fuel consumed 26% of its revenue last year — versus 17–20% for Delta, American, and United. When jet fuel prices rise, Spirit's ticket prices must rise proportionally more than legacy carriers', destroying the only competitive advantage a pure-cost carrier has. Spirit filed for bankruptcy in May 2026, the latest entrant in a long parade of LCC collapses.

4. Discount airlines should be mentally categorized as finite-duration assets — like royalties from a movie, song, oil well, or video game. They can be positive expected value, but their cash flows structurally tend toward zero over a cycle. Spirit's collapse doesn't destroy demand: the planes, pilots, mechanics, and airports still exist, and the passengers still want to fly to Florida. Assets get reshuffled with some friction, but end up slightly better aligned with where real demand is.

5. The newsletter's shorter notes extend the same capital-structure lens to other assets. GameStop is using its meme-inflated stock (turned from a $381m loss to $418m profit via cost cuts) as currency to bid for eBay, which just posted its biggest YoY gross merchandise volume gain since 2021 — the problem is that "we're giving you overpriced shares" is not a pitch eBay shareholders will find compelling. Separately, S&P relaxing index inclusion criteria mirrors Nasdaq because indexes have become brands (Big Companies, Tech Companies) and retail investors will assume SpaceX or Anthropic joins the index the day it IPOs — so matching the index to investor expectations is actually more market-efficient than strict rule-following.

**Quotable:**
> "The general rule might be that discount carriers are an asset like the cash flows from a specific movie, song, oil well, or video game: they can be positive expected value, but their cash flows will go to zero in a few years." — *on why LCC collapses are structurally inevitable, not surprising*

### The Founder's Playbook For India's AI 📎
**The Core** · email · 7 mins

1. Royal Enfield has executed the most dramatic power shift in motorcycling history: in 2025 it sold over 1.2 million motorcycles globally while Harley-Davidson barely cleared 100,000. Eicher Motors (Royal Enfield's parent) commands a market cap north of $21 billion; Harley's has shrunk to roughly $2.7 billion. Royal Enfield now exports more motorcycles than Harley-Davidson produces in total.

2. The conquest is deliberate and geographically pointed — in 2015, Royal Enfield opened its North American headquarters in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, directly in Harley's backyard. Harley lost because it kept selling overpriced, over-complicated heavyweights to an aging demographic; Royal Enfield won by going analog and affordable — for the price of one Harley, buyers get roughly two Enfields, plus a 123-year heritage and a fiercely loyal global community that mirrors the very culture Harley built.

3. India's ongoing debate about whether to build homegrown LLMs or just adopt ChatGPT/Claude for enterprise productivity is a distraction, not a strategic question. If a visionary founder wants to build an Indian LLM, they will secure capital, assemble talent, and build it — they won't wait for social media validation or a government white paper, just as Eicher didn't consult armchair commentators before marching into Milwaukee.

4. India's IT miracle is the direct precedent: when NR Narayana Murthy, Nandan Nilekani, and co-founders launched Infosys in 1981, the software services industry flourished precisely because the government had no idea what it was. Spared state meddling and central planning, founders built on hunger and passion alone. The lesson generalises — India's next global AI champion will be built in a garage or boardroom, not legitimised in a comment section.

**Quotable:**
> "They will be built in the garages and boardrooms of driven founders, not in the comment sections of their doubters." — *on whether India can produce a global AI champion*

**Andrew Ross Sorkin** · email · 8 mins

The trigger: Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview, a restricted model available to only a few dozen corporate and government users, can identify and exploit software vulnerabilities faster than any traditional cybersecurity team. Officials fear Anthropic lacks sufficient compute to serve high-priority government users during a crisis, potentially locking them out of the tools needed to patch critical infrastructure.

2. **Google wants the government contract but faces a classified-computing problem.** The administration is actively diversifying away from Claude toward Google Gemini, OpenAI's GPT models, and others — and the Pentagon has already signed deals to use additional models on classified work. The obstacle for Google: its proprietary AI chips (TPUs) lack the security certifications required in classified environments. Anthropic had an easy path in because its models run on Amazon Web Services, which spent billions to achieve Impact Level 6 security clearance. The government is now working to accelerate equivalent certification for Google TPUs.

3. **GameStop's Ryan Cohen is attempting a $55 billion hostile takeover of eBay — four times his company's market value.** The bid offers $125 per share (half cash, half stock), roughly 20% above eBay's pre-announcement price. His strategy hinges partly on meme-stock retail investors buying GameStop shares to inflate the stock-based portion of his bid — but GameStop is down nearly 39% over five years, the proxy deadline for eBay's June annual meeting has already passed, and Cohen's prior activist bets at Bed Bath & Beyond (exited in five months before it went bankrupt) and Nordstrom (proxy fight withdrawn) undercut his credibility as an operational turnaround artist.

### Can Spirit be saved … by crowdfunding? 📎
**Bloomberg Opinion** · email · 7 mins

1. Spirit Airlines is fully dead: spirit.com now redirects to spiritrestructuring.com, a two-tab skeleton site with only a "claims agent" phone number — one that explicitly cannot help with refunds, because there is no remaining customer call center or email address. The collapse leaves behind a customer base that, per Bloomberg's Thomas Black, flew infrequently, packed everything into backpacks, bought no extras, and simply wanted on-time departures, transparent fees, and courteous treatment.

2. TikTok users launched a grassroots crowdfunding campaign at letsbuyspiritair.com to create "Spirit 2.0," and by 3 p.m. New York time on May 4, 2026, 124,755 people had pledged $88 million — enough to crash the website but almost certainly not enough to resurrect an airline. 3. The crowdfunding fantasy misidentifies the real problem. Spirit's death doesn't mean the ultra-low-cost model is unviable — it means Spirit specifically failed to make it work. Thomas Black's actual prescription for price-conscious flyers: root for Frontier or another carrier to prove that no-frills flying can survive in the US market, rather than betting on a social-media-driven buyout with no operating infrastructure behind it.

## Geopolitics & Energy

### Why markets think the US is winning the blockade 📌
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. The Strait of Hormuz has been "double-blockaded" by both the US and Iran for three weeks — a nominal ceasefire exists (no bombing of terrestrial targets) but both sides continue using force. Brent crude futures for end-2026 have hit new highs, and 2027 prices are also elevated, meaning markets expect the supply disruption to be lasting and severe.

2. US stocks are at a record high and volatility is low despite the blockade, largely because markets believe Iran's weak position forces it toward a deal before the US needs to commit ground troops. Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution explains the three-part pressure on Iran: the US blockade starves it of export revenue, eventually exhausts Iran's oil storage capacity, and — most immediately — is already triggering capital flight out of Iran.

3. European assets are taking far more damage than US assets: Stoxx index volatility is materially higher than the S&P 500's since the war began, and European stocks have sold off while the US rebounded. The US compounded Europe's pain by simultaneously announcing fresh tariffs on European cars and withdrawing troops from Germany — though a weakened, poorer Europe that buys fewer US exports may yet prove an Achilles heel.

4. The US economy is K-shaped and the lower half is under mounting strain. Gas prices now exceed $4/gallon, and Sam Tombs of Pantheon Macro estimates oil prices will translate into a ~1% annualized quarterly decline in real after-tax income in Q2. The personal savings rate is already just 3.9%, leaving little buffer. The New York Fed's Economic Heterogeneity Indicators show retail spending concentrated among households earning over $125,000/year — a fragile foundation. Spirit Airlines, a low-cost carrier for lower-income Americans that depends on cheap jet fuel, collapsed over the weekend as a concrete illustration of this asymmetric exposure.

5. Bank of America's Aditya Bhave estimates higher gas prices have already eroded nearly half the income boost from tax refunds, and the full-year "gas tax" from the energy shock amounts to roughly 0.5% of GDP — equivalent to the entire economic boost expected from either the One Big Beautiful Bill or AI capital expenditure. Japan's Ministry of Finance spent ~$34 billion intervening to support the yen when it dropped below ¥160, producing the biggest daily yen gain since 2022, but the currency has still fallen 30% on a trade-weighted basis over five years and Mizuho's Jordan Rochester warns it will remain under pressure as long as the Hormuz blockade keeps oil prices elevated.

**Quotable:**
> "We estimate the full-year 'gas tax' to be around 0.5% of GDP. That is about equal to the boost we estimate from the One Big Beautiful Bill or AI capex. This drag could be much higher as we only focus on gasoline, and the energy shock should drive up the costs of other goods and services." — *Aditya Bhave, Bank of America, on the household burden from Hormuz-driven energy prices*

### What the Iran war costs the world 📎
**Bloomberg Businessweek** · email · 7 mins

1. The US-Iran war, now in its 10th week despite an original White House promise of "just four to six weeks," has closed the Strait of Hormuz and stripped the world of at least 10% of its oil supply. The International Energy Agency — which had forecast an unprecedented oil surplus at the start of 2026 — now calls this the biggest disruption in energy-market history. Crude rose above $110/barrel before a fragile April 8 ceasefire briefly pulled it back to ~$95; with a US blockade still restricting Iranian exports, prices are climbing back toward pre-truce highs.

2. Higher oil is cascading into everything else: fertilizer costs have risen dramatically (pushing food prices up), transportation of virtually all goods has become more expensive, and borrowing costs are rising. Before the war, markets expected US interest rate cuts in 2026; investors now see rates holding through December as the Fed worries about inflation. In Europe and the UK, where energy supply shocks bite harder, consumers and investors are bracing for further tightening.

3. Bloomberg Economics' base case projects global growth slowing from 3.4% in 2025 to 2.9% in 2026 — the weakest since 2020. The uncertainty is so severe that the IMF dropped its usual single-number forecast and instead published three distinct scenarios for the global outlook, signaling that conventional modeling has broken down.

4. Trump's blockade strategy has a built-in contradiction: the blockade may well cripple Iran's economy, but economic collapse rarely translates into regime change, and Iran's leadership has "gone into a protective crouch." If the blockade fails to extract concessions, renewed US bombing is the logical next step. Escalation paths are specific and credible — a US strike on Kharg Island (Iran's main export hub), Houthi attacks on the Bab el-Mandeb chokepoint at the Red Sea's mouth, or Iranian strikes on Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline linking oil fields to the Red Sea port of Yanbu — any one of which would drive crude prices sharply higher. A prolonged Hormuz closure would deplete global stockpiles and trigger a self-reinforcing demand destruction cycle.

**Quotable:**
> "The path ahead is as narrow as the Strait of Hormuz: If the fragile ceasefire fails to become a lasting peace, a return to hostilities seems inevitable." — *Ziad Daoud, chief emerging markets economist, Bloomberg Economics, writing from the UAE amid four missile alerts in three hours*
