## Today's Top 3

### Prediction Markets Are the New Public Markets ⚡
**Bloomberg Money Stuff** · rss · 18 mins

1. The 90-year bargain of US securities law — companies get access to public capital only if they publicly disclose material financial information — is visibly breaking down. Hot tech startups like SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic collectively hold trillions in valuation that has accrued entirely to insiders and institutional investors, leaving retail participants out. Two problems result: retail investors can't participate in private-market value creation, and society loses the transparency that public-company disclosures provide.

2. Polymarket launched prediction markets tied to private company valuations — "Will OpenAI's valuation hit $X by December 31?" — in partnership with Nasdaq Private Market as the resolution data provider. This is framed as "democratizing access to financial information," but the actual mechanism is the opposite of transparency: you get more ways to trade, but zero disclosure about the underlying company's finances. The binary bets are economically inferior to owning the stock, but Polymarket's Shayne Coplan calls it access to "one of the last frontiers of financial markets."

3. "Prediction markets" is simply the 2026 rebranding of what was called "tokenization" in 2024-2025. Robinhood's Vlad Tenev and BlackRock's Larry Fink had previously championed tokenizing private-company stocks as a way to give retail investors exposure without triggering securities-law disclosure requirements. That regulatory framing failed; prediction markets accomplish the same thing under a different label, and the SEC's apparent tolerance (Nasdaq's partnership signals regulatory comfort) suggests it is working.

4. Polymarket's markets are technically "security-based swaps" under US law, which should make them hard for a regulated US exchange to list. US investors are also technically prohibited from Polymarket's main site. None of this has mattered: Polymarket launched its US app and Nasdaq partnered with it anyway, illustrating how the gap between formal securities law and tolerated practice has widened dramatically under the current regulatory environment.

5. Anthropic is fighting a parallel battle over transfer restrictions on its own shares — and losing ground on multiple fronts. It updated its website to declare eight named firms' offerings void, explicitly banned SPV structures for secondary sales, and warned that forward contracts may not be honored at IPO. Menlo Ventures, an early Anthropic backer, had pooled its $500 million investment using an SPV (per an SEC filing reported by Business Insider), though Menlo's Matt Murphy said his firm didn't use the "unauthorized" type.

6. Holding Anthropic stock and wanting liquidity now leaves investors in a legal corner: direct sales require Anthropic's permission, forward contracts are arguably prohibited, SPVs are explicitly banned. The theoretical escape valve is Polymarket — an Anthropic shareholder with $90 million in stock could sell 100 million units of "Will Anthropic hit $1 trillion valuation?" at ~$0.91 each for ~$91 million, hedged if the valuation hits the threshold, and keeps both the cash and the stock if it doesn't. The market is too thin to execute this today, but the structural logic exists.

7. A San Francisco jury unanimously rejected Elon Musk's lawsuit claiming OpenAI "stole a charity" when it converted from nonprofit to for-profit. The central legal question was the statute of limitations: OpenAI argued Musk knew of the for-profit shift since March 2019, when OpenAI L.P. was publicly announced and Altman personally notified Musk, who declined equity and raised no objection. Musk argued he didn't fully understand the commercial nature until Microsoft's $10 billion investment in 2023. After about two hours of deliberation, the jury sided with OpenAI; Musk's attorneys vowed an appeal.

8. Citi and HPS Investment Partners (BlackRock's private credit unit, $193 billion AUM) announced a Citi/HPS Private Capital Program targeting up to €15 billion ($17.5 billion) in direct lending across Europe over five years, focusing on sub-investment-grade corporate and private-equity-backed debt. The deal illustrates the structural oddity of modern finance: Citi has $2.8 trillion in assets with ~7% equity-to-assets ratio, HPS has $193 billion with ~50% equity-to-assets — yet HPS is the one with deployable capital. Citi brings origination and client relationships; HPS brings the money.

9. Perpetuals.com launched UpsideOnly, a platform where amateur traders submit paper trades predicting prices of equities, oil, gold, and other assets. An in-house AI model (BayesShield AI) selects the most promising signals, Perpetuals trades on them with its own capital, and successful predictors receive 50% of profits with no downside risk. The model is a distributed alpha-capture system designed to source trading talent that can't afford to compete with multistrategy funds like Millennium — for amateurs, "half the upside, zero downside" beats the alternative of trading their own money.

**Quotable:**
> "We seem to be reaching the end of securities regulation. For almost 100 years, the bargain in US securities law was that companies could sell stock to ordinary individual investors only if they publicly disclosed material information. Everyone seems tired of that bargain and now it is breaking down." — *on Polymarket's private-company prediction markets*

### Money Stuff: Don't Free Little Joe ⚡
**Bloomberg Money Stuff** · rss · 18 mins

1. Polymarket has listed a bet on whether Little Joe — a gorilla who escaped Boston's Franklin Park Zoo in 2003 and attacked a child — will escape again after his scheduled move to Pittsburgh. The contract carries a ~3% implied probability of escape by end of July and, unusually, explicitly cites the Animal Enterprise Terrorism Act (AETA, 18 U.S.C. § 43), warning that anyone who frees the gorilla to win the bet faces fines exceeding $1,000,000 and life imprisonment. This is a direct response to last month's scandal in which Polymarket traders physically tampered with a temperature sensor at Paris's Charles de Gaulle airport — a sensor described as "important for the safe operation of the airport" — to win a weather prediction bet.

2. Prediction markets once promised to be "truth machines" — crowd-sourced probability estimates that simply observe the future. At sufficient scale, they become truth *modification* machines: when an event pays out real money, someone will try to engineer that event. The asymmetry is stark — a long-shot "Yes" contract might return 25x while the corresponding "No" earns only 3%, concentrating manipulation incentives on the event-triggering side. Kalshi declines to list contracts on public figures' deaths for this exact reason; Polymarket, less squeamish, instead itemizes the relevant felony statutes in the contract spec.

3. Individuals and homeowners cannot legally sell *equity* in themselves or their assets — "stock" is a statutory attribute of corporations only. What they can sell is a *contract* that says "I give you cash now; you give me Y% of your future income/home appreciation." That contract is economically equity-like but legally resembles debt, creating a structural time bomb: if the underlying asset appreciates enormously, the seller starts arguing their effective interest rate is hundreds of percent and falls under usury protections.

4. Baseball player Fernando Tatis Jr. tested this in court after Big League Advance gave him cash in 2017 — when he was an underpaid minor leaguer — in exchange for a share of future earnings. After he signed a $340 million MLB contract, Tatis sued, claiming BLA "ran an unlicensed lending business." BLA's position: they bought equity. A 2024 lawsuit against BloomTech (an income-share-agreement coding school) raised the same structural dispute — the contract looks like equity to the seller and like debt to the beneficiary once the upside materializes.

5. Home equity investments (HEIs) are a fast-growing version of the same product applied to houses. Fortress, Carlyle, Bain Capital, and Blue Owl Capital (which committed $2.5 billion in December 2025) have all backed HEI originators, drawn by the $34 trillion in American home equity that is otherwise illiquid. Homeowners receive lump-sum cash in exchange for a share of future price appreciation, with no monthly payments — but the lien, maturity date, and threat of forced sale make the "investment, not loan" framing increasingly hard to maintain at repayment time.

6. The contracts are structured to be nearly fail-proof for investors. Point, a major HEI provider, discounts the home's starting value by 15–30% when calculating the homeowner's repayment obligation — a structural feature that virtually guarantees investor profit. A DBRS Morningstar analysis of 2,700 Point contracts found over 98% generated positive investor returns; the Urban Institute found that an underlying home could decline 3% annually for six consecutive years and a Point contract would still be in the money.

7. In February 2025, the Massachusetts Attorney General sued Boston-based Hometap, alleging its contracts were predatory loans "designed to strip home equity away from vulnerable consumers." Hometap's CEO declined to comment on the lawsuit but endorsed consumer education and regulatory oversight. The National Consumer Law Center calls HEIs "subprime mortgages with predatory rates, disguised to bypass consumer protection laws" — and notes that calculating the true cost requires forecasting real estate appreciation and inflation a decade forward, an impossible task for most homeowners.

8. AllianceBernstein, Brookfield Asset Management, and Carlyle Group announced a joint 401(k) product called ABC ONE, which packages private equity, private credit, and real assets into defined-contribution workplace retirement plans. The product sits alongside existing target-date funds and adjusts private-asset exposure based on expected retirement date. This continues a broader trend: the financial industry, finding 401(k)s inferior to defined-benefit pensions for deploying capital into illiquid private markets, is effectively re-engineering pensions from scratch inside DC plan wrappers.

9. Strategy Inc. (Michael Saylor's company) has purchased 171,238 Bitcoin year-to-date in 2026 — more than the entire global Bitcoin mining output of ~62,000 BTC over the same period — and appears to represent the majority of net corporate and ETF-related Bitcoin accumulation this year. It funds purchases via "Stretch" (ticker STRC), a perpetual preferred stock paying an 11.5% floating annual dividend designed to keep shares near $100 par; Strategy sold $1.95 billion of Stretch in a single week to buy Bitcoin. Analysts at 10x Research note that traditional Bitcoin demand indicators (ETF inflows, futures open interest, stablecoin inflows) have not meaningfully accelerated in 2026, meaning current Bitcoin price support is almost entirely the product of Strategy's financial engineering rather than organic market demand.

**Quotable:**
> "They are truth *modification* machines: If you can make lots of money predicting some relatively trivial thing, you should just make the thing happen. Reality responds to incentives: If the market pays out money for an event happening, someone is going to try to make the event happen." — *on prediction markets at scale*

### Google I/O, World Models, I/O Spaghetti ⚡
**Ben Thompson** · rss · 10 mins

1. Google I/O 2026 was defined by volume over coherence: Bloomberg focused on search redesign and agentic features, while the WSJ led with Gemini Spark and new model releases — two major outlets covering the same keynote with zero overlap. This divergence is itself the story: Google announced so many products that no single narrative could contain it.

2. Demis Hassabis appeared briefly despite being featured in Sundar Pichai's pre-show hype tweet. His core thesis — pursued since building simulation games at age 17 — is that language models are distillations of human text output, which leaves out spatial dynamics, intuitive physics, and sensory experience that can only be captured through world models trained on realistic simulations, not language corpora.

3. The "generalizability gap" in current AI is the key unsolved problem: frontier LLMs have steep performance valleys just off their peaks because they only learned from humanity's *outputs*, not the reasoning chains behind them. Anthropic's bet is that recursive self-improvement through coding solves this (hence hiring Andrej Karpathy to lead that effort); DeepMind's bet is realistic world simulation does.

4. Google's relative weakness in coding and long-running agentic workflows — where it trails both Anthropic and OpenAI — may simply reflect DeepMind's research priorities rather than capability limits. The coding portions of the keynote were handled by a separate team called "Antigravity," not DeepMind, and Gemini 3.5 Flash's early reception was notably negative, with users reporting higher cost-per-task due to token usage despite the speed claims.

5. Google's "spaghetti" strategy — Ask YouTube, voice-powered Docs, Gemini Omni Flash, Gemini Spark, Gemini 3.5 Flash, Antigravity 2.0, Google Pics, Samsung/Warby Parker/Gentle Monster glasses, Gemini for Science, and more — is simultaneously its enduring strength and its analytical frustration. Google survived the mobile paradigm shift and is faring better than expected in AI precisely because its Larry Page/Sergey Brin college-campus culture keeps throwing things at walls without requiring strategic coherence.

**Quotable:**
> "I just suspect that if the company's AI leader doesn't care about such prosaic matters, such prosaic matters may not, in the long run, go that far. And, if Hassabis is right and Google gets to AGI first, it may not matter." — *on the tension between DeepMind's AGI focus and Google's commercial AI products*

## Finance & Markets

### Going Private: Citi, BlackRock HPS unite in high-stakes private credit bet 📌
**Bloomberg** · email · 11 mins

1. **Citi and BlackRock HPS have launched a €15 billion ($17.5B) European direct lending program, the *Private Capital Program*, targeting sub-investment grade corporate and PE clients across the UK and mainland Europe over five years.** Citi brings deal origination via its relationship-banking network; HPS (acquired by BlackRock for ~$12 billion) supplies the capital and makes final credit decisions — its existing direct lending book already stands at $129 billion per Moody's. The structure explicitly keeps risk off Citi's balance sheet while HPS gains European speculative-grade exposure that typically pays higher yields to compensate for default risk.

2. **This is not a one-off — bank/nonbank co-lending is becoming structurally embedded in credit markets, not a crisis-driven workaround.** Citi already struck a $25 billion private credit partnership with Apollo in the US in 2024, and Apollo has been in talks with BNP Paribas for a similar European tie-up. Moody's Global Private Credit managing director Marc Pinto called the Citi/HPS deal a "meaningful marker" and described the bank-origination / asset-manager-balance-sheet split as "credit positive" for both sides and now "a feature of the credit landscape rather than an opportunistic arrangement."

3. **Borrowers gain flexibility but face a structural conflict: three parties (arranger, lender, and borrower) with divergent interests now sit on every deal.** Naresh Aggarwal of the UK's Association of Corporate Treasurers welcomed the gap-filling potential but warned that if banks "try to game the system they may lose out on lending and ancillary business." Regulators share the concern from above — leverage and underwriting quality in these opaque, multi-party structures are harder to track, raising the systemic-shock risk in a downturn.

4. **The SDNY is examining valuation practices at BlackRock TCP Capital Corp (TCPC), a BDC whose shares have fallen 29% this year.** Executives have been interviewed by Manhattan US Attorney personnel; the probe may or may not widen to a sector-level investigation, but no charges have been filed. TCPC disclosed in January it expected to cut its Q4 net asset value by 19%, ultimately reporting $7.07 per share — down from $8.71 at end of Q3 — triggering shareholder class-action lawsuits alleging "materially false" statements. In one specific case, TCPC marked a $25 million loan to Infinite Commerce Holdings at zero just three months after assigning it a flawless valuation.

5. **Private credit's valuation opacity is the industry's exposed nerve: quarterly marks determine investor entry/exit prices and manager fees, yet investors have no public trade data to challenge them.** Apollo announced it would provide daily marks on its $830 billion credit book by end-September to head off this criticism. The problem is documented: late 2025, Apollo's own fund marked a Medallia Inc. loan at 77 cents (distressed territory) while a rival FS/KKR-managed fund held the same loan at 91 cents. SDNY chief Jay Clayton said at a Managed Funds Association conference that mismarking to inflate fees is "always been a no-no" and that "people should know that the financial regulators and the department are looking at those."

6. **US commercial real estate lenders have stopped pretending — distressed properties are being offloaded at prices as low as 15 cents on the dollar after years of "extend and pretend."** CMBS loans in foreclosure hit $17 billion in March 2026, up from $7 billion in 2024 and the highest since the GFC resolution period (Trepp). For the first time since 2022, Q1 2026 workouts of troubled loans exceeded new additions to the $132 billion distressed commercial property pile (MSCI). Shanghai Commercial Bank sold a stalled Manhattan condo-conversion loan at an 85% discount; Netflix is in talks to buy LA's Radford Studio Center for a fraction of its $1.85 billion 2021 sale price after Goldman-led lenders took over.

**Quotable:**
> "The bank/nonbank co-lending structure is becoming a feature of the credit landscape rather than an opportunistic arrangement." — *Marc Pinto, Managing Director of Global Private Credit, Moody's Ratings, on the Citi/HPS deal*

### Bond market fright may end cheap funds era 📎
**John Authers** · email · 8 mins

1. **30-year Treasury yields hit 5.19% on May 20, 2026 — the highest since June 2007 — and the parallel is unsettling.** In June 2007, Authers co-wrote a piece warning that yields at those levels might burst higher, crash stocks, and put unbearable pressure on credit. Within weeks, two Bear Stearns hedge funds collapsed and the Global Financial Crisis spiral had begun. The question today is identical: will financial conditions let strong corporate earnings continue, or will the bond market crack them?

2. **Equity investors are behaving exactly as they did at peak 2007 optimism: buying stocks despite the bond alarm.** The latest Bank of America global fund manager survey shows equity allocations were raised by the most in recorded history in April, even though a majority of respondents expect stagflation over the next 12 months. BofA's June 2007 survey similarly captured "peak economic optimism and robust risk appetite right before the initial tremors of the subprime mortgage crisis."

3. **Today's setup is structurally more dangerous than 2007 on two key metrics.** US government debt has risen from ~60% of GDP to ~120%, meaning higher rates inflict far more fiscal damage on Uncle Sam. And Robert Shiller's cyclically adjusted P/E ratio (CAPE) stands at 39.6 versus 27.4 in 2007 — a level exceeded only during the top 20 months of the dot-com bubble before it burst in March 2000. The combination of equities priced like 2000 and bonds priced like 2007 is without modern precedent.

4. **Nvidia's earnings (due Wednesday after the close) are the AI bull case's stress test: analysts project 79% revenue growth to $78.9 billion, with datacenter representing ~90% of that.** Hyperscaler capex projections for the top four tech companies alone have been revised up 89% year-to-date, reaching $812B and $968B for 2026 and 2027. But Nvidia has underperformed the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index over the past six months despite two GPU conferences and two beat-and-raise quarters — the AI narrative has broadened to the whole SOX index, diluting Nvidia's premium.

5. **Bond investors are already pricing the capex risk that equity investors are ignoring.** Pimco's Lotfi Karoui shows that hyperscaler bond spreads shifted from 15% tighter than investment-grade peers in 2024 to 25% wider now — a 40-percentage-point swing signaling credit market alarm. Using Robert Merton's framework, Karoui explains the asymmetry: equity holders are long a call on AI infrastructure (upside justifies the gamble), but bondholders are short a put on the same assets (the downside is real and the upside belongs to someone else). History says capex-heavy companies underperform the broad US index by 2.2% per year — and S&P 500 free cash flow margins have already reverted to their pre-Covid average of 9.5% even as headline profit margins rose 1.7 percentage points to 14%.

**Quotable:**
> "The combination of equities like 2000 and bonds like 2007 is unnerving. The big difference is that this time, there is a genuine force powering positively through the economy — the artificial intelligence revolution. Nothing remotely comparable was afoot in 2007." — *on the only thing preventing a direct replay of crisis conditions*

## AI's Impact on Work & Society

### 10 Job-Search Rules That Just Broke 📌
**'Lenny's Newsletter' via PubsforSubs** · email · 13 mins

Nikhyl Singhal (Skip Coach) interviewed three senior product leaders who recently completed job searches — Dana Ingraham (now leading agent products at Harvey), Briana Ings (now leading Loom at Atlassian), and Pei-Chin Wang (now founding her own company) — and extracted ten outdated assumptions about senior career transitions.

1. **AI collapses the new-job context vacuum.** The brutal first year at a new senior role used to stem from a context gap — not knowing products, people, politics, or history. Dana connected AI agents to every Harvey system on day one (Slack, docs, customer records, deal pipeline) and compressed months of ramp-up into days. Briana resolves unknown internal acronyms by querying an agent instead of tracking down colleagues. The "dumb-question tax" is gone, so imposter syndrome has less fuel.

2. **Founding is now fast, fun, and skill-additive even if it fails.** Pei-Chin left Modern Animal expecting to join another company and instead co-founded with two friends — three weeks before the conversation. Key shift: validation that used to take a year now arrives in a quarter, so the downside bet is smaller. Even if the venture fails, forced daily AI building produces hands-on fluency that makes the next senior employment role better than the one you left.

3. **Remote access is blocked at the frontier-lab tier specifically, not the whole market.** Briana (San Diego) cut 80% of her public big-tech list due to location requirements; Pei-Chin (Seattle) cut 70% even targeting private companies. The hardest doors are at young, SF-concentrated frontier AI labs. Hot mature companies that hire remote do exist — Atlassian is one — but finding them requires looking past the household AI brand names. The exception bar at frontier labs is "AI guru in the industry."

4. **Don't filter on "AI company" — filter on what you actually want.** Briana and Dana deprioritized "AI company" as a hard filter. Harvey's product IS AI; Atlassian treats AI as its top strategic priority across the board, with the most distribution to defend. Growing companies are heading to AI regardless of their founding story. Pei-Chin is the exception: her non-negotiable was AI as the core product, not a feature layer, which pulled her toward a narrower set.

5. **Titles and brand-name credentials are out; specific built work is in.** Pei-Chin's CPO title didn't open conversations at AI-native companies: "They don't care that I was a CPO. What have you built?" Dana receives cold outreach daily and opens almost none of it — except messages with an HTML prototype attached showing a feature Harvey should build. At interview, Briana found "almost every product question was AI related" and the bar was hands-on fluency she could trace to her own builds, not memorized theory.

6. **At a target company, take any interview including IC roles.** Briana said yes to individual contributor interviews at companies she genuinely wanted to join. The bet: a slightly smaller role at a top company beats a level-protecting role at a less compelling one. In a turbulent market, company quality is harder to recover than level. The caveat is that this only works with high conviction about the company — otherwise you're just settling.

7. **A non-traditional resume is an advantage if you teach the interviewer.** When an Atlassian interviewer told Briana "I literally don't know what to ask you. I don't know how to deal with your resume," she turned it into an opening: she walked him through her story and coached him on what to ask. The translation move — "The problems I solved at a property management company are similar to the problems you solved here, you just haven't heard of them" — demonstrates the exact quality a senior leader needs: structuring ambiguity in real time.

8. **Don't take an AI class — build something you actually want.** None of the three followed a curriculum. Dana started with Claude Code, felt like "a sorcerer," and pinged her Airbnb engineers asking if this was what they always felt like. She built iOS apps for her daughter and personal travel tools. Pei-Chin's first Claude Code afternoon flipped her emotional state from "I'm behind" anxiety to self-actualization joy within weeks. Hands-on building produces a fluency that other senior leaders detect in three minutes of conversation; reciting read concepts doesn't.

9. **Signal your constraints explicitly and watch whether the response is real or scripted.** All three ruled out 9-9-6 companies upfront. Inside the remaining set, Dana tested whether she was "around a lot of parents" and whether founders were "mature enough to understand what it means to have a life outside of work." The tell is behavioral, not verbal: do parents actually leave at 5, or are they in Slack at 7? Dana uses a physical brick device that locks her out of her phone from 5–7 PM; Briana holds hard blocks at 7–8:30 AM and 5–7:30 PM. Both found teams that respected these without negotiation.

10. **Hold professional identity loosely — it's getting reformatted whether you agree or not.** Dana (the PM) now owns agent platforms and ships iOS apps herself. Briana (startup CPO) leads a division at a far larger company. Pei-Chin (lifelong employee) is a founder. Each shift looked unlikely a year ago. What united all three on the other side was "smiling exhaustion" — working hard, going long, and surprised by how good it feels. The word "joy" came up four times in the first thirty minutes of the conversation.

**Quotable:**
> "My credentials didn't matter. They don't care that I was a CPO. What have you built?" — *Pei-Chin Wang on interviewing at AI-native companies*

### Neil Dutta on how economists are missing the macro impact of AI 📎
**Bloomberg Odd Lots** · email · 8 mins

1. **The "AI barely moves GDP" narrative is technically correct but economically misleading.** AI-related business investment — covering information processing equipment, software, data centers, manufacturing, and power facilities — now totals nearly 6% of US GDP, exceeding the dot-com peak. But because much of the hardware is sourced from Taiwan and Korea, standard GDP accounting nets out those imports, leaving only a modest quarterly contribution to aggregate demand over the past four quarters.

2. **AI capex dollars don't vanish — they create a real-economy boom in Asia that loops back to US equities.** Korean and Taiwanese export growth has been remarkable, and companies like Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom, and Micron book substantial US revenues from that supply chain. Treating AI capex as a closed-economy accounting exercise misses the earnings channel through which this spending eventually inflates US equity prices.

3. **A $2 trillion slice of industrial America now trades like a semiconductor ETF.** Running a 1-year daily-return correlation between every S&P 500 name and the SMH semiconductor ETF reveals 15 non-tech companies — collectively worth ~$2 trillion — with correlations of 0.50 or higher. Twelve are Industrials: Vertiv (0.68), Eaton (0.65), Caterpillar (0.62), Cummins (0.61), plus Hubbell, Comfort Systems, EMCOR, Quanta, Emerson, Rockwell, Wabtec, and GE Vernova. Their books have effectively become AI capex order books (Caterpillar sells generators into hyperscalers; Vertiv sells cooling; GE Vernova sells gas turbines for data center power) — but GICS sector classifications have not caught up.

4. **Equity wealth — now at a record 42% of US household financial assets — is doing more work for consumption than income.** Real personal consumption expenditures are running more than twice the pace of real disposable income over the last year. A consumption function estimated from 1997Q1–2025Q4 (excluding the COVID transfer years) yields an equity MPC of ~1.1 cents per dollar of wealth. With home price appreciation flat and real per-capita earned incomes declining, equity gains are the primary marginal driver of spending — making the AI equity boom structurally equivalent to an AI consumption boom.

5. **California alone has collected $66 billion in extra tax revenue since 2024 because of AI-driven equity gains — entirely invisible to investment-side accounting.** When RSUs vest, employers withhold at the share price on vest date, so rising equity prices mechanically inflate state income tax receipts. California's five largest tech firms (Apple, Google, Nvidia, Broadcom, Meta) are worth $15 trillion and represent over 60% of the Nasdaq 100's total value. The state's Legislative Analyst's Office attributes one-third to one-half of withholding growth to equity compensation; had revenue grown at the pre-AI 2018–19 pace, California would have collected $196 billion over eight quarters instead of the $262 billion it actually collected. Every 10pp gain in equities translates into roughly 5.5pp of California PIT growth; the Mag 7 delivered 30.5% YoY in the most recent quarter versus an S&P 500 historical median of 8–10%, so the same coefficient is applied to a much larger return. New York, Washington, Massachusetts, and Texas have variants of the same channel.

6. **The real risk is not that AI is oversold — it's that a correction would hit much harder than headline market beta implies.** Rapid productivity growth from AI remains forecast rather than present-day reality; labor productivity has stalled in recent quarters, and IT commodity prices are rising while real incomes fall — unusual for a supposed productivity boom. Meanwhile, equity markets are already pricing company profitability near record highs over the next twelve months. If the AI cycle cools, the wealth-effect drag on consumption would not be confined to the Mag 7 — it would flow through $2 trillion of industrial equity, state government budgets in five large states, and household spending that has no income or housing-wealth backstop.
